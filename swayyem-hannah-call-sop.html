Call 1 — Discovery and the Gap

The Opening Synopsis — Use This First

Give a 20-second overview before you ask anything

If they seem unclear on what SwayyEm does — or if they haven't said anything that signals they know — give the synopsis before going into discovery questions. Without it, they'll feel like you're asking them questions for a call they don't understand, and they'll get frustrated. You can still ask first on calls where it's obvious they already know, but when in doubt, lead with the synopsis and then go straight into questions.

Standard opening (use this most of the time)

"Real quick before we dive in — we help brands get featured in the biggest publications like Forbes and USA Today, the ones that show up when your customers search on Google or ask ChatGPT. We work with companies like Intuit, QuickBooks, Visa, and Binance. The goal of this call is just to understand what you guys are doing right now and see if there's a fit to go deeper. Does that make sense?"

If they clearly don't know what you do at all

"So, just to give you a quick picture — SwayyEm lives at the intersection of digital PR, SEO, and GEO. We get clients placed on publications like Forbes and USA Today, and those placements are what show up in Google search and AI results when your buyers are researching solutions. We work with companies like Intuit, Visa, and Binance. That's what we do. Now — tell me a bit about what you're doing right now for digital visibility."

The rule: The synopsis is not a pitch — it's 20 seconds of context so they know what this call is about. Say it and move on. Don't over-explain. The goal is to create enough understanding that they can answer your questions, not to close them on the synopsis alone.

Opening · First 5-7 minutes

Run discovery as a clear progression — not random questions

Every question should be moving them toward one outcome: they see the gap, and they want to get on Call 2 to see the analysis. Questions that don't serve that progression waste time and create confusion. Below is the order that works.

Question 1 — Current state

"What are you currently doing for digital visibility — SEO, paid ads, any digital PR, anything like that?"

Question 2 — What's working / not working

"What's been working, and where have you hit walls?"

Question 3 — What they're trying to achieve

"What's the main thing you're trying to drive — new customers, brand visibility, something else?"

Question 4 — Competitors (for the analysis)

"Who are your main competitors? Even just one or two names."

From here: pull up the search results live, show the gap, and move to booking Call 2. That is the whole progression. Don't deviate.

Call 2 — The Analysis and the Close

Before Call 2

Build the analysis — keep it simple and visual

Matt has updated the analysis format. It's no longer heavy SEO data — it's clean, visual, and easy for any business owner to understand in 5 minutes. The goal is: show them the gap visually, recommend the publications, explain the rollout, and go to pricing. That's it.

  • Section 1 — Where you're not showing up: Screenshot Google AI and ChatGPT results for their keyword. Competitors present, they're not. No explanation needed — it's self-evident. Add a line under each: "You're not here."
  • Section 2 — Recommended publications: 3–5 specific publications that make sense for their industry and their ICPs. Why each one. Brands they'll recognize (Forbes, USA Today, VentureBeat, or sector-specific). Keep this geographic + industry focused, not keyword-technical.
  • Section 3 — What a rollout looks like: Simple timeline — what gets placed when, what they can expect to see in 30/60/90 days. Not a guarantee of traffic, but a clear picture of what execution looks like.
  • Section 4 — Pricing: Package recommendation based on their goals. Walk them through it on the call.

If they have no search traffic or minimal digital presence, skip the traffic graph — it won't tell a useful story. Instead, lead with the competitor gap (who is showing up that they should be next to) and the publications that cover their space. Simple is better.

To close the next step

Situations You'll Run Into — Know How to Handle These

Situation 01

You don't know the answer to something technical

Do not guess. Do not stumble around it. Matt is okay with you not knowing technical details — what he's not okay with is you making something up or giving a vague non-answer. Pick one of these and move on.

If Matt is on the call

"Actually, Matt's on the call with me — Matt, do you want to take that one?"

If Matt is not on the call

"That's a great question — I want to make sure I give you an accurate answer on that rather than guessing. Let me bring that back to our team and I'll get you the answer before our next call."

You have permission to not know. "I don't know, let me check" is a sign of professionalism, not incompetence. What makes you look incompetent is giving a wrong or wishy-washy answer. Say you'll follow up, and then actually follow up.

Situation 02

They claim they already show up in AI results

Some prospects will say something like "Oh, we show up in ChatGPT sometimes — it changes day to day." That is factually wrong, and if you let it go, they'll walk away believing something false that will make them think your service isn't necessary.

How to handle it

"Actually, that's a really common misconception. AI models like ChatGPT don't change their results day to day — they train on data over much longer cycles, months at a time. What you might be seeing is variation in how you phrased the search. If you're ranking one day and not the next, that would be traditional Google results, not AI. The AI results are actually more stable — and if you're not in them consistently, you're not in them. Want to pull it up right now and take a look?"

Situation 03

They push hard on ROI — "How do I know this will work?"

If someone is ROI-focused (usually e-commerce or conversion-focused businesses), don't just ask one number. You need the full picture to make the case. Do not answer this with just LTV.

  • What's their current monthly organic traffic?
  • What's their conversion rate on that traffic?
  • What's their average order value?
  • What's their LTV?
If you have all those numbers, here's how to frame it

"Okay, so let's say we run a campaign similar to what we did for Campaign Monitor — where we drove a 200% monthly organic traffic increase and 150,000 new organic visits over the course of the engagement. If we took your current conversion rate of [X%] and applied it to that new traffic, that's [Y] new customers, at your average order value of [Z] — that's [total]. What would it mean for your business to acquire [total] in new revenue off of [package price]? That's the math we're working with."

Not every prospect needs the ROI conversation. B2B companies often don't have clean conversion data and aren't looking at it that way — they just want brand presence and authority. Read who you're talking to. If they're not asking about ROI, don't introduce the math unprompted.

Situation 04

The first 30 seconds determine the whole call

Whatever behavior you allow in the first 30 seconds will repeat itself throughout the entire call. If they take over, talk over you, or turn it into a Q&A where they're asking the questions — and you let it happen — that will be the dynamic for the rest of the call. You won't recover it.

The fix is simple: own the first 30 seconds. Give the synopsis, set the structure ("the goal of this call is X, I'd love to start by understanding Y"), and move into your first question. Do that and you control the call. Skip it and you're reacting the whole time.

Opening frame — say this in the first 30 seconds

"Cool — so just so we're making good use of the next 15 minutes: we help brands get placed in publications like Forbes and USA Today, the kind that show up when your customers Google or use ChatGPT. We work with Intuit, Visa, QuickBooks — brands at that level. What I want to do on this call is just understand what you guys are doing right now for visibility and whether there's a fit to go deeper. Sound good? So — what are you currently doing on the digital side?"
