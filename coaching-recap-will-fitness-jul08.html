JJ's Diagnosis — Why It's Different From Other Niches

Fitness high-ticket is uniquely hard to sell to a large creator's audience: ROI is intangible (can't put a number on "better physique" the way you can on a money offer), and Will's younger followers (~24–26 skew) don't have the runway to shrug off a $4K ask the first time they hear it.

Eubank's Team Comparable
$200–300K/month, but entirely on payment plans (Klarna/ClarityPay), no one paying in full, buyers skewing 18–21. Took months of nurturing before the audience saw him as a personal-branding authority rather than just "the fitness guy."
Ashton Hall Turnaround Playbook JJ Reused
Ashton was self-taking calls into a cold, chaotic audience. JJ's team stopped booking calls entirely for ~2 weeks, scripted daily high-level talking-head content on becoming a fitness coach, then relaunched with a new VSL and a straight "book a call" funnel — no webinar. Result: ~$150K/mo baseline to $370K the first month, $582K the second (before the month was even over). Content quality, not audience size, was the lever — same principle JJ points to in "Mr. Free Value" (John)'s content. Caveat: the growth spiral also triggered fulfillment breakdowns and a near class-action situation — a cautionary note on scaling faster than fulfillment can hold.
Setter Feedback — Confirms the Diagnosis
Tom's setters reported prospects are genuine fans who relate to Will, but either can't financially commit right now, or don't yet see him as the fitness authority — they'd rather go to a local 1:1 trainer with hundreds of reviews and a track record. At $4K–6K, Will is being compared against a commodity, and losing on proof.
04

Recommendation — Low-to-High, MRR First

JJ's call: step back from high-ticket for 2–6 weeks (not months — depends on how fast the audience responds to rebuilt content), and launch a low-ticket entry offer / MRR community first to build trust and buy-in before re-approaching high-ticket.

  • Entry offer in the $50–100/month MRR range — low barrier, builds runway and a real base before asking for $4K+
  • MRR covers Will's lifestyle overhead; everything beyond that is profit, and the agency's 30% cut of a much larger recurring pool beats a trickle of high-ticket deals
  • Low-ticket buy-in builds proximity and trust — makes the eventual high-ticket ask feel like a natural next step, not a cold ask
  • Keeps fulfillment risk low — JJ's stated concern with fitness high-ticket specifically is capacity: a batch of $10K sales that can't be fulfilled well leads to chargebacks. Fitness is the only niche JJ would run this low-to-high sequencing on
  • Reference case: Michaela Milhouse (~1M followers, ~$20K/mo off low-effort PDF sales) — projected to ~$90K/mo running a $197 low-to-high entry sequence instead
Optional Price-Ascension Play (Iman Playbook)
  • Open enrollment low (~$100), publicly document price increases ($150 → $300–400) with real screenshots of the raises and of people being turned away at the old price — manufactures FOMO and buy-in at scale
  • Once the base is built (~300 people), one-on-one upsell ascension: push CTAs in community chat and live calls, give priority access to existing members
  • Break the fourth wall — have Will publicly own the personal-branding pivot ("didn't feel aligned, want to refocus on fitness coaching and changing lives") to reconnect authentically before the relaunch
  • Find a unique differentiating mechanism/hook (comp: Nate Belmar's ~$300K single-day launch off a distinct visual angle) so Will isn't competing as a commodity trainer

Second Account? — Keep It on Main, Gate a Private IG

Tom raised whether to run a second, more niche account. JJ's recommendation: start on Will's main account. Use a private/backend Instagram as a reward mechanism — access unlocked only after the community paywall — for spamming CTAs and ascension offers to people who are already bought in. Not everyone gets access to the private account.

7K Untouched DMs — Setter Deployment

Instagram inbound has ~7,000 untouched DMs (6K+ in the last 30 days alone). Tom flagged that the 3 newly trained setters could go idle if the account shifts toward a lower-touch, low-ticket funnel.

  • JJ's proposal: point setters at the untouched DM backlog — real conversations, not link-drops, that lead into the low-ticket offer
  • Comp structure floated: ~15% commission to the setter on the low-ticket sale itself; the recurring MRR flows to Will/the agency
  • Framed as a volume play (JJ's storage-unit/live-shopping analogy) — individually thin margins, but it compounds at scale
  • Setters can also be shifted to other Sales.io offers if there isn't enough volume on Will's account to keep them full-time
  • Tom confirmed current setter coaching feedback is positive — no overpromising going in, so no complaints about the pivot so far
