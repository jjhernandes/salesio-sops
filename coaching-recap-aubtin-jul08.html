Aubtin's Own Catch — The VA Prospect

Aubtin brought his own recent example unprompted: a prospect paying a VA $30K/year, half of it spent on one reporting task. Pitch was "automate that task, save half the salary, redeploy that time elsewhere" — logically sound, but it didn't land as worth $25K. Aubtin's self-diagnosis: he'd split focus between that angle and another pain point, going halfway deep on both instead of all the way on one.

JJ's Sharpened Version
"Save half the salary" invites resistance — the prospect starts thinking about what else they'd want that salary to cover instead of losing the frame. The fix: once they name a pain, ask why it's the most important thing to resolve — and if the answer doesn't hold up ("is that really the number one thing? Is there something else I'm not seeing?"), push there instead of accepting the first answer at face value. Certainty on the real number beats a generic efficiency argument every time.
Aubtin's Reaction
"That was gold." He connected it immediately to his own pattern — building value by touching a bunch of things (going horizontal) instead of committing to one vertical and proving certainty on it before moving to solution.

The Framework — Simplest Form

Future Pace

Intro/rapport, then a wide read on current reality — what they're doing now, across the board. They'll usually surface their own problems unprompted. The moment multiple pains show up, ask directly: "What's the one thing we're trying to resolve for today?" Everything downstream — problem awareness, solution awareness, future pacing — gets built on that single anchor. Cost-of-inaction/consequence framing can follow if the emotion is genuinely there, but JJ doesn't recommend pushing it hard with sophisticated buyers — it reads as manipulative to people who are used to being sold to.

Every Indecision Is Rooted in Confusion
Not always obvious confusion ("I don't understand") — sometimes it's "I don't see how this actually applies to my situation," which was exactly Kale's issue. Getting hyper-specific on one use case removes that fog. JJ's own numbers when he ran this discipline: 78% close rate in his best month at Emonz, never dropped below 50% — purely from getting fast and automatic at isolating the one thing.

Why Humans Buy — Sent Live on the Call

Normally week 7 material — JJ unlocked it early and sent the HTML doc directly in the Zoom chat because it's directly relevant to the clarity gap. Core premise: people decide on emotion and justify with logic. Once you find which driver is live for a given prospect, the sale becomes about surfacing it, not persuading them.

Escape Pain
Highest-leverage driver, every time. If the pain is real and specific (Kale's $56K), lead here first.
Reach a Future Identity
Buying who they'll become — e.g. "I want to be an AI-first company." They're buying the identity, not the tool.
Protect / Reinforce Identity
Illustrated with the Josh Lyons story — surface reason was ROI logic, real driver was status: "he has eyeballs I don't have, and he's beating me."
Trust in You
JJ's read: not a gap for Aubtin at all — he earns this consistently, partly through the offer itself, partly through Dan Martell association.

A fifth angle sits underneath all of them: once you show someone the gap between where they are and where they could be, they can't unsee it. That's the mechanism behind digging into specifics (the $56K, the pregnant employee's role, the exact monthly cost) rather than staying conceptual.

Leverage Already Available — Assumed Authority

Working with Dan Martell is real leverage in the room — prospects register it as "this guy is legit" before Aubtin says a word. JJ's own parallel: at Emon's, he built a background that echoed the founder's setup, and prospects assumed real proximity to the founder — it measurably shifted how calls opened. The advice isn't to lean on the association instead of substance — it's to combine it with real clarity. Confident conviction plus specific certainty about the one thing is what actually closes; conviction alone just feels like a pitch.

Certainty Sells at Any Price
People pay $25K, $50K, $100K for certainty of ROI, not for the offer itself. JJ's own personal examples: $200/week for a cleaning service because the tangible payoff (better week, better environment) is obvious to him. $1,000/week for a trainer because he can trace it directly to $10K/week in results. Nobody buys without seeing that line clearly — the job on the call is to draw it, not to convince someone the line exists.
