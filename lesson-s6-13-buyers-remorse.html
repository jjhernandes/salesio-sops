Handling Buyer's Remorse

The sale doesn't end when they say yes. The 24-48 hours after the close is when doubt peaks. What you do at the moment of close determines whether the commitment holds.

What Buyer's Remorse Actually Is

Buyer's remorse is the discomfort that follows a big decision. It's not a sign the person made the wrong choice — it's a neurological response to commitment. The brain second-guesses large decisions as a protective mechanism.

It peaks in the first 24-48 hours after the call. This is the window where chargebacks happen, texts come in saying "actually I need to cancel," and deals that seemed solid dissolve.

The antidote is not a better close. It's what you say at the moment of close and in the immediate follow-up. You're not fighting remorse after it arrives — you're inoculating against it while you're still on the phone.

The Mental Vaccine — Lines at the Moment of Close

Use these at the close, once they've committed — not before. The goal is to reinforce the decision they just made, not the program they just bought.

Vaccine 1 — Reinforce the decision, not the product
"I want to say something. A lot of people get off a call like this and second-guess themselves for the next 48 hours. I want you to know — the decision you just made wasn't about a program. It was about deciding you're serious. That part doesn't change."
Why: Separates the decision from the price. The decision was about identity. Identity is harder to walk back than a financial commitment.
Vaccine 2 — Name the doubt before it hits
"In the next day or two, you might get a thought that says 'wait, did I just make the right call?' That thought is normal. It shows up for basically everyone who makes a serious investment in themselves. When it does — come back to why you made this decision today, not the number."
Why: Naming the doubt before it arrives defuses it when it does. The prospect expects it now instead of being caught off guard by it.
Vaccine 3 — Lock in the next step immediately
"Here's what's happening right now: [onboarding step, first call date, Slack invite]. The fastest way to feel good about this decision is to start taking action on it today."
Why: Momentum is the antidote to remorse. Getting them into action immediately anchors them in the decision through behavior, not just emotion.

Post-Close Follow-Up Protocol

  • Same day (within 2 hours): Send a brief, warm message — not a sales message. "Fired up to have you in. Your first [session/call] is [date]. Here's what to expect." Makes the program feel real immediately.
  • Evening of day 1: If you sense any hesitation during the call, or if it was a big decision for them — a brief check-in. "How are you feeling? Any questions since we talked?" Shows you care about the person, not just the transaction.
  • Day 2-3: Introduce them to the community or next step. The longer they wait to engage, the more remorse builds. Action is the cure.

If Remorse Shows Up Anyway

If you get the "I changed my mind" message: Don't panic. Don't immediately defend or justify. Ask one question first: "What specifically shifted for you since we talked?" The answer tells you whether this is fixable (usually it is) or whether they were never fully bought in (and you need to let them go with grace).

  • Don't argue the value. Arguing about why they should stay sounds like you need the money. It accelerates the exit.
  • Get specific. "What specifically is the concern?" — vague remorse is fixable. A specific concern is a specific conversation.
  • Bring them back to the decision. "You told me X was the reason you were serious about this. Is that still true?" Remind them of their own words from discovery.
  • Know when to release. Some people aren't ready. Holding someone in a program they've mentally left damages the community and your reputation. Release them cleanly when that's the right call.
