Qualifying on the Set

What to know before you book, how to find out without it feeling like an interrogation, and what to do when someone is almost — but not quite — a fit.

What to Qualify

Four things must be true for a booked call to have a real chance of converting. Budget capacity. Decision-making authority. Timeline. Genuine interest. Know these before you send the calendar link.

You don't need their exact finances. You don't need a verbal commitment. You need enough signal on each one to know they belong on the closer's calendar.

01
Qualifier
Budget Capacity
Not "do you have $7,500?" — that's too direct and creates defensiveness. Instead: confirm they have income coming in and that investing in their growth is something they'd consider. "Are you actively making money in sales right now, or are you pre-revenue?" and "Is investing in your skill set something you're in a position to do, or is timing off?" are your two budget signals.
"Is investing in that kind of growth something you're in a position to do right now, or is timing off?" — Simple. Non-threatening. Tells you what you need to know.
02
Qualifier
Decision-Making Authority
Will they be the one deciding? Or is there a partner, spouse, or financial gatekeeper who needs to be part of the conversation? You don't need to ask "are you the decision maker" — that sounds like a B2B form. Just ask naturally whether they typically talk major decisions through with someone or handle them independently.
"For something like this — a real investment in yourself — is that a solo decision for you or is there someone else you typically loop in?" Low friction. Surfaces a potential obstacle before the closer hits it.
03
Qualifier
Timeline
Are they looking to make a change now, or is this exploratory? "Now" doesn't mean today — it means within the current quarter. Someone who's "thinking about it" for next year is a follow-up, not a booking. Don't book a closer's time for a prospect who isn't in motion.
"Is this something you're actively working on right now, or is it more of a down-the-road thing?" If they say down the road, ask what needs to change for them to be ready. That tells you what the real objection is.
04
Qualifier
Genuine Interest
Are they engaging with real curiosity, or just being polite? A prospect who is asking follow-up questions, revealing their situation, and showing initiative in the conversation is interested. A prospect who gives one-word answers and is waiting for you to finish is being polite. Don't book the second type.
Genuine interest sounds like: "Yeah I've actually been thinking about this for a while — I just didn't know the right path." Polite tolerance sounds like: "Yeah, sure, that sounds interesting I guess."

How to Qualify Without an Interrogation

The Conversation That Qualifies

Qualification is not a checklist you run through. It happens inside the conversation you're already having. If you've opened well and the prospect is talking, most qualification surfaces naturally — you just have to be listening for it.

When they tell you they've been closing for a year, you know they have income. When they say "my wife and I have been talking about this," you know there's a second decision maker. When they say "I want to make a change this quarter," you know the timeline. You don't have to ask — you have to listen.

The bridge question does two jobs: it qualifies readiness to invest AND transitions naturally toward a call — without making it feel like a form or a gate.

Example: "Is investing in that skill set something you're in a position to do right now, or is timing off?" This confirms budget and urgency at the same time. One question. Two answers.

When to Ask Directly

If the conversation has been going well but you haven't gotten signal on one qualifier, you can ask — but frame it as clarifying, not gatekeeping. "One thing I want to make sure of before I get you connected with our coach" — this positions the question as a service to them, not a filter you're running them through.

The Minimum Qualification Standard

What Must Be True Before You Book

The closer's calendar is not a dumping ground. Every booking should meet this minimum bar before you send the link.

  • ✓
    Active income from sales or a related field. They're not pre-revenue. They have some capacity to invest even if it requires planning.
  • ✓
    Expressed a specific problem. Not "I want to make more money" in the abstract — a specific pain point that connects to what Sales.io solves.
  • ✓
    Open to investing in a solution. Said or implied that they would consider a real investment to solve the problem. Not just "yeah sounds interesting."
  • ✓
    Decision is theirs to make (or partner informed). If there's a spouse or partner, they know a call is happening and aren't blindsided by the investment conversation.
  • ✗
    Do NOT book if: They're pre-revenue and have no near-term income source, they're just "curious" with no urgency, they're in active financial crisis, or they gave you no genuine engagement — just polite tolerance.

When Someone Passes on Some Criteria but Not All

Don't force a booking to hit your numbers. If someone passes on interest and timeline but fails on budget or decision-making authority, you have a follow-up lead — not a call. Booking them anyway wastes the closer's time and usually results in a no-show or an unqualifiable call.

Situation Right Move
Interested + problem identified, but budget is tight right now Book the call. Budget conversation belongs to the closer. Your job is to confirm capacity to invest in principle — not get a commitment on price.
Interested + budget fine, but decision involves a spouse they haven't told Ask them to give their partner a heads-up that the call is happening before you book. A blindsided spouse kills the close.
Interested, but timeline is "next quarter" Don't book now. Ask what needs to happen before they're ready. Follow up at the right time. Patience on this one pays off more than a forced booking.
Engages politely but expresses no real pain or urgency Don't book. Follow up in 2-4 weeks. Forced bookings from people who aren't ready produce no-shows, not closings.

Exchange — Qualifying Without an Interrogation

LIVE QUALIFY — Natural Conversation That Surfaces All Four Criteria
Prospect
"Yeah I've been closing in the health coaching space for about nine months. My close rate is around 16%."
Income: confirmed (active closer). Timeline signal: 9 months in, actively working.
Setter
"16% for 9 months in is honest — there's clearly something breaking down in a specific part of the process. What feels like the weakest link when you replay your calls?"
Doesn't jump to the book. Gets specific pain surfaced. Prospect is now doing the work of identifying their problem.
Prospect
"Honestly it's whenever they say they need to think about it. I just kind of fold and let them go. My manager says I'm leaving 40% of my closes on the table."
Specific pain. Motivated. Problem identified. The closer has a clear angle before the call starts.
Setter
"That's one of the most fixable things in sales — it's not technique, it's identity. Is investing in fixing this something you're in a position to do right now, or is timing off?"
Budget + timeline qualifier in one sentence. Doesn't feel like a form. Follows naturally from what just happened in the conversation.
Prospect
"Yeah — I mean it'd be a real investment but I'm making money. I'd just need to know it's worth it."
Budget: confirmed. Interest: confirmed. That's the booking signal. Now you close for the call — not the program.
Setter
"That's a completely fair position. The call with our head coach will give you exactly that — not a pitch, just a real conversation about what's breaking down and what fixing it would actually look like. Do you make decisions like this solo or do you typically talk it through with someone first?"
Decision authority: confirmed. Then books with a binary choice.
