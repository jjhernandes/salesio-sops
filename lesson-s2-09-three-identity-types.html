The Three Identity Types

Every prospect is navigating three versions of themselves on a call. The rep who understands which one is driving the conversation can pitch to the deepest possible level of motivation.

Identity as a Sales Tool

Identity is how someone answers the question "who am I?" in a given context. On a sales call, that question runs in the background of everything they say. Their fears, their resistance, their excitement — all of it is filtered through their sense of self.

There are three layers of identity that show up on calls: Historical (who they've been), Role (who they believe they are in their function), and Social (who they are in their group). Each drives a different form of buying behavior and a different type of resistance.

The Three Identity Types

H
Historical Identity
Who they've been — the story they carry
Historical identity is made up of all the times they tried something and it didn't work. Every program that overpromised. Every goal they didn't hit. Every version of themselves they tried to become and fell back from. This identity drives fear of change and deep skepticism of new commitments.
Language: "I've tried things like this before," "I'm someone who starts things and doesn't finish," "I'm not sure if I'm actually capable of this." The historical self is speaking. It's protecting them from another disappointment.
How to pitch to it: don't dismiss the history. Acknowledge it. Then create a gap: "The reason the other things didn't work isn't that you failed — it's that they were addressing the wrong layer. This is different and here's why." Give them a reason the past doesn't predict the future.
R
Role Identity
Who they believe they are in their function
Role identity is about professional self-concept. "I'm a closer," "I'm a setter," "I'm someone who figures things out." It carries pride and shame simultaneously — pride in who they are, shame in the gap between that self-image and their actual results.
Language: "I feel like I should already know this," "I've been in sales for three years so I get it but..." (trailing off). The role identity is talking. They feel like admitting the need for help threatens their self-image as someone competent in their field.
How to pitch to it: honor the identity, not the gap. "The fact that you're even thinking this way tells me you're already someone who takes this seriously. What we're building here isn't remedial — it's elite-level refinement. The reps in this program are good. They're just trying to become exceptional."
S
Social Identity
Who they are in their group
Social identity is about belonging and reputation within a peer group — their team, their industry circle, their family. Decisions that might shift their status in that group create resistance. Decisions that elevate their standing create pull. This identity is often the most powerful motivator and the most hidden.
Language: "What would my team think if I..." or "I don't want to be the guy who..." or "I want to be someone people look up to." The social identity is driving. Belonging and recognition are the real currency.
How to pitch to it: "The people who come out of this aren't just better closers. They become the people their team asks questions. That's what this does to your reputation in your space."

How to Use Identity in Your Pitch and Close

Listen for the dominant identity

In the first 10 minutes, you'll hear which identity is loudest. References to past failures = historical identity active. Resistance framed around professional pride = role identity. Mentions of peer group, team, or family = social identity. The dominant one tells you where the real decision is happening.

Don't fight the dominant identity. Arguing with someone's historical identity ("that was different, this works") just makes it louder. Honoring it and reframing it moves people: "The fact that you've tried things means you've been taking this seriously for a long time. That's actually a signal — you're ready for something that works."

Call Exchange — Identifying and Pitching to the Historical Identity

VERSION 1 — Rep dismisses the history, triggers resistance
Prospect
"I've done coaching before and it didn't really move the needle. So I'm a little cautious."
Rep
"Yeah, most programs out there are honestly pretty surface level. This is completely different — we go way deeper than anything you've seen."
Dismissed the history by jumping to differentiation. Prospect's guard goes up. They've heard "we're different" before. It confirms their skepticism rather than addressing it.
VERSION 2 — Rep honors the history, reframes it
Prospect
"I've done coaching before and it didn't really move the needle. So I'm a little cautious."
Rep
"That caution is legitimate. What did those programs actually address — tactics, mindset, both?"
Took the history seriously. Asked a diagnostic question instead of defending.
Prospect
"Mostly tactics. Scripts, frameworks, objection handlers. Some mindset stuff but it felt generic."
Rep
"Then the thing that actually drives your results — who you are, how you show up, the internal state you bring into calls — was never touched. That's why the tactics didn't stick. They were built on a foundation that wasn't addressed. That's what's different here. We start with the identity layer, because everything else is downstream of that."
Reframed the failure. Named the mechanism. Historical identity is no longer an obstacle — it's evidence that they've been working on the wrong thing, not that they're incapable.
