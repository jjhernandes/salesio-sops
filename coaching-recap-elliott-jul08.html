Cognitive Dissonance — Elliott's Question

JJ's definition: cognitive dissonance is the gap between who someone believes they are and what the evidence actually proves. Example walked through live — someone claims "I'm super on top of my calendar and routine," but under questioning ("what does that look like day to day?") the evidence shows otherwise. That gap is where the tension — and the sale — lives.

Why It Matters
Never attack the identity claim directly ("no you're not") — that produces humiliation and defensiveness, not buy-in. Instead, ask questions that let the prospect surface the gap themselves. There's a dedicated Cognitive Dissonance & Persuasion lesson JJ is sending Elliott separately to go deeper.

The Anti-Fragility System

Originally developed from an Elon Musk mental model, passed to JJ by a mentor. The core idea: people become fragile because they only plan for the goal and never plan for failure. Anti-fragility means defining what falling short looks like before you start, so a setback is data, not a crisis.

The Structure — R.I.S.B.
R
Resources
I
Initiation
S
Speed
B
Breaking Point

You get the resources (framework, process, people to roleplay with), take the initiation to start, move with speed, and eventually hit a breaking point — the moment failure or success gets confirmed. At that point you iterate with what you learned and go again.

Worked Example — Elliott's Track
Desired Outcome
→ Get skilled enough to pass the baseline test
Failure Envisioned
→ Falling short in one specific stage — discovery, objections, or energy

Key correction JJ made: failure envisioned isn't just "not landing the job" — that's too vague to act on. Get specific to the exact stage or skill where you'd stall, so when it happens you know precisely what to iterate on rather than spiraling into "I'm not good enough."

Why It's Called Anti-Fragility
Most people fail and internalize it as a character flaw. The system reframes it: failure was already anticipated, so it's not a referendum on your ability — it's a checkpoint. Go back, take the resources you gained, move with more initiation and speed, hit the next breaking point. JJ: "There's no such thing as failure because you're not good enough — the only reason you won't get somewhere is because you stop."
