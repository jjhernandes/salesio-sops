The Decision — Scale Now, Optimize Later
The question was: do you optimize the team to 40% close rate first, or do you bring in more closers who can do 31% and scale the volume?

Scale. The unit economics are already working. $150 cost per booked call, $500 CAC, $3,500 AOV, 7:1 return. Micro-optimizations in the sales process are ongoing and never truly done. A single high-performing closer generates more upside than weeks of call review and process tweaks across the existing team.

The caveat: you scale what's SOPed, not what's chaotic. Before bringing on new closers, the top closer's process — pre-call, call, post-call, follow-up — needs to be documented. That becomes the standard everyone is hired against and trained to.

The Metric to Track
Operational Drag — Revenue Per Call by Closer
On enterprise teams doing $1M–$2M/month with 15+ closers, the performance gap between top and bottom is tracked as operational drag. The calculation is simple: how much revenue does each closer generate per call taken?

Your benchmark is your top performer. Everyone below that benchmark is costing you — either directly (calls they're taking but not converting) or as an opportunity cost (calls that could be going to someone better).

This makes scaling decisions clean: you know exactly what a new hire needs to produce to justify their seat, and you know which existing closers are dragging the floor down. JJ will send you the document with how this is tracked.
Once you have the SOP and this metric in place, every new hire has a benchmark to hit and a process to follow from day one. That's what makes scaling replicable instead of just adding chaos.

Selling to ADHD Clients — What's Different
ADHD clients operate on two layers that every closer needs to understand:

Ancestral scripts — hardwired fear responses that everyone shares. A cop behind you, a loud sound, being caught off guard. These are physiological and universal.

Life scripts — learned behavioral patterns. For many ADHD clients, that pattern is avoidance: non-commitment, always saying "I need to think about it," never finishing what they start. When a life script of avoidance is combined with a fear trigger on a sales call, there is no objection handling that fixes it. They shut off hard and leave.

You cannot close a fear response. You can only prevent one from starting.

The right closer for your clients is one who never lets the fear arise in the first place — through genuine safety, warmth, and composure that the prospect can feel from the first sentence. When ADHD clients trust the person they're talking to, they lean in harder than any other demographic. They feel it deeper. That's the upside. Safety first, then conviction. In that order, every time.

If closers understand this dynamic and can adjust their energy accordingly, 40–50% close rates are realistic.
Hiring Framework — Who to Look For
Non-Negotiables and What to Screen Out
Genuine curiosity: They actually want to understand the person, not just qualify them. You feel it — it's not a technique, it's a trait. They ask follow-up questions because they care what the answer is.
Composure under challenge: When you poke them or push back, they don't get anxious, defensive, or over-assertive. They stay even. They're not trying to control the frame consciously — they're just not rattled. That's the difference between posture and presence.
Transformational view of sales: Ask them how they define it. "Transference of belief" or "helping someone become who they want to be" — hire. "Convincing someone" or "closing the deal" — pass. The mindset shapes everything downstream.
Good energy that's not performed: The best closers for ADHD clients make you feel comfortable without trying to. It comes through in the first 30 seconds. Your Loom-prediction accuracy is strong — trust it.
Screen out — "Squares": Assertive but mechanical. Hits all the process steps but feels robotic. ADHD clients shut off fast when they sense they're being run through a script.
Screen out — Covert nice guys: Warm and likable but they won't challenge. Won't drive the sale forward. Won't hold frame when resistance comes. Good energy that gets walked over.
Screen out — NEPQ / script-trained closers: The inauthenticity is visible. ADHD clients pick up on it faster than most. If they came from a heavily scripted program, they need unlearning before they can serve your clients.
Interview Questions That Actually Reveal Something
Three Questions JJ Uses
Ask: "How would you define sales?"
If they say "convincing people" or anything transactional — pass. If they say "transference of belief," "transformation," or something that reflects care for the other person — strong signal. This tells you more about their calling than their resume does.
Ask: "On the highway — are you driving fast or at the speed limit?"
Tests self-regulation. A closer who can't regulate in small, low-stakes situations won't regulate in hard objection moments. Self-control in ordinary life carries over to composure on calls. This isn't a trick — it's a real signal.
Ask: "How much have you made in commissions? Can you show me?"
Not a test of the number — it's a test of honesty under pressure. Most people exaggerate and then freeze when challenged to prove it. Anyone who can answer honestly and show it without hesitation has integrity. That transfers directly to how they handle trust on a sales call.
