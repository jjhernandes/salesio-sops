The Math Problem

With four closers splitting $150K in revenue, even if you're outperforming everyone else, your ceiling is capped well below what the revenue should support.

Scenario Revenue Closers Your Take
Current (4 closers, 15%) $150K 4 people splitting ~$6K max

The $6K ceiling isn't because you're not good. It's structural. Four people splitting the same pool means even a stud performance can't break through. The revenue isn't the problem — the split is.

"The behavior that generates the most revenue should be incentivized. Doesn't matter if it's setting, closing, or confirming. Pay people based on the value they bring."

Compensation Variations to Consider
Option A: 2 closers at 15% + 2 setters at 5%. No base. Pure performance.

Option B: 2 closers at 10–12% + small base + 2 setters at 5% + bonuses for hitting thresholds. Attracts better setters, creates predictable incentive behavior.

Option C: Tiered commission — lower % until a cash-collected threshold, then higher % above it. Used at Daniel Isles' scale: 3% until $300K collected, then significantly higher. Creates a ramp and a reward.

The goal isn't just higher pay — it's that when the team is incentivized correctly, total revenue goes up and everyone earns more.

I've seen this work at Iman's team, Hormozi's team, and at scale with Daniel Isles. When you shrink the closing team and let setters handle pipeline, closers make more, setters make more, and the business does more revenue. It's not theory — it's what actually happens.
How to Bring This to Your Sales Manager

You said he listens — that he's implemented changes before when you've brought good ideas. That's a good sign. Here's how to frame it:

The Frame
Don't come in with a complaint. Come in with the math and a model. Show him the current structure, show him the gap, show him what the team could generate under a two-closer, two-setter model with the right incentives.

The argument isn't "I deserve more." The argument is "here's a structure that generates more revenue for the company and more earnings for the reps." He benefits either way — more revenue means his override goes up too. It's not a negotiation, it's a proposal.

If he's resistant, that's a signal. Not necessarily a reason to leave — but something to file away.
