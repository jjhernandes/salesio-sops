Why This Is Still a Win for the Manager

The case isn't "pay the team more for the same output." The case is specialized roles produce more revenue — and at $150K with $7K deals, there's a clear ceiling on what full-cycle reps can do on their own.

Full-Cycle vs. Specialized — The Real Cost
A full-cycle rep splits their time between prospecting, following up, and closing. A dedicated setter can book 2–3x more qualified calls per week than a closer who's also responsible for setting. A dedicated closer, with a full pipeline handed to them, will close at a higher rate because every call they're on is their one job.

The math doesn't even need revenue to grow much to work. If dedicated setters push the team from $150K to $180K — which is conservative — here's what that looks like:

At $180K — a 20% revenue increase from dedicated setters — the company keeps more money in absolute dollars, every rep earns more, and Ryan nearly 5x's his income. The manager's override on a higher revenue base also goes up.

"The behavior that generates the most revenue should be incentivized. Doesn't matter if it's setting, closing, or confirming. Incentivize people based on the value they bring."

How to Bring This to Your Manager

Don't frame it as "I deserve more." Frame it as "here's a model that generates more revenue and keeps the team happier."

The Pitch
"I've been thinking about how we can push past $150K consistently. One structure I've seen work at high-performing teams is separating the setting and closing functions — two dedicated setters feeding two closers.

The setters can focus entirely on booking quality calls. The closers can focus entirely on converting. Right now our full-cycle guys are splitting energy between both, and so are we. If we specialize — even temporarily to test it — the data suggests we should see a meaningful lift in booked calls and close rate.

I ran the math. Even if revenue just goes from $150K to $180K, the company keeps more in absolute dollars, and everyone on the team earns more. I can show you the breakdown."
→
Show him the table. Let the numbers speak. Don't oversell it verbally.
→
Propose a 30-day test — one setter, one closer pair — to see if booked call volume goes up before committing to a full restructure.
→
If he's open, suggest Option B for the setters — the base softens the transition and keeps them motivated. Pure 5% with no floor is a harder sell internally.
