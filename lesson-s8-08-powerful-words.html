Powerful Words

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. Some words open minds. Others close them before you even get to the content. Know which is which.

Words That Open Minds

The Principle

Words that open minds are curious, collaborative, and warm. They signal that you're with the prospect, not at them. They invite rather than instruct. They give the prospect room to move rather than pinning them into a corner.

These are not weak words. Collaborative language in the hands of a confident closer signals total security — you're not guarding your position because you don't need to.

Elite closers use language that feels like they're thinking alongside the prospect, not trying to convince them. The curiosity is real. The collaboration is real. The result is a prospect who arrives at the decision with you, not because you pushed them there.

  • "Help me understand..." — signals genuine curiosity, zero judgment
  • "Walk me through..." — invites narrative, gets you the full picture
  • "What would that look like for you..." — future-pacing without pressure
  • "It sounds like..." — tentative, opens rather than declares
  • "What's been getting in the way..." — collaborative problem framing
  • "What matters most to you about..." — clarifies their values, not yours
  • "Tell me more about that..." — the simplest opener to depth
  • "What do you think is missing..." — lets them diagnose themselves

Words That Close Minds

These phrases trigger resistance. Not because they're mean — because they signal you have an agenda. They show up in patterns: the fake-transparency opener, the "let me be real" pivot before a pitch, the "honestly" that means you've been less than honest until now.

Prospects have heard all of them. They know what comes next. The guard goes up the moment they hear the pattern.

Resistance-Triggering Phrase Why It Backfires Replace With
"Honestly..." Implies the rest of the conversation was dishonest. Signals incoming pivot or spin. Just say the thing. No preamble.
"To be fair..." Sounds defensive. Signals you're about to make a case for something that needs defending. State the fact directly without the qualifier.
"I'll be transparent with you..." Same as "honestly." If you have to announce transparency, you've already undermined it. Just be transparent. The content will show it.
"Let me be real with you..." Classic pre-pitch manipulator phrase. Prospects brace for the pressure to come. "What I'd say is..." or just state the reframe directly.
"No offense, but..." Offense is already implied. Puts prospect on defense before the sentence lands. Say it with care in the delivery. No disclaimer needed if the intent is clean.
"Trust me..." The two words that kill trust fastest. No one who's trustworthy needs to say this. Demonstrate trustworthiness through specifics, not claims.
"This is a no-brainer." Dismisses the prospect's intelligence. If it's so obvious, why are they hesitating? "Based on everything you've told me, this seems like a clear fit — what do you think?"
"I'm not going to pressure you, but..." Pressure is exactly what follows. Prospect already knows it. Drop the disclaimer. State whatever comes after the "but" cleanly.

Sales.io Language Guidelines

Words We Use
  • "Help me understand" over "Why do you think"
  • "Walk me through" over "Tell me about"
  • "What would that look like" over "Would you want"
  • "It sounds like" over "I understand"
  • "What's getting in the way" over "Why haven't you fixed this"
  • "When you come on board" over "If you decide to join"
  • "What matters most" over "Do you care about"
Words We Eliminate
  • "Honestly" / "To be fair" / "Let me be real" — fake transparency signals
  • "Trust me" — kills trust by claiming it
  • "No-brainer" — condescending
  • "Does that make sense?" — insecurity filler, kills flow
  • "Just" — weakens every statement it's in ("I just wanted to check in")
  • "Hopefully" — signals you're not certain of the outcome
  • "I feel like" — undercuts everything that follows with hedged ownership

Audit your own language first. Record your next five calls. Transcribe one. Count how many times you use the words in the "eliminate" list. That number is a direct indicator of how many credibility leaks are in your current communication.
