The Consciousness Levels

Where you are on the Map of Consciousness determines what you're capable of on a call. You can't access a state above your current level — you can only build toward it.

The Map of Consciousness

Source

This framework comes from Dr. David Hawkins' book "Letting Go." JJ teaches it because it gives you a precise language for something you've always felt but couldn't name: why some days you're unstoppable on calls and other days you can't get out of your own way. The map tells you where you are and what the next level looks like.

Every emotion, belief, and internal state calibrates to a level. Below 200 is where force lives — manipulation, pressure, scarcity. Above 200 is where power lives — genuine influence, service, connection. The threshold is everything.

Below the Threshold — Force

20
Shame
On a call

The rep who apologizes for existing. Shrinks under any pushback. Assumes they're bothering the prospect. Closes almost nothing.

30
Guilt
On a call

Feels bad about asking for the sale. Unconsciously sabotages closes because at some level they feel they don't deserve the commission.

50
Apathy
On a call

Goes through the motions. No energy. The prospect can feel the rep doesn't care whether this goes anywhere. Prospects match the energy and disengage.

75
Grief
On a call

Often follows a bad streak. The rep is calling from a place of loss — you can hear it in the flatness of their voice. Every no feels like confirmation they'll never figure this out.

100
Fear
On a call

Afraid of losing the deal. Every objection feels threatening. Reps in fear either freeze or over-push. Both signals transmit to the prospect who becomes appropriately cautious.

125
Desire
On a call

Wanting the sale badly. This is where most commission-motivated reps live. Desire is still force — wanting something from someone. Prospects feel the pull and resist it instinctively.

150
Anger
On a call

Can briefly spike performance — anger has energy. But it creates aggression that pushes prospects away. Common in reps on a bad streak who "decide to just go for it." Closes fast, burns bridges, generates refunds and chargebacks.

175
Pride
On a call

The rep who needs to be right more than they need to close. Defends their method when challenged. Can't receive feedback. Closes well on prospects who admire them, closes poorly on anyone who pushes back on their ego.

200 · The Courage Threshold — Force becomes Power

This is the line everything is built on. Below 200, you're operating through force — trying to get something from prospects. Above 200, you're operating through power — offering something to them. The prospect feels the difference. Courage is the first level where you can actually lose the sale and be okay. That's the key. Until you can genuinely be okay losing a deal, you're still below 200.

200
Courage
On a call

Can ask the hard question. Can sit with uncomfortable silence. Can tell a prospect this isn't the right fit and mean it. This is the baseline for consistent high performance. The rep is no longer afraid of the outcome.

250
Neutrality
On a call

Genuinely non-attached to outcome. Can hear "no" the same way they hear "yes" — both are useful information. This feels like extreme confidence from the outside but is actually just freedom from needing a specific result.

310
Willingness
On a call

Actively engaged in the prospect's best interest. Asking questions they actually want the answer to. The call feels collaborative because it is — the rep is genuinely trying to figure out what's right for this person.

350
Acceptance
On a call

Sees the prospect completely as they are — not as a commission opportunity, not as a category of objector, as a specific person with a specific reality. High empathy. High close rate. Never pushy.

400
Reason
On a call

Can instantly diagnose the real objection beneath the stated one. Their framework mastery is so internalized they don't think in frameworks anymore — they just think clearly about what's actually happening.

500
Love
On a call

The rep who genuinely cares more about the prospect's outcome than their own commission. Paradoxically, this is the highest-converting state. Prospects can feel total service and total non-attachment simultaneously. They buy because they trust completely.

The Two Traps Most Reps Live In

The Desire Trap (125). The entire commission-motivated sales culture breeds this. You're told to want it badly, to see the vision, to stay hungry. But Desire is still force. Wanting the sale creates the neediness that kills close rates. The irony: the harder you want it, the less likely you are to get it. This is why "wanting it" is not a strategy.

The Pride Trap (175). This is where skilled reps get stuck. They've developed real ability — they've closed deals, they've learned the frameworks, they've got results to point to. But pride makes them defensive. They can't take feedback without it feeling like an attack. They argue with prospects instead of getting curious. They'd rather be right than close.

The move from Pride to Courage requires humility — specifically, the willingness to be wrong about your approach. Most reps resist this because it feels like losing. It's actually the only way up.

The "Letting Go" Technique

From David Hawkins

When you feel something on a call — triggered, nervous, frustrated, needing it — the instinct is to suppress it or act on it. Both keep you below 200. The third option: feel it completely, then let it move through you without doing anything about it.

This isn't meditation or spiritual practice. It's a practical skill. When the objection comes in and you feel the familiar tightening in your chest, instead of rushing to respond: notice the feeling. Let it be there. Don't fight it, don't feed it. Most of the time it passes in under 10 seconds. What's on the other side of it is the response you actually want to give — calm, curious, certain.

The practical version for calls: build your 2-second pause before responses into your automatic habit. Those 2 seconds are not silence — they're the letting-go moment. You feel the reaction, you let it pass, you respond from clarity instead of from reactivity. That's the 200-threshold in practice.

Calibrating Before You Dial

Ask yourself honestly: what level am I operating from right now? Not what level you want to be at — what level you actually are. Be specific about the emotion. Fear looks like: "what if they say no." Desire looks like: "I really need this one to close." Pride looks like: "I know how to handle this." Courage looks like: "whatever happens here is fine — my job is to understand them."

  • ! Below 200 check: Am I going into this call thinking about what I need from it? (commission, a win, momentum after a bad week) — that's force. Stop. Reset before you dial.
  • ✓ At 200 check: Can I genuinely say "if this person isn't the right fit, I'll tell them" — and mean it? If yes, proceed. If not, you're still below 200.
  • ✓ Above 200 practice: Spend 2 minutes before the call thinking about one thing you're genuinely curious about regarding this specific person. Not rapport-building — actual curiosity. What made them book? What's their actual situation?
  • ✓ After a bad call: Use the letting-go technique on whatever you're feeling. Don't journal it, don't analyze it obsessively, don't call your friend to vent. Feel it, let it move through, come back to neutral. Then get on the next call clean.
