Step 4 — Solution Awareness

"What would need to be true?" Get the prospect to define their own solution criteria before you ever open your mouth about what you offer.

Why This Step Exists

Most reps define the solution for the prospect. They know the problem (Step 3) and immediately map it to what their program offers. That's a mistake — because you're now pitching to your criteria, not theirs.

When the prospect defines the solution criteria themselves, your pitch becomes a mirror. You're reflecting back what they said they need. That's not selling — that's confirming. And confirmation is far easier to close than persuasion.

The Difference in Close Rate

A rep who pitches their solution to a problem they identified is doing half the work the prospect needs. A rep who pitches a solution the prospect already defined and agreed on is running at a fundamentally higher close rate — because objections at price are almost always resistance to someone else's solution, not their own.

The Core Question

"If this was actually resolved — what would need to be different from how things are right now?"

Variations That Work

"What would need to change for this to stop being a problem?" — good for concrete, behavior-based issues.

"What does the version of this that works actually look like?" — good for reps who've tried things before and failed.

"If you imagine yourself six months from now having figured this out — what specifically is different?" — good for prospects who are motivated by future state but haven't articulated it clearly yet.

Don't answer this question for them. It's tempting to say "Well, what you'd probably need is..." No. Ask the question and hold silence. The prospect needs to speak their own answer. If they speak it, they own it. If you suggest it, they evaluate it — and evaluation creates resistance.

How to Anchor the Pitch to This Answer

The Technique: Note Their Exact Words

When the prospect answers the solution awareness question, they give you language. Their specific words. Write them down or remember them — because your pitch needs to use their vocabulary, not yours. If they said "I need someone to actually watch my calls and tell me what I'm doing wrong," your pitch leads with call review. In their words.

What Prospect SaidHow to Reference in Pitch
"I need accountability — I self-sabotage without it" "The weekly 1-on-1 with your coach is specifically designed for what you just described — someone watching your progress and calling you on your own patterns"
"I need to understand why people buy, not just scripts to say" "That's exactly what we built this around — the psychology of why people make decisions, not a word track you memorize"
"I need a job on a better offer" "We have 60-80 vetted companies on our job board every week and we guarantee placement — that's how we solve that piece specifically"

What You Know After This Step

  • ✓
    The prospect's own definition of a solution — in their language, not yours
  • ✓
    Which parts of your offer to lead with (the ones that match what they said)
  • ✓
    Which parts of your offer to skip or de-emphasize (the ones that didn't come up)
  • ✓
    A built-in close anchor — "Does what you just heard match what you said you needed?"
  • ✗
    What you do NOT know yet: how badly they want it (that's Step 5 and 6)

Call Exchange — Pitching Your Criteria vs. Theirs

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Defines the Solution
Prospect
"I just feel like I need to get better at closing."
Rep
"Yeah, what you'd need is a structured process — like a framework you can follow on every call so you don't have to wing it. We cover exactly that in our program."
Rep answered for the prospect. Now the prospect is evaluating the rep's solution — not their own. If they don't resonate with "framework," they push back. The rep has created unnecessary resistance.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Gets the Prospect to Define It
Prospect
"I just feel like I need to get better at closing."
Rep
"If you imagine yourself as a rep who closes confidently — what specifically would be different about how you're showing up on calls?"
Prospect
"I think I'd feel less desperate. Like I'd be able to sit with the silence instead of jumping in to save the deal. And I'd actually understand why the person is or isn't buying instead of just hoping."
Rep
"So you'd want composure under pressure and a read on what the prospect is actually thinking. Got it."
Now the rep has the criteria in the prospect's exact words. The pitch will lead with composure and buyer psychology — because that's what the prospect said they need. This pitch will feel like it was built for them personally.
