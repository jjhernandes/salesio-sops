The Core Principle

You're not hiring based on look and feel.
You're hiring based on the strengths you need
and the weaknesses you're willing to tolerate.
Every hire is a tradeoff. Know what you need most, and decide in advance what imperfections you can work with — before the interview starts.
What this means in practice

Before you post the role or review a single application, write down two things: the three strengths this role absolutely requires, and the one or two weaknesses you could coach through. This becomes your filter for every stage of the process.

If you don't know what you're looking for before you start, you'll make decisions based on energy and likability. That's how you hire the wrong person twice in a row.

The goal of the process isn't just to find someone good. It's to make them earn the right to be on the team. When they earn it, they protect it. When you hand it to them, they take it for granted — and you lose your leverage before they've taken a single call.

Reviewing the Loom Application

What you're actually looking for

A loom review isn't about whether they seem confident on camera. It's about what they reveal versus what they keep close to their chest. Transparency under low stakes tells you everything about how they'll perform under pressure.

Green Signals
  • Names the space specifically — "I've been setting in high ticket" not "I've been in sales"
  • Shares specific numbers — close rates, cash collected, offer sizes
  • Acknowledges a gap or weakness without prompting
  • References real context — offer type, niche, setter/closer structure
  • Asks a smart question at the end rather than just pitching themselves
  • Energy is genuine — not rehearsed, not performative
Red Flags
  • Vague about everything — "I've been in the sales space for a while"
  • No specific numbers mentioned at all
  • Sells themselves the entire time — no curiosity about the role
  • Heavy use of buzzwords with no substance behind them
  • Can't describe a real mistake or a tough call they lost
  • Appears to be reading from a script or notes

The key tell: How much are they hiding versus how much they're sharing? Someone who says "I've been in sales for three months" versus "I've been setting in high ticket for three months" — that's a very big difference. One is evasive. One is precise. Precise people know where they stand. Evasive people are managing your perception of them.

The 3-Stage Interview Process

Why three stages

A single interview with one person gives you one data point. Three stages give you culture fit, skills validation, and accountability — and more importantly, they make the candidate work for it. The longer the path, the more they value the outcome.

01
Stage 1 — Tom
Initial Screen
This is your filter. You're not trying to close them on the role. You're qualifying them the same way you'd qualify a prospect. Ask about their background, what they've set, what they've closed, and what they're looking for. Listen for specificity vs. vagueness.
  • Walk me through your last role — what were you setting, who was closing, what was the offer size?
  • Give me your best month in numbers — cash collected, show rate, set-to-close ratio.
  • What's a set you lost that you should have had? What happened?
  • Why are you leaving where you are now? (Listen for how they talk about their last team.)
  • End the call without a decision — "I'll be in touch by end of week."
02
Stage 2 — Taha or Team Lead
Culture Interview
This stage is about team and values fit. Whoever runs it should not be coaching them — they're assessing whether this person fits the environment. Are they coachable? Do they have ego problems? Are they a team player or a lone wolf?
  • What's your relationship like with the managers you've worked under?
  • Tell me about a time the team was struggling — what did you do?
  • What does a good team culture look like to you?
  • How do you handle feedback that's blunt or uncomfortable?
  • Outcome: a simple green/yellow/red on culture fit passed back to you.
03
Stage 3 — Tom
Skills Test / Role Play
This is where they earn the right. Give them a real scenario — an inbound lead, a cold DM, a warm follow-up — and watch how they operate. You're not grading perfection. You're grading coachability, instinct, and composure under pressure.
  • Give them a realistic setting scenario tied to the actual offer they'd be working.
  • Let them run it. Don't interrupt. Note where they go shallow vs. deep.
  • After the role play, ask them to self-assess first — what do they think went well? What would they do differently?
  • Then give your feedback. Watch how they receive it. Defensiveness here is a hard stop.
  • If they pass — make the offer. If they almost pass — tell them specifically what needs to improve and give them a week to come back. Don't lower the bar.

The principle that makes this work: When someone goes through three stages to earn a spot, they carry the identity of "I earned this." That identity is your retention strategy. They won't throw it away easily. The person who was handed the job doesn't have that — and you'll feel the difference within the first two weeks.

Red Flags That Are Hard Stops

Talks badly about their last manager or team. If they bad-mouth the last person they worked for, you're one bad month away from being next. How they talk about where they came from is how they'll talk about you.

Defends themselves when given feedback during the role play. Coachability is the most important trait a setter can have. If they can't take feedback in a low-stakes practice scenario, they will not take it on a live call. Pass on them.

Can't give specific numbers for anything. If they can't tell you what they set, what closed, and at what rate — they either weren't paying attention or they're hiding the numbers. Neither is acceptable on a data-driven team.

They're desperate. If you can feel them needing this job, that energy will show up in their setting. The person who sets from a place of need will push too hard, get ghosted, and burn leads. You want someone who sees this as the right opportunity — not their only one.

Trust your read — but don't trust it alone. Your gut is useful data. But it's not the decision. Run all three stages. A gut feeling that contradicts the role play result is something to investigate, not override the process for.

After the Hire

First 30 days

The first 30 days is a probationary mindset, even if you don't call it that. They should be earning their place every week through their numbers and their behavior. Set a BAM (Bare Ass Minimum) in week one — a floor they commit to publicly. If they don't hit it, you have the conversation immediately. If you let a miss slide in week one, you've set the standard for the rest of their time with you.

Firing

Firing is only hard when you haven't been clear about the standard from the start. If you set a BAM in week one and they know what's expected, a fire conversation is just closing the loop on an agreement they didn't hold. Be direct. Be specific. Be final. Don't negotiate a performance out of someone who's already shown you who they are.

The best thing you can do for your team is set the standard high at the front and hold it consistently. One person who underperforms without consequence tells the whole team the standard is negotiable. The fastest way to lose your best people is to keep your worst ones.
