Topics Covered

What was discussed

01
DM setter overlap — coverage without dropped leads
Micah and Stacy used to run shifts (Micah mornings, Stacy evenings) but moved to a go-to-goal model, which created a new problem: when both are in the DMs at once, or when Micah works late to hit his daily number, how do they avoid stepping on each other or leaving leads to go cold?
Attempted first: Instagram lead-labeling (available on business accounts). Khalil's team tried this on Jazz's account, but tagging a lead (e.g. "call booked") triggered an automatic message to that lead asking things like "did you book a call?" — so they turned it off.
Jalen will check with another team running lead-labeling successfully to see what's different, and report back to JJ to pass along.
Jalen's actual fix: Track meaningful conversations separately from raw volume — only the leads you get 2-3 sentences deep with, not everyone. Log username, name, and date in a shared Google Sheet Micah and Stacy both have access to, so either can pick up where the other left off. Paired with simple verbal communication ("hopping out for 15," "done for the day, grab anything new") — JJ added that this kind of over-communication is what the best teams he's seen (Hormozi's included) do by default, and it resolves ~99% of these handoff issues on its own.
02
Commission structure for overlapping sets
Khalil walked through the history: straight shifts → ManyChat auto-routing by lead (broke down because a setter's leads went cold whenever they logged off) → a commission pool split by percentage of qualified sets (broke down when one setter meaningfully outperformed the other). Open question: if Stacy carries a conversation but logs off and Micah sends the actual booking link, who gets the credit?
Jalen's take: Confirmed both Micah and Stacy are pure DM setters (not full-cycle). His view — it's not fair to give commission to whoever merely sent the booking link. Credit should go to whoever carried the majority of the conversation. His broader framing: don't chase raw volume, focus on converting the conversations already in motion; a team he currently runs holds an 80% show rate this way.
This matches what Khalil's team is already doing informally — Micah confirmed the working rule today is "most meaningful part of the conversation gets the commission, not who books it." Jalen agreed this is the right call.
JJ's addition: Referencing an earlier, larger DM team he and Jalen ran together (3-4 setters, thousands of messages/day) — separate the timing/response-rate structure from the commission structure. Don't force fixed shifts, but do set a clear response-time expectation (e.g. respond within 10 minutes in your active window). Layer in bonuses for response rate and activity, not just booked links — otherwise whoever is fastest to send a link "cherry-picks" every deal, which is what the current setup is designed to avoid.
03
Triage vs. phone setting — definitions, from zero
Khalil flagged this as the team's biggest open area — they've never run either function and he has no experience coaching it. Jalen drew the line: phone setting is calling a lead who came through the funnel but didn't book (or didn't finish the application) to get them on the calendar. Triage is calling a lead who already booked, when their intake/LeadFi data raises a flag, to confirm details before they get on with the closer. Clean LeadFi data means no triage call needed — it runs as-is.
Jalen's recommendation: it's worth having someone dedicated to phone setting specifically, since speed-to-lead — catching a prospect while their emotion is still high — is, in his view, the single most important lever a setting team has. Open question left for Khalil to decide: whether this is a standalone seat or split across existing DM setters and closers.
04
Jalen's 4-step setting framework
Applies to phone sets, triage calls, and DM sets alike. Phone intro: name the team, then immediately anchor back to what the lead filled the form out for ("you filled out a form with us to help with X") before asking for 5-10 minutes — this consistently gets a yes. Frame the call simply: "I want to see where you're at, where you want to go, and connect you to the right person if we can help."
01
Problem isolation — "Where are you at right now in your [X] journey that made you want to fill out the form?" Nothing else happens until this is answered.
02
Duration — "How long has this been an issue?" Longer duration = more frustration = more urgency. (Steps 2 and 3 can flip.)
03
What they've tried — surfaces whether past attempts failed, reinforcing the case for a different approach now.
04
Why now — "You've dealt with this for [duration] and tried [X] — why reach out for help now, and not a year ago?" Their answer becomes the buy-in/urgency used to prompt the booking.
Khalil's follow-up: how deep to probe without stepping on the closer's territory. Jalen's answer — stop once the prospect names where the problem is creating real friction in their life (relationships, day-to-day, etc.) and starts using words like "frustrated," "stuck," "trapped." That's the signal to stop digging and move to booking. He offered to send a full coaching call recording on this specific skill via JJ.
05
DM opening framework — "nurture and shoot your shot"
Jalen's core principle: the prospect has to opt themselves into the sales conversation — starting with "what's your goal?" or "what are you struggling with?" on a cold DM builds on a "muddy foundation" because the prospect doesn't realize they're in a sales conversation yet.
Ad responders (already opted in): open with authority, not gratitude — skip "thanks for reaching out." Ask a specific, context-dense question tied to the ad: "What are you currently struggling with in your fitness journey that made you reach out based on the ad you saw?"
New followers (cold, not yet opted in): send a soft, low-pressure message ("saw you followed, glad to have you here, let me know if I can help with anything") and wait. A generic "thanks, excited to be here" reply means they're not in buying mode — let the marketing keep nurturing them instead of forcing a sales conversation. If they instead volunteer a goal or problem unprompted in their reply, that's them opening the door — now shoot your shot.
Why it matters: Running a sales-qualifying sequence on every new follower and getting a "no" creates resistance and unfollows, which compounds and damages account health over time. Letting people self-select protects the follower base while still catching the ones who are ready.
Khalil's team's current DM opener (ask if they're on a weight-loss journey or just here for content, then ask permission before going deeper) got a green light from Jalen as directionally solid — the account-health framing above is the refinement to layer on top, especially for new followers rather than ad responders.
06
DM ads — scaling note
Jalen flagged that DM ads are currently performing very well across accounts he works with (one fitness offer got 8 sets in a single day off DM ads alone) and recommended Khalil's team lean back into them.
Khalil confirmed DM ads were FitMama's bread and butter historically, but hit a wall once spend passed ~$30K — the inbox got overwhelmed (this was the same volume crunch that broke the earlier 3-setter shift system). Proposed fix, which Jalen endorsed: tighten the qualification criteria on the ad itself to raise the barrier to entry and pre-filter volume before it hits the DMs.
