Stage 1 — The 30-Second Hook
Stage 1
The 30-Second Hook
Purpose: Make them feel like you already understand their problem before they say a word. This is not small talk. It is a pattern interrupt that signals you are different from the last rep they talked to.
Say This First
30-Second Hook

"You know how a lot of [their type — e.g. business owners / sales reps] reach a point where they're putting in the work but feel like the results should be better than they are — they're frustrated because the effort is there but the number isn't moving, and they start wondering if there's something they're missing that everyone else seems to know. What I do is help people like that figure out exactly what that thing is and actually fix it — not with another framework, but by changing the way they operate at the fundamental level."

Situation Bridge — Choose One
Option A — Ask about their current approach

"Can I ask — what does your [sales process / training / approach] actually look like right now?"

Option B — Check if it resonates

"Does any of that feel familiar — or is that not quite where you're at?"

Rule: Customize the hook to match the exact frustration your avatar walks in with. The more specific you are, the more they feel like you are already inside their head. Generic hooks produce generic responses.
Stage 2 — Control the Frame
Stage 2
Control the Frame
Purpose: Set the tone, establish why they're here, and put the focus entirely on them. You are not pitching yet. You are creating a space where they feel safe enough to tell you the truth.
Opening Line
Confirm the call

"Hey [Name] — this is [your name] from Sales.io. You booked a call with us [day they booked] — looks like you were looking into getting some help with [what they opted in around]. Is now still a good time to talk?"

Frame-Control Questions — Use 1 or 2 Max
"Before I get into anything — have you already figured out what you're looking for, or are you still in the 'exploring options' stage?"
"Do you have a clear picture of what solving this would look like for you, or is that part still fuzzy?"
"When you went through [the content / ad / post] that brought you here — what specifically made you stop and book?"
"What were you hoping would come out of this conversation?"
Rule: The question "what were you hoping would come out of this call" is one of the most powerful openers in sales. Their answer tells you their goal, their awareness level, and how bought-in they already are. Never skip it.

The Engagement Stage — 85% of the close happens here

Stage 3 — Map Their Reality
Stage 3
Map Their Reality
Purpose: Understand exactly what they are currently doing before you suggest they do anything different. This is not small talk. Every answer here feeds a later stage.
Situation Questions
"Walk me through what your [sales / training / growth approach] looks like right now — what are you actually doing?"
"How long have you been running it that way?"
"What made you set it up that way to begin with — what was the thinking?"
Probe
After each answer: stay curious. "What do you mean by that?" and "How so?" keep them talking without you having to ask a new question. Use them constantly.
Rule: Do not evaluate what they tell you. Do not say "that makes sense" or "interesting." Stay neutral and curious. The moment you react, they start editing their answers to manage your response instead of just telling you the truth.
Stage 4 — The Dig
Stage 4
The Dig
Purpose: Find out what they actually do not like about their current situation. Most people will not volunteer this. You have to make it safe to say it — and then ask follow-up questions until you get to the real thing.
Open the Door
"Honestly, how do you feel about how things are set up right now — is it working the way you'd want it to?"
Branch — Based on Their Response
If they say things are fine
"What specifically is working well?"
"Is there anything else that's been good?"
Then pivot: "It sounds like you've built something solid. If there was one thing you could change or improve right now — what would it be?"
If they have a problem
"What specifically isn't working?"
"What do you mean by that exactly?"
"How long has that been going on?"
"Has it had a real impact — in what way?"
Deepen the Pain
"Just so I understand what's actually driving this — beyond wanting to fix [what they said], what is the real reason you're actually looking to change things rather than just staying the course?"
Rule: Nobody loves 100% of their current situation. Even if they open with "things are going well," there is always something they would change. The pivot question — "if you could change one thing" — unlocks it without making them feel like they failed.
Stage 5 — The Pivot
Stage 5
The Pivot
Purpose: Even if they opened positive, find the thing they would change. Nobody stays in a conversation about improving something unless something needs improving. Surface it.
Pivot Line
The Pivot

"It sounds like overall things are moving — but if you could wave a wand and change one thing about where you're at right now, what would it be? There's always something."

If They Give You Something Vague
"What do you mean by that? Can you give me a specific example?"
"How has that shown up recently — like in the last 30 days?"
"When you say [their vague word], what does that actually look like day to day?"
Rule: Vague answers mean they do not fully trust you yet or have not thought it through. Ask them to make it specific. The more concrete the problem becomes in their own mind, the more real the pain is — and the more urgent the solution feels.
Stage 6 — Get Specific
Stage 6
Get Specific
Purpose: Attach numbers and reality to the problem. Abstract pain is easy to ignore. Specific pain — hours, dollars, time lost — is not.
Specificity Questions — Customize the metrics to your offer
"How many hours a week are you actually putting into [the problem area] right now?"
"And if you could get that down — how many hours would feel right?"
"If you had to put a number on what this is costing you — time, money, missed opportunities — what would you say?"
"What does a week look like when [the problem] is really bad?"
Probe
Numbers make pain real. If they give a vague answer, push: "What would you say if you had to put an actual number on it?" Then be quiet and let them do the math out loud.
Stage 7 — Pattern History
Stage 7
Pattern History
Purpose: Find out what they have already tried and why it did not work. This tells you their skepticism level, their investment history, and what you are actually up against.
History Questions
"Before today, had you been actively looking for a way to fix this — or is this the first time you've explored it?"
If they haven't looked before
"What kept you from addressing this earlier?"
"What changed that made now the time?"
If they've tried something before
"How did that work out?"
"What actually happened — and why do you think it didn't stick?"
If They Tried But Did Not Commit
"What stopped you from pulling the trigger with that company?"
Criteria Question — Always Ask This
"Just so I make sure what we do actually fits what you need — outside of fixing [their main problem], what would your ideal outcome look like? What are you actually looking for in something like this?"
Probe
"Why is that important to you right now?" and "Is there anything else?" after they answer the criteria question. Get the full list before you move on.
Rule: Past failed investments are not red flags — they are proof the problem is real enough that they already spent money on it. Use that. Someone who has tried and failed is often more ready to buy than someone who has never tried at all.
Stage 8 — Heaven Lock
Stage 8
Heaven Lock
Purpose: Get them emotionally invested in a specific, vivid future state — in their own words. This is the Heaven side of the Heaven/Hell framework. The more real the future becomes, the more painful the present feels by comparison.
Income / Goal Anchor
"You mentioned wanting to make more — what's the actual number you'd want to be at? Annually, what would feel like winning?"
If they say a low number
"Is that really what you're aiming for, or is that just what feels realistic right now?"
"What would you actually want if you could have it?"
If they say a real number
"With what you're doing right now — how close are you to [their number] a year? Be honest with me."
"If you keep doing exactly what you're doing — how many years before you're there?"
Vision Lock Questions
"Let's say we actually got you to [their number] in the next 12 months. What does that let you do that you can't do now?"
"What would that actually do for you — personally, not just financially?"
"How would your life look different at that number vs. where you are today?"
"How would that feel — really?"
Probe
When they say "less stress" or "more freedom" — dig deeper: "What do you mean by that? What would be different specifically?" You want their emotional truth, not a headline.

"The vision has to be theirs. If you paint it, they're just listening to you talk. If they paint it — they own it. Your job is to ask the right questions and stay quiet long enough for them to see it."

Stage 9 — The Cost
Stage 9
The Cost
Purpose: Make the cost of inaction more real than the cost of investment. This is the Hell side of the framework. People do not change because they see the upside. They change because staying still finally hurts more than moving.
Consequence Questions
"What happens if nothing changes — if you keep doing exactly what you've been doing for the next 3, 6, 12 months?"
"Why is this actually important to you right now? Why not just address it in a year?"
"If it keeps going this way — what does that do to [the thing they said matters to them]?"
"Is staying in this situation something you're willing to settle for?"
"And who decides whether you settle or not?"
If They Minimize the Consequence
"Have you thought about what it actually looks like if this compounds — not just stays the same, but gets harder to fix the longer you wait?"
"What's the realistic impact on [their income / team / goals] if this is still unresolved this time next year?"
Rule: Do not answer these questions for them. Ask, then stay quiet. The discomfort of hearing themselves describe the cost of inaction is what creates urgency. The moment you fill the silence, you take the pressure off.
Stage 10 — Conviction Check
Stage 10
Conviction Check
Purpose: Confirm they are ready to move, not just interested. Interest does not close. Conviction does. If this is missing, presenting is a waste of time for both of you.
Check the Readiness
"So how important is it for you to actually change this — right now, not eventually?"
"On a 1 to 10 — how serious are you about solving this today?"
"If you found something today that you genuinely believed would get you there — is there anything that would realistically stop you from moving on it?"
Bridge to Presentation

"Sounds like it's time to actually do something about this then."

Rule: If they say 7 or below on the urgency question, they are not ready. Ask what would make it a 10 before you present anything. Presenting to a 6 is how you end up chasing people who are "still thinking about it."

The Close

Stage 11 — The Bridge
Stage 11
The Bridge
Purpose: Transition from their problem directly to your solution using their exact words. The bridge is not a pitch. It is a mirror — they hear their own pain reflected back, and then you position the solution as the natural answer to what they just described.
Bridge Formula

"Based on everything you've told me — what we do might be exactly what you're looking for.

Because you mentioned [repeat their logical problem back to them] — and that it's been [how long it has been going on].

And on top of that, it's causing you to feel [repeat their emotional response back to them].

Here's what I do, and here is specifically how it would work for you..."

Rule: Use their exact words. Not a paraphrase — their words. When people hear their own language come back to them, they feel understood at a level that no amount of rapport-building achieves. This is what makes the transition feel inevitable instead of pushy.
Stage 12 — The Reveal
Stage 12
The Reveal
Purpose: Present the solution as the direct answer to the specific problems they described. Not a feature list. Not a demo for its own sake. Every pillar you present should be tied back to something they said.
Anchor Before You Present
Call Back Their Pain

"You mentioned you're [spending too many hours / not seeing the results / stuck at X number] — with your permission I want to walk you through a few things we do that directly address that."

Present in 3 Pillars — Tie Each One to What They Said
P1
[Ease / Getting Started] — "We don't just hand you something and leave. We build this with you so from day one you're not starting from scratch."
P2
[Built for Them] — "This isn't a generic program. We look at exactly where you are and build around your specific situation."
P3
[The Result] — "We're not done until it's working. Not set-and-forget — we stay in it until you're actually seeing the outcome you described."
Check for Agreement Throughout
"Does that make sense so far?"
"Are we tracking on the same thing?"
"What are your thoughts on that?"
"How does that feel compared to what you've tried before?"
Rule: Present your solution AFTER you understand the problem — never before. The presentation that happens before the pain work is dead on arrival. By Stage 12, they should already feel like the solution is inevitable. You are just giving them the specifics.
Stage 13 — The Decision
Stage 13
The Decision
Purpose: Pull a real decision — not a polite response. Neutral, confident close language. You are not begging. You are making it easy for them to say yes to something they already want.
Committing Questions — Use One, Then Silence
"Do you feel like this is actually the answer to what you described earlier?"
"Why do you feel that way — what specifically is clicking for you?"
"Do you feel like this gets you where you said you want to go?"
If Yes — Move to Next Steps Immediately
Next Steps Line

"Perfect. Based on everything you've shared — the next step is straightforward. We'd get you into [program / onboarding / call], you would work with [coach / team], and we start immediately. From there it's just about getting to work. Does that sound right?"

If Objections Come Up
Loop Back

"Tell me more about that — what specifically is the concern?" (Then address it and return to the close.)

Rule: One close line. Then silence. The rep who talks after asking the closing question is the one who loses. If they pause, let them. That pause is them buying — not them about to say no.
Stage 14 — Resistance Handling
Stage 14
Resistance Handling
Purpose: Address the real concern underneath the surface objection. Most objections at this stage are not about the thing they say they are about. Find the real one.
Resistance — Third-Party Approval
"I need to talk to my [partner / spouse / business partner] first."
"How does your [partner] feel about you investing in [the outcome they want] — are they supportive of that?"
"What would you do if they were not on board — would that stop you from doing something you believe is right for your future?"
"How are you going to get to [their stated goal] without solving this?"
Resistance — Money
"I don't have the money right now."
"If the money was not a factor — would this be the right move for you?"
If yes: "So the only thing standing between you and solving this is the funding — how do you think you could make that work?"
"What other ways could you access the funds to actually solve this — what options do you have?"
"Have you thought about financing — using what you make once this is working to cover the cost of getting there?"
Rule: When they say money is the issue, do not lower the price. Ask if money was not the issue, would they move. If yes — the real work is helping them find the path to the funds, not convincing them the price is fair. The value conversation was already won in Stages 8 and 9.
Stage 15 — Warm Extension
Stage 15
Warm Extension
Purpose: Every closed deal has a network attached to it. Get referrals while the relationship is at its highest point — right after they have made a decision they feel good about.
Open the Referral Conversation
"I appreciate the conversation today — can I ask, what do you feel was most valuable about what we talked through?"
"With that in mind — who do you know that is dealing with the same kind of thing you came in with today? Probably someone in [their world — industry / network / circle]."
"Tell me a little about them — why do you think I could help?"
Facilitate the Introduction
"What's the best way to reach them — and do you think it would make more sense for you to mention it to them first, or are you comfortable with me reaching out directly?"
"What do you think you'd say to them if you were to bring it up?"
"What if you just shared the problem you came in with today — and let them tell you if they're dealing with the same thing? If they are, then I can take it from there."
"Anyone else you can think of that might be in a similar spot?"
When Calling a Referral

"Hey [Name] — my name is [your name] from Sales.io. I was just working with [referrer's name] and they mentioned you might be dealing with something similar to what they came to us with. They said it would be worth a quick conversation. Is now an okay time?"

Rule: Referrals asked for at the right moment — right after a yes — convert at 3 to 5x the rate of cold outreach. Do not skip this stage. One deal consistently opens two more if you ask the right question at the right time.
