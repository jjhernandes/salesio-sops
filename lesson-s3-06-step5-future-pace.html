Step 5 — Future Pace

The specific life they actually want. "More money" is not a future. A vivid, emotional, concrete picture of what better looks like — that's what closes deals.

Vague Future vs. Specific Future

What Prospects Usually Say

"I want to make more money." "I want to be financially free." "I want to get to the next level." These are directions, not destinations. They don't produce urgency because the prospect hasn't actually visualized them. They're aspirational bumper stickers, not emotional targets.

The goal of Step 5 is to get them from the bumper sticker to the specific, sensory-rich version of what that future actually looks like. What's the number? What changes at that number? What does it feel like in their body to be there? Who else is affected?

When a prospect can see and feel their desired future state specifically, the gap between now and then becomes a source of motivation. That gap is your close.

How to Draw Out Specificity Without Interrogating

The Wrong Way

Rapid-fire questions about the future feel like an interrogation. "What number do you want? Why that number? What would you do with it? How long have you wanted that?" — the prospect starts to feel cross-examined instead of understood. The guard goes up.

The Right Way

One layered question that invites them to paint the picture. Then follow the thread of what they give you. You're exploring with them, not extracting from them. The tone is curiosity, not interrogation.

The future pace question: "If you actually hit that — what does the version of your life look like six months from now? Like walk me through what a day looks like, what changes, what you're doing differently."

Don't rush past vague answers. If they say "I'd have more freedom," ask: "What does freedom actually look like for you — like concretely?" Every time they give you a vague answer, go one layer deeper. You want a picture, not a category.

Why Specificity Determines Close Urgency

Future Pace QualityResult
Vague: "I want to make more money and have freedom" Low urgency. The prospect doesn't feel the pull of the future state. "Thinking about it" becomes the default because they're not standing at the edge of something that excites them.
Specific: "I want to be at $20K/month so I can move my mom out of the situation she's in. I want to stop feeling like I'm behind every month." High urgency. The prospect just heard themselves say something real. There's emotion in it. The decision now carries the weight of something they care about — not an abstract goal.
Why Emotion Drives Decisions

People buy based on emotions and compensate logically. The future pace step is where you give the emotional side of the decision something concrete to attach to. A prospect who has articulated a vivid, specific future will close faster and with less resistance than one who's operating from a vague aspiration.

What to Listen For

  • ✓
    Specificity: exact numbers, timelines, people involved, places, feelings. The more specific, the more real it is to them.
  • ✓
    Emotional language: words that indicate they're feeling the future state, not just describing it. "I want to stop feeling..." "I'd finally be able to..." "That would mean..."
  • ✓
    Stakes beyond money: family, freedom, identity, proof to themselves. These are the real motivators. Money is usually a proxy for one of these.
  • ✗
    Generic aspirations with no texture: "I want to be successful." This is not enough. Follow up: "What does successful actually look like for you — like what's the picture?"

Call Exchange — Vague vs. Vivid Future

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Accepts the Vague Future
Rep
"So what's the goal for you — like where do you want to be?"
Prospect
"I just want to be making good money and have my freedom."
Rep
"Yeah, totally. That's the goal for most people. Okay, so let me tell you about what we do..."
Accepted the vague answer. The prospect's "goal" has no weight to it. The close will be harder because there's nothing vivid to close toward. "Thinking about it" is easy when the future state isn't real to you.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Builds the Vivid Future
Rep
"If this was actually resolved — what does six months from now look like for you? Like walk me through it."
Prospect
"I mean, I want to be at $20K a month. I want to be able to actually pay off my debt and feel like I'm not drowning."
Rep
"What changes for you personally when the debt is gone — like how does your day-to-day feel different?"
Prospect
"Honestly? I'd stop waking up with that knot in my stomach. I'd feel like I'm actually building something instead of just surviving."
Rep
"So the number matters but what's underneath it is the feeling of being out of survival mode. That's real. How long have you been in that mode?"
Now the future has emotional weight. The rep knows what the close is actually about. This isn't $20K for its own sake — it's freedom from a feeling. The pitch and the close will speak directly to that.
