"Is This Worth It?" — The Three Doors Framework

When a prospect asks whether this is worth it, they're actually asking one of three completely different questions. The rep who gives the same answer every time is wrong twice out of three.

What "Is This Worth It?" Is Really Asking

The Surface vs. The Reality

On the surface, "is this worth it?" looks like a value question — the prospect is asking whether your program justifies the price. But one level down, it's almost never a simple value calculation. It's almost always one of three very specific questions, each of which requires a completely different response.

The rep who treats "is this worth it?" as a generic value objection gives a generic answer — usually an ROI pitch or a features recitation — and loses the call because they answered the wrong question.

The Three Doors Framework: Every "is this worth it?" objection is actually one of three doors. Identify which door the prospect is standing in front of before you say anything else.

The Three Doors

D1
Door One
"Does the program actually work?"
This is a program credibility question. The prospect is skeptical about whether Sales.io delivers results at all. They may have been burned by other programs. They want evidence that the method is real and that other people have gotten outcomes from it.
Language signals: "How do I know this actually works?", "What kind of results do your students typically get?", "Is this proven?" They're questioning the program, not themselves.
D2
Door Two
"Will it work for me specifically?"
The prospect isn't questioning whether the program works in general — they believe it does. They're questioning whether their specific situation, background, circumstances, or starting point means it won't work for them. This is a fit-and-specificity question, not a credibility question.
Language signals: "But my situation is a little different because...", "I'm not sure if this is designed for someone like me", "I'm pretty early in my sales journey — is this even right for me?" They're worried about their own fit, not the program's validity.
D3
Door Three
"Am I the kind of person who does something like this?"
This is the deepest and most invisible door. The prospect believes the program works. They even believe it might work for them. The hesitation is identity-level: they've never made a move this decisive before, or they don't see themselves as someone who invests in themselves, or they're afraid of what it means about them if they try and fail. This is an identity objection disguised as a value question.
Language signals: "I just need to really think through whether this is the right move for me", "I don't usually do things like this", "I'm just not 100% sure I'm ready." No specific criticism of the program. Vague hesitation that circles back to themselves.

Identifying Which Door — The Question to Ask

Before you respond to any "is this worth it?" objection, ask: "When you say you're not sure if it's worth it — what specifically makes you uncertain? Is it more about whether the program delivers results, whether it's right for your situation, or whether now is the right time for you?"

You're giving them three options that map to the three doors. Most prospects will self-identify. The ones who don't will give you enough language to read which door they're at.

The Response for Each Door

Response to Door 1 — Program Credibility

Lead with specific results from real people in similar situations. Not testimonials in aggregate — one person, similar background, what happened in 60-90 days. Add the guarantee: "If you put in the work and don't get results, you get your money back. That's not a marketing line — we've refunded one person in our program history, and it was a medical emergency."

Response to Door 2 — Personal Fit

"Let's talk about your specific situation directly. You mentioned [X] — I've seen people come in with exactly that starting point and here's what happened." Make it surgical. Address their specific concern about fit by finding the closest analog in your track record. If they think they're too early, show them someone who was also early. If they think their offer type is a problem, address the offer type.

Response to Door 3 — Identity

"Here's what I notice — when someone is unsure whether they're the kind of person who does this, it usually means they've spent a long time waiting for certainty before taking action. And the people who are most certain right before they start are usually the people who've been at the same number for two years. The ones who move forward anyway are the ones who change their situation."

Call Exchange — All Three Doors in Sequence

EXCHANGE — Rep Who Identifies Each Door and Responds Correctly
Prospect
"I guess my question is whether this is actually worth the investment. Like I want to make sure I'm not throwing money at something that's not going to move the needle."
Rep
"That's a fair question to sit with. When you say you're unsure — is it more about whether the program actually delivers results for people, whether it's the right fit for your specific situation, or something more about the timing or the decision itself?"
The three-door question. Rep doesn't assume which door. Waits for the prospect to identify.
Prospect
"I think it's more... I'm not sure how it translates to my situation. Like I'm doing remote sales but it's B2C not B2B, and I don't know if the approach works for what I do."
Door Two. Specific fit concern — not program skepticism, not identity. This is a B2C fit question.
Rep
"Good — that's exactly the right question to ask, and I can answer it specifically. Actually the majority of people in this program are B2C. The psychology-based approach we teach is built for emotional decision-making — which is exactly what drives B2C purchases. Let me tell you about someone who came in doing B2C solar and what happened."
Door Two response. Specific, direct, relevant. Addresses the fit concern with a real comparable example.
Prospect
"Okay that's actually reassuring. I guess the other thing is — I've never really invested this much in myself before. I just want to make sure I'm ready."
Door Three. Identity objection. Now the fit concern is resolved and the identity question surfaces.
Rep
"I hear that. The people who say 'I want to make sure I'm ready' almost never become more ready by waiting. What they do is accumulate more reasons why now isn't quite the right time. What would 'ready' actually look like for you — what would be different?"
Door Three response. Challenges the identity of waiting for readiness without being dismissive. Forces the prospect to define what "ready" means — which usually reveals there's no real condition, just fear.
