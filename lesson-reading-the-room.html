Reading
the Room

Using PASE to identify dominant energy in real time — and adjust your pacing, questioning, and approach on the fly.

What This Skill Actually Is

Not a one-time assessment

Reading the room is not something you do once at the start of a call. It's a continuous calibration that runs beneath the entire conversation. You make an initial read in the first 60 seconds, update it as the call progresses, and stay alert to the moments when the energy shifts — because it will.

The PASE method gives you the framework. This lesson shows you how to apply it in real time — the observable signals for each energy type, how to adjust your approach based on what you're reading, and what to do when the read changes mid-call.

The best reps in any room are the best readers in any room. They're not running the same call every time. They're running a different version of the same framework, calibrated to the specific human in front of them. That calibration is the skill. PASE is the lens.

What you're actually reading
  • Pace and rhythm — how fast they talk, how long their answers are, whether they pause or rush
  • Word choice — data words vs. feeling words, action language vs. deliberate language
  • Questions they ask — what someone asks first tells you what they care about most
  • How they open up — do they give personal context immediately or wait to be asked
  • Response to depth — when you probe deeper, do they lean in or get more guarded
  • Energy direction — are they moving toward the conversation or managing their distance from it

The First 60 Seconds

Your initial read window

The dominant PASE energy almost always shows up within the first 60 seconds — before the prospect has had time to perform a version of themselves for you. How they answer your first question, what they do with silence, whether they ask you something before you ask them, whether they joke or stay serious — all of it is signal.

You are not committing to a permanent judgment in this window. You are forming a working hypothesis. Everything after confirms, adjusts, or overturns it.

What to listen for immediately
  • Practical: They confirm the time, get straight to context, or ask a clarifying question before you've said much. Measured opener. Minimal warmth. They're assessing you.
  • Action: Fast hello, high energy, immediately says something bold or directional. "Yeah let's get into it." Already wants to move.
  • Social: Asks how you are. Mentions something personal unprompted. Laughs early. Wants to establish warmth before there's anything on the table.
  • Emotional: Quieter opener. Pauses. More deliberate in how they answer your first question. Feeling out the space before they give you anything real.

The biggest first-60-seconds mistake: Defaulting to your own dominant energy instead of responding to theirs. The Action rep who opens every call the same way will immediately create friction with a Practical or Emotional prospect. The Social rep who always warms up first will lose an Action prospect's attention in the first two minutes. Your energy is not the point. Theirs is.

PASE Signals & Real-Time Adjustments

Each energy type shows up in specific, observable ways throughout a call. These aren't personality boxes — they're behavioral signals. The more you know what to look for, the faster you can calibrate your approach to meet them exactly where they are.

P — Practical
Measured. Deliberate. Assessing everything.
They walked in with a framework for evaluating you. They're deciding whether you're worth their time. Earn their respect before you earn their trust — and earn their trust before anything else.
What you'll observe
  • Deliberate, measured speech — they choose their words
  • Short, precise answers — no filler, no extras
  • May ask a clarifying question before answering yours
  • References timelines, data, or ROI without being prompted
  • Comfortable with silence — doesn't fill it nervously
  • Minimal personal warmth early on — not cold, just strategic
  • May push back on something you say to test your certainty
  • If you're imprecise, they notice immediately
How to adjust
  • Slow down. Match their deliberate pace — don't rush them.
  • Be precise. Numbers, specifics, proof. Vague claims will lose them fast.
  • Lead with respect before rapport. Earn credibility first.
  • Don't fill silences they create. Let them process.
  • If they push back — don't fold and don't get defensive. Hold your position calmly.
  • Skip the social warmup unless they initiate it.
  • Structure your answers. No rambling. Get to the point.
Watch for: They go quiet after you say something. This almost never means they're satisfied — it means they're processing something that didn't add up. Ask: "What are you thinking?" Don't barrel forward past the silence.
A — Action
Fast. Decisive. Already thinking about the next step.
They don't want to be slowed down. They want to see you move at their speed, match their energy, and get to what matters. The worst thing you can do is bore them into checking out.
What you'll observe
  • Fast talker — high energy from the first sentence
  • Interrupts or jumps ahead mid-sentence
  • Talks about goals, growth, money, competition
  • Short answers that don't ask for more — they want to keep moving
  • May start closing themselves: "Okay so what's next?"
  • Gets restless with over-explanation or slow pacing
  • Bold claims — testing to see if you'll match their confidence
  • Very low tolerance for being managed or patronized
How to adjust
  • Match their pace immediately — don't start slow
  • Get to the substance fast. Skip the extended warmup.
  • Be direct and bold. Don't hedge your language.
  • Create forward momentum — keep things moving
  • When you need to slow them for discovery, lower your own pace deliberately — they'll follow
  • Don't over-explain once they signal they've got it
  • Match their energy on the close — be equally decisive
Watch for: The Action prospect slows down mid-call. This either means you hit a real nerve — they're actually processing something — or the call lost momentum and they're checking out. Read which one it is before you proceed.
S — Social
Warm. Talkative. Needs connection before commitment.
They're likable and they like you — but they buy the relationship before they buy the offer. Don't rush them. Don't go transactional. And when they start to open up, know that what sounds like casual conversation is actually them deciding whether you're trustworthy.
What you'll observe
  • Asks how you are — genuinely
  • Personal details surface unprompted early on
  • Laughs easily and often, even at small things
  • Gives long answers with a lot of context and story
  • Agreement-seeking language: "you know what I mean?"
  • Struggles to be direct about negative things — avoids confrontation
  • Talks about their friends, team, community
  • May steer conversation back toward warmth if it gets too clinical
How to adjust
  • Meet the warmth genuinely — not performed, not rushed past
  • Let them set the relational tone in the first few minutes
  • Use social proof: "others in the community," "people in your situation"
  • Keep your energy warm throughout — even in discovery
  • Guide into substance gently: ask a focused question that creates direction
  • When you need honesty from them, give explicit permission: "you don't have to give me the polished version"
  • Frame the offer as joining something, not buying something
Watch for: The Social prospect pulls back or goes quieter. This is almost always a signal that something felt inauthentic or the call got too transactional. Don't push. Reconnect: "Let me step back — how are you actually feeling about all of this?"
E — Emotional
Intuitive. Careful. Reads everything — including you.
They feel the energy of a call before they process the content. They'll know immediately if you're being genuine or running a sequence. The only way to reach them is to actually be present — not perform presence. When they trust you, they go deep. Until then, they give you almost nothing.
What you'll observe
  • Pauses before answering — processing before speaking
  • Chooses words carefully — doesn't rush to fill silence
  • Feeling language: "I feel like," "it doesn't sit right," "something's off"
  • Short initial answers — testing safety before they open up
  • Highly sensitive to tone shifts — if you get clinical, they close
  • When they open up, they go deep and personal quickly
  • May bring personal context without being asked
  • Notices incongruence immediately — if your words and energy don't match, they feel it
How to adjust
  • Slow everything down — your pace, your pauses, your pressure
  • Let them finish. Do not interrupt. Do not rush them.
  • Mirror their language — if they use feeling words, respond with feeling words
  • Acknowledge before you probe: "I hear that" then pause before the next question
  • Create explicit safety: "you don't have to give me the polished version"
  • Stay consistent throughout — any energy shift toward clinical or pushy will lose them
  • Don't rush to solutions. Understanding before answers.
Watch for: The Emotional prospect suddenly sounds formal or gives short clipped answers after being open. You moved too fast or lost congruence somewhere. Back up: "I feel like something shifted — is there something I'm not reading right?"

Matching vs. Leading

Two different tools — both necessary

Matching means meeting the prospect where they are — adapting your energy to theirs so they feel understood and comfortable. Leading means gradually shifting the energy toward what the call needs — pulling a fast prospect into depth, guiding a warm prospect toward substance. You almost always need to match before you can lead. Skip matching and the lead feels like a push.

Matching
Meet them where they are first
Adapting your pace, tone, and energy to theirs. This establishes trust and makes them feel heard before you've asked for anything. You don't match forever — you match to earn the right to lead.
Action prospect opens fast and bold → you open fast and direct. Social prospect warms up first → you warm up with them. Practical prospect is measured → you get measured.
Leading
Guide the energy toward what the call needs
Once trust is established, you can start to shift the energy. This is subtle — you don't announce it. You model the energy you need and let them follow. A gradual shift. Never a sudden gear change.
Action prospect needs depth → lower your own pace, ask a slower question, hold the silence. Social prospect needs focus → ask one specific question that creates direction. The call needs to go deeper → you go deeper first.
Practical examples of leading each energy type
  • Leading Practical deeper: They're giving you facts but no feeling. Lower your voice slightly, slow the pace, and ask: "What has sitting in this situation actually been like for you?" The deliberateness of the question signals it's safe to go beneath the data.
  • Leading Action into depth: They want to move fast. Match the speed first, then slow one question down: "Hold on — before we go further. How long have you been in this place?" The contrast of the slower question against the fast energy signals this matters.
  • Leading Social to substance: They've been warming the call for 10 minutes. Meet it, then ask one focused question: "What actually made you book this call?" That question creates a direction without breaking the warmth.
  • Leading Emotional to ownership: They've been careful and brief. You've been patient. Now: "What's the part you haven't said yet?" — asked quietly, with space after it. The gentleness signals safety and the question signals you know there's more.

The 5 Calibration Checkpoints

Your read isn't a one-time thing. There are five natural moments in every call where you should consciously check your calibration — update the read, confirm you're still meeting their energy, and adjust if something has shifted. Missing these moments means drifting without realizing it.

01
First 60 Seconds
Form the initial hypothesis
How did they answer the first question? How fast did they talk? Did they ask you something first? Did they warm up or stay straight to business? This is your first read. Hold it loosely. It's a starting point, not a verdict.
Practical → measured, direct opener Action → fast, high energy Social → personal warmth first Emotional → careful, quiet, feeling out
02
First Real Answer
Confirm or adjust the hypothesis
When you ask your first real question and they give a full answer — this confirms or adjusts the initial read. Length, language, whether they volunteered anything beyond what was asked, how they handled a pause — all update your calibration.
Short, precise, data-heavy → Practical confirmed Long, bold, forward-focused → Action confirmed Story-heavy with personal context → Social confirmed Careful, measured, checked themselves → Emotional confirmed
03
When You Probe Deeper
How do they respond to depth?
This is the most revealing calibration point. When you probe below the surface — ask for the feeling behind the fact, ask about the personal cost — how do they respond? Do they lean in or get more guarded? That response tells you where their trust level actually is and whether you're in their energy or fighting against it.
04
At the Pitch
Does the energy in the room match what you're about to do?
Before you transition to the offer — read the room. Is their energy high and leaning in? Are they asking logistical questions? Or is it flat and polite? Only pitch into warm energy. If it's flat, you have more discovery to do. The energy in the room at pitch time tells you whether the urgency was built or whether you're about to fight for it.
05
At the Close
Final energy read — decide or go back in
At the close, energy is your final diagnostic. High, committed, forward-leaning → close. Flat, polite, agreeable but not committed → go back in. An objection that surfaces at the close was created somewhere in the call — and the energy at that moment tells you whether it's a real objection or a discovery gap you need to revisit.

When the Energy Shifts

The most important read on the call

Energy shifts mid-call are more important than the initial read. A shift almost always means something happened — you said something that didn't land, you moved too fast, you lost congruence, or you actually hit a nerve and the prospect is now processing something real. Your ability to catch the shift and respond to it is what separates reps who lose at the objection stage from reps who never get there.

The Shift What It Usually Means How to Respond
Practical goes quiet after you make a claim Something didn't add up. They're not satisfied — they're calculating. Don't barrel forward. "What are you thinking right now?" Then let them answer fully before you say anything else.
Action person slows down suddenly Either you hit a real nerve and they're actually processing — or the call lost momentum and they're checking out. Check which one. "You went quiet — is that hitting something real for you, or did I lose you somewhere?"
Social person pulls back or gives shorter answers The call got too transactional or something felt inauthentic. They're protecting themselves. "Let me step back — how are you actually feeling about all of this?" Reconnect before you continue.
Emotional person suddenly sounds formal You moved too fast or lost congruence. They felt rushed or unsafe and closed back up. "I feel like something shifted — am I reading that wrong?" Say it quietly. Give them space to respond.
Any prospect becomes very agreeable but flat They've disconnected from the conversation. They're being polite, not engaged. The real close risk is here. Break the fourth wall. "You're agreeing with everything I'm saying but I can't tell if any of it is actually landing. What's really going on?"
Any prospect suddenly asks a logistical question They've decided. This is urgency showing up. They're not asking about how it works — they're asking because they want to start. Don't keep selling. Shift to close. They've made the decision — you just need to confirm it.

Mixed Energies

Nobody is purely one thing

The PASE doc explains that P and E are correlated, and so are S and A. In practice, most prospects show a clear primary energy with a secondary that emerges as trust builds. The secondary usually doesn't appear until 10–15 minutes into the call — after they've decided you're worth opening up to. Knowing the common pairings tells you what's likely coming and how to lean into it.

Action Social
Action-Social — the most common pairing
Starts fast, bold, and directional. As the call progresses and trust builds, the warmth surfaces. They start making jokes, dropping personal context, referencing their team or their circle. The secondary Social energy coming out is a trust signal — it means they like you and they're in.
Tell: Their answers get longer and more personal as the call goes on. They stop managing their image and start just talking to you.
Practical Emotional
Practical-Emotional — the most misread pairing
Starts guarded, measured, and data-focused. Looks cold. But underneath — and especially when you've earned their trust — there's a deep emotional driver. The goal, the family, the promise to themselves. When this person opens up emotionally, they go deep and personal fast. Most reps never reach this layer because they give up on the depth early.
Tell: After 15+ minutes, the language shifts from data to meaning. "The reason I actually care about this is..." That's the secondary layer surfacing. When it does — stay with it.
Social Emotional
Social-Emotional — the open book
Warm, personal, and feeling-driven from the start. They'll tell you a lot early. The challenge is that they can be hard to keep on track — the conversation sprawls because they follow every emotional thread. Use focused questions to guide the depth. Don't let the warmth substitute for actual discovery.
Tell: They give you lots of context but the context stays broad. Your job is to take one thread and go deep on it: "That piece right there — walk me through that specifically."
Action Practical
Action-Practical — the operator
Moves fast and wants results — but also has a framework for evaluating everything. They'll push forward quickly then stop to ask a precision question. They need both momentum and proof. Keep things moving, but don't skip the data when they ask for it.
Tell: Switches between "let's move" and "wait — how does that actually work?" Read the gears and respond to whichever one is running at that moment.

The Internal Monologue

What's running in the background while you talk

A rep who reads the room well isn't consciously thinking through PASE categories during the call. They've internalized the patterns to the point where the read happens automatically. But here's what that internal monologue looks like in the early stages — before it becomes second nature.

Slow down further. Mirror the feeling language. Don't rush past this moment.

"Something shifted. This isn't ready. Discovery gap somewhere."
Break the fourth wall. "I can feel something shifted — am I missing something?"

Reading the room is not a skill you turn on at the start of a call. It's a continuous state of attention. The rep who is fully present — not preparing their next question while the prospect is still talking, not managing their own anxiety — is the rep who catches the subtle shifts, reads the secondary energy when it surfaces, and knows exactly when to slow down, when to push deeper, and when to close. That presence is the skill. PASE is what you're using it to see.
