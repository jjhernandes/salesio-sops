Embedded Commands

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. Embedded commands let you place directives inside the conversation without the prospect ever noticing they were given one.

What Embedded Commands Are

The Definition

An embedded command is a directive hidden inside a larger sentence. The conscious mind processes the full sentence. The unconscious mind receives the command embedded within it.

The larger sentence gives the conscious mind something to track. While it's busy evaluating the container, the embedded directive lands without resistance.

The most common structure: "As you [embedded command], you'll notice [outcome]."

Example: "As you picture yourself closing more consistently, you start to realize why the fundamentals matter so much." The conscious mind follows "you start to realize." The unconscious receives the directive to picture closing consistently. Both happen at the same time.

The Underlying Mechanism

The brain cannot process a negation without first constructing the thing being negated. Similarly, it cannot process a complex sentence without executing the subordinate clauses inside it. The embedded command gets processed even when the conscious mind is focused elsewhere.

This is not manipulation. It's how language already works. Every skilled communicator uses this structure. The difference is whether you use it with awareness or not.

The Core Patterns

Six structural patterns. Learn these, then use them naturally. They break when they sound rehearsed.

01
Pattern
"As you..." + Command
Pairs a natural action with a directive. The "as you" makes the command feel like a logical observation, not an instruction.
"As you think about where you want to be in six months, you realize this isn't really a cost — it's a direction."
02
Pattern
"When you decide..." (Not "if")
"When" presupposes the action. "If" leaves the door open. One of the simplest swaps with the biggest framing effect.
"When you decide to move forward, the onboarding is the same day — we keep it fast."
03
Pattern
"People in your situation find that..."
Social proof as the container. The embedded command is what "people in their situation" do — which is what you want them to do.
"People in your exact situation find that once they commit to the process, the first 30 days are the most clarifying of their career."
04
Pattern
"I'm not going to tell you to..." (Then do exactly that)
Classic negation structure. Stating what you won't tell them to do delivers the directive while the conscious mind is focused on the disclaimer.
"I'm not going to tell you to make a decision today. What I'll say is — people who wait usually come back six months later saying they wish they'd moved sooner."
05
Pattern
"You might notice..." + Internal State
Naming an internal state they might notice actually induces it. The tentative "might" removes resistance while still planting the experience.
"You might notice a sense of relief when you realize the structure is already built — you're just plugging in."
06
Pattern
"The question isn't whether... it's when..."
Reframes the decision as already made, reducing the close to a timing question rather than a yes-or-no question.
"The question isn't whether you're going to fix this — you already know you need to. The question is whether you do it now or give it another six months."

Before and After Examples

Weak Version With Embedded Command
"Do you want to picture what your results could look like?" "As you picture what your results look like six months from now, what changes first?"
"If you sign up, we start right away." "When you come on board, we start the same day."
"You should think seriously about this." "I'm not going to pressure you — I'd just say that the reps who moved on this in April are already closing $30K months."
"This program could really help you." "People who were exactly where you are six months ago are now running $25K-$40K months. The pattern is consistent."
"You need to decide soon." "The question isn't whether this moves the needle — you already know it will. The question is how much longer you're comfortable leaving money on the table."

When They Fail

They sound scripted. Embedded commands are invisible when they flow naturally. The moment a prospect senses you're running a linguistic pattern, the trust breaks. They feel handled, not heard. That's worse than saying nothing clever at all.

Rule: Never string more than two embedded commands together in a single exchange. One lands well. Two is a technique. Three is a script. Stay in conversation.

The Right Condition

Embedded commands work when the prospect is already engaged and somewhat open. They don't work as openers and they don't work on a fully resistant prospect. They're a tool for the middle of the call, when you're deepening an already-active conversation.

If the prospect is shut down, go back to questions. Open them up first. Then use language to guide the direction.

Call Exchange

Rep Using Embedded Commands Naturally
Prospect
"I'm just not sure if the timing is right. I've got a lot going on."
Rep
"That makes sense. As you think about what 'the right timing' actually looks like for you, what would need to be different?"
Embedded command: "think about what the right timing looks like" — moves prospect toward clarifying the actual condition, not retreating from it.
Prospect
"I guess I'd want to have a little more coming in first before I invest."
Rep
"I hear that. People in your exact situation — wanting more income before investing in the skill that produces more income — find that it's a circular problem. The income doesn't come until the skill does. When you decide to break that loop, which direction do you want to go?"
"When you decide" presupposes a decision is coming. "Which direction" offers two paths that both assume forward movement. No pressure. No push.
Prospect
"Yeah... I mean when you put it that way, I see what you mean."
