The Closer Script — How to Use It

The company gave you a closer script. You asked whether to follow it or align it with what you're learning in Sales.io. The answer is both — here's how to think about it.

What they're doing
Their framework is "How/Hell in Heaven" — current state → future state. They're building a gap. It's the same structure as JJ's Heaven and Hell framework: where are you now, where do you want to be, and what's the cost of that gap? The language is different. The bones are the same.

You don't need to choose between their script and what you're learning. They're pointing at the same thing.

Don't do this
Memorize it as gospel. Use it as a checklist. Ask the financial qualification questions because the script says to, even when you already have emotional buy-in.

That's de-selling. It pulls you into logic at the worst moment.
Do this instead
Learn it until you understand why it works. Then keep it in your back pocket. Improv in the gaps, return to the framework when you need direction.

If you're reading the emotion and you feel it — you can pitch early. Trust that.
"People make decisions with emotion and compensate logically. There's never going to be a moment where logic closes the deal for you. All you need is emotion — and if you're asking analytical questions when you already have it, you're losing the sale."

You're an actor. That's a real asset here — you already know how to be present in a moment, read a scene, and respond to what's actually happening. The goal is to bring that same instinct to your calls. The script gives you structure. Your instincts navigate within it.

Your Emotional Baseline — Map of Consciousness

You shared where you think you're operating on the Map of Consciousness. Here's what came out of that conversation and what to work with going forward.

Your baseline
When things are working — you operate around Love to Joy. High empathy, genuinely care about people, good energy off the rip.

When you're around family or people from your past for long enough — you start getting pulled toward Pride. Not arrogance — more like a protective layer. You get more guarded the more someone gets to know you. Less open, not more.

That last piece is worth sitting with. Most people assume being more guarded with people you know means trust — but if it's the pattern of closing off, it can also mean protection. What are you protecting against?

The family dynamic
Your mom suggesting you go back to school when you already knew your path. The feeling of not being valued until the money or the car shows up. The frustration of people who haven't done it trying to coach you.

That reaction — especially the pride response — is almost always guilt and shame underneath. Theirs. Not yours.
The reframe
You can't fix anyone. You can lead by example, have honest conversations, and give them something real to see — not to prove yourself, but because you genuinely want them there.

"It's never your circumstances that limit you — it's the guilt and shame you hold onto."

When you feel judgment rising — whether toward your dad, toward a difficult prospect, toward anyone — the question to ask is: what are they afraid of, and where does that fear come from? Once you can see the hurt underneath the behavior, the judgment loses its grip. This isn't soft — it makes you sharper and harder to rattle.
