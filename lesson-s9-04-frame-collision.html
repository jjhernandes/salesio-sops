Frame Collision — Absorb, Validate, Redirect Without Yielding

The prospect will try to take the frame. It happens on almost every high-ticket call. The question is not whether it happens — it's whether you know what to do when it does.

What Frame Collision Is

The Mechanics

Frame collision happens when the prospect makes a move designed — consciously or not — to shift control of the interaction. They ask a question that puts you on the defensive. They challenge your credibility. They introduce time pressure. They compare you to a competitor. They make a statement that positions them as the evaluator rather than the one being evaluated.

These are not just objections. They're frame attacks. And the reason they're dangerous is that most reps respond to them as content problems — trying to answer the question or counter the challenge — when they're actually structural problems. The content of your response matters far less than whether you hold your frame while delivering it.

The mistake most reps make: They hear a frame attack and immediately go into content mode — explaining, justifying, defending. The second you start defending yourself, you've already conceded the frame. The prospect attacked, you reacted. They now control the interaction even if your answer is technically correct.

The three-step response to any frame collision: Absorb. Validate. Redirect. In that order. Without yielding position at any point. The prospect's attempt to take the frame gets acknowledged but not rewarded. You stay on the field you set.

The Three-Step Response

01
Step One
Absorb — Don't React
The first thing you do when a frame attack lands is nothing. Not literally — but physiologically, you don't react. You don't flinch. You don't speed up. You don't raise your voice. You take a breath, you hold space for a second, and you stay exactly where you were before they said it. This is the most important step. If you react, you've already lost something.
The prospect says "I've talked to three other programs and they were all cheaper." You don't respond immediately. You breathe. You let the statement land. You let there be a brief silence. Then you move to step two.
02
Step Two
Validate — Acknowledge It
You acknowledge what they said without agreeing that it's a problem. This is not empty validation — it's genuine acknowledgment that what they raised is real and you heard it. The reason you validate is because it disarms the attack. They expected defensiveness. Instead they get a calm acknowledgment. The dynamic immediately shifts.
"That's fair — price is worth paying attention to." Not "yeah you're right we're expensive" (conceding). Not "well actually if you look at the ROI" (defending). Just: what you said is real, I hear it.
03
Step Three
Redirect — Move the Frame Back
Without yielding position, you redirect the conversation toward the actual decision that matters. Not addressing the surface challenge — the underlying need or question. This redirect stays in your frame, on your terms. The prospect's attack has been absorbed and acknowledged, and the conversation continues in the direction you're driving it.
"What I'd want to understand is — what specifically are you looking to get out of this? Because the price comparison only matters if the programs are solving the same problem, and from what you've described, they're probably not."

The Three Most Common Frame Collisions

Collision 1 The Price Attack

"That's a lot of money." / "I can get the same thing cheaper somewhere else." / "That seems expensive for what you're offering."

Price attacks are the most common frame collision. The prospect is testing whether you believe in your own price. If you flinch, discount preemptively, or immediately start justifying, you've just confirmed their suspicion that the price is negotiable — or that you're not fully certain it's worth it.

Absorb, Validate, Redirect
[Pause] "Yeah, $7,500 is real money. It should feel like a real decision." [Hold.] "Tell me — what have you tried before, and what was the result?"
Collision 2 The Authority Challenge

"How long have you been doing this?" / "What are your credentials?" / "Have you personally closed at the level you're teaching?"

Authority challenges are designed to flip the evaluator role back to the prospect. Suddenly they're interviewing you rather than the other way around. The weak frame response is to answer eagerly and defensively — listing credentials in a rush, trying to prove yourself. This reinforces their position as judge.

Absorb, Validate, Redirect
[Pause] "That's a fair thing to ask." [Short, specific answer — not a list.] "Here's what I'd rather show you than tell you — let's talk about your situation, and you can judge from the quality of the questions I ask and the insights I give you whether I know what I'm talking about."
Collision 3 The Time Pressure Reversal

"I only have 15 minutes." / "Can you just give me the quick version?" / "I'm actually pretty busy this week."

The prospect is trying to impose their time frame on your conversation. Most reps immediately comply — compress the pitch, apologize, rush. This signals that the prospect's time is more valuable than the quality of the decision they're about to make.

Absorb, Validate, Redirect
"I hear you on time. The thing is — a compressed version of this conversation isn't actually useful for either of us. This is a $7,500 decision. Should we reschedule for when you can actually give this your attention?"

Call Exchange — Losing vs. Holding Frame Collision

PRICE ATTACK — Rep Who Loses the Frame Collision
Prospect
"Seven thousand five hundred dollars? That's pretty steep. I've seen programs that teach the same stuff for like $2,000."
Rep
"Yeah I know it sounds like a lot but when you break it down it's actually really competitive — like we have the job placement guarantee, the one-on-one coaching, the WhatsApp community, the weekly calls, so if you add all of that up separately it would cost way more. And we also have payment plans so you don't have to pay it all at once. And our ROI guarantee means if you don't make your money back we refund you so really the risk is zero."
Immediate defensive spiral. Justifying the price without being asked to. Introducing payment plans before they're needed. Frame: "I need you to think the price is fair." Already lost.
PRICE ATTACK — Rep Who Holds the Frame Collision
Prospect
"Seven thousand five hundred dollars? That's pretty steep. I've seen programs that teach the same stuff for like $2,000."
Rep
[2-second pause] "Yeah, it's real money." [Holds again.] "What were those programs?"
Doesn't defend. Acknowledges it's real money — because it is. Then redirects to comparison, which they'll almost certainly lose. Frame never moved.
Prospect
"I mean, I haven't actually tried them. I've just seen them advertised."
Rep
"Got it. So we're comparing a program you haven't taken to one you're evaluating right now. Let's just talk about what you actually need — and then you can decide if what we do is worth what we charge."
Clean redirect. Frame fully restored. Prospect is now genuinely curious rather than defensive.
