"What If It Doesn't Work?" — The Fear of Failure Objection

The prospect who asks "what if it doesn't work?" is not being pessimistic. They've already invested emotionally — they want this to work. The fear is of disappointment, not of failure itself.

What's Really Happening When They Ask This

This is actually a good sign. A prospect who asks "what if it doesn't work?" has already emotionally invested in this working. They can see themselves in it. They can see the outcome. That's why they're afraid of not getting it.

A prospect who doesn't care whether it works doesn't ask this question. They just say "I'm not interested." The fear of failure is the evidence of desire. Handle it accordingly.

The Two Layers

Surface layer: "Does this program deliver results?" This can be addressed with the guarantee, proof, and track record.

Deeper layer: "What if I don't work out? What if I put in everything and I'm still the one who doesn't get there?" This is the real fear — and it's about them, not the program. It requires a different response than the guarantee.

The Core Reframe — Define "Doesn't Work" Together

The question to ask: "Let's talk about what 'doesn't work' would actually mean for you — what would that look like?"

This does something important: it forces the prospect to define failure. Most of the time, the definition of failure they name is something you can address, quantify, or guarantee around. You can't work with a vague fear. You can work with a specific scenario.

What Happens When You Ask

Most prospects will say something like: "I'd put in the time and still be at the same number" or "I'd spend the money and not get a return on it." These are specific fears with specific answers: the guarantee addresses financial risk, the track record addresses results, and your coaching accountability addresses effort-without-outcome.

Occasionally a prospect will say "I'd feel like a failure." That's the deeper layer. Address it directly and with respect — "That's a real concern. Let me tell you something about how we approach the people in this program when they hit walls."

How to Use the Guarantee — and When Not To Lead With It

The mistake: Leading with the guarantee as the first response to this objection. When you open with "we have a money-back guarantee" before you understand what they're actually afraid of, it sounds like a standard pitch response. It doesn't address the fear — it just reduces the financial risk, which may not be the primary concern.

Use the Guarantee as a Landing Point, Not a Lead

First: ask what "doesn't work" means to them. Second: address the specific fear they name. Third: use the guarantee to close the loop on financial risk after the emotional concern has been heard and addressed.

"So based on what you described — you'd want to make sure you're not investing money you won't see again. The guarantee is specifically built for that. If you put in the work and don't make your investment back in 90 days, you get a full refund. That's not a marketing line — we stand behind it."

The Guarantee as a Statement About the Program, Not Just Risk Mitigation

Frame the guarantee not just as financial protection but as a signal of confidence: "The reason we offer a guarantee is because we know what happens when people actually go through this program. A guarantee only makes financial sense for a company that doesn't expect to give refunds. We don't."

Separating Effort From Outcome — When the Fear Is About Themselves

When the Deeper Fear Surfaces

If the prospect's fear is about their own ability to execute — "what if I put in the work and I'm still not good enough" — this is Barrier Three from Lesson 3. They're protecting against the pain of maximum effort with no result. Address it from a place of earned authority, not reassurance.

"The people who ask that question are the ones most likely to get results. Because the ones who don't care about failing don't actually try hard enough to get them. The fact that you're asking this tells me you'd put in the work. And this program is built for people who put in the work."

The Accountability Frame

If effort and follow-through is the concern: "Here's what you should know — this isn't a program where you're on your own. You have weekly 1-on-1 coaching specifically to catch people when they're stuck, before the stagnation becomes a problem. If you hit a wall, there's a person who is specifically there to help you through it. You're not doing this alone."

Call Exchange — Right vs. Wrong Response

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Leads With the Guarantee
Prospect
"I guess my concern is — what if I go through this and it doesn't work out? What if I just don't get the results?"
Rep
"That's why we have our money-back guarantee. If you put in the work and don't get results in 90 days, we refund you in full. So honestly the risk is pretty minimal."
Went straight to financial risk mitigation. Didn't ask what "doesn't work" means to the prospect. Didn't address the emotional layer. The prospect wanted to feel heard — they got a policy.
Prospect
"Yeah... okay. I still just need to think about it."
The guarantee didn't close the real fear. The real fear was never surfaced.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Surfaces and Addresses the Real Fear
Prospect
"I guess my concern is — what if I go through this and it doesn't work out? What if I just don't get the results?"
Rep
"That's worth talking through. What does 'doesn't work' look like for you — specifically? Like what's the scenario you're imagining?"
Asks the defining question. Forces the fear to become concrete before addressing it.
Prospect
"I guess... I'd spend the money and still be stuck at the same income level. Like I'd put in the effort and nothing would actually change."
Rep
"Okay — two things. First, the fact that you said 'put in the effort' tells me something. The people who don't get results from this program are the ones who don't engage. You're already thinking about putting in the work — that's the profile of someone who gets results here. Second — to address the financial side directly — we guarantee that you'll make your investment back in 90 days or we refund you. That exists specifically for the scenario you just described."
Addresses both layers: the personal fear about effort and the financial fear about return. In the right order — emotion first, then logistics. The guarantee lands as closure, not as a deflection.
