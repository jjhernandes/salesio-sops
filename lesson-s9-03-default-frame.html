Identifying Your Default Frame — What Are You Actually Running?

Most reps don't know what frame they're running. They find out under pressure. By then, it's too late to change it mid-call. This lesson is about seeing it before it costs you.

Pressure Reveals the Default

The Truth About Default Frames

When everything is going well on a call, almost every rep looks like they have a strong frame. They're confident, easy, hitting their marks. The frame only becomes visible when something goes wrong — a hard objection, a credibility challenge, the "I need to think about it" moment.

Under pressure, the constructed frame evaporates. What's left is the default — the operating belief you run from when your conscious mind isn't managing it anymore. And for the vast majority of sales reps, that default belief is one of three things, and all three of them lose.

"You don't find out what frame you're running until it gets tested. The call that goes perfectly reveals nothing. The call where things go sideways reveals everything."

— JJ Hernandez

The Three Default Frames That Lose

Default 1 The Pleaser Frame

Core belief: "I need you to like me."

What it looks like
The Pleaser is constantly monitoring the prospect's emotional state and adjusting to maintain approval. They laugh at things that aren't funny, over-agree with opinions they don't hold, soften every edge, avoid anything that might create friction. They're performing connection rather than creating it.
Why it loses
People don't buy from people who need their approval. They buy from people who have standards and conviction. The Pleaser reads as approval-seeking, which registers as low status. Prospects unconsciously feel less safe making a $7,500 decision with someone who seems to need their validation.
The tell
"I totally understand that" (every 45 seconds). "No, yeah, absolutely, that makes total sense." Laughing after statements that weren't jokes. Never pushing back, even when the prospect is clearly wrong about something.
Default 2 The Supplicant Frame

Core belief: "I need this sale."

What it looks like
The Supplicant is operating from scarcity. They came in knowing they need this deal — bad month, pipeline is thin, pressure from above. The entire call is unconsciously oriented around extracting a yes from the prospect. The prospect feels like a solution to a problem rather than a person being helped.
Why it loses
The person who needs the sale the most will always lose. Desperation is odorless but somehow everyone can smell it. The moment a prospect senses that you need their yes, they become suspicious. Why does this person need this so badly? What's wrong with it that they can't fill their program otherwise?
The tell
Preemptively discounting before price is objected to. Offering payment plans before they're asked. Speedingup when the prospect hesitates. Filling every silence. Answering the objection before the prospect finishes stating it.
Default 3 The Performer Frame

Core belief: "Watch how good I am."

What it looks like
The Performer is good at sales — and they know it. The call is less about the prospect's situation and more about demonstrating skill. They love objection handling because it lets them shine. They're running a performance, not a conversation. Smooth, impressive, and ultimately about themselves.
Why it loses
People sense when they're being performed at rather than talked with. The Performer's call feels slick but hollow. The prospect walks away impressed but not bought in. Trust doesn't come from watching someone be good. It comes from feeling genuinely understood. The Performer can't do that because they're too busy being impressive.
The tell
Long, beautifully structured answers to simple questions. Pivoting every objection into a prepared speech. Asking discovery questions they clearly already know the answer to — just to set up the next move. Lots of clever lines. Not much genuine curiosity.

Self-Assessment — Which One Are You?

Answer honestly. The goal is not to feel bad — the goal is to see clearly so you can change it.

Situation What you actually do (be honest)
Prospect goes quiet after you pitch Do you hold it? Or fill the silence within 3 seconds?
Prospect says "I've heard this before" Do you get curious? Or immediately justify why you're different?
Prospect seems annoyed or skeptical Do you hold your tone? Or soften, apologize, agree with them?
Prospect asks about price early Do you state it confidently and hold? Or hedge and qualify it?
You misspeak or stumble Do you recover without mentioning it? Or over-apologize?
"I need to think about it" Do you get curious about what they're weighing? Or immediately accommodate?

The honest read: If you're filling silences, softening prices, apologizing for your pitch, or performing your best stuff rather than listening — you already know which frame you're running. It's okay. Most reps are running one of these three. The ones who know it are the ones who can change it.

How to Replace Your Default With the Detachment Frame

The goal is not to eliminate your personality. The Detachment Frame doesn't make you cold or robotic. It means you genuinely care about the prospect's outcome more than you care about the commission. When that's actually true — not performed, actually true — your frame becomes unshakeable because it's not dependent on anything the prospect does.

  • ✓
    Build genuine certainty about the offer before every call. Before you pick up the phone, remind yourself why this program works. Not a pep talk — a factual review. Results you've seen. People who've come in where this prospect is and come out ahead. If you can't articulate why the program genuinely works, you'll default to needing the sale because you're not sure of the value.
  • ✓
    The pre-call anchor: "My job is to find out if this is the right move for this person." Not "close the deal." Not "be impressive." Find out if it's right. This reorientation is not a trick — it's a genuine shift in intent that changes everything downstream. When you're actually trying to figure out if it's a fit, you stop performing and start listening.
  • ✓
    Notice the moment you start performing vs. talking. There's a feeling — a slight shift where you stop responding to what's actually happening and start executing a plan. Notice it. Come back. Ask the next honest question instead of delivering the next prepared point.
  • →
    This takes reps, not insight. Reading this lesson does not fix your default frame. Doing 20 calls in a row where you consciously hold the Detachment Frame starts to. The default only changes through repetition. Put it in practice immediately.

Call Exchange — Same Objection, Three Default Frames

Prospect: "I need to think about it." — Three Responses
Rep — Pleaser Frame
"Yeah, absolutely, totally makes sense — take all the time you need! I'll send you some info and we can reconnect whenever works for you. No pressure at all."
Immediately folds. No curiosity. Needs the prospect to feel okay about ending the call. Frame: "please still like me."
Rep — Supplicant Frame
"Of course — but just so you know, we actually have a spot opening in the next group and it might not be there when you come back. And we do have a payment plan option if that helps. I just want to make sure you don't miss out."
Manufactured urgency + preemptive concession. Stinks of desperation. The prospect didn't ask about payment plans.
Rep — Performer Frame
"That's interesting — typically when someone says they need to think, it's because one of three things is true: they haven't fully connected the cost to the outcome, there's a secondary decision-maker involved, or there's a specific fear underneath it. Which one is it for you?"
Impressive and smart. Also completely disconnected from this specific person. The prospect feels like a case study, not a human.
Rep — Detachment Frame
[Pause] "That's fair. What specifically are you working through?"
Six words. Genuine curiosity. No agenda. No performance. Frame: I'm here for you, not for the close. This is the one that works.
