"Tell Me More About That"

Going deeper without interrogating. Four words that open every door. The most versatile tool in discovery and the one most underused by people who think they already know how to ask questions.

Why It Works

"Tell me more about that" works because it is completely open, completely non-leading, and signals genuine curiosity without directing where the prospect should take the conversation next.

Most follow-up questions are secretly prescriptive. "Was it frustrating?" tells the prospect what they should feel. "How did that affect your income?" narrows the scope. "Did you try X?" introduces your agenda.

"Tell me more about that" has no agenda. It invites the prospect to go wherever the truth lives. And wherever the truth lives is exactly where you need to go.

The Curiosity Signal

Beyond what the words do structurally, "tell me more about that" communicates something important about the rep's posture: genuine interest. When a prospect feels you are actually curious — not just checking boxes — they give you more. More honesty. More depth. More of the specific emotional content that closes deals.

Curiosity is not a technique. It is a state. But "tell me more about that" can trigger the state in you even when you need to consciously access it, and it communicates the state to the prospect regardless.

When to Deploy It

  • ✓
    When a prospect mentions something with emotional weight but does not go deep into it. "I've had some frustrating experiences with training programs before." Stop there. "Tell me more about that." They said something real. Invite them to expand it before moving on.
  • ✓
    When you sense there is more behind the answer than what was said. The answer felt careful or abbreviated. Something was being held back. "Tell me more about that" creates permission to share what was being measured.
  • ✓
    After an objection. Instead of going into your prepared counter: "Tell me more about that." The stated objection is almost never the real one. Let them elaborate and the real one will surface.
  • ✓
    When a prospect uses a significant word or phrase you want to explore. They say "I've been frustrated with myself" — stop. "Tell me more about that." The word "myself" is pointing at something identity-level. Follow it.
  • ✓
    When you are not sure what direction to go next. "Tell me more about that" buys you time while simultaneously producing the information that will tell you where to go. It is the safest next move when you are uncertain.

Variations That Work

The Core Four Alternatives

"Walk me through that." Asks for a narrative, not just a description. Good when you want the story, not just the summary.

"What was that like?" Invites an emotional description. Use this when the statement had emotional texture. "I had a really hard month" — "What was that like?" puts the prospect back inside the experience.

"Say more about the..." Targets a specific element of what they said without leading. "Say more about the frustration part." You are not prescribing what the frustration meant — just inviting them to expand it specifically.

"And what happened after that?" Follows a story. Keeps the narrative moving. Good when the prospect told you about an event and stopped before the consequence or the feeling.

Do not use these interchangeably without thought. "Tell me more about that" is the broadest. "What was that like?" is more emotionally targeted. "Walk me through that" asks for sequence. Know which one is right for the moment you are in based on what the prospect just gave you.

What to Do With What They Give You

Do Not Immediately Pivot

The most common mistake after a prospect answers "tell me more about that" is to immediately pivot to the next pre-planned question. The prospect just gave you something real. Reflect it first. Make sure they know you absorbed it. Then decide whether to go deeper or move laterally.

Pivoting immediately signals that you asked the question to get information, not because you were actually interested. Reflecting signals that you were actually listening. The difference in how the prospect feels about the conversation is significant.

Look for the Thread

Every time a prospect expands on something, there is a thread inside what they said. A word, a feeling, a connection to something else. Follow the thread. The thread leads to the emotional core. The emotional core is where closes live. "Tell me more about that" is the tool that surfaces the thread. Following it is the skill.

Call Exchange — Pivoting vs. Going Deeper

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Pivots Instead of Going Deeper
Prospect
"I had a really rough Q1 honestly. Lost three deals I thought were done and it really messed with my head."
Rep
"Yeah that can be tough. So tell me — what kind of objections are you typically running into? Is it mostly price or..."
Moved past the emotional content immediately. "Messed with my head" was a door. The rep walked past it. The conversation stays tactical and never reaches the emotional depth that produces closes.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Goes Through the Door
Prospect
"I had a really rough Q1 honestly. Lost three deals I thought were done and it really messed with my head."
Rep
"Tell me more about that — the part about it messing with your head."
Following the emotional thread specifically. Not asking about the lost deals. Asking about what it did to the person internally. That is where the close lives.
Prospect
"Like, I started second-guessing myself on every call. I'd get someone to the close and then hesitate because I was worried I'd lose them too. And of course that made it worse."
Rep
"Walk me through what the hesitation actually looks like on the call. What happens?"
Going one more level deeper. "Walk me through" asks for the narrative — the specific moment — not the category. The prospect is now describing a real pattern with real behavioral detail.
Prospect
"I'll get to the price and instead of just saying it and waiting, I start over-explaining. Like I'm trying to justify it before they even push back. And then they pick up on that and it falls apart."
Highly specific, self-aware description of the exact breakdown pattern. The rep now knows exactly what to address in the pitch. This depth would never have appeared if the rep had pivoted at the first exchange.
