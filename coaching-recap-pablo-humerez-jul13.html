Coaching Moments

What was worked through

01
De-selling — the thing most reps don't know they're doing
When someone is warm and ready to go, continuing to ask tactical questions doesn't build them up — it takes them out of their emotional state and back into their head. They start second-guessing. That's de-selling.
"You think they're here, and then you're like slowly trying to build them up — but they're really up here, ready to go, and you're just taking way too long."
JJ Hernandez
The couple Chris called (31-year married, ALS mom, daughter's wedding, $4K/month income drop, mounting debt) had already given him everything. He didn't need 10 more questions. He just needed to go into the numbers and let them confront their own reality.
02
Verbal buying cues — "we're ready" is not small
The couple had followed the brand for over a year. When the husband said "we're ready" — that's certainty language. That's commitment. Compare it to maybe, probably, I think, I guess. Those are wishy-washy. "We're ready" is not.
JJ's coaching: you still don't rush. You still take it slow. But you know internally the sale is yours to lose. The one question you should have asked right there: "It's been a year — what's kept you from pulling the trigger until now?" That curiosity isn't just good sales. It's what makes you actually understand someone.
"Don't ask the question you don't know the answer to? Man, what the fuck — you don't have any answers. You don't know this guy. You don't know where he's from. We have zero."
JJ Hernandez — on bad sales advice
03
The data brings out the emotions — not the other way around
Going into the numbers isn't tactical coldness — it's where the emotional weight builds. Making someone look at $100K of debt, nothing in savings, nothing in retirement, a 4K monthly gap they can't close — that's intense. That discomfort is the emotion.
Chris didn't push further on why they couldn't go back to Dave Ramsey. He didn't need to. She opened the door to the numbers. He walked through it. Once you're comfortable going through their numbers together — the close is almost done. What follows is just confirming they're ready to let someone help.
"As long as they're comfortable giving you the numbers, the rest is pretty much smooth sailing."
JJ Hernandez
04
Slowness = composure = lowered anxiety
All three closers were played at 1.3–1.4x speed because they're all that slow in real time. Spencer especially. He waited for full presence before starting. He didn't rush through the tech hiccup — just held space until it was sorted. He asked if they were still free for 40 minutes and didn't accept a vague nod — he waited for a real answer.
"Slower is composure. It slows down their anxiety every single time. The slower you are, the better."
JJ Hernandez
Pablo's natural energy maps closest to Spencer's. JJ's note: the energy Pablo had on the Tristan call — that casual, comfortable, real-person energy — is exactly what needs to come into his sales calls.
05
Problem aware vs. solution aware — Pablo's question
Pablo asked: if they're problem-aware and solution-aware and still haven't bought — what certainty do they need? JJ's correction: they're not solution-aware yet. Following a brand = knowing you have a problem. Solution-aware means knowing how the program works, what the execution looks like, what they're actually buying into.
What gets them to solution-aware is the presentation. The numbers create the emotional pressure. The pitch shows them the exit. Then certainty closes the gap. The sale on a warm prospect like this is often just: open strong → go through numbers → make the gap vivid → present the bridge → confirm they see it.
06
Clarity is for you, not them
You're asking questions to understand their situation — not to perform curiosity. If you don't actually understand why they're there, you cannot close the call. The questions aren't a script you run. They're how you get clarity for yourself.
"If you don't know why they're here, you can't continue the call. You literally cannot."
JJ Hernandez
Some reps will catch the key moment fast (Pablo's pattern recognition is already strong). Some will need to hear it 2–3 times in context. Ryan might take a few more sessions. Neither is wrong — it's reps. The point is to build the pattern until you spot it automatically, in the moment, on a live call.
