Step 11 — Objection Handling

Objections are questions, not rejections. The rep who argues with an objection always loses — because they just turned a question into a debate.

The Meta-Rule

An objection is not the prospect saying no. It's the prospect saying: "I'm not fully convinced — and here's the specific thing that's in the way."

The rep who hears an objection as rejection tries to overcome it. The rep who hears it as a question gets curious about it. One posture creates a debate. The other creates a conversation. Conversations close deals. Debates don't.

Why Arguing Makes Objections Stronger

When you argue with an objection, you're implying the prospect is wrong. That triggers their ego — now they have to defend the position they took. The original objection had maybe 50% conviction. After you argue with it, it has 90% conviction. You just made the problem worse.

The Objection Handling Sequence

1
Validate
Acknowledge Without Agreeing
The first response to any objection is a brief acknowledgment that the concern is understandable. Not "I completely agree" — not agreement. Just: "That makes sense" or "I hear that." It disarms the defensive posture and creates space for the next question.
"That makes sense — that's a real concern and I don't want to gloss over it."
2
Get Specific
Ask One Question to Clarify
Almost every stated objection is a surface version of a deeper concern. "I need to think about it" might mean "I need to talk to my spouse," or "I'm afraid of investing and not following through," or "I don't actually believe I'll get the results." One clarifying question finds the real thing.
"What specifically are you thinking through?" / "When you say it's too expensive — expensive relative to what?"
3
Address
Respond to the Specific Thing They Said
Once you know what the objection is actually about, address it directly and honestly. Not with a rebuttal. With information, a reframe, or an honest acknowledgment of the limitation — whichever is true. Then return to the close.
"Here's how that works..." / "That's fair — let me tell you what we see from people who've been in your exact situation..."

The 3 Most Common Objections and How to Handle Each

OBJECTION 1 — "I Need to Think About It"
Prospect
"I need to think about it."
Rep
[2-second pause] "That makes sense. What specifically are you weighing?"
Don't concede or push. Get specific. "Think about it" always has a specific thing underneath it.
Prospect
"Honestly, I'm not sure I'll actually follow through on this."
Rep
"That's the most honest thing you've said all call. What makes you doubt that about yourself?"
The real objection is self-trust — not price, not timing. Address that directly.
OBJECTION 2 — "It's Too Expensive"
Prospect
"It's too expensive."
Rep
[pause] "Expensive relative to what?"
The most important question here. Expensive compared to other programs? Expensive compared to what they can access right now? Expensive given the ROI? Each has a different answer.
Prospect
"I just don't have $7,500 right now."
Rep
"That's cash flow, not price — those are different things. We have a split-pay that brings it to $3,750 or Klarna that can get it under $500 a month. Is the question whether you have the cash, or whether it's worth it?"
Distinguishes cash flow from perceived value. Now you know which conversation you're actually having.
OBJECTION 3 — "I Need to Talk to My Spouse/Partner"
Prospect
"I need to run this by my wife first."
Rep
"That's completely fair. If she was on this call right now and heard everything — what do you think her main question would be?"
Surfaces what's actually behind the partner conversation. Often it's her concern about risk, results, or budget — all of which can be addressed now rather than in a third-party conversation where you have no presence.
