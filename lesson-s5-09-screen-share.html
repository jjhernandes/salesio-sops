When to Use Screen Share

Screen share is for proof, not for pitching. Use it to punctuate the conversation with something they can see. Don't let it replace the conversation itself.

The Rules

Screen share is a proof tool, not a pitch tool. The core pitch — the Mirror Recap, the feature-to-pain connections, the Heaven and Hell arc — is done in conversation. Screen share comes after a specific claim has been made, to show something visual that reinforces that claim.

The test: "If I said this without screen share, would it still land?" If yes — don't screen share. If no — there's something specific you need to show. That's when it's worth using.

The Core Principle

Screen share should punctuate the pitch, not carry it. A conversation-led pitch with one or two well-placed proof elements is powerful. A screen-share-led pitch where the rep is essentially reading slides is a presentation — and presentations don't close deals the way conversations do.

When Screen Share Is Appropriate

Only when the visual element significantly adds to what you've already said in conversation, and only for elements the prospect has already been primed to care about. Showing the job board works if you've already told them about the job board and they responded positively. Showing it cold before they're invested is just a website demo.

When Screen Share Adds Value

These are the use cases where screen share creates genuine impact — because the visual proof is materially more convincing than the verbal claim alone.

  • ✓
    The job board — for a prospect who cited a weak offer as their main concern. After making the verbal claim about 60-80 vetted companies, screen sharing the live board for 30 seconds makes it real. They can see the postings, the company names, the commission structures. The visual eliminates doubt in a way the verbal claim can't fully achieve.
  • ✓
    A student transformation — for a prospect who's skeptical of results. If you have a before/after income screenshot or a testimonial from someone whose story matches their identity profile — showing it briefly, for 20-30 seconds, right after you've told the story verbally — amplifies the proof. The visual confirms what you just said.
  • ✓
    A specific module or curriculum piece — for a prospect who asked about a specific type of training. If they asked "do you cover B2B specifically?" and you said yes, pulling up the B2B module in Skool for 15 seconds shows them it's real and it's substantial. Specific response to a specific question.
  • ✓
    Community activity — for a prospect who cited isolation or lack of support as a past failure. A quick look at WhatsApp or a Slack channel showing real activity from real students makes the community claim tangible. Two minutes maximum. Then back to conversation.

When Screen Share Kills Momentum

Every time you start a screen share, you create a small interruption in the conversation. You're asking the prospect to shift attention from listening to watching. Done once or twice, this is fine. Done too much, and the call loses its natural conversational energy — and the prospect's emotional engagement drops with it.

Screen Share Mistake Why It Kills Momentum
Using a slide deck as the primary pitch structure Turns a conversation into a presentation. Prospect passively watches instead of actively engaging. Close rate drops significantly when the deck becomes the pitch.
Screen sharing before any emotional connection is built Visual content without emotional priming is just content. They're not invested yet. They're evaluating, not feeling. The emotional work has to happen in conversation first.
Showing things the prospect didn't ask about or express interest in Creates distraction. Prospect sees something that raises new questions. You're introducing variables you didn't need to introduce. Stick to what they said they cared about.
Staying on screen share too long (more than 2 minutes per element) The screen becomes the focal point instead of the conversation. You lose the direct, face-to-face energy that closes deals. Come in fast, make the point, get back to conversation.
Using screen share to stall when the call isn't going well Reps sometimes default to "let me show you something" when they feel the prospect slipping. This delays the real issue rather than addressing it. The thing to show is curiosity, not your screen.

The screen share crutch is real. Reps who aren't confident in conversational selling often over-rely on visual aids because it feels safer to have something to point at. The screen gives you something to do when the silence feels uncomfortable. This is the opposite of how elite closers operate. If you notice you default to screen share when you're uncertain, that's the signal to work on conversational confidence — not to use better slides.

How to Execute Screen Share Correctly

The Setup

Never start a screen share without verbal framing. Tell them what they're about to see and why it's relevant to something specific they said. "I want to show you the job board for a second — because you mentioned your current offer isn't strong, and I want you to see what's available right now." The framing sets the purpose. Without it, they're just watching you navigate a website.

The Time Limit

30 seconds to 2 minutes maximum per screen share element. Come in, make the specific point, and close it. "You can see there are 67 postings right now — that's what's live today. Okay." Stop sharing. Return to conversation. The return to camera-to-camera eye contact reinforces the personal, conversational nature of the call.

Maximum Two Screen Shares Per Call

If you're screen sharing more than twice in a pitch, you're overusing it. Two well-placed proof elements are more persuasive than five scattered ones. And fewer visual interruptions means more conversation, which is always where the close happens.

Call Exchange — Screen Share Done Right vs. Wrong

VERSION 1 — Screen Share as a Crutch
Rep
"Let me just share my screen and walk you through everything. Okay so here you can see — this is our Skool platform, there's all the modules right there. And then if I click over here this is the job board — you can see all the companies. And then this is what the WhatsApp community looks like — super active. And here's some student testimonials..."
Screen share for the entire pitch. Prospect is watching, not participating. Rep is narrating a website tour instead of having a conversation. No connection between any element and what the prospect said in discovery.
Prospect
"Yeah that's cool. I'd want to think about it and do some more research."
"Do some more research" — they didn't feel personally addressed. The presentation gave them information but not relevance. They're going to Google competitors.
VERSION 2 — Screen Share as Punctuation
Rep
"You said your current offer isn't strong — that's actually the thing I want to show you directly, because I don't want you to take my word for it. Let me pull up the job board for literally 30 seconds."
Clear framing. Specific reason tied to their words. Short time commitment stated upfront.
Rep
[screen shares job board] "You can see right now — 71 postings. These are updated weekly. These commission structures are real — $200k OTE on some of these. That's what's available to you starting in your first 30 days. Okay — let me come off screen." [stops sharing] "What's your reaction to that?"
Under 60 seconds. Makes the specific point. Stops immediately. Returns to conversation. Check-in question reactivates engagement.
Prospect
"That's actually really impressive. That was one of my main concerns — I've been burned by offers that aren't real before."
Active, emotional response. The screen share did what it was supposed to: remove a specific doubt with visual proof. Now the conversation continues.
