The Framework — Simplest Form

02

03

04
Pitch

Current reality is entirely the prospect's perspective — how much ground JJ needs to cover there depends on what they volunteer, and sophisticated buyers tend to hand over more, faster, because they don't want to waste time either. Problem is narrowing to the one thing worth resolving for, and getting definitive about it — "do you know that's the problem, or do you think that's the problem?" Solution means the prospect has to believe they know, and knows that you know, you can fix it. After that, JJ's advice was blunt: "your option, your choice — and I would just go to pitch." No elaborate future-pacing required for this audience.

Future Pacing Is Conditional
Future pacing ("what are you trying to accomplish?") can fall flat with sophisticated buyers already in real pain — they don't need a goal painted, they need out. Cost-of-inaction framing is the same: JJ called it "very B2C" and said he'd only use it if the emotion is already at the surface — strong enough that a single pattern-interrupt question ("what happens if we just... stayed exactly where we are?") lands as almost unbearable. Used on someone who isn't there yet, it reads as a canned sales tactic.

Worked Example — The 10-Year Closer

JJ ran a live improvised example to show the framework in motion: a prospect with 10 years in sales, decent at handling objections, struggling with discovery, who'd just landed a new offer. Current reality came free — the prospect over-explained everything (payroll, admin, accounts receivable) unprompted, because sophisticated buyers front-load context to save time. The narrowing question JJ modeled: "What's the number one thing we're resolving for today?" — followed immediately by testing how solid that answer actually is.

The Certainty Test
"How do you know that's the issue? How do you know that's the problem?" A soft answer ("well, it's just what keeps me up at night") gets pushed once more before moving on — the goal is a problem stated with real definiteness, not just the first thing that surfaced. Same discipline applies to "why us": "why are you coming to us for this?" — a name-drop or podcast mention isn't a reason on its own; dig until the prospect can say specifically what they saw that convinced them this could be the exact resolution.

The Real Bottleneck — Curiosity vs. Intent

Aubtin's actual friction: prospects who say, in effect, "I'm already on Claude Code, it's amazing, honestly just wanted to see what you guys do" — with no clear pain and no answer when asked what would even make them switch. JJ's frame: curiosity and intent are different things, and underneath curiosity there can still be real intention — it just hasn't been named yet. The job is to find out which one is actually in the room before treating it like a real discovery call.

The Married-to-Claude-Code Line
JJ's analogy for the call itself: "If you found your wife — if you're married to Claude Code — you wouldn't go out looking for a girlfriend. So what else are you looking for?" If the prospect can't answer, that silence itself is diagnostic — it means there's a gap they haven't articulated, or there's genuinely nothing to resolve and the call shouldn't proceed as a normal discovery conversation.
The Push-Away Move
JJ's scripted version for when nothing resolves: tell the prospect directly that this isn't going to be a productive conversation if they don't know what they're trying to resolve for — that the call is a consultation, not a pitch session, and it's not a viable use of either person's time otherwise. Push for a reschedule rather than trying to manufacture a problem. Reasoning: people chase what runs from them and run from what chases them — over-explaining Apex to someone with no defined need makes them feel sold to, not helped.

The gap: some prospects still arrive having skipped the prep videos entirely — "saw the videos, but really didn't dig into it, wanted to just jump on the call and see." Those are the calls most likely to land as curious-but-unaware.

06

The Fix — Gate the Videos

JJ's recommendation: stop treating the prep videos as optional homework and make watching them a condition of keeping the call. Leverage argument — anyone on this list has already waited a month or two, so a 2-minute ask isn't unreasonable, and it can be framed as a requirement for the conversation to be useful rather than a favor being requested.

Suggested Framing
"We only ask you to watch this for two minutes... in order to actually understand and know what we do, and for this conversation to make sense and be tactical, you have to watch at least this video." JJ's stronger version even floats the call being cancelled or rescheduled if it's skipped — the more bought-in a prospect is walking in, the less time gets burned rediscovering things the page already answers, and the fewer curious-not-intentional calls make it to Aubtin's calendar at all.
