Building Urgency

Real urgency is never created at the close. It's built in the discovery — or it isn't there at all.

What Urgency Actually Is

The misunderstanding

Most reps think urgency is something you apply to a prospect at the close. A limited-time offer. A price that expires Friday. Telling them two spots are left when there aren't. That's not urgency. That's pressure. And there's a fundamental difference between the two.

Pressure is external. It comes from you. The prospect feels it as manipulation — and they resist it, consciously or not. They say they need to think about it. They ghost. They come back weeks later and the decision they almost made has cooled completely.

Real urgency is internal. It lives inside the prospect. It's the feeling that comes from fully understanding what staying in their current situation is actually costing them — every month, every week, every day. You don't create it. You reveal it. Your job is to build the discovery so thoroughly that the prospect arrives at urgency themselves, before you've said a single word about why they should decide now.

The test for real urgency

Ask yourself this after any call where you lost: did the prospect feel the cost of inaction? Not acknowledge it — feel it. There is a massive difference between a prospect who says "yeah I know I need to figure this out" and a prospect who says "I can't keep going another year like this." One is intellectual agreement. The other is urgency. Intellectual agreement does not close.

"The emotions are only built in the discovery. If you didn't build them there, they won't open up in the objections either."

— JJ Hernandez

Where It Comes From

Built in discovery — not injected at the close

Every objection you get at the end of a call was created somewhere in the first 40 minutes. "I need to think about it" means the pain wasn't real enough. "I can't afford it" means you never made the cost of staying put feel greater than the cost of moving. "Let me talk to my partner" means they don't feel certain enough to own the decision — which means you never got them to L4 depth.

These aren't closing problems. They are discovery problems wearing closing masks. When the discovery is done right, urgency shows up before the pitch. The prospect starts leaning in. Their questions shift from skeptical to logistical. They stop asking "will this work?" and start asking "how do I get started?" That's urgency. You didn't create it at the close — you built it in the previous 30 minutes.

If you're trying to build urgency at the close, you're already behind. You can recover — and this doc will show you how — but understand that every tactic you run at the close is an attempt to do in two minutes what should have been built in thirty. It's possible. It's just harder. Do the work in the discovery and the close becomes a formality.

The three conditions for genuine urgency
  • Hell is real and felt. Not described — felt. The prospect has articulated their current situation at an emotional level, not just a factual one. They've connected the pain to their identity, their relationships, or their time.
  • Heaven is specific and personal. Not "I want to make more money." The number, what it changes, who it changes it for, and who they become when they get there.
  • The cost of the gap is quantified. Not abstract — specific. How much per month. How many months. What that adds up to. What they've already spent in time and money going in circles. The math makes the urgency undeniable.

The 5 Sources of Urgency

Every piece of genuine urgency you build in a call comes from one of these five places. Knowing which source is most alive for your specific prospect tells you where to probe deeper and which angle to run when building the gap. Most prospects have two or three active sources — find them and stack them.

01
Time Urgency
Every month they wait is a month they don't get back.
Time is the most finite resource a person has. When you make a prospect calculate what another 6 or 12 months of inaction actually costs them — in income, in identity, in opportunity — time stops feeling abstract. It becomes a real asset they're bleeding out. Time urgency isn't about deadlines you create. It's about deadlines that already exist in their life.
"You've been in this place for 8 months. How many more months is this acceptable to you before you make a real change?"
02
Cost Urgency
Staying put is expensive. Make the math impossible to ignore.
The cost of inaction is almost always higher than the cost of the solution — but prospects never calculate it because no one makes them. Your job is to quantify it. What is the monthly gap between where they are and where they want to be? Multiply that by 12. Add in the money they've already spent on things that didn't work. Now the cost of doing nothing is concrete, real, and sitting in the room with both of you.
"The gap between where you are and where you want to be is about $15K a month. That's $180K over the next 12 months you're leaving on the table. What does that number feel like when you say it out loud?"
03
Identity Urgency
They made a promise to themselves. They haven't kept it.
This is the deepest and most powerful source of urgency. When a prospect has been telling themselves — and maybe others — that they're going to figure this out, every month that passes without movement widens the gap between who they said they'd become and who they actually are. That gap is identity-level pain. When you surface it, the urgency to close it is not manufactured. It's real, personal, and already burning.
"You said you've been telling yourself you'd have this figured out for over a year. What does another year of that same story cost you — beyond just the money?"
04
Pattern Urgency
They've tried before. Without changing the approach, the result is already decided.
When a prospect has tried multiple things and none of them worked, they're sitting in a pattern. Most people don't recognize their own patterns until someone names them. When you identify the pattern — different tools, same result, same stopping point — you create urgency by making the inevitable future visible. They can see exactly where this path goes if nothing changes. That's not pressure. That's clarity.
"You've tried three different approaches in 18 months. What's been different about your approach each time — or has the approach actually changed?" Then: "So what makes this time different if the approach stays the same?"
05
Relational Urgency
Someone else is watching. Someone else is affected.
One of the most powerful urgency triggers is when a prospect realizes their inaction is not just affecting them. A partner who's been patient. A parent they promised they'd take care of. A child who's watching. When the cost of staying stuck extends beyond the individual — to someone who depends on them or believes in them — the urgency becomes responsibility. That's a completely different type of motivation than self-interest, and it runs deeper.
"Who else in your life is affected by where you are right now — financially, emotionally, in terms of what they see from you?" Then: "What do you want them to see happen in the next 12 months?"

Fake Urgency vs. Real Urgency

Fake urgency is urgency you invented. Real urgency is urgency that already existed in the prospect's life — you just made it visible. The difference is not just ethical. It's practical. Fake urgency is countered the moment the prospect walks away and realizes nothing actually expired. Real urgency doesn't go away. It goes home with them.

Fake Urgency Real Urgency What Happens
"This price is only available today." "Every month at $5K instead of $20K costs you $15K. That's already happened for 8 months." Fake creates resentment when they realize nothing expired. Real creates urgency that compounds — it was always true, you just said it out loud.
"We only have 2 spots left." "You've tried three things. The pattern isn't the tools — it's the approach. What changes if the approach doesn't?" Fake is forgotten the moment they leave the call. Real is a question they'll still be sitting with at midnight.
"My manager said I can only hold this deal for 24 hours." "You told me your daughter is watching you build this. What do you want the next 12 months to show her?" Fake creates suspicion. Real creates emotional commitment that doesn't evaporate after the call ends.
"A lot of people are looking at this right now." "A year from now, you're still here. What does that mean for the promise you made yourself?" Fake is ignored by any prospect who has been on more than two sales calls. Real lands because it belongs to them, not to you.
"This is the best offer you're going to find anywhere." "You said this isn't the first time you've tried to solve this. What was different about every other attempt — and why does this feel different?" Fake invites comparison and objection. Real invites self-reflection and commitment. One makes the prospect defensive. The other makes them honest.

The Cost of Inaction Framework

Make the invisible visible

The biggest source of urgency that reps almost never use is simple math. The prospect has a gap. That gap has a cost. That cost compounds every month they don't close it. Most people are living inside this equation without ever doing the arithmetic. When you do it with them — out loud, with their real numbers — the decision to move stops being about your offer and starts being about what it's costing them not to.

The Cost of Inaction — Step by Step
Run this framework live with the prospect's actual numbers.
Step 01
"What are you making right now — give me a real monthly number."
Current: $5,000 / month
Step 02
"What's the number that actually changes things for you?"
Target: $20,000 / month
Step 03
"So the gap right now is — $15,000 a month. Say that back to me."
Monthly gap: $15,000
Step 04
"How long have you been in this gap?"
8 months at $15K/month gap
Step 05
"So you've already left $120,000 on the table in the last 8 months. What does that number feel like when you say it out loud?"
$15,000 × 8 = $120,000 already gone
Step 06
"If nothing changes in the next 12 months — that's another $180,000. What does staying here actually cost you?"
$15,000 × 12 = $180,000 cost of another year of inaction

The most important moment in this framework is when you make them say the number out loud. Don't say it for them. "So you've left about... $120,000 on the table." That lands differently when they do the math themselves. Ask the question and wait. Let the number exist in the room. That is the moment urgency becomes real.

Beyond money — the full cost

After you've run the financial cost, go wider. "And beyond the money — what else has staying here cost you?" This opens the door to identity cost, relational cost, opportunity cost, and time cost. Each one stacks on top of the last. By the time you pitch, the prospect isn't comparing your price to their bank account. They're comparing it to everything they've already lost.

Urgency Language — Reading the Room

What you're listening for

Before you close, scan for urgency signals in what the prospect is saying. Their language tells you whether urgency has been built or whether you need to go back in. A prospect who has real urgency sounds fundamentally different from one who doesn't. These aren't tricks — they're diagnostic signals that tell you where you actually are in the call.

No Urgency — Stay in Discovery
  • "I'll have to look into that."
  • "It sounds interesting."
  • "I think it could work."
  • "Yeah, that makes sense."
  • "I'll talk to my partner about it."
  • "I want to think it through."
  • "Maybe in a couple months."
  • "Things are busy right now."
  • "I like the idea, I'm just not sure."
Urgency Present — Move Toward the Close
  • "I can't keep going like this."
  • "I know I need to do something different."
  • "How do I actually get started?"
  • "I've waited long enough on this."
  • "What would the first 30 days look like?"
  • "I don't want to be having this same conversation in a year."
  • "I know this is what I need."
  • "I'm done trying things that don't work."
  • "I'm ready — what does it take?"

The think-vs-know gap. "I think this could work" and "I know this is what I need" are not the same commitment. The first is intellectual. The second is decided. Before you close, name it: "Do you think this is the right move — or do you know?" Let them answer. If they say think, there's still a gap to close. If they say know, you're done building. Present the investment and ask for the decision.

When Urgency Isn't There at the Close

You're at the close. It doesn't feel right. What now?

You can feel when urgency isn't there. The energy is flat. Their answers are polite. They're agreeable but not committed. If you pitch anyway, you'll get an objection you can't handle because the objection isn't really the objection — the real problem is that the cost of staying put doesn't feel greater than the cost of moving.

Do not panic. Do not push harder. Go back in. You have more discovery to do.

01
Break the fourth wall
Name what you're sensing. Don't run a script.
The most powerful thing you can do when energy shifts is say it out loud. Don't pretend it didn't happen. The prospect already knows something feels off — they're just waiting to see if you'll acknowledge it or push through it.
"I can feel something shifted — am I missing something, or is there something you haven't told me yet that's relevant here?"
"Before I walk you through everything — I want to make sure I actually understand where you are. Can I ask you something directly?"
02
Run the cost framework
Make the monthly number impossible to ignore.
If the financial gap hasn't been quantified yet, do it now. Take them through the math. Current number. Target number. Monthly gap. Months already in that gap. Total already left on the table. Another 12 months of the same. Let them sit with what those numbers add up to.
"Before we go further — let me ask you something. What is the gap right now between where you are and where you want to be, per month?"
03
Surface the pattern
Name the cycle they're in. Make the future predictable.
If this isn't the first time they've looked at solving this problem, the pattern itself becomes a source of urgency. You're not attacking their history — you're making the future visible. Without a change in approach, the next iteration of this same cycle is already written.
"Help me understand — is this the first time you've looked at something like this? ... So what was different about the last attempt? ... What makes this time different if the approach doesn't change?"
04
Project forward — one year
Make them face where they end up if nothing changes.
Pull the prospect out of the present moment and put them into a future version of their same situation. The question is not "what if this doesn't work?" It's "what if nothing changes?" Most people have spent so much time optimizing and hoping that they haven't faced what staying stuck actually looks like as a long-term outcome.
"If we're having this same conversation 12 months from now and nothing has changed — what does that mean for you? Not just the number. What does it mean for you personally?"

The Mistakes That Kill Urgency

Trying to create urgency through tactics instead of discovery. Artificial deadlines and inventory scarcity are a crutch for reps who didn't do the work in the discovery. The prospect feels the manipulation, even if they can't name it. It either kills trust or produces a fragile yes that unravels before payment clears.

Assuming urgency is there because they scheduled the call. Booking a call means they're interested. It does not mean they're urgent. People book calls all the time without any intention of deciding. Urgency has to be built on the call, from scratch, every time.

Pitching before the gap is felt. You know they have a problem. You know your offer solves it. So you pitch. But they haven't felt the weight of the problem yet — they've only described it. Description without emotion produces no urgency. The pitch lands flat. They say they need to think about it. And they mean it.

Letting "I'll think about it" end the conversation. "I need to think about it" is almost never about thinking. It's a signal that urgency wasn't built. The response is not to push — it's to go back in. "Of course — what specifically do you need to work through?" That question almost always surfaces the real objection, which is almost always a gap in the discovery.

Comforting the pain away the moment it surfaces. The prospect says something real — frustrated, embarrassed, scared. And the rep says "don't worry, you're in the right place now." That sentence killed the urgency. The emotion was the urgency. When you comfort it, you dissolve it. Let it breathe. Let them sit in it. Then keep going.

Not doing the math with them. The gap between $6K and $20K is $14K a month. Most reps never say that number out loud. They leave a $168,000-a-year urgency argument on the table because they're afraid the math makes them sound salesy. It doesn't. It makes them sound honest. Run the numbers. Every time.

The rep who makes the prospect feel the full weight of where they are — and clearly shows them the path to where they want to be — never needs a tactic at the close. The decision is already made by the time the pitch starts. That's not magic. That's what happens when you do the discovery right.
