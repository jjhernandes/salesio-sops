The Frame Attack Drill — Holding State Under 5 Consecutive Challenges

Reading about frame doesn't build frame. This drill does. Run it until you can take all five attacks without flinching once.

What the Drill Is

The Setup

You need a partner — a teammate, a fellow student, a coach. One person plays the rep. The other plays the prospect and fires five consecutive frame attacks without letting up. The rep's job is to hold their frame through all five: composure, pace, genuine curiosity. No defensiveness, no aggression, no speeding up.

The attacks are fired in sequence, one right after the other, with barely any time between them. The goal is to simulate the pressure buildup that happens in a real difficult call — where one objection follows another and the cumulative weight of them is what breaks most reps.

This drill is recorded. After the drill, you watch it back. You're looking for the moment where your composure shifts — where you start talking faster, where your tone changes, where you start justifying instead of staying curious. You can't fix what you can't see.

What Holding State Looks Like

Holding state does not mean being robotic or cold. It means that your pace, your tone, your underlying energy stays consistent regardless of what gets thrown at you. You can acknowledge a hard question, you can even feel the weight of it — but you return to center before responding. The prospect watching you hold state through five consecutive attacks reads you as genuinely secure. That security is the product.

The Five Attacks

01
The Price Attack
"Seven thousand five hundred dollars. That's really expensive. I've seen other programs for a fraction of that price."
What this tests
Certainty in the offer's value. Whether you flinch at the number. Whether you need the prospect to think the price is fair.
Failure mode
Immediately justifying the price, listing features, offering payment plans without being asked, or apologizing for the cost.
Frame-holding response
[Pause] "Yeah, it's real money." [Hold.] "What were those other programs?"
02
The Authority Challenge
"How long have you personally been doing this? Have you actually closed at the level you're talking about teaching?"
What this tests
Your ability to absorb a credibility challenge without becoming defensive or rushing to prove yourself.
Failure mode
Listing your entire resume quickly, getting louder, voice going slightly defensive. Or over-compensating with a very long answer that signals insecurity.
Frame-holding response
[Pause] "Fair question." [One specific answer — not a list.] "Here's what I'd rather you judge than my resume — the quality of this conversation. Let's keep going."
03
"I've Heard This Before"
"I've already heard all this stuff. Sales psychology, mindset, identity — every program says this. What's actually different here?"
What this tests
Whether you can hold composure when your offer is commoditized or dismissed. Whether you need the prospect to see you as unique before you feel secure.
Failure mode
Immediately launching into a differentiation pitch. Getting slightly urgent. Starting sentences with "The thing is though..." in a defensive tone.
Frame-holding response
[Pause] "What did you do with it?" [Pause.] "Because there's a difference between hearing something and having it change how you operate. We're interested in the second one."
04
Time Pressure Reversal
"Look, I appreciate everything but I need to think about this. I'm not ready to make a decision today. Can we reconnect in a few weeks?"
What this tests
Whether you maintain your frame as the call moves toward close. This is where most reps either panic-push or immediately capitulate. Both lose.
Failure mode
Immediately scheduling the follow-up to relieve the tension. Or immediately manufacturing urgency ("We have limited spots!"). Both signal the frame is gone.
Frame-holding response
[Pause] "That's fair. What specifically are you weighing?" [Hold.] "I want to make sure a follow-up is actually useful — so let's figure out what's actually in the way first."
05
"Why Should I Trust You?"
"Honestly, I don't know you. I've never worked with you. Why should I trust that you can actually deliver what you're promising?"
What this tests
The final and hardest attack. It's a direct challenge to your legitimacy. This is where reps get flustered, where their voice wavers, where they try too hard to persuade. It's also the moment that, if handled right, closes more deals than any smooth pitch.
Failure mode
Over-explaining. Listing names and testimonials desperately. Voice getting slightly higher or faster. Any sign that you needed them to trust you.
Frame-holding response
[Pause] "You probably shouldn't yet. You just met me." [Pause.] "What would need to be true about this conversation for you to feel like you're making a sound decision — not based on trust, but based on evidence?"

Failure Modes to Watch On the Recording

  • ✕
    Getting defensive. Any response that starts with "Well, actually..." or "No, but..." — this is defensiveness. You're arguing with the attack rather than absorbing it. The frame is being treated as something that needs defending. A strong frame doesn't need defending.
  • ✕
    Speeding up. Watch your pace on the recording. If you're talking faster after attack 3 than you were before attack 1, your frame is weakening under pressure. The goal is the opposite — your pace should stay the same or slow down.
  • ✕
    Getting quieter. Some reps shrink under pressure — their volume drops, they get smaller. This is equally a frame signal as getting louder. Consistent volume = consistent frame.
  • ✕
    Conceding before being asked to. Offering discounts or extras nobody asked for. Agreeing with the attack's premise without it being accurate. Qualifying your earlier statements to soften them.
  • ✕
    Losing curiosity. When your responses shift from genuine questions to prepared defenses, you've lost the frame. The strongest reps stay curious through all five attacks. Every attack is just more information about what this person needs.

The Full Drill — All Five Attacks in Sequence

FULL DRILL — Rep Holding Frame Through All Five Attacks
Partner (Attack 1 — Price)
"$7,500. That's a lot. I've seen programs doing similar stuff for $2,000."
Rep
[Pause] "Yeah, real money." [Holds.] "What were those programs?"
Calm. Doesn't justify. Redirects with curiosity.
Partner (Attack 2 — Authority)
"And honestly — how long have you been doing this? Have you actually closed at the level you teach?"
Rep
[Pause] "Fair question." [Brief specific answer.] "Let's let the quality of this conversation speak for itself — keep going."
One answer, no resume dump. Reframes the test.
Partner (Attack 3 — Commodity)
"I've heard all this mindset and identity stuff before. Every sales trainer says it. What's actually different here?"
Rep
[Pause] "What did you do with it when you heard it before?" [Pause.] "Hearing it and having it change how you operate are two different things."
Still curious. Not defending. Prospect has to engage now.
Partner (Attack 4 — Time)
"I need more time. I'm not deciding today. Let's reconnect in a few weeks."
Rep
[Pause] "What are you weighing?" [Holds.] "I want to make sure a follow-up is actually useful — let's understand what's in the way first."
Frame holds. No panic. No pressure play. Real curiosity.
Partner (Attack 5 — Trust)
"Look — I don't know you. I just met you. Why should I trust that you can actually deliver on any of this?"
Rep
[Pause] "You probably shouldn't yet. You just met me." [Pause.] "What would you need to see in this conversation — not as a pitch, but as actual evidence — to feel like you're making a sound decision?"
Best response in the drill. Honest, undefensive, completely secure. Frame held through all five. This is what you're building toward.

How to Run This Drill

Protocol

Run the drill three times per session: once as the rep, twice as the attacker. The person playing the attacker should vary their delivery — sometimes casual, sometimes aggressive, sometimes disappointed. The rep's frame should hold regardless of the delivery style.

Record every rep. Review every recording looking for the failure modes above. The two most common: speeding up by attack 3 and going defensive by attack 5. Both have the same root cause — you care about the outcome more than the process. The fix is always the same: anchor on genuine curiosity about the person, not the outcome of the drill.

Do this drill before you get on real calls, not instead of them. The drill builds tolerance under controlled pressure. Real calls are where you test whether that tolerance transfers. Run the drill, do the call, review the recording, repeat. This is how frame gets built.
