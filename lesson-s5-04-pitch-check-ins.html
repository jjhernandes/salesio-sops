Pitch Check-Ins

Most reps pitch in a monologue. The prospect drifts at minute two and never comes back. Check-ins are how you keep them in the conversation instead of just listening to it.

The Problem With Monologue Pitching

When you pitch without checking in, you have no idea what's landing and what isn't. You're talking into a void and hoping the prospect is tracking with you. Most of the time, they aren't — not because your pitch is bad, but because sustained passive listening is hard, especially on a sales call where the prospect is simultaneously evaluating whether to spend $7,500.

The monologue pitch feels efficient to the rep. Cover the points, get to the close. But it bypasses the prospect's active participation, which is exactly what you need for a decision to feel earned rather than pushed.

What Drifting Looks Like

Short responses. "Mmhm." "Yeah." "Got it." These are not engagement signals. These are polite acknowledgments from someone who has partially checked out. A prospect who is genuinely engaged asks questions, adds context, makes connections to their own situation. If you're 90 seconds into a pitch and getting only one-word responses, they've drifted.

The rep who doesn't check in pushes forward. The rep who checks in catches the drift early and pulls the prospect back in.

The Check-In Phrases

Check in every 60-90 seconds during the pitch. Not every sentence — that's interrogation. Not never — that's a monologue. Every minute to ninety seconds is the rhythm that keeps a prospect active without disrupting your flow.

Phrases That Work

These check-ins are short, genuine, and open enough to get a real answer — not a polite "yes."

01
Check-In
"Does that make sense for your situation?"
The word "situation" is important. It's not "does that make sense?" which invites a reflexive "yeah." Adding "for your situation" forces them to map what you just said to their own reality — which is active engagement, not passive agreement.
02
Check-In
"Is that the kind of thing you were looking for?"
This one is especially useful after presenting a feature. It invites them to confirm or clarify. If it's not what they were looking for, you need to know that now — not at the close.
03
Check-In
"How does that land for you?"
More open than the first two. Invites any response — positive, uncertain, or questioning. The goal isn't confirmation. It's information. You want to know what they're actually thinking in real time.
04
Use Sparingly
"Does that resonate?"
Fine to use once. If you use it more than once per call it starts to feel like a tic — a filler phrase that signals you're fishing for agreement rather than genuinely checking in. Vary your check-in language.
The goal of every check-in is a real answer, not a "yes." If they're saying "yeah" to every check-in without adding anything, your questions are too closed. Open them up.

What a Check-In Tells You

Reading the Response

The check-in is a diagnostic tool. What the prospect says in response tells you exactly where you are in the pitch.

Response Type What It Means What You Do
Engaged — adds context, makes a connection, asks a follow-up They're with you. The pitch is landing. Keep going. Validate briefly: "Exactly." Then move to the next point.
Neutral — "yeah," "mmhm," "sounds good" Polite but not tracking. They may have drifted. Anchor to them directly: "Based on what you told me about X — does this feel like the piece that was missing?"
Hesitant — pause, "I guess," "maybe" Something didn't land or conflicted with what they believe. Stop. "Tell me what's making you hesitate on that." Get the real objection now, not at the close.
Objecting internally — sudden question about price, logistics, or comparison They've hit a friction point and redirected to a safer topic. Address the surface question briefly, then re-anchor: "Before we go there — does what I just described feel like what you need?"

When a Check-In Reveals Misalignment

Do not push forward when a check-in reveals misalignment. This is the most common mistake. Rep gets a hesitant response, and instead of addressing it, they move to the next point hoping it'll smooth over. It won't. The misalignment compounds. By the close, the prospect has three or four unresolved friction points and they default to "I need to think about it."

How to Handle It

Stop. Name what you noticed. "Sounds like that one didn't land the same way — what's coming up for you?" Let them tell you. The real concern is almost always more specific and more solvable than what the surface hesitation suggests.

Once you have the real concern, address it directly, then reconfirm before moving forward. "Does that answer what was coming up?" Only continue the pitch after they've confirmed alignment. Moving forward without confirmation is how monologue pitching happens even when you're asking check-in questions.

The Adjust-and-Continue

Sometimes a check-in reveals that the point you just made isn't relevant to them. The feature doesn't apply. That's fine. Acknowledge it: "Yeah, that one might not be the most relevant for where you are right now — the more important piece is probably..." And redirect to something that is relevant. Adjusting mid-pitch is not a sign of weakness. It's a sign you're listening.

Call Exchange — Monologue vs. Check-In Pitch

VERSION 1 — Monologue Pitch (No Check-Ins)
Rep
"So with the 1-on-1 coaching we really dig into your specific call patterns and identify what's capping your close rate. And then with the group calls there are seven per week covering everything from setting to psychology to B2B strategy, so you're getting reinforcement from multiple angles. The job board has 60 to 80 companies posting every week and we guarantee placement. And then there's the community — WhatsApp, Skool, the whole thing. Plus the money-back guarantee on the ROI side. Does that make sense?"
Four minutes of pitch delivered without pause. "Does that make sense?" at the end is a box-check, not a real check-in. Prospect has been passive the entire time.
Prospect
"Yeah... yeah that sounds good. What's the cost again?"
"Sounds good" with no specifics means nothing landed specifically. "What's the cost again?" is not a buying signal — it's the only question they have because they weren't actively engaged.
VERSION 2 — Pitch With Check-Ins
Rep
"So the 1-on-1 coaching is where we go into your specific calls — not generic coaching, we look at your patterns, find the ceiling, and break it. Does that feel like the piece that's been missing for you?"
One point. Then a check-in. Tight. Personal.
Prospect
"Yeah — yeah, that's actually what I've been wanting. Like someone to actually look at my calls and tell me what I'm doing wrong."
Real engagement. They elaborated. They're active in the conversation now.
Rep
"Exactly. And connected to that — you mentioned you're using a script and it feels fake. The scriptless framework we teach is specifically designed to replace that. Natural conversation, guided by genuine curiosity. Is that the kind of approach you were looking for?"
Second point. Check-in again. Still personal, still connected to discovery.
Prospect
"Honestly yes. I hate the script. I want to actually sound like myself on calls."
Active, emotional, engaged. They're selling themselves.
Rep
"Good — that's going to change a lot for you. And last thing — your offer situation. The job board solves that. 60-80 vetted companies weekly, guaranteed placement within your first month. Does that handle the concern you had around not being on a strong offer?"
Third point. Direct confirmation check. Pitch complete.
Prospect
"Yeah. I mean, that would be huge actually. What does enrollment look like?"
Buying signal. They pulled the next step. Check-ins turned a passive listener into an active participant who closed themselves.
