You are strong in a lot of the areas that matter most in this role. This doc covers what is working, what to sharpen, and specific things to do differently on the next call.

Overall

What is working

  • Warm, natural rapport from the first 30 seconds
  • Strong empathy when prospects share personal situations
  • Good opening question: "what had you book a call with us"
  • Gets to income goals and numbers quickly
  • Tailors the recommendation to each person's situation
  • Handles couples and partners well
  • Books follow-ups cleanly with urgency built in
  • Confident presenting pricing and payment options

Where to sharpen

  • Feature pitch runs too long without connecting back to what they said
  • Buying signals get acknowledged but not closed on
  • Third-party dependencies let prospects delay unnecessarily
  • Guarantee is undersold as a close tool
  • Discovery stops too early before the pitch starts
  • Urgency comes at the end instead of being woven in

What is Working

Your rapport is a real asset

Both calls felt warm and human from the start. Felipa opened up about losing her brother within the first minute, and you responded with genuine care before pivoting back to the discovery. That is not easy to do and it matters. Prospects who feel heard are more likely to trust your recommendation. Keep this exactly as it is.

You get to the numbers fast and without awkwardness

You asked Felipa directly what she needed per month, and when she gave you a vague answer you followed up until you had a real number. With Cindy and Fred you broke down their income into a monthly figure on your own and confirmed it with them. That is a skill. A lot of closers let the financial conversation stay vague and it hurts the rest of the call. You do not do that.

You personalize the recommendation well

On the Felipa call, you correctly identified Platinum for her and Diamond for Brian based on what they each shared about their goals. You did not just default to one package for everyone. On the Cindy and Fred call, you were already mapping their income targets to specific outcomes. That kind of customization builds trust and makes the close feel like a natural fit rather than a pitch.

You close the follow-up cleanly

Both calls ended with a specific next step and a time on the calendar. You used the 48-hour discount window on the Felipa call to create a real reason to come back quickly. That is the right instinct. The follow-up call is booked, there is a deadline, and the prospect knows what to do next.

Where to Sharpen

1. The feature pitch runs too long and loses connection to their goals

On the Felipa call, after a strong discovery section you went into a sequential list of everything included: dedicated communication channel, strategy sessions, live calls, community, chatbot, curriculum updates, onboarding, sprints. Each feature got explained fully before moving to the next. By the time you finished, Felipa was mostly just saying "yeah" and "okay." The features are real, but they need to be tied back to what Felipa specifically told you she needed. She said she wanted guidance and someone to take her from zero to her goal. Every feature you mention should connect back to that. "The dedicated communication channel exists specifically so you are never stuck waiting for an answer when you hit a roadblock" lands differently than just describing what the channel is.

Felipa said she wanted guidance and someone to take her from zero to her goal. Six minutes later Sarah is listing the chatbot, the community, the curriculum updates, without tying any of them back to that specific goal. Felipa said "yeah, yeah" throughout, but she was not actively engaged.
2. You see the buying signals but do not close on them

Felipa said "I just need to know the pricing" at the 12-minute mark. That is a buying signal. A prospect who only wants to know the price is ready to move. Instead of pivoting to close, the call continued for another 20 minutes covering features and logistics. The same thing happened when Felipa said "I'd like what I hear" and "we just want to start." Those are moments to stop presenting and start closing. When a prospect tells you they are in, trust it and move toward the decision.

Felipa at 12:32: "I don't have any questions. I just want to see what the pricing is because actually my friend got me into this." That is the moment to present pricing and ask if she is ready to move forward. Instead the call continued for 20 more minutes.
3. Third-party dependencies are letting prospects delay when they do not need to

On the Felipa call, Brian was not on the call. Felipa came in ready, asked about pricing, asked about plans, asked about financing, and was clearly interested. But the conversation shifted toward what Brian might want and what Brian would need to decide. By the end, the next step was "talk to Brian and come back tomorrow" when Felipa may have been ready to move on her own. It is fine to note Brian as a potential partner, but Felipa's decision does not need to wait for him. Ask her directly: "Setting Brian aside for a moment, are you ready to move forward for yourself?" Let Brian be a separate conversation. Do not let someone else's availability become the reason a willing prospect delays.

Felipa had enough information to make a decision for herself. She asked about financing for herself specifically. But the call ended with the decision deferred to a joint call with Brian the next day, when she could have enrolled that evening.
4. The guarantee is being undersold

The $20,000 sales month guarantee on Platinum and the $50,000 guarantee on Diamond are among the strongest things in this offer. They remove risk completely. If a prospect does not hit that number in six months, they get their money back. That is a powerful reason to stop hesitating. Right now the guarantee comes up in the middle of the feature list and gets the same amount of time as the chatbot and the community. It deserves its own moment. Introduce it separately, pause on it, and connect it directly to whatever fear or hesitation the prospect has expressed.

The guarantee was introduced as item number two or three in the list of features. It should be its own moment: "Before I keep going, I want to make sure you heard what Samar said about the guarantee, because this is the part that most people want to talk about..."
5. Discovery stops when the pitch starts

Both calls had a solid discovery section at the beginning. You asked about their goals, their timeline, their income target. Then the pitch started and discovery stopped. But prospects reveal important information throughout a call, not just at the beginning. On the Cindy and Fred call, Fred mentioned he would need to replace $500,000 a year to leave his job. That is a significant detail that should have changed how you framed the opportunity for him and what you said about the timeline to get there. When new information surfaces mid-call, pause the pitch and work with it.

Fred sharing his $500K income came up naturally in conversation. A strong response would be: "Okay, that changes things. Let me ask you a few more questions about Fred's situation before I keep going, because what I recommend for him might look different."
6. Urgency is coming at the end instead of being woven in

The 48-hour discount window is real and it works. But it lands best when it comes up naturally during the call rather than as the last thing before you hang up. If you mention it earlier, you set the frame for the whole conversation: there is a real deadline and a real financial reason to decide. When it only appears at the end, it can feel like a tactic rather than a real constraint. Bring it in earlier, around the time you present pricing, and let it sit in the background for the rest of the call.

Felipa call, 31:45: "Now, I will tell you, if you guys want to take advantage of those discounts that Samar gave you, he gave you 48 hours to do that." This came up in the last 5 minutes of the call. It would have more impact introduced when pricing first comes up.

Three Things to Do on Your Next Call

1. When a prospect says they are ready, stop presenting and start closing

The moment a prospect tells you they like what they hear, they want to know the pricing, or they are asking logistical questions like how payment works, that is your signal. Stop the feature walk. Present the pricing, anchor the guarantee, and ask if they are ready to move forward. You do not need to finish the pitch if they are already sold.

"It sounds like you're liking what you're hearing. Before I keep going, let me just share the investment with you and then we can figure out what makes the most sense for your situation. Does that work?"
2. Separate the prospect's decision from the third party

When a partner, spouse, or friend is not on the call, do not let their absence become the reason the prospect cannot decide. Ask the prospect directly what they want to do for themselves. Let the third party be a separate question.

"Setting Brian aside for a second, because I know he's not here, I want to make sure we figure out what makes sense for you. Are you ready to move forward on your end, and then we can talk about what Brian wants to do when you connect with him?"
3. Give the guarantee its own moment

The guarantee is the biggest objection-handler in the offer and it is not getting the weight it deserves. When you introduce it, pause on it. Make the prospect sit with it. Then connect it directly to whatever hesitation they expressed during discovery.

"I want to make sure you really heard what Samar said about the guarantee, because this is the part that changes things for most people. If you follow the framework and you do not hit a $20,000 sales month within six months, you get your investment back. Fully. So the only way you lose here is if you don't show up and do the work. Based on what you've told me about why you're here, I don't think that's a concern for you."

What to keep doing

Your tone and your ability to connect with people quickly are real strengths. Both Felipa and Cindy opened up to you fast, and that is not by accident. Keep the warm opening, keep the empathy, and keep getting to the financial numbers early. Those things are already working. The adjustments above are about the back half of the call, not the beginning.

Bottom Line

Sarah, you are already doing the hardest part of this job well. You make people feel comfortable, you ask good questions, and you build real trust fast. The gap is in the close. You are leaving calls before the prospect has fully committed, either because the pitch runs long and they disengage, or because a third party becomes the reason to wait. Trust your read of the room. When someone tells you they are interested, believe them and move toward the decision. You have earned the right to ask for the close on these calls. Start using it.
