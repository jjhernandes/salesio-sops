What is Working

Cindy and Fred, two experienced attorneys who ask hard questions, stayed engaged and collaborative for a full 65 minutes. You asked Felipa directly what she needed per month, followed up when she gave a vague answer, and got a real number. With Cindy and Fred you broke down their income into a monthly figure on your own and confirmed it with them. With Casey you tied his stated goal of $5K/month to the profit planner right away. Where to Sharpen

1. The feature pitch runs too long and loses connection to their goals

On the Felipa call, after a strong discovery section you went into a sequential list of everything included: dedicated communication channel, strategy sessions, live calls, community, chatbot, curriculum updates, onboarding, sprints. Each feature got explained fully before moving to the next. The features are real, but they need to be tied back to what each specific prospect told you they needed. Felipa said she wanted guidance and someone to take her from zero to her goal. Every feature should connect back to that: "The dedicated communication channel exists specifically so you are never stuck waiting when you hit a roadblock" lands differently than just describing what the channel is.

Felipa said she wanted guidance and someone to take her from zero to her goal. Six minutes later Sarah is listing the chatbot, the community, and curriculum updates without tying any of them back to that specific goal. Felipa said "yeah, yeah" throughout, but she was not actively engaged.
2. You see the buying signals but do not close on them

Felipa said "I just need to know the pricing" at the 12-minute mark. That is a buying signal. A prospect who only wants to know the price is ready to move. Instead of pivoting to close, the call continued for another 20 minutes. Casey came in pre-sold — "If I pass the interview, I want to get started" — and had already secured his wife's buy-in before the call. He was qualifying the program, not the other way around. Both of them gave you clear signals that they were in. When a prospect tells you they are ready, stop presenting and start closing.

Felipa at 12:32: "I don't have any questions. I just want to see what the pricing is." Casey early in the call: "I'm ready. I already have $5K set aside. If this conversation goes well on your side, I want to get started." Both are done deciding. Present the price and ask if they want to move forward.
3. Third-party dependencies are letting prospects delay when they do not need to

On the Felipa call, Brian was not on the call. Felipa came in ready, asked about pricing, asked about plans, asked about financing, and was clearly interested. But the conversation shifted toward what Brian might want and the next step became "talk to Brian and come back tomorrow." Felipa may have been ready to move on her own. It is fine to note Brian as a potential partner, but Felipa's decision does not need to wait for him. Ask her directly: "Setting Brian aside for a moment, are you ready to move forward for yourself?" Let Brian be a separate conversation.

Felipa had enough information to make a decision for herself. She asked about financing for herself specifically. But the call ended with the decision deferred to a joint call the next day, when she could have enrolled that evening.
4. The guarantee is being undersold

The $20,000 sales month guarantee on Platinum and the $50,000 guarantee on Diamond are among the strongest things in this offer. They remove risk completely. If a prospect does not hit that number in the measurement window, they get their money back. That is a powerful reason to stop hesitating. Right now the guarantee comes up in the middle of the feature list and gets the same amount of time as the chatbot and the community. It deserves its own moment. Introduce it separately, pause on it, and connect it directly to whatever concern the prospect has expressed. On the Cindy and Fred call, Cindy actually asked a great legal-style question about the guarantee requirements — you answered it well. That is the tone to use on every call.

The guarantee was introduced as one item in a list of features. It should be its own moment: "Before I keep going, I want to make sure you really heard what Samar said about the guarantee, because this is the part that changes things for most people..."
5. Discovery stops when the pitch starts

All three calls had solid discovery at the beginning. But discovery stopped when the pitch started, even though prospects kept revealing important information. On the Cindy and Fred call, Fred mentioned mid-call that he would need to replace nearly $500,000 a year to leave his job. That is a significant detail that changes the timeline and what Diamond means for him specifically. When new information surfaces mid-call, pause the pitch and work with it. Ask a follow-up question. Adjust what you say next. Let the pitch be shaped by what they are telling you throughout the call, not just at the beginning.

Fred sharing his $500K income came up naturally mid-conversation. A strong response: "Okay, that changes things a bit. Before I keep going, let me ask you a couple more questions about what the path looks like for you specifically, because it's a little different from Cindy's."
6. Pricing confusion needs to be resolved on the call, not after

On the Casey call, when he clicked the payment link and saw $9,000 instead of the $7,000 he thought he'd seen in Samar's training, that became the reason he did not complete it. Your response was to say you would go back and check your notes on what Samar had said. That deferred the resolution to after the call and let Casey leave with an open question. The right move is to resolve it right then: "The standard price is $11,000 — Samar gave everyone on that training a $2,000 discount, which brings it to $9,000. The $7,000 you may have noted was probably a different number. You're looking at the correct price. Let me help you get this done while we're still here." Casey had Maddie's buy-in already. He was sold. The pricing confusion was the only thing stopping him, and it could have been handled in 60 seconds.

Casey, 43:19: "I'm not going to do it right now just because I got a 7 instead of a 9." He had already said he was ready to pull the trigger. A real-time answer to the pricing question would have closed the deal on that call.

Four Things to Do on Your Next Call

1. When a prospect says they are ready, stop presenting and start closing

The moment a prospect tells you they like what they hear, they want to know the pricing, or they are asking logistical questions like how payment works — that is your signal. Stop the feature walk. Present the pricing, anchor the guarantee, and ask if they are ready to move forward. You do not need to finish the pitch if they are already sold.

"It sounds like you're liking what you're hearing. Before I keep going, let me just share the investment with you and we can figure out what makes the most sense for your situation. Does that work?"
2. Separate the prospect's decision from the third party

When a partner, spouse, or friend is not on the call, do not let their absence become the reason the prospect cannot decide. Ask the prospect directly what they want to do for themselves. Let the third party be a separate question. If the prospect already told you the other person is on board, name it: "You already mentioned Maddie is in — what specifically do you want to run by her?" That turns a vague delay into a real question you can answer right now.

"Setting Brian aside for a second, because I know he's not here — I want to make sure we figure out what makes sense for you. Are you ready to move forward on your end, and then we can talk about what Brian wants to do when you connect with him?"
3. Give the guarantee its own moment

The guarantee is the biggest objection-handler in the offer and it is not getting the weight it deserves. When you introduce it, pause on it. Make the prospect sit with it. Then connect it directly to whatever hesitation they expressed during discovery. For a lawyer like Cindy asking about contractual requirements, or a sales rep like Casey who wants to be realistic about timelines, the guarantee answers exactly what they are worried about.

"I want to make sure you really heard what Samar said about the guarantee, because this is the part that changes things for most people. If you follow the framework and you do not hit a $20,000 sales month within six months, you get your investment back. Fully. So the only way this does not work is if you don't show up and do the work. Based on everything you've told me today, I don't think that's a concern for you."
4. Resolve pricing questions on the call, not after

When a prospect raises a pricing question or sees a number that does not match what they expected, resolve it right then. Saying you will go back and check later hands the prospect an open question to sit with overnight. Prospects who leave with open questions do not always come back. Know the pricing structure cold: $11,000 standard, $2,000 discount for training attendees, $9,000 for Platinum. When the number surprises them, explain it in one sentence and keep going toward the close.

"The standard price is $11,000. Samar gave everyone on that training a $2,000 discount, which brings it to $9,000. What you wrote down as $7,000 was probably a different number — you're looking at the right price now. Let me help you get this done while we're still here."
