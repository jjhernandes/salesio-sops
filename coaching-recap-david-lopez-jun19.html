What Went Wrong in the First Video
The video looked like someone acting like a sales rep — structured, stiff, trying to check the right boxes. That energy kills your chances before a hiring manager finishes watching. It's the opposite of what actually gets you placed.
The Frame to Carry Into This Video
Sales and Dating Are the Same Thing
When you meet someone you're interested in, you don't walk up and list everything about yourself. You ask questions. You're curious. You're chill. You let it come to you. The second you start trying to perform, they feel it — and it kills the connection.

Your intro video works the same way. The person watching is deciding if they want to invite you onto their team. A real human with a real story beats a polished pitch every single time. You're not auditioning — you're introducing yourself like you would at a bar.
You're already funny, chill, and easy to be around. That's what needs to come through. The video should feel like you're talking to a friend — not presenting to a stranger.

What You Took Away From This
You don't have to follow a rigid structure — you have to adapt the structure to sound like you. There's a difference between knowing the format and owning it.
Working in environments that put you in a box — restaurant, college — has trained you to suppress who you are. That pattern has leaked into how you present yourself professionally. Recognize it and break it deliberately.
The goal of the video isn't to look professional. It's to make someone feel like they already know you — and want to.
