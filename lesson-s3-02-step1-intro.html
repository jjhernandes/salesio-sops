Step 1 — Intro

The first 60 seconds set the entire tone of the call. Most reps spend those seconds performing. That's the trap.

What the Intro Is Actually For

The intro has one job: establish that this is a professional conversation between two adults. Not a sales call. Not a pitch. A conversation where one person understands what the other is trying to solve.

You are not trying to be liked in the first 60 seconds. You are trying to be trusted. Those are different things — and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in the opening of a call.

The Only Two Things the Intro Needs to Do

First: establish clarity on why you're both on the call. Second: hand control of the conversation to the prospect by asking them to speak first. That's it. Anything beyond that is you performing.

The Likability Trap

Most reps are trained to "build rapport" in the first few minutes. Ask about the weather. Notice something in the background. Comment on their name. This is almost always a mistake.

When you're trying to be liked, the prospect can feel it. It reads as performance — as someone setting up a sale rather than showing up to help. The smarter the prospect, the faster they detect it, and the more their guard goes up.

Why Reps Fall Into It

They're nervous. They want a warm opening before they start asking hard questions. So they delay the real conversation with small talk that feels friendly but signals to the prospect: "I need you to like me before I ask you for something." That's a neediness frame — and it starts the call in the wrong position.

The rule: Rapport is built through competence, not connection. When a prospect feels like you understand their situation deeply, they trust you. Asking about their weekend before you've done anything useful hasn't earned that trust.

What Respect Looks Like in the First 60 Seconds

Performing (Likability First)Grounded (Respect First)
"Hey! So great to finally talk to you. I love your background — is that a home office? That's so cool." "Hey [name], appreciate you making the time. Before I say anything, I want to make sure I actually understand your situation — so tell me what's going on."
Fast pace, high energy, performing enthusiasm they don't actually feel Calm, unhurried, comfortable with a beat of silence before speaking
Fills every gap with something friendly to maintain warmth Lets the prospect land before jumping in — treats silence as normal
Non-Needy Energy

Non-needy doesn't mean cold. It means you're not performing. You're calm. You're here. You don't need the call to go a specific way in the first 30 seconds — and that lack of need is what makes the prospect feel safe enough to be honest with you.

The One Question That Opens Every Call Right

After a brief acknowledgment, ask one question that hands the floor to the prospect and signals that this is about them, not about your pitch:

"Before I jump into anything on our end — tell me what's going on for you. What made you want to have this conversation?"

Why This Works

It immediately positions you as someone who listens before presenting. It gives the prospect permission to tell you exactly where they are — without you having to drag it out of them. And it signals that you're not going to pitch until you understand what they actually need.

Every other step of the framework depends on what comes out of this question. Don't shortcut it.

Call Exchange — Two Openings

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Opens With Performance
Rep
"Hey Marcus! So great to connect. Man, I've been looking forward to this one. How's your day going? Did you have a good week?"
High energy, unfounded. Prospect has done nothing to earn this enthusiasm. Reads as a warm-up act before a pitch.
Prospect
"Uh, yeah, good. Pretty busy."
Rep
"Awesome! So let me tell you a little bit about what we do here at Sales.io — we've helped over 2,100 reps..."
Prospect hasn't said a real word yet. Rep is already presenting. Prospect's guard is up. Discovery is over before it started.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Opens With Respect
Rep
"Hey Marcus. Appreciate you making the time. Before I get into anything — what made you want to jump on this call?"
Calm. No performance. Immediately hands the floor to the prospect. The call is now about them, not about the rep's pitch.
Prospect
"Honestly, I've been stuck at the same income level for six months and I don't know what I'm missing. I feel like I'm doing the right things but the results aren't there."
Rep
"Got it. Six months at the same level — walk me through what the day-to-day looks like. What are you actually doing that isn't converting?"
Stayed with them. No pitch impulse. Moved naturally into Step 2 — Current Reality. The call has real momentum now.
