Lesson 6 · Section 8

Tactical Empathy

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. "I understand" proves nothing. Demonstrating that you understand — that's what creates trust.

"I Understand" Is the Laziest Phrase in Sales

"I understand." Two words. Zero proof. Every rep on every bad sales call says them. Every prospect has heard them from people who clearly didn't understand anything.

When you say "I understand," you're claiming empathy. But you're providing no evidence. The prospect has no reason to believe you. They've been told "I understand" by reps who immediately followed it up with a pitch that proved they'd understood nothing.

The Difference

Claimed empathy: "I understand how you feel. A lot of people feel that way at first."

Demonstrated empathy: "It sounds like you've been down this road before — invested time and money, tried to do the right things — and you still haven't broken through. That's a really specific kind of frustration."

One of these proves you were listening. The other proves you have a script.

Tactical Empathy — The Voss Framework

From Never Split the Difference

Chris Voss defines tactical empathy as understanding the feelings and mindset of another person in the moment, and hearing what's behind those feelings. The "tactical" part means you're using this understanding to navigate the conversation — not as a manipulation, but as a genuine attempt to connect before you try to move.

The implementation is almost entirely through labeling (S8 Lesson 4) and mirroring — reflecting back what you hear in a way that invites the other person to expand and correct.

The mental shift: your job is not to be understood first. It's to make them feel understood. When a prospect feels fully understood, their resistance drops. Not because you convinced them — because they don't feel like they're being sold to.

Side-by-Side: How Each Lands

Prospect Says Claimed Empathy Tactical Empathy
"I've tried things before and they haven't worked." "I totally understand. A lot of people feel that way. What we do is different." "It sounds like you've invested in solutions that didn't deliver on what they promised. That wears on you."
"I'm just not sure about the timing." "I understand. Timing is always a factor. Here's why now actually makes sense..." "It seems like there's something making you hesitate — and I don't think it's just the calendar. What's actually driving that?"
"It's a lot of money." "I completely understand. We do have payment plans if that helps." "It sounds like you want to be sure the investment makes sense before you commit — not just that you can afford it, but that it's worth it."
"I need to talk to my partner." "Of course, that totally makes sense. When could we get them on a call?" "It sounds like you want to be aligned with them before you move. Is it more about them having input, or are there concerns you're not sure how to explain to them yet?"

Full Exchange — Tactical Empathy Through a Difficult Moment

Prospect Is Fully Resistant — Rep Uses Tactical Empathy to Re-Open
Prospect
"Look, I've been on calls like this before. I know you're going to tell me this is the best program out there and I need to move on it today. I'm not doing that."
Rep
"You're right that that's how a lot of calls go. And it sounds like you've been on enough of those that you're walking into this one expecting it." [pause]
Doesn't defend. Labels the experience they've had, not what they're feeling right now. Validates the pattern.
Prospect
"Yeah. Every program is 'the one that actually works.' I'm skeptical."
Rep
"That skepticism sounds earned. Like you didn't start cynical — it came from somewhere specific." [pause] "What happened?"
Labels the skepticism as earned, not as a problem to overcome. Calibrated question at the end opens the story without demanding it.
Prospect
"I spent $5K on a coaching program last year. Barely got any personal attention. Just pre-recorded stuff."
Rep
"So you paid for what felt like access to something real and got a content library. That's a specific kind of disappointment — because you had to convince yourself it was worth it and then it wasn't."
Specific label. Names the internal experience (having to convince yourself, then being let down). Not generic. This only lands this way because the rep was actually listening.
Prospect
"Exactly. So what makes you any different?"
Prospect asks the question — which means they're actually open to the answer. Tactical empathy got them from closed-off to curious.
