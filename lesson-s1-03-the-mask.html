The Mask

The version of yourself you put on for calls is the reason they don't work. The best closers don't sound like closers.

What the Mask Is

Every rep has one. It's the performance version of yourself that activates when you enter sales mode. You speak differently. You use phrases you'd never use in a normal conversation. Your voice changes — the pitch, the pace, the warmth. There's a "sales you" and a "real you," and somewhere along the way, you learned to switch between them when the stakes got real.

The mask isn't always obvious. It's not always the cartoonish slick-talking closer. Sometimes it's subtler: a slight formality that wasn't there before. A sentence structure that doesn't quite sound like how you talk. A specific vocabulary that only comes out when you're selling.

What it looks like in practice

A rep who's completely natural and funny with their friends — engaging, real, easy to be around — suddenly becomes stiff and procedural the moment they log onto a sales call. The intelligence is still there. The humor disappeared. So did the warmth. What showed up instead was a character. A sales rep character. And the prospect is talking to the character, not the person.

Why You Built It

Fear of rejection — but rejection as yourself specifically. If you perform a character and the prospect says no, the character got rejected. The real you stayed protected. The mask is a defense mechanism. It disguises itself as a sales strategy.

Most reps built the mask without realizing it. They watched other closers, consumed sales content, heard what "good sales" was supposed to sound like, and gradually assembled a persona that matched that image. Nobody sat down and said "I'm going to build a fake sales version of myself." But over time, it accumulated.

The intention behind the mask was good: be more professional, be more effective, be taken seriously. The result was the opposite. The mask traded authenticity for performance — and performance is what prospects push back against.

What the Prospect Experiences

The prospect can't always name what's off. But they feel it. Something doesn't quite line up. The words sound professional, the energy says something else. The claims sound confident, the body language says uncertain. There's a slight uncanny valley quality to the interaction — close enough to a real conversation to feel like one, but just off enough to register as a performance.

This misalignment is the source of "I need to think about it" from people who were warm 10 minutes ago. They didn't lose interest in the offer. They lost trust in the person presenting it. They couldn't quite connect to them. And you don't buy from people you can't connect to — regardless of what the offer is.

The prospect isn't evaluating your technique. They're evaluating you. And if the you they're evaluating is a performance, they're making a judgment call based on incomplete information — because they never actually met you.

The Authenticity Paradox

The harder you try to be good at sales, the worse you perform. The reps closing everything are often the ones who look like they're not even trying — because they aren't. They're not performing. They're just being.

The paradox: the skills you're trying to develop — confidence, authority, connection, trust — all degrade when you're performing. Confidence doesn't come from the words you say. It comes from the certainty underneath them. Authority isn't projected, it's felt. Connection can't be manufactured. All of these things require you to actually be present as yourself.

"The best closers don't sound like closers."

— JJ Hernandez

What makes someone great at sales has almost nothing to do with sounding like a salesperson. It has everything to do with being genuinely engaged, genuinely curious, and not needing the outcome more than the prospect needs the solution. None of that can be performed. It's either there or it isn't — and the mask guarantees it isn't.

Recognizing Your Mask

The mask is often invisible to the person wearing it. You've been wearing it so long it feels like you. These are specific, observable signs that the mask is on.

  • ! Your voice pitch shifts when you go into sales mode. Higher, tighter, more "up" than your natural register. Record yourself and compare your first 60 seconds on a call to how you sound telling a friend a story.
  • ! You use phrases you'd never say in a normal conversation. "Absolutely." "100%." "That's a great question." "What I'm hearing you say is..." Borrowed language from sales training that sounds fine in a course and robotic on an actual call.
  • ! You feel slightly separate from yourself mid-call. Like you're watching yourself perform rather than being present in the conversation. A subtle dissociation — the observer and the performer at once.
  • ! You're performing rather than connecting. You're thinking about how you sound more than you're listening to what they're saying. The call is happening to you rather than you being in it.
  • ! You feel relieved when the call ends. Not because it was hard, but because you can stop performing. That relief is the mask coming off. Notice it.
  • ! Your humor and personality disappear. You're funny in real life. You're serious on calls. Why? Because somewhere you learned that "serious = professional = trustworthy." None of that is true. What's actually trustworthy is being the same person in both contexts.

How to Take It Off

Not a switch. Not a mindset shift. A practice. And a slow one. The mask has years of reinforcement behind it. Taking it off is a deliberate, iterative process of collapsing the gap between how you are in real conversations and how you are on sales calls.

1
Find the Gap
Record both — a real conversation and a sales call
Call a friend and just talk. Record it. Then record your next sales call. Listen to both back to back. The difference between those two recordings is the mask. You'll hear it immediately — the shift in vocabulary, pacing, warmth, naturalness.
Most reps are shocked when they do this. They sound like two different people. That gap is exactly what you're closing.
2
Identify the Specific Phrases
List the words and phrases you'd never say normally
Go through your call recording and mark every phrase that felt borrowed — that came from sales training, not from how you actually talk. These are the first things to drop. Replace each one with how you'd naturally say the same thing to a friend.
"That's a great question" → "Oh, that's a good one, let me think." "What I'm hearing you say is..." → "So it sounds like..." "Absolutely" (said after everything) → silence, or actual agreement.
3
Run the Friend Test
Pitch something fake to a friend, then call a real prospect
Call a friend and pitch them on something you don't actually sell — a fake product, a made-up service, a course that doesn't exist. Your natural, relaxed, funny communication style will come through because the stakes feel lower. Then immediately get on a real call. Try to bring the same energy.
4
Collapse the Gap, Slowly
Every call, move one inch closer to your natural self
Don't try to rip the mask off entirely. That creates different problems. Instead, on each call, pick one thing to do more naturally. Let one joke land instead of staying formal. Let one silence breathe instead of filling it. Let your actual opinion come through on one point. Over weeks, the gap closes.
The goal: the day where someone who knows you personally could listen to your sales call and think "yeah, that sounds exactly like them."

The Opener — Mask On vs. Mask Off

Version A — Mask on: professional, slightly "sales-ey," performing warmth
Rep
"Hey Marcus! So great to connect with you today. I've been really looking forward to this conversation. I just want to make sure we get the most out of your time today, so before we dive in — can you tell me a little bit about yourself and what you're hoping to get out of this call?"
Performative enthusiasm ("so great," "really looking forward"). "Get the most out of your time" sounds scripted. "Tell me about yourself" is a job interview opener. The mask is on — warm-sounding words with no real warmth behind them.
Prospect
"Yeah, so uh — I've been doing remote sales for about two years..."
Surface-level. Giving the rote answer because the question felt procedural. Mirroring the energy — professional, guarded, formal.
Version B — Mask off: same rep, actually present, being themselves
Rep
"Marcus, hey — glad you're here. I looked at your background a bit before this, and I'm genuinely curious what made you reach out. What's going on?"
"Genuinely curious" is backed by actual prep — they looked at his background. "What's going on?" is how a person talks, not how a sales call starts. No performance. The rep is actually interested. The prospect can feel that.
Prospect
"Honestly, I've been feeling kind of stuck. I'm making okay money but it's not consistent — some months are great, some are bad — and I can't figure out what I'm doing differently month to month. It's been frustrating."
Open, specific, real. He's not giving the resume answer — he's telling the truth about where he is. This happened because the rep showed up as a person, not a sales process. That's the data you need to actually help him, and to close.

The mask made the first conversation safe. It didn't make it effective. The second one felt slightly riskier to the rep — less controlled, more improvised. That discomfort is what taking the mask off feels like. Lean into it. That's where the closes live.
