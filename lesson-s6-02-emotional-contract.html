The Emotional Contract — Diagnosing Before Price

Price objections almost never happen because of price. They happen because the prospect hasn't internally agreed that this is right for them yet. The emotional contract is what changes that.

What the Emotional Contract Is

The emotional contract is the internal agreement a prospect makes with themselves before you ever reveal the price. It's the moment they've decided — not consciously, not out loud — that this is the right thing for them.

When the emotional contract is in place, price becomes a logistics question, not a decision question. When it's not in place, price becomes the reason to say no — even when money isn't the real issue.

Why It Matters

Most reps reveal price too early. They present the offer, state the investment, and wait. If the emotional contract isn't in place, the prospect's brain hasn't yet done the work of deciding this is right. So it does the only logical thing it can: evaluates whether the price feels worth it.

Without the emotional contract, the prospect is not ready to hear a number. They're still in evaluation mode. The number hits before the internal yes has formed — and it almost always lands as too much.

Signs the Emotional Contract Is (and Isn't) in Place

Contract NOT in Place Contract IS in Place
Answers are short. One or two words. Feels like pulling teeth to get information. Prospect is volunteering information. Telling you things you didn't ask about.
Prospect is asking analytical questions: "How many calls per week? What's included exactly?" Prospect is asking implementation questions: "How quickly could I start? What does onboarding look like?"
Energy on the call feels flat or guarded. Short pauses after your questions. Energy is forward-leaning. Prospect is mapping this to their life. They're seeing themselves in it.
Prospect hasn't said anything that tells you they personally connect with the problem being solved. Prospect has made at least one statement that sounds like "this is exactly what I've been dealing with."
Prospect hasn't confirmed the future state is something they actually want. Prospect has articulated what their life looks like if this works. They can see the future.

The Fit Check — Testing the Contract Before Price

The Principle

Before you reveal price, you need to know if the emotional contract is in place. The fit check is how you test it. It's not a close. It's not a pressure move. It's a genuine checkpoint — and it protects both you and the prospect from a premature price conversation.

The fit check question: "Before I get into the investment side of things — based on what you've shared with me and what I've walked you through, does this feel like it's in the right direction for where you're trying to go?"

Reading the Response

A strong "yes" — even a casual one — is the emotional contract forming. The prospect is internally agreeing. Now you can reveal price and it lands on a foundation of yes.

A hesitant response, a "I think so," or a deflection means the contract isn't there yet. Do not proceed to price. Go back. Find what's unresolved. Ask: "What would need to be true for this to feel like a clear fit for you?"

The mistake: Skipping the fit check and going straight to price because the call feels good. A call can feel good and the emotional contract can still be absent. Calls feel good when the prospect is being polite. Polite is not the same as decided.

What Happens When You Reveal Price Without the Contract

When you reveal price before the emotional contract exists, you force the prospect's brain to do a cold calculation. Is this number worth it? Without internal alignment on the fact that this is right for them, the brain defaults to no — or to a stall that will eventually become a no.

The prospect isn't being difficult. They're doing exactly what a rational person does when asked to spend money on something they haven't fully decided on yet. You put them in that position. The price objection that follows is not really about price.

1
Without the Contract
Price as a Decision Point
Prospect hears $7,500 and the number becomes the thing they're deciding on — not whether this is right for their life. The conversation shifts to price negotiation, payment plan requests, and stalls. You're now defending the investment instead of the fit.
2
With the Contract
Price as a Logistics Question
Prospect has already internally decided this is right. The $7,500 becomes: "How do we make this work?" Not "Is this worth it?" The question has already been answered. Price is just the mechanism for getting started.

Call Exchange — Fit Check in Action

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Reveals Price Before the Contract Exists
Rep
"Okay so that's basically the overview of the program. The investment is $7,500, we also have a split pay option of two payments of $3,750. What do you think?"
No fit check. Jumped straight to price. The prospect hasn't confirmed internal alignment. The number lands cold.
Prospect
"That's... a lot more than I was expecting. I'm going to need to think about whether this is worth it for me."
Rep
"I get that — let me show you the ROI breakdown. If you close just two deals in the first month..."
Now defending price instead of addressing the emotional gap. The prospect isn't doing a math problem. They haven't decided this is right. No ROI calculation fixes that.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Tests the Contract First
Rep
"Before I get into the investment side of things — based on what you shared with me about where you're stuck, and what I've shown you about how this works, does this feel like it's pointing in the right direction for you?"
Fit check. Creates space for the prospect to confirm or reveal where they're still uncertain.
Prospect
"Yeah, I mean — honestly this is exactly the kind of thing I've been looking for. The psychology angle is what gets me. I've done the script stuff and it hasn't clicked."
Rep
"Good. That's the whole foundation of what we do — and the fact that you recognized that tells me you're the kind of person who'd actually get a lot from this. The investment is $7,500."
Emotional contract confirmed. Price lands on a foundation of yes. The conversation is now about how, not whether.
Prospect
"Okay. So do you have payment options?"
Implementation question. Not "is this worth it." The emotional contract was in place. Price became logistics.
