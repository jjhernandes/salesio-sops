Lesson 14 · Section 1

True Charisma

Why the least interesting person in the room closes the most. Charisma isn't a performance. It isn't energy or humor or great stories. It's something else entirely — and the harder you try for it, the further away it gets.

The Inversion

What Everyone Gets Wrong

Ask any sales rep what a charismatic person is like and they'll describe someone who is interesting — captivating stories, magnetic energy, quick wit, great with names, knows how to work a room. And that description is accurate for a certain kind of person. The problem is it describes a performance, not a quality.

The charismatic rep everyone remembers isn't the one who told the best story on the call. It's the one who made you feel like your story was the most interesting one they'd ever heard. Those are completely different things — and they produce completely different results in sales.

Real charisma is other-directed attention. It is the experience of being with someone who makes you feel fully seen, fully heard, and genuinely interesting. You don't walk away thinking "wow, that person was amazing." You walk away thinking "wow, that was a great conversation" — which means you walk away feeling good about yourself.

In sales, this is the difference between a prospect who says "that rep was impressive" (interesting rep) and a prospect who says "I just feel like they really got where I'm coming from" (charismatic rep). One of those closes. The other gets remembered and not bought.

"Connection is what people actually buy."

— JJ Hernandez

The Paradox of Performance

The harder you try to be charismatic, the less charismatic you are. This is not motivational talk — it's a mechanical reality. When you're performing, your attention is on yourself: how am I coming across, is this landing, do they like me, am I being interesting enough? That self-directed attention is what makes you boring. Because the person across from you can feel that your attention is on you, not them.

The rep who spent an hour before the call practicing their opening story, rehearsing their best lines, planning how to seem relaxed — that rep walks in performing. And performance reads as performance. Prospects can't always name it, but they feel it. Something is slightly off. Slightly calculated. Slightly fake. And trust doesn't build on top of fake.

The Effort Trap

Most reps who struggle with rapport have been told to "be more engaging" or "work on their personality." So they study charismatic people, they try to replicate the behaviors they see, and they put more effort into the performance. The effort goes up. The result gets worse. And they have no idea why.

The answer is counterintuitive: less effort in the direction of being interesting, more attention in the direction of being interested. The shift is from broadcasting to receiving. From talking to listening. From performing to being present.

The tell: If after a call you can recall in detail the clever things you said but you can't recall the details of the prospect's situation — you were performing, not connecting. Charisma leaves a specific footprint: the other person feels heard.

The Root of Charisma

Internal Security

True charisma is a byproduct of internal security. That's it. When you're not needing something from the other person — their approval, their validation, their yes — you become genuinely free to be curious about them. And genuine curiosity is the most magnetic quality a human being can have in conversation.

Internal security means: I'm okay regardless of how this call goes. Not that I don't care — I care a lot. But I'm not dependent on a specific outcome to feel like I'm enough. That security frees your attention from yourself and puts it fully on the person in front of you.

Neediness Is the Enemy

Any time you need something from a prospect — their agreement, their approval, their purchase — your charisma drops. This is why people who are typically charismatic in social settings sometimes become robotic on sales calls: the stakes introduce need, and the need kills the quality that makes them magnetic.

The most charismatic person in the room is almost always someone who genuinely doesn't need to be there. They're present by choice, interested out of real curiosity, and completely comfortable with however the interaction goes. That's the state you're trying to build.

Genuine Curiosity Is the Mechanism

Curiosity and charisma are nearly synonymous. When you are genuinely curious about someone — not performing curiosity, not asking the "right questions" from a framework, but actually wondering about this specific person's situation, their reasoning, their life — they feel it. People can always tell the difference between someone who is asking to check a box and someone who actually wants to know.

The rep who asks "what made you interested in getting into sales?" and then genuinely listens to the answer — follows up on the details, tracks the emotional thread, asks the obvious follow-up that shows they were paying attention — is more charismatic in that moment than any opener, story, or energy hack could make them.

Performing Charisma vs. Being Charismatic

What You're Doing Performing Charisma Being Charismatic
Opening the call Starting with a prepared hook, a name-drop, or a high-energy opener you practiced Genuinely asking how they're doing and actually listening to the answer before saying anything else
Building rapport Working through a mental checklist of "rapport-building" topics — sports, weather, family Following the thread of something the prospect already said, asking a specific follow-up, letting the conversation go wherever the prospect's energy takes it
Handling a long story Waiting for them to finish so you can get back to your agenda; nodding and saying "totally" on autopilot Tracking the emotional content of the story. Asking about the detail that reveals what they actually care about most.
Disagreeing with the prospect Either validating everything they say to maintain rapport, or over-explaining your position to seem authoritative Saying "I actually see that differently" and then genuinely explaining why, without needing them to agree with you

After the call Prospect remembers that the rep seemed confident and professional Prospect tells their spouse "I talked to this person today — I don't know, they just really got it." They can't explain why. That's charisma.

How Charisma Kills Resistance

The Guard Only Goes Up When Someone Needs Something From You

Prospects come into calls with their guard up because they've been sold to before. They've had reps who were "nice" for the first ten minutes and then pivoted into a pitch. They know the structure. They're waiting for the moment the real agenda reveals itself.

The charismatic rep dismantles this expectation not by being slicker but by being genuinely present. When a person feels like you actually want to understand them — and you have no agenda beyond understanding — the guard comes down on its own. You don't have to dismantle it. It dissolves.

Resistance is a response to pressure. You can't create resistance in someone who doesn't feel any pressure from you. When your full attention is on the prospect and you're genuinely unconcerned about the outcome, there is no pressure to resist. The objection "I feel like you're just trying to sell me something" literally cannot arise in that environment.

The Trust Shortcut

Trust normally takes time. But feeling understood is a massive shortcut to trust. A prospect who feels genuinely understood by a rep they just met trusts that rep more than they trust some advisors they've known for years. That's the power of charisma in sales: it doesn't just make calls more pleasant — it accelerates the single most important variable in the close.

Call Exchange — Performing vs. Embodying

REP A — Performing Charisma
Rep A
"Hey Marcus! Great to meet you man — I saw you're out in Austin, I actually know that area, awesome city. How's it treating you?"
Opener is prepared. Name-drop of city. Designed to seem familiar and warm. Has done this 50 times today.
Prospect
"Haha yeah it's good, pretty hot."
Rep A
"Ha! Always. Well hey — tell me a little bit about yourself and what you're trying to accomplish."
Standard pivot. Didn't respond to what Marcus actually said. Moving through the structure. Marcus can feel the agenda underneath the friendliness.
Prospect
"Yeah, so I've been in sales for about two years, I'm doing okay but I feel like I'm kind of plateauing..."
Rep A
"Yeah totally, plateauing is super common. So let me ask you — what does your current income look like and where are you trying to get to?"
Bulldozed past "plateauing." Went straight to qualification metrics. The word that revealed everything about Marcus's situation was left sitting there. Marcus notices, even if he can't name it.
REP B — Embodying Charisma
Rep B
"Hey Marcus, good to meet you. How are you doing?"
Simple. No performance. Actually waiting for an answer.
Prospect
"Pretty good, pretty good. A little tired — it's been a long week."
Rep B
"What happened this week?"
Did not move past it. Asked the obvious human follow-up. Marcus is now about to tell Rep B something real about his life.
Prospect
"Honestly, just had a tough stretch of calls. Losing deals I thought I had. It's frustrating."
Rep B
"I know that feeling. What kind of deals — same stage they're falling off, or is it different each time?"
Genuine empathy. No pivot. Asked a specific, smart follow-up that shows Rep B is actually thinking about Marcus's situation, not running a sequence. The conversation is now real. The guard is already coming down and nothing has been sold yet.

Building It — Practice, Not Technique

This is not a technique you deploy on calls. You cannot decide to "be more interested" in someone. You cannot force genuine curiosity. What you can do is build the internal conditions that make genuine curiosity natural — and those conditions are built off the call, not on it.

01
Practice
Get Your Attention Off Yourself in Daily Life
Charisma on a call is a reflection of where your attention naturally lives. If your attention is chronically on yourself off the call — how you're perceived, how you're doing, what people think of you — it will default there on calls too. Practice redirecting attention in ordinary conversations. Next time you're talking to anyone, make it a game to learn three specific things about them you didn't know before. No agenda. Just practice the direction.
02
Practice
Stop Completing People's Sentences (In Your Head)
Most reps have already formed their response by the time the prospect is halfway through talking. That's the opposite of listening. The practice: when someone is talking, actively suppress the urge to build your response. Stay with what they're saying until they finish. Then pause. Then respond. This sounds simple and it is excruciating for people who are used to thinking ahead. That discomfort is the practice.
On your next five calls: say nothing until you've given at least a two-second pause after the prospect stops talking. See what you notice about what they actually said versus what you expected them to say.
03
Practice
Kill the Outcome Attachment Before the Call
The single thing that most reliably destroys presence on a call is needing a specific outcome. Pre-call, settle this: your job is to understand this person completely. If there's a fit, it will be obvious. You don't need to engineer the close — you need to engineer the understanding. This mental shift frees your attention from monitoring the deal and puts it back on the person.
04
Practice
Follow the Thread That Reveals Real Emotion
Every call has a surface conversation and a real conversation. The surface conversation is what's being said. The real conversation is what those words point to emotionally. Practice identifying the single word or phrase in a prospect's response that carries the most emotional charge — and following that instead of your script.
"I've just been in sales for three years and honestly I don't know if I'm cut out for this." The emotional thread is in the last nine words. The charismatic rep asks about that. The performing rep moves on to qualification.
05
Advanced Practice
Let Yourself Be Surprised
Charismatic people are genuinely surprisable. They don't already know everything. When something unexpected happens in a conversation, they respond to it instead of routing around it. The practice: notice moments on your calls when something catches you off guard. Instead of recovering quickly to stay on plan, follow the surprise. "Wait — that's interesting, say more about that." Those detours are where real connection happens.

The Charisma Diagnostic

After any call, run through these. Honest answers only. If most of these are no, you were performing. If most are yes, you were present.

  • ✓
    Can you recall at least three specific things the prospect said that surprised you or that you didn't expect?
  • ✓
    Did you ask at least one question that wasn't in your script or framework — a question that arose purely because of something they said?
  • ✓
    If you were describing the prospect's situation to someone else, could you convey how they felt about it — not just what the situation was?
  • ✓
    At any point on the call, were you genuinely curious about the prospect's answer before they gave it?
  • ✗
    Did you find yourself waiting for the prospect to finish talking so you could make your point?
  • ✗
    Did you think about how the call was going for you (your performance) while the prospect was talking?
  • ✗
    Did the call follow roughly the same structure as your last ten calls, regardless of what the prospect said?
  • ✓
    If the prospect mentioned something personal or unexpected early in the call, did you follow it — even briefly — before continuing?
  • ✓
    Did you feel relatively unconcerned about the outcome during the call — not apathetic, just not dependent on it?

The goal isn't to check every box. The goal is to use the pattern over time. If you're consistently hitting the fail checks and missing the pass checks, the problem isn't technique. It's where your attention lives on a call. And that's fixable — but it's fixed off the call, not on it.
