Key Coaching from This Call

Core Concept

Hard vs. Unfamiliar

Pablo admitted his brain resists the mindset and identity work — it can feel abstract, "woo-woo," not real. JJ reframed this completely: the brain doesn't label things as hard. It labels them as familiar or unfamiliar. When something feels resistant, it's not because it's difficult — it's because it's new territory for your nervous system.

"Your brain doesn't know the difference between hard and unfamiliar. It only knows familiar and unfamiliar. The discomfort you feel is just newness — not difficulty."

This reframe matters on calls too. When something in the process feels uncomfortable, the rep isn't "bad at it" — they just haven't done it enough for it to feel natural yet.

Core Concept

Behavioral Magnetics — You Repeat What's Familiar

Your behavior is magnetic to what you've already experienced. If you grew up in chaos, chaos feels like home. If you grew up in low emotional expression, high-emotion selling feels uncomfortable. You gravitate back to what's familiar even when you logically know better.

This is why reps plateau. It's not lack of knowledge or effort — it's that the new behavior hasn't become familiar yet. The work is repetition until the unfamiliar becomes the baseline.

Key Pattern

What Pulls Pablo Down

JJ mapped Pablo on the consciousness scale — he operates in the green zone: acceptance, willingness, neutrality, reasoning. That's a solid baseline. But there's a specific trigger that drops him fast: when a prospect resists after already agreeing.

The internal reaction: "This guy doesn't deserve success." Pride and anger show up. And the moment that happens, the sale is over — not because of what Pablo says, but because of what he projects. The mirror flips.

"The moment you think 'this person doesn't deserve it,' you've already lost the sale. Not because of your words — because of your state."

The Fix

Reframe the Resistance, Not the Person

When a prospect resists or pushes back after agreeing, the internal question has to shift. Not "what's wrong with this person?" — that puts you in judgment. Instead: "What's the reason they're resisting right now?"

That one question keeps you curious instead of reactive. Curiosity is a neutral state. It keeps you in the green zone. And it lets you actually hear what they're saying instead of defending against it.

"There's always a reason. If you can stay curious about the reason instead of judging the person, you stay in control of yourself — which means you stay in control of the call."

Core Concept

Sales Is a Mirror

The rep is always a mirror to the prospect. Whatever state you're in — that's what they feel. You can't fake certainty. You can't project calm if you're internally judging them. You can't create trust if you're operating from pride or fear.

The 98% close rate JJ cited from the last 3 months came directly from this. Not better scripts. Not more frameworks. Understanding that you have to BE in the state you want them to feel — and then projecting it.

"You can't sell certainty from a place of doubt. You can't sell calm from a place of frustration. You have to be in the state first. Then they catch it."

Case Study

Yahya Sidebar — Letting the Frame Go

During the call, Yahya jumped in with a situation: a prospect named Sarah who kept trying to control the conversation direction. Yahya let the prospect take the frame, got defensive when pushed, and ended up apologizing instead of leading.

JJ's take: once the rep lets the prospect control the direction, they've already lost the outcome. You can be flexible in tone and warm in delivery — but the frame of where the conversation is going is the rep's job to hold. The second you get defensive, you've given it up.

"You can be warm. You can be empathetic. But you can't be defensive. The moment you're defending yourself, you've handed them the wheel."

Technique

Label in the Positive, Never the Negative

You can name what you observe in a prospect — but only upward. Labeling them in the negative ("you're just scared," "you're being irrational") creates resistance and shuts them down. Labeling in the positive ("I think you actually want this more than you're letting on") pulls them forward.

The rule: if your label would make someone feel called out in a bad way, don't use it. If it would make them feel understood and seen, use it.

  • ✗  "You're just scared of committing."
  • ✓  "Why do you think that would stop you?" — lets them arrive at the fear themselves.

Key Principles

Hard isn't real. Unfamiliar is. The discomfort you feel in new territory is your nervous system registering novelty — not evidence that you can't do it. Repetition turns unfamiliar into familiar.

You repeat what's familiar. Your behavior gravitates back to your baseline. The work is raising the baseline — not forcing yourself to act differently, but making the better behavior the familiar one.

Sales is a mirror. You can't project a state you're not in. If you want them to feel certainty, you have to feel it. If you want them to feel calm, you can't be frustrated. The mirror is always on.

Curiosity beats judgment. When a prospect resists, the internal question determines your state. "What's wrong with them?" drops you. "What's the reason they're resisting?" keeps you curious, neutral, and in control.

Label up, never down. You can name what you observe — but only in ways that make them feel seen, not called out. Positive labeling opens people. Negative labeling shuts them down and puts you on defense.
