Root Cause — What's Really Going On

The life script problem. The behavior you allow in the first 30-60 seconds will repeat for the entire call. Hannah let Sam set the frame early — Q&A mode, him asking questions, her answering. Once that pattern was established, she was playing defense the rest of the way.

Cognitive Dissonance in Action

Hannah was asking surface-level, "safe" questions — not because she didn't know better, but because she was protecting her identity. Asking a deep question she doesn't know the answer to risks looking incompetent in front of the founder. So the brain asks easy questions it knows won't backfire.

This is neuroscience. Your brain hates humiliation. It will burn the call to avoid that feeling. The fix isn't more knowledge — it's permission to not know.

"The behavior she allowed in the beginning was more or less like Q&A — he asked a question, she responded. So it feels like this random spray from the hip, hope and pray we're gonna get somewhere kind of approach. Because that's what the conversation started as."

— JJ
The Permission Gap

She also wanted to seem competent in front of Matt on the call. That pressure made her avoid saying "I don't know." Give her explicit permission: if she doesn't know the answer, she can say "I'm not sure, I'll go back to the team" — or simply pull Matt in on the spot. That's not weakness. That's professionalism.

"The first 30 seconds to a minute is the most important thing in the world. If respect and vulnerability is never gained — it doesn't matter. You're both just waiting for the call to finish."

— JJ

The Correct Call Progression

The goal of call 1: Qualify, create curiosity, book call 2. Pricing never comes up. The analysis and recommendation come on call 2 — that's where you close.

  1. 1
    Frame set + 2-line elevator pitch
    Own the call in the first 90 seconds. Set the agenda. Give them a 10-20 second high-level on what SWAM does so they have context for every question that follows. This avoids the "what do you guys do?" breakdown mid-call.
  2. 2
    Discovery — current state
    What are you doing now for digital visibility? SEO, paid, digital PR? What's working, what isn't? Who's your SEO firm, what are they doing — link building, on-page? How much of your traffic comes from organic vs. paid vs. direct?
  3. 3
    AI visibility gap (pre-pulled research)
    Before the call, pull up their site in ChatGPT and Google search using their key keywords. Have the tab open. Screen share and show them: "Here's what we're seeing — your competitors show up, you don't." Let the visual do the work. Then: are these your competitors, or is there anyone else we should be looking at?
  4. 4
    Bridge to call 2
    We're going to do a full analysis — keyword landscape, where you're showing up vs. where you should be, and a sample publication list. We provide the first placement free if you move forward. Let's get that in front of you — does [date] or [date] work better?
Pre-Call Prep (Non-Negotiable)

Before every call: run their primary keywords through ChatGPT and Google. Have tabs open. Know their competitors. Pull a quick Ahrefs snapshot if possible. The demo should happen live but be pre-staged — not searched on the spot.

Key Rule

The simpler the pitch, the more curiosity it creates. If they don't fully understand it — good. "How would that work for me?" is the response you want. That's your segue into discovery.

Specific Moments to Fix

The Guarantee Question

When Sam asked if placements are guaranteed, Hannah said: "We essentially guarantee... we kind of collect a handful..." — wishy-washy language that kills certainty instantly.

What to say instead: "Yes — we guarantee the total number of placements in your campaign. We have a 95% success rate getting you placed on the publications you pre-approve. If something like Forbes isn't ready that month, we shift it and publish you on another pre-approved site."

The AI Rankings Lie

Sam claimed: "One morning we were ranked in the top 5 on ChatGPT. That afternoon we didn't show up at all." This is factually wrong — AI models don't update daily. They retrain every 6 months or so. Hannah didn't push back.

What to do: Challenge it directly but curiously. "That's actually really interesting — can you walk me through what you searched?" Then: "What you're describing sounds more like how Google search works. AI results are actually locked into a training cycle, so they're much more stable — which is why the content we get published now can hold a position for months." That shift in belief is where the value lands.

Pricing on Call 1

Sam asked for costs. Hannah pulled up the slide deck. Mistake — not because pricing is wrong to discuss eventually, but because Sam literally said "I didn't have a budget, I didn't know you were going to email me." There is zero value built. Any number she gives is just a number he'll reject.

What to say instead: "Yeah, totally fair question. What I'd rather do is use this call to understand your situation, and then on the next call we'll put together an actual roadmap with a recommendation that makes sense for your business. Pricing is a range and it depends entirely on what's right for you — we'll cover all of that then."

ROI Conversation — Half-Done

Hannah asked about LTV ($300-400) but stopped there. If you're going down the ROI road, you need all the data: current organic traffic, conversion rate, average order value, and LTV. Then you can build a real picture: "If we brought you 50K new visitors and you convert at 3.5%, that's 1,750 new customers at $100 AOV — $175K. Does a $10-15K investment make sense for that?" Go all the way or don't go at all.
