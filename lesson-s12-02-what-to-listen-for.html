What to Listen For in Your Own Calls

Most reps listen to their calls and hear noise. You need to know exactly what to listen for. Five specific things — and the one question that ties them all together.

The 5 Things to Listen For

These are not vague. They're specific, audible, and trainable. You'll hear all five on every call. Your job is to find the moment each one shifted.

01
Listen For
Tonality Shifts
Your tonality is a real-time readout of your internal state. When you're calm and curious, your tone is even, measured, unhurried. When you get nervous or feel the deal slipping, your tone changes — it tightens, goes up in pitch, or becomes artificially flat. Listen for the exact moment your tone shifts. That moment almost always corresponds to something the prospect said that triggered discomfort.
What to listen for: voice tightening after a price reveal. Pitch rising when challenged. Tone going artificially cheerful when you're actually uncertain. The "rep" voice replacing the natural voice.
02
Listen For
Pace Changes
Pace is the clearest external signal of internal state. A calm rep is slow. A nervous rep speeds up. On tape you'll hear exactly when your words per minute jumped. Map it to the call: what happened right before? An objection? A silence? The mention of the price? That trigger is your work.
What to listen for: sentences compressing, breathing disappearing between thoughts, filling natural pauses with fillers ("so," "like," "you know"), the prospect having to slow you down.
03
Listen For
The Moment You Went Tactical
There is a distinct difference between a natural conversation and a rep executing a script. On tape you'll hear it clearly. The natural conversation has pauses, genuine responses, follow-up questions that come from real curiosity. The tactical mode sounds different — formulaic, slightly rehearsed, slightly disconnected from what the prospect just said.
What to listen for: a question that doesn't connect to what was just said. A transition that sounds canned. The moment you stopped responding to the prospect and started executing a process.
04
Listen For
Where the Energy Shifted
Every call has a moment where the energy changes — where the prospect's engagement shifts. Sometimes it goes up, the prospect gets more animated and invested. Sometimes it drops. Your job on tape is to find both moments: when it went up and what caused it, and when it dropped and what caused it. The dropped moment is almost always the real problem — and it's rarely where you think it is.
What to listen for: changes in the prospect's question volume, shorter answers, longer answers, a shift from exploring to deflecting, an energy increase when you hit a nerve, a drop when you pivoted away too fast.
05
Listen For
The First Sign of Rapport Breaking
Rapport doesn't disappear all at once. It erodes. There is a first sign — a small signal before the bigger ones — and if you can identify it, you can trace it back to what caused it. On tape you'll hear it: a slight formality creeping in, shorter answers, a prospect who was leaning in who started pulling back. Finding the first sign is the highest-value thing you can do on a tape review.
What to listen for: the prospect's language becoming more formal. One-word answers replacing full sentences. A laugh that doesn't quite land. The moment the conversation stopped feeling like a conversation and started feeling like a sales call.

Why You Can't Hear This During the Call

You are inside the performance. You cannot simultaneously be in the conversation and observe the conversation. Every cognitive resource you have is allocated to what's happening right now — processing the prospect's words, managing your own state, tracking the structure of the call, deciding what to say next.

There is no bandwidth left for objective observation. The call is not the time to self-evaluate. That's what the tape is for.

The Attention Problem

Even experienced reps can't accurately evaluate their own performance in real time. Studies on expert performance consistently show that what people think happened during a high-stakes interaction is systematically different from what actually happened. Memory is reconstructive. You fill gaps with what you expected to happen, not what occurred.

The tape is the truth. Your memory of the call is an interpretation. Use the tape.

How to Listen Objectively

The Mental Shift

When you press play, you are no longer JJ. You are the coach. The rep on that recording is a student. You are watching their technique, identifying where they broke down, looking for the specific moment to fix. This is not your call anymore. It's a training film.

This single reframe removes most of the emotional charge from tape review. You're not watching yourself fail. You're watching a student who needs coaching.

Approach Emotional Listening Objective Listening
Internal frame "That was so bad. I can't believe I said that." "Interesting — the energy dropped right there. What triggered it?"
When you cringe Skip forward. Avoid the moment. Don't go back. Rewind. Listen twice. Find exactly what happened and why.
After the review Feel bad about the call. Tell yourself to "do better." Write down one specific thing to change. Apply it next call.
What you do with good moments Ignore them. Focus on what went wrong. Note them too. Understanding what worked is as important as what didn't.
  • ✓
    Use headphones. You pick up far more from audio when there are no distractions. Headphones pull you in. Laptop speakers give you the surface.
  • ✓
    Take notes with timestamps. When you hear something, write the timestamp and what you noticed. This creates a record you can go back to and a pattern you can track across calls.
  • ✓
    Don't rewrite the call in your head. Don't narrate what you "should have said" while you listen. Stay with what happened. The should-have happens after the review, not during it.
  • ✗
    Don't review while distracted. Half-listening while doing something else produces no useful insight. Full attention or don't do it.

The One Question — After Every Review

At the end of every tape review, ask one question and answer it honestly:

"At what moment did I stop being present?"

Presence is the master variable. Every failure on a call — every tonality shift, every tactical pivot, every rapport break — traces back to a moment where you left the conversation and went into your head. The moment you stopped being present is the moment things started going wrong. Find it. That's your work.

Why This Question Works

It bypasses the symptom and goes to the cause. You could spend an entire review session noting individual mistakes — the rushed pace, the defensive objection handling, the weak close. All of those are symptoms. The cause is always the same: you lost presence. Something pulled you out of the conversation and into your head. Find that moment and you've found everything.

"Presence is not a soft skill. It's the most technically demanding thing a closer does. The moment you leave presence, your close rate drops. It's that simple."

— JJ Hernandez
