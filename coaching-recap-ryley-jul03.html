Core Teaching

The Framing Problem

What Ryley Said in His First Loom

"Hi, my name is Riley Richards. I currently have no sales experience. I started working with JJ Hernandez at Sales.io to help me learn and grow, and hopefully land a role in this high-ticket sales space. I currently work at Costco."

Why This is the Wrong Frame

Ryley has 4 years of in-person sales experience — credit cards and tires at Costco. That is real persuasion, real objection handling, real face-to-face influence. He framed it as nothing.

The rule: reality doesn't matter, perception of reality does. You never lie — but how you describe your truth is entirely within your control. Every rep who says "I have no experience" is usually sitting on years of transferable skills they're choosing not to see.

Ryley's Actual Experience

  • 4 years in person — selling credit cards and tires at Costco
  • Credit card sales — cold approach, convincing people to hit their credit on the spot. That is pure persuasion.
  • Tire sales — $1,200/set. Automotive upsells. Customer already came in for something else — you closed them on more.
  • No commission, but the skill is identical. Commission is a comp structure, not a measure of whether you were selling.

The Principle JJ Taught

"I will never tell you to lie. Integrity and honesty are non-negotiable. But when you can frame things in a certain way — that's all that matters. Humans make decisions based on emotions and compensate logically. If you believe your experience doesn't count, that belief is the problem — not the experience."

The Pattern JJ Sees All the Time

  • Guys with 5 years selling phones saying "I have no high-ticket experience"
  • A guy from Walmart who framed e-commerce dropshipping as "$90K in online sales" — and it worked
  • Anand, a financial advisor with no traditional high-ticket sales background — his first loom was almost identical to Ryley's. JJ fixed it the same way.
  • Sabian came from being a mechanic. Now closing deals.

The rep who wins the interview is not the one with the best resume. It's the one who frames their experience with the most conviction.

Loom Reframe

Original — Don't Send This

"Hi, my name is Riley Richards. I currently have no sales experience. I started working with JJ Hernandez at Sales.io to help me learn and grow, and hopefully land a role in this high-ticket sales space. I currently work at Costco."

Reframe — Record This Version

"Hey, what's going on everyone — Riley Richards here. I've been in the sales space, mostly in person, for the last 4 years — selling in both automotive and finance. I've been working closely with JJ at Sales.io and I'm looking to transition into a high-ticket role where I can have more versatility, work remotely, and have more alignment with the company I'm part of. My biggest reason for making this move is to make more impact and help more people — which is what I truly believe sales is. Hope that gives you a picture of who I am, and looking forward to talking soon."

Key Framing Choices

"In the sales space... for the last 4 years" — true, accurate, and lands completely differently than "I work at Costco."

"Automotive and finance" — also true. Tires = automotive. Credit cards = finance. Both are real words that describe what he actually did.

"Working closely with JJ at Sales.io" — positions him as being developed, not just hoping to be hired.

"Make more impact" — ends on mission, not desperation. "Hopefully land a role" sounds like begging. "Make more impact" sounds like a decision.

Skills Work

Mock Call Review

Where Ryley Is Right Now

Has been studying Jalen's call framework. Has been practicing with Eli. Feels decent on the flow — not bad, but not confident yet. JJ walked through Eric's intro call SOP in real time to show how it sounds in practice (not just on paper).

1

Frame the Call

"Hey [Name], it's Riley here from [Company]. Did I catch you at a bad time? I've got about 15 minutes before my next meeting — goal of this call is simple: understand where you're at, what you're looking for help with, and from there see if I can help or point you in a better direction. Sound good?"

2

Opening Question

"I've got a few details from our DM conversation. In your opinion, what's the biggest reason you're looking into marketing and growing your brand on Instagram right now?"

3

Find the Problem & Summarize

Listen for the pain point hiding inside their answer. Summarize it back with precision before going deeper.

"So it sounds like you're wanting to scale, but the problem is you're not able to do that through Google Ads alone. Is that right?"

4

Data Gathering

Get specific numbers — lead volume, appointments booked, close rate, price point, upsells, last month's revenue, profit margin, hours worked, team size. Do not rush this. Numbers make the emotional pain real.

5

Get Emotional — But Stay Human

Don't ask scripted pain questions ("What does that cost you personally?"). Just be curious about their life outside the numbers.

"How's that impacting you outside the business?"  /  "Does it feel like this is capping what you can actually do?"

6

Transition & Pitch the Second Call

"So it sounds like the main issue is the income rollercoaster — you don't have control over lead flow. That's causing longer hours and uncertainty in your ability to scale. It comes down to one thing: you don't have a consistent marketing system. I've got another call in a few, but it sounds like something I can help with. Let me book you with Eric where we can go deeper and build a custom gameplan to get you to [their goal]. How does that sound?"

7

Homework Before Call 2

"Before we talk next time, I'm going to send you a short video — gives you context on how we work and saves us 10–15 minutes on the call. Takes about 5 minutes. Can you commit to watching it before we talk?"

JJ's Core Reminder on Data Questions

Humans make decisions based on emotions and compensate logically. The data gathering phase is not just qualification — it's building the emotional tension that makes the second call feel urgent. Don't rush through it. The deeper you go on data, the more they feel the gap.

Mindset

Money & Safety

JJ's Philosophy on Money

The most uncomfortable feeling in the world is having someone dig into your finances when you're not where you want to be. JJ said even at multiple six figures a month, looking at the bank account can still get uncomfortable sometimes — when expenses nearly match income, when you can see the overhead.

The fix: look at your bank account every single day. The discomfort decreases as you stop avoiding the number. It also protects you — JJ caught 7 fraudulent transactions the week of the webinar because he checks daily.

The One Thing That Matters at the Highest Level

"Nothing beats being able to know that if anything came down to it — if I needed to drop $10K, $20K, $30K tomorrow — I won't be like 'oh my god.' Safety. Peace of mind. Everything else is secondary. I promise you, everything else is secondary. People understand that, they just don't get that that's what matters at the highest level."

The Practical Version

  • JJ has a Tesla, not a Lambo. Almost bought an R8, didn't.
  • $50 watch. $2K/month office. Lives in a house — probably cheaper rent than Eli's apartment.
  • Company makes six figures a month. Expenses nearly match it some months.
  • The goal is not the display. The goal is the option — being able to move $30K tomorrow without blinking.
  • Prioritize building safety first. Status comes after, if at all.
