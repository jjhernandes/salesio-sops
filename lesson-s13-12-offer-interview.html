The Offer Interview

You're not applying for a job. You're evaluating a business partner. The rep who interviews the company gets the offer. The rep who begs for the offer doesn't.

The Frame That Changes Everything

Most reps go into an interview in supplicant mode: "Please hire me, I'll be great, I promise." This is the worst frame you can bring to a sales interview — because they can see it, and they know exactly what it means on a sales call.

The rep who performs in an interview interview is performing. Elite closers show their character by how they conduct themselves — not by trying to impress. The frame of mutual evaluation changes everything.

The reframe: You're both evaluating fit. They're evaluating whether you can close their offer. You're evaluating whether their offer is worth closing. Both sides have standards. Both sides get to say no. This frame, genuinely held, makes you magnetic.

The Interview in Practice

Confident mutual evaluation — what it sounds like
Hiring managerSo tell me about yourself and why you're interested in this role.
RepHappy to. I've been in high-ticket sales for about 18 months — most of my experience has been on coaching and education offers in the $5k-$15k range. I've consistently closed in the 20-25% range. Right now I'm looking for an offer where the product genuinely delivers, because my close rate is directly tied to the product's reputation. I'd love to hear — what does your current close rate look like with your top closers?
Led with real numbers, stated what they're looking for, immediately turned it into a mutual evaluation.
Hiring managerWe're around 18% average, top closer is at 28%.
RepThat's a healthy spread. What's the main difference between your top closer and the average — is it discovery, objection handling, or something else?
Asked a question that only someone who knows sales would ask. Continues the mutual evaluation — now they're being interviewed.

Questions to Ask in the Interview

These questions do two things simultaneously: they give you the information you need to evaluate the offer, and they demonstrate that you're a serious, informed rep who knows the right questions to ask.

1. What's your current close rate across the team? What's your top closer doing?
2. What's the average deal size? What's the split pay structure?
3. How is commission paid — weekly, monthly? What's the chargeback policy?
4. What does the lead pipeline look like — where does most of your traffic come from?
5. What happened with the last closer who left? Why did they leave?
6. What does a typical booked call look like — what kind of prospect?
7. What support does the closing team have — setter handoff notes, call recordings, coaching?

If they can't answer questions 1-3 with specific numbers, that's a red flag. Legitimate offers track their numbers. If a company can't tell you their close rate, they either don't know it (bad) or are hiding it (worse).
