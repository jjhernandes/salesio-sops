The Map of Consciousness

Introduced the consciousness scale from David Hawkins' Power vs. Force. The key principle: everything emits energy — positive or negative. The scale runs 20 to 1,000. Courage at 200 is the critical threshold — below it is destructive and draining, above it is life-supportive and generative.

True power strengthens. Force weakens. Above 200, people seek you out because you give energy. Below 200, people avoid you because you take it.

Malachi's Baseline

Self-identified baseline: between Desire (125) and Anger (150).

Highest he's experienced: Joy (540). He knows what that state feels like — it's accessible, not foreign.

Lowest: around Guilt — the "why the f*** did I do that" feeling. Not shame-level, but the self-directed frustration after a bad decision.

JJ's Take

Anger and desire create movement — that's not a problem. Most sales reps operate out of fear (100), which stops them. Desire and anger push forward. Michael Jordan, Kobe Bryant, Hormozi — all operate(d) from anger/desire. "I'll show you" is a powerful fuel.

The issue isn't the state itself. It's the mental bandwidth it consumes and the way it skews decisions — chasing what others are doing rather than what's best for you personally.

"I see other people performing and doing better than me and talking down to me, and I know that I have the capability of performing to where they're at, or maybe even exceeding that."
Malachi — describing what anger and desire look like for him

What Brings Him Up

Malachi's honest answer: money. Specifically, making enough to provide for the people he loves — his parents.

"When I made the most amount of money I have, I had all the joy because I was able to provide for the people I wanted to."

The Problem With This

Value attribution. When your emotional state is tied to the number in your bank account, you will inevitably fluctuate. You can't hold a high baseline when your baseline is conditional on an outcome you don't fully control.

This isn't about money being bad. It's about what money means to him. The actual driver is providing for his family and feeling capable — not the dollar amount itself. That's the thing to anchor to, not the number.

JJ's Reframe

Conviction in skill solves this. If you know your skill is so solid that you could lose everything today and rebuild to six figures by next month — the bank account stops being the variable. The skill is the constant. That conviction is what puts you above 200 regardless of circumstances.

"We can create a circumstance where you are in a vehicle to make more money. That is literally not an issue." The vehicle gets fixed. The skill is already there.

What Brings Him Down

  • Not being able to provide for his family. His parents are sick — dad has a broken shoulder needing surgery, mom is unwell. They're in Alaska. He's been bouncing between Florida and Alaska to help.
  • Environmental instability. Constant travel disrupted any sense of routine or stability. Now committing to staying in Florida for 4–5 months.
  • Comparison and competition. Measuring his progress against others rather than his own benchmark. Creates reactive decisions.
  • No neutral influence. Some people in his current environment are pulling him down, not up.
Key Insight

The environment has been the primary destabilizer — not mindset, not skill, not effort. Constant travel + sick parents + financial pressure + team environment that may not be lifting him = a cocktail that would strain anyone's baseline.

Stabilizing environment (staying in Florida) removes a major drag before anything else changes.

Patterns Identified

Pattern 1 — Anticipation Anxiety

A physiological response that precedes the cognitive one. The body feels the anxiety before the brain processes it. This creates avoidance — Malachi knows what he needs to do, can put it on paper, but still doesn't do it. It's not laziness. It's the body protecting itself from perceived threat by shutting down.

Triggers: money fluctuations, relationship uncertainty, environmental changes, upcoming decisions. Any change creates the response.

JJ's note: "It might not even be an actual problem. It could be the environment creating chaos around you, which is creating this distance in your brain between what needs to get done and what you feel."

Pattern 2 — Driving as Escape

Currently spending more time in the car than at his desk. Not procrastination — it's regulation. Driving shuts the mind off and provides relief from the emotional noise. The problem: it delays the things that would actually fix the underlying feeling.

JJ's read: "You're probably escaping the things you know you need to address." Named it not as a criticism but as recognition.

Pattern 3 — Value Attribution to Money

Emotional state tracks the bank account. When money is good, he feels capable. When it's not, he questions himself. This creates a loop where slow periods hit harder than they should, making it even harder to perform when it matters most.

The fix isn't to care less about money. It's to anchor identity to skill and capability rather than outcome. Skill doesn't fluctuate the way a commission check does.

"You can't expect to operate at a specific baseline and operate at a higher level of consciousness if your baseline is tied to the amount of money in your bank account. It'd be impossible."
JJ
