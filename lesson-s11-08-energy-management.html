Energy Management

Sales is a physical performance sport. The rep who manages their energy across a full call day closes more in the last call than the rep who burns bright in the morning and flatlines by 3pm.

This Is a Physical Performance Sport

The Framework

Top athletes don't just train their skills. They manage their energy across a performance — conserving, expending, and recovering deliberately so they can perform when it matters most. High-ticket sales is the same.

A full call day is a performance. You have a finite amount of high-quality presence to give. How you spend it in the hours before your calls, between your calls, and on calls that don't matter as much determines how much you have left for the calls that do.

Your fifth call of the day should hit just as hard as your first. That's the standard. If your fifth call is consistently worse than your first — if your energy is lower, your patience is shorter, and your presence is thinner — that's an energy management problem, not a skill problem.

The common mistake: Treating call days as desk days with calls scattered through them. Email between calls. Slack notifications. Difficult conversations slotted right before your most important call. Every one of these decisions costs you energy that should be available for your prospects. Stop spending energy carelessly on non-call days and then wondering why you're flat by call three.

The Energy Drain Points

These are the things that reliably cost you the most energy on call days. Identify which ones are in your schedule and start eliminating or moving them.

01
High Drain
Difficult prospects or no-shows
A prospect who is hostile, dismissive, or simply not qualified pulls energy from you even when you handle it well. A no-show is deceptively draining — the emotional preparation of getting ready for a call that never happens costs energy without producing value. Acknowledge this and give yourself a real recovery window after both.
After a difficult prospect or a no-show, do a 3-minute physical break before your next call. Walk around the block. Don't go straight into the next one.
02
High Drain
Email and Slack during call blocks
Every time you switch between call mode and communication mode, your brain context-switches. That switch costs energy. Checking Slack between calls to respond to a message and then trying to get back into a high-presence state for the next prospect is harder than it seems. The transition is expensive.
Batch communications. Do not touch Slack or email during call blocks. Set a 30-minute post-call-block window for all messaging and close it completely before your first call.
03
Medium Drain
Heavy meals before or during calls
Your body pulls blood flow toward digestion after a large meal. Energy drops. Clarity drops. The hour after a heavy lunch is one of the most reliably low-performance windows in the day — which is also when many reps schedule their afternoon calls. Plan around this.
Eat light during call days. Save the full meals for after your call block. A protein-forward snack between calls beats a full lunch that leaves you sluggish by call four.
04
Medium Drain
Emotional conversations right before calls
An argument, a difficult conversation with a team member, a stressful personal situation — these pull on the same emotional bandwidth that your calls need. If a difficult conversation happens right before a call block, acknowledge that you're carrying emotional weight and give yourself a real transition window.
If something emotionally heavy happens right before a call, do the full pre-call check. Don't skip it. The reset protocol exists for exactly this scenario.

Between-Call Recovery Protocols

The Goal

You have 10-15 minutes between most calls. The default use of that time — checking your phone, refreshing email, staring at the ceiling — does not restore anything. Deliberate recovery in those same 10 minutes can bring you back close to baseline before the next call.

  • ✓
    Move your body. Stand up, walk around, do 10 jumping jacks — anything that moves blood and breaks the sitting-at-a-desk posture. Two minutes of movement has a measurable effect on alertness and energy. This is not optional on call days.
  • ✓
    Get outside briefly. Natural light and fresh air are among the fastest state-changers available. Even 3 minutes outside between calls resets your nervous system in a way that no amount of water or coffee can. If you can get outside between every call, do it.
  • ✓
    Run the debrief, then release. Do your 5-minute post-call debrief immediately after the call. Capture the data. Then deliberately set that call down before picking up the next one. The failure to release the previous call is the primary mechanism for energy bleed between calls.
  • ✓
    Hydrate. Actually hydrate. Not coffee. Water. Dehydration is one of the most common causes of afternoon mental fatigue and most reps are running 3-5 calls deep on a liter of water and two espressos. Fix this. It is a performance variable.
  • ✓
    Run the pre-call check before every single call. Not just the first call of the day. Every call. This is how you catch energy drops before they run your next call instead of discovering the drop on the prospect's time.

Scheduling for Peak Performance

The Scheduling Principle

Protect the calls that matter most by not scheduling energy-depleting tasks before them. You know which calls on your calendar are the highest-stakes — the warm lead who's been following up for a week, the referral from a current student, the re-engaged prospect. Those calls deserve your best energy. Schedule around that.

Your Peak Window

Most people have a 3-4 hour window per day where they're at their highest cognitive and emotional capacity. Identify yours. Put your most important calls there. Don't put administrative work in that window and leave the important calls for when you're already depleted.

For most reps this is mid-morning — after the initial wake-up window but before the post-lunch energy dip. Know yours. Guard it.

"Who you are off your sales calls is who you are on your sales calls. Your energy doesn't start when the call starts. It starts the night before."

— JJ Hernandez

Sleep, exercise, nutrition, and recovery are not separate from sales performance. They are sales performance. The rep who sleeps 6 hours and closes 4 of 20 calls is leaving 2-4 additional closes per month on the table because their energy and presence are running below capacity. That is a real number. Treat your body like the performance tool it is.
