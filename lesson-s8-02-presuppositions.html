Linguistic Presuppositions

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. A presupposition assumes the outcome inside the grammar — and the prospect's brain accepts it before their conscious mind can evaluate it.

What a Presupposition Is

The Definition

A presupposition is something the sentence assumes to be true in order for the sentence to make sense. It's not stated as a claim — it's baked into the grammar. The listener processes it as background fact, not foreground assertion.

Example: "What made you decide to reach out today?" presupposes the prospect made a decision. The question only makes sense if they did. Contrast with: "Why did you reach out?" which is neutral on decision-making and invites them to rationalize instead.

The power isn't in what you assert. It's in what you assume inside the structure of the sentence. Assertions invite debate. Presuppositions slip past debate entirely because they're never put up for evaluation.

Presupposition Pattern Library

Pattern Type Neutral Version With Presupposition
Decision assumed "Why did you reach out?" "What made you decide to reach out today?"
Change assumed "Would you want things to be different?" "When things change, what shifts first for you?"
Progress assumed "Do you think you could grow from here?" "As you keep growing, what's the next benchmark?"
Investment assumed "Would you consider investing in training?" "What do you want to see most from the investment?"
Outcome assumed "Do you think this could work for you?" "Once this is working, what does your week look like?"
Enrollment assumed "If you joined, we'd start right away." "When you come on board, we move fast — same day."

How Presuppositions Shift the Frame

The Frame Effect

Every question you ask creates a frame — a set of assumptions the prospect operates from while answering. A presupposition-loaded question creates a frame where the assumed outcome is already real.

When a prospect answers "What do you want to see most from the investment?" they are already operating inside a reality where the investment is happening. They aren't deciding whether to invest. They're deciding what to expect from it. That's a fundamentally different mental position.

The Conversational Frame Is Cumulative

Each presupposition you land builds on the last. By the time you reach the close, the prospect has been answering questions inside a frame where forward movement is assumed. The close feels like a natural continuation, not a new ask.

"Frame control isn't about arguing for your position. It's about building a reality where your position is already the starting assumption."

— Sales.io

When They Backfire

Too early. Presuppositions only work when the prospect has mentally arrived at some level of openness. If you use them before you've established rapport or before you understand the prospect's world, they feel presumptuous, not natural. The prospect senses you're ahead of them and pulls back.

Watch for resistance signals. If the prospect corrects your presupposition ("Well, I haven't decided anything..."), don't double down. Acknowledge it, slow down, and go back to questions. The presupposition landed wrong. That's information.

The Readiness Test

Before using a presupposition, ask yourself: has this person already accepted the premise I'm about to assume? If yes, the presupposition accelerates. If no, it creates friction. The tool only works in fertile ground.

Call Exchange

Presuppositions Stacked Through Discovery
Rep
"What made you decide to book this call today — like, what was the thing that pushed you to finally do something about it?"
Presupposes: they decided, and there's been a build-up they've been avoiding. Invites the real story.
Prospect
"Honestly I've been stuck at the same income for eight months and I'm tired of it."
Rep
"Eight months is a long time to push without seeing the movement. As you think about what changes first once you break through that ceiling — what matters most to you?"
Presupposes they will break through. Invites them to picture the other side, not debate whether it's possible.
Prospect
"Just the financial stability. And honestly the confidence that I actually know what I'm doing."
Rep
"Good. When you have that — when the skill is there and the results are backing it up — what does that do for the rest of your life? Not just income."
Presupposes "when" not "if." They're already building the future state in their mind. The decision is being made inside the conversation, not after it.
