The Structure

Why a cadence matters

A team with no structure will create its own structure — and it won't be the one you want. The weekly cadence isn't about filling time. It's about setting a rhythm that holds people accountable without you having to chase them. Do this right and the team runs itself by week four.

This is what we ran at E-Monks. Adapt it to your team size and offer mix, but don't cut the structure down just because it feels like a lot. The structure is what creates the standard. The standard is what creates the results.

Mon: Goals  ·  Tue: Call Review or Role Play  ·  Wed: Midweek Stats + Cross-Reference  ·  Thu: Call Review or Role Play  ·  Fri: Culture Day

Monday — Goal Setting

Monday
Set the BAM. Set the Reach. Own the week.

A team with no goal to hit is a team with nothing to track. Monday is where you create the standard for the week. You're not motivating anyone — you're building an internal commitment they made publicly. That's the whole mechanism.

  • Go around the room. Every rep states their BAM (Bare Ass Minimum) and their Reach Goal for the week.
  • They write it. They say it out loud. You write it down. Now it's real.
  • End with a Q&A — "What are you seeing out there? What can we improve? What do you need from me?"
  • Keep Monday tight. 20–30 minutes max. Start the week fast.
The tone to set

This isn't a motivational speech. You're not cheerleading. You're setting a contract. "Hey, what are you putting your name on the line for this week?" — and then you hold the silence until they answer. The discomfort of the pause is part of it. It makes the commitment feel real.

Tuesday — Call Review or Role Play

Tuesday
Call Review or Role Play — your choice.

Rotate between the two week to week. Call review is reactive — you're pulling actual calls from the team and working through what happened. Role play is proactive — you're building skills before they need them live.

  • Call review: pull one recording, listen as a group, let the rep self-assess first before anyone else speaks.
  • Role play: give a realistic scenario from the actual offer — inbound DM, phone setting, a common objection.
  • Always debrief after. What went well? What would you change? What's the one thing to focus on this week?
  • Keep it practical, not theoretical. If it doesn't connect to a real situation they'll face this week, cut it.

Wednesday — Midweek Stat Check

Wednesday
Where are you vs. your BAM? What needs to shift?

Wednesday is the gut check. Half the week is gone. You pull the numbers and you go rep by rep. No one gets to coast — you create the conditions where Friday's conversation is never a surprise. This is also where you run the setter-closer cross-reference if you have both sides of the team in.

  • Pull each rep's numbers: sets booked, shows, qualified sets, disqualified sets.
  • Where are they vs. their BAM? Have a direct conversation about the gap — not later, now.
  • Bring closers and setters into the same room for the cross-reference (see Set Quality Tracking below).
  • If someone is already at their BAM by Wednesday — what's their stretch for the back half?

Thursday — Call Review or Role Play

Thursday
Whichever you didn't do Tuesday — do it today.

Thursday is the pressure valve before Friday. If Tuesday was call review, Thursday is role play. If Tuesday was role play, Thursday is call review. You're reinforcing the skill or the accountability pattern — whichever one the team needed more of that week.

  • Keep energy up — people are tired by Thursday. Make the session sharp and short.
  • Use Thursday role play to simulate end-of-week pressure — reps who are behind need reps, not pep talks.
  • If you have a rep who's consistently behind by Thursday, have a private conversation before Friday.

Friday — Culture Day

Friday
Wins and Shout Outs. All hands on deck.

Everyone in the room — setters, closers, managers. No data review, no pressure. This is wins and shout outs. A personal win from your week, and a genuine shout out to someone on the team who showed up the way you want this team to show up.

  • You go first. Share a real personal win — not work, anything. Then give a genuine shout out to someone specific.
  • Go around the room. Everyone participates. No exceptions.
  • Let it be natural. Don't rush it. Don't script it.
  • Keep it consistent every week. The power builds over time, not in week one.

Watch who gets shouted out consistently. Watch who never does. Over two to three weeks, the pattern tells you more than your data does.

How to run the shout out

It should feel genuine — because it should be genuine. You're creating a space where the people who are actually showing up get recognized. Over time, the people who aren't showing up will feel the absence of that recognition. You don't have to say anything. They'll already know. And that's when you have the private conversation.

The BAM System

What BAM stands for

BAM = Bare Ass Minimum. It's not your goal. It's your floor. The number you're putting your name on the line for no matter what. If you hit nothing else this week, you hit this. The Reach is what you're chasing selfishly — the number that gets you out of bed on a Saturday if you're behind.

Role BAM Example Reach Example What it does
Setter (DM) 12 qualified sets 20 qualified sets Creates daily minimum activity and a target worth chasing
Setter (Phone) 8 booked shows 14 booked shows Same principle — floor vs. ceiling
Closer $20K cash collected $40K cash collected The reach makes them push through Friday even if BAM is hit

The mechanism: When a rep commits to their BAM publicly on Monday, missing it by Friday isn't just a number problem — it's a character problem. They said it. Everyone heard it. That internal pressure is what drives weekend effort without you having to ask for it. You're not managing their calendar. You're managing their identity.

Never let a miss slide without a conversation. The first time you let someone miss their BAM without saying anything, you've told the whole team the BAM is optional. Silence is endorsement. Address it the same day Friday ends.

Set Quality Tracking — Wednesday Cross-Reference

The core problem this solves

Setters set to look good on their numbers. Closers mark leads as unqualified to explain away bad closes. Without a cross-reference, you never know who's actually creating the problem. This process forces both sides of the table to own their performance.

How the cross-reference works
Closer John flags 3 sets from this week as unqualified.
Question Where did those 3 sets come from? All from Ben (setter).
Setter Ben explains: "The prospect said they had money, they were interested, and they confirmed the call."
Review You pull the set recording. Ben went surface level on qualification — never went deeper, never confirmed real intent.
Outcome Is the problem Ben setting just to hit his numbers? Or is John being a softie on objections? You now have the evidence to make that call.
What to do with the data
  • Track qualified vs. unqualified sets weekly — by setter, by closer, and by offer.
  • Run the cross-reference every Wednesday for the first four to six weeks. After that, patterns are established and the team self-corrects.
  • Always review the recording before making a judgment call. Data plus evidence. Never just data.
  • Celebrate when a setter catches themselves about to take a weak set and passes. That's the standard you're building.

Within four weeks of running this consistently, unqualified sets become rare. Not because you scared the setters — because the team now has a shared, concrete understanding of what a qualified set looks like. The standard becomes self-enforcing.

Managing People — Not Just a Sales Team

The thing most managers miss

You're managing people and their emotions. Not just a sales team. The data matters — and the data will never lie to you. But behind every number is a human who has something going on in their life that's either helping or hurting their performance.

If someone has problems in their personal life, it will show up on their calls. If someone has money stress, it will show up in how they handle objections. This is what "who you are off your calls is who you are on your calls" means for your role as a manager — it's your job to see it before the numbers confirm it.

"Culture is an accumulation — the standard you set, the energy you bring, the vibe of the room. You build that. That is on you."

— JJ Hernandez
What to watch for
  • A rep whose numbers drop suddenly — don't jump straight to accountability. Ask first. Something changed.
  • A rep who gets quieter in team meetings — energy is data. Don't ignore it.
  • A rep who stops getting shouted out on Fridays — by week three, this is a signal, not a coincidence.
  • A rep who works harder when praised and deflates when ignored — know your people.

The most important thing you can build is a team that doesn't want to let each other down. Not a team that's afraid of you. One where the culture is strong enough that an off week feels wrong — not because you said something, but because it doesn't match who they've decided they are. That's when you've built something real.
