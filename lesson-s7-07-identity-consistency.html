Identity Consistency

People act in ways consistent with what they've said about themselves. Elicit the statement in discovery. Use it at the close. The most powerful close you'll ever run is the prospect's own words.

The Consistency Principle

Cialdini — Influence

One of Cialdini's six principles of influence is commitment and consistency. Once people make a statement — especially a public one about who they are — they feel internal and social pressure to act in ways that are consistent with that statement. Inconsistency with our own stated identity is psychologically uncomfortable. We move to resolve it.

This is different from cognitive dissonance in an important way. Cognitive dissonance is about the gap between identity and results. Identity consistency is about the gap between what you've stated about yourself and the action you're considering. When you elicit an identity statement and then show how an action is consistent with it, you remove the internal friction that creates hesitation.

The close that uses the prospect's own words is the most natural close in sales. You're not convincing them of anything — you're simply pointing out that the action they're considering is exactly what the person they said they are would do.

Eliciting Identity Statements in Discovery

The Questions That Produce Identity Statements

These questions invite the prospect to describe themselves in terms of values, identity, and self-concept — not just goals or results. The answers become the material you'll use at the close.

Question Sample Identity Statement It Produces
"What do you believe about your potential in this career?" "I genuinely believe I have what it takes — I just haven't had the right guidance."
"What kind of person do you want to be professionally?" "Someone who takes their development seriously and doesn't make excuses."
"When you've made big decisions in the past, what do you think drives them?" "I invest in myself. When I see something that will genuinely help me, I don't hesitate."
"What separates the people in your field who reach the top from those who don't?" "The ones who get there don't wait around for things to change on their own. They make moves."

Write it down. When a prospect makes a strong identity statement in discovery, remember the exact phrasing. Paraphrase it back at the close using their words — not your interpretation of their words. The closer the language, the more powerful the consistency pull.

The Commitment Bridge at the Close

How to Use the Statement

At the close, you bridge directly from what they said about themselves to the action in front of them. The structure is simple: remind them of their own statement, then show how the decision is a direct expression of it.

01
Step 1
Recall the Statement
Bring their exact words back. "You mentioned earlier that you're someone who invests in yourself when you see something that's genuinely going to help you."
02
Step 2
Connect It to the Decision
"This is that decision. This is the one that closes the gap between where you are and where you said you know you can be."
03
Step 3
Silence
Don't add anything else. The prospect is now holding a choice between saying yes (consistent with their stated identity) or saying no (inconsistent with who they said they are). That internal pressure is very real. Let it work.

Call Exchange — Identity Consistency at the Close

Elicitation in Discovery + Close Using Their Own Words
Rep (discovery)
"What kind of professional do you want to be — not just in terms of income, but in terms of how you show up and how serious you are about your own development?"
Prospect
"I want to be someone who doesn't cut corners. Someone who actually does the work and invests in themselves. I'm tired of doing things halfway."
Identity statement locked in. Rep notes the exact phrasing: "invests in themselves," "doesn't cut corners," "tired of doing things halfway."
Rep (30 minutes later, at close)
"Earlier you said something that stuck with me. You said you're tired of doing things halfway. That you want to be someone who actually does the work and invests in themselves. This is that moment. Saying yes to this is the most consistent thing you could do with who you said you are. Saying no is going halfway — again."
Their own words. No new pressure. Just a mirror. Saying no now means contradicting their own stated identity. The internal pressure to be consistent is doing the work.
Prospect
"Yeah... you're right. Let's do it."
