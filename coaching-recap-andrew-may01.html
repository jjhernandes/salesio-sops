Data Tracking — The Fix

You did it for 3 days, then forgot. That's a habit problem, not a motivation problem. The solution is making it frictionless.

The Problem Before

Tracking while dialing 150-200 people felt overwhelming. School scattered your attention. The sheet didn't feel urgent.

The Fix Going Forward

Notebook next to you while dialing. When someone picks up, make a mark. End of day: count the marks, plug the numbers in. That's it.

You're dialing 150-200 people a day but only 10-20 actually pick up. That's the only number that matters in the moment — connects. Everything else flows from there. It's not that much to log.

Tracking reveals where money is leaking. Without it, every slow month just feels like bad luck. With it, you find the lever.

Social Confidence — New People

You're confident on calls and comfortable with existing friends. With new people, you go quiet when you don't know what to say. You asked about "leadership" — what you're really asking about is presence.

What's Actually Happening

It's not a skill gap. It's a belief — a "life script" that learned to be more guarded with new people. Circumstantial and specific. Not a fundamental trait.

The Real Cause

Not caring enough about the person in front of you to go looking for things to ask. When you're genuinely curious, there's always something to say.

"There's always so much to say. Last night, I walked my dog, talked to my neighbor, and almost missed a sales call. Her husband came outside like — what are you doing, you've been out here an hour."

The fix: waiters and service workers. Every time you're at a restaurant, talk to your server. Ask where they're from. Ask how they ended up here. Let it go wherever it goes. You're already doing this in Ubers — expand it.

This also fixes the same pattern on calls. When you don't know what to say after someone tells you something, you default to a scripted question. More genuine curiosity = no scripts needed = better sets.
