How to Know When to Leave an Offer

Staying too long on the wrong offer is one of the most common close rate killers. It's also the most defended — because leaving feels like giving up. It's not. It's intelligence.

Why Reps Stay Too Long

The sunk cost fallacy: "I've put three months into this offer, I can't leave now." But sunk costs are gone — the question is only whether the next month will be better than a new offer would be. Usually, it won't be.

Fear of being seen as a quitter. Fear of the transition. Comfort with the familiar, even when the familiar isn't working. All of these are psychological, not strategic. And they keep reps on bad offers for months longer than they should be.

The rule: If you're consistently doing the right inputs at the right quality and the results aren't there after 60 days, the offer is likely the constraint — not you. Good reps on bad offers still close badly.

The Signals — Red Flags on an Active Offer

Commission isn't being paid on time
This is non-negotiable. If payments are late once, investigate. If it happens twice, you have your answer. Commission disputes and delays are the most common source of financial damage to reps who stay too long.
Chargeback rate is unusually high
If more than 3-5% of your closes are charging back, the product isn't delivering. You can't build a sustainable income on an offer that regularly reverses. Your reputation travels with your results — and chargebacks are your results too.
Leadership is unresponsive to legitimate concerns
If you raise a legitimate issue — bad leads, declining offer quality, payment delays — and leadership dismisses it or goes silent, that tells you everything about how you'll be treated when things get harder.
Close rate was good, then declined for no identifiable technique reason
If your technique hasn't changed, your state is managed, and your close rate dropped — the offer quality (lead quality, product reputation, market trust) has likely declined. Investigate before leaving, but don't ignore it.
You've lost belief in the product
This is the quietest and most damaging signal. If you no longer genuinely believe the product delivers what you're selling, your close rate will decline — because certainty transfer is the foundation of the close. You can't transfer certainty you don't have.

How to Leave Well

How you leave defines your reputation in this market. The high-ticket sales world is small. The person you left badly today may be connected to the offer you want to join next month. Leave well, even when you have every reason not to.

  • Give notice. Two weeks if possible. Handle your existing pipeline with the same professionalism you would if you were staying.
  • Don't badmouth the offer or leadership. Even in private. Even to other reps. What you say travels.
  • Document your results before you go. Close rate, deal size, notable closes. You'll need these for your next offer interview.
  • Leave the relationship intact. A "this isn't the right fit right now, I wish you well" exits cleanly. The market remembers how people leave more than how they arrived.

After You Leave

Take 1-3 days before starting the next search. Not as a vacation — as a debrief. Why wasn't that offer right? What would a better offer look like specifically? What will you look for in the vetting process next time that you didn't look for last time?

The rep who leaves one offer and immediately rushes to the next one without reflection tends to repeat the same pattern. The rep who debriefs carries the lesson forward.

The Sales.io job board is live. 4-6 new vetted offers posted daily. When you're ready to move, the supply is there. Be intentional about what you move to — a wrong offer that looks good on paper is worse than waiting two extra weeks for the right one.
