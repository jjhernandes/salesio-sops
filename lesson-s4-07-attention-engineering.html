Attention Engineering

What you make vivid gets bought. The brain does not purchase what it hears — it purchases what it pictures. Specificity is the tool.

The Core Principle

The brain does not buy what it hears. It buys what it pictures.

When information is abstract and vague, it passes through the mind without creating any feeling. When information is specific, concrete, and sensory, it creates an internal experience. That experience is what drives buying decisions.

Attention engineering is the practice of making both the current problem and the desired future vivid and specific enough that the prospect's brain has an actual internal experience of each. The contrast between those two experiences is what creates urgency and buying emotion.

Why Vague Futures Do Not Create Urgency

"Making more money" does not create urgency. It is a category, not a picture. The brain cannot hold "more money" and feel anything about it. It can hold "paying off the house by December and taking my wife to Italy in the spring and not checking my bank account before I do it" — and feel something powerful about that.

The gap between vague and specific is the gap between a prospect who "wants to think about it" and a prospect who is ready to move. Specificity is not a detail preference. It is the mechanism that makes desire real.

Making Both Sides Vivid Simultaneously

The Current Problem — Made Vivid

Most reps hear about a problem in the abstract. "I'm not making enough." "My close rate is low." "I can't break through the ceiling." These statements describe a situation without making it felt. Your job is to make the problem specific through questions: "Walk me through the last call where you felt the ceiling. What happened?" Now the prospect is replaying a specific moment. The brain is processing something with texture.

The Future State — Made Vivid

Pair the vivid problem with a vivid future. Not "imagine making more money" — "walk me through a Tuesday morning when you have already hit your number for the month and you have three calls scheduled that you actually want to take. What does the drive in feel like? What's different?" The contrast between these two vivid pictures creates the tension that moves people.

Vivid future without vivid problem produces nice feelings but no urgency. Vivid problem without vivid future produces discomfort but no direction. You need both operating simultaneously. The brain moves away from pain and toward pleasure — but only when both are specific enough to actually feel.

Language Patterns That Engineer Attention

Kills Attention (Vague) Engineers Attention (Specific)
"Imagine making more money." "Walk me through a month where you hit $25K. What do you do differently on the first day?"
"This will help your close rate." "Tell me about the last call where you felt yourself losing the prospect. Walk me through the moment it shifted."
"You'll have more freedom." "When that number is real, what is the first thing you do differently on a Friday afternoon?"
"Our students get great results." "This student was at $5K months, two kids, working from a spare bedroom. Month three: $28K. He sent me a photo from Italy."
"Things will be different." "Walk me through what changes first. Not eventually — first. The first week something shifts."

Present Tense as an Attention Tool

Why Tense Matters

Past tense and future tense create emotional distance. Present tense collapses that distance. When you ask a prospect to speak in present tense about a future state — "you ARE at $30K months, you ARE waking up without checking your pipeline in a panic" — the brain begins to process the experience as real in a way that future tense does not allow.

This is why the best discovery questions use present tense even when referring to a future state. "In that version of your life, what are you doing on a Monday morning?" Not "what would you be doing." What ARE you doing. The shift is subtle but the impact on brain processing is not.

  • ✓
    Use present tense for future projection. "In that life, you ARE..." not "in that life, you would be..."
  • ✓
    Use sensory detail. What do they see, hear, feel in the future state? "What does it feel like to close the laptop on Friday knowing the month is already done?"
  • ✓
    Use specific numbers and timelines. "$30K months" is more vivid than "good money." "By Q2" is more vivid than "eventually."
  • ✓
    Name the specific moment, not the category. "The last call where you felt you lost them" is more vivid than "when you struggle to close."

Call Exchange — Vague vs. Vivid

VERSION 1 — Rep Keeping Everything Vague
Rep
"So what are you hoping to get out of this? What's the goal?"
Prospect
"Just more success in sales. Better numbers, more freedom, you know."
Rep
"Yeah absolutely, that's a great goal. Our program is designed to help reps get exactly that — let me walk you through what we offer..."
Both sides stayed vague. The prospect has no vivid picture of either their problem or their future. The pitch lands on an emotionally flat prospect. No urgency was built.
VERSION 2 — Rep Engineering Attention Through Specificity
Rep
"Tell me about the last call where you felt yourself losing control of it — not in general, a specific call. Walk me through what happened."
Making the problem specific and vivid. The prospect's brain is now replaying a real experience.
Prospect
"Two weeks ago. Guy was warm, good energy. Then I got to the price and he went quiet. I panicked, started explaining everything again, and I could feel him checking out. Ended with 'I'll think about it.'"
Rep
"That silence after the price — what was going through your head in those three seconds?"
Going one layer deeper into the specific moment. The emotional texture is building.
Prospect
"Honestly? Pure panic. I just needed him to say something. So I started talking to fill the space."
Rep
"Now flip that. You are on that same call. Price goes out. Silence. And this time — you are completely comfortable in it. You hold it. What does that version of you look like in that moment?"
Pivoting to the vivid future using present tense. The brain is now holding two specific pictures — one of the problem, one of the solution. The contrast creates urgency that no pitch can manufacture.
Prospect
"That version of me is just... calm. Sitting back. Waiting. Knowing I don't need that specific deal to be okay."
The prospect has articulated their own vision of success in vivid, present-tense, emotionally loaded terms. The pitch now connects to something real.
