Call Reviewed — Brian (Local Plumber, Maricopa / Yuma)

Prospect Context

40-year plumbing business. Small town — only 6 licensed plumbers, 100K people. 700 steady clients, mostly word of mouth. Comfortable, no urgency. Deleted his Facebook page two days before the call. Said he's "comfortable" and already gives overflow to other plumbers. Hopped on the call curious, not sold on anything.

What's Working
  • High energy and natural likability
  • Decent frame set at the top
  • Pushed to understand their needs rather than just pitching
  • Self-aware enough to catch mistakes during review
  • Booked a follow-up touchpoint after the call
What Needs Work
  • Accepting vague answers and moving on
  • Making statements instead of asking questions
  • Pitching before pain is established
  • Agreeing with limiting beliefs instead of probing them
  • Overselling enthusiasm to a skeptical, established prospect
  • Not asking "why are we on this call?" when urgency is unclear

Key Coaching from This Call

Core Issue

Fluffy questions yield fluffy responses

John asked "how are things going?" — got "probably better than well." He moved on. JJ's flag: that's not an answer. Define "well." You have to extract specificity before you can build anything on what they're telling you. Vague questions give you vague data. Vague data can't drive a sale.

When the prospect said "I'm just interested in what's out there that fits my needs" — John nodded and moved on. The question he needed to ask was: "When you say your needs — what do you mean?" That's the entire discovery right there.

"They'll give you all the ammunition. The quality of your questions dictates the quality of the response."

Core Issue

Why are we on this call?

The prospect said he's doing well, doesn't need more work, has 700 clients, and is comfortable. John accepted this at face value and kept talking. The one question that would have cracked everything open:

"If things are going that well — why'd you hop on this call?"

There's always a reason. You find the reason, you find the urgency. If you can't find it, there's no sale — and you should move on faster. Either way, you need the answer.

JJ role-played this with a Peter example: "I've done this for 10 years, things are great, I make good money." The question Peter never asked: "Okay — then why are we talking?" That's the question that breaks open the real reason they're there.

Technique

Questions build authority — statements don't

When John wanted to show credibility around Facebook marketing, he said: "I'm a business owner too — I understand the opportunity." JJ flagged it: you don't need to say that. It sounds like you're trying to qualify yourself, which is the opposite of authority.

The question version does the same job and works better:

"You've been running this company 40 years. In all that time, you've never had success with social media? Why do you think that is?"

Now he's dissecting his own experience. He'll surface his own gap. You don't have to tell him anything — he'll arrive there himself.

The rule: anything you're tempted to say as a statement, ask as a question instead. Let them explain it back to you. When they say their own reasons out loud, they believe them more than anything you could tell them.

Technique

Don't agree with limiting beliefs — probe them

The prospect said he deleted his Facebook page. John said "yeah, I wouldn't blame you." JJ flagged it: you just validated the thing that's in the way of the sale. Never agree with limiting beliefs. Ask about them.

"Wait, why'd you delete it? What was going on with it?"

Let him explain. He was doing it wrong — he'll tell you that. And once he does, you have both the problem and the opening.

Framework

Level 1, Level 2, Level 3 — earn the next step

Don't accept a Level 1 answer and move on. Every vague answer has a Level 2 underneath it. Every Level 2 has a Level 3. You have to earn the next part of the conversation by extracting what's real.

L1
"I'm in a bind." — means nothing yet. Ask what it means.
L2
"$50,000 in debt." — now you have a number. Ask how it's impacting the business.
L3
"My runway is 3 months." — now you have urgency. Now you can connect a solution.

You can't skip to the pitch until you've reached Level 3. Everything before that is surface. Surface doesn't drive decisions.

Pattern

Dial back the energy with established buyers

John's warmth and enthusiasm is a real asset — but it's a mismatch with a 40-year business owner who's seen every sales pitch. The moment you say "I'm excited to hear about your business," someone like that is thinking: "You're excited because you're trying to sell me." It creates skepticism, not connection.

With this demographic: be direct, be brief. Ask one tight question. Let them lead. Your energy is best used in the connection phase — once you're past that, let your questions do the work.

"The second he smells that you're excited to sell him, it's over."

Core Concept

Customer awareness and your awareness must move together

JJ laid out the core dynamic that breaks most calls: the rep and the prospect end up out of sync. If the prospect comes in ready to buy and you're still in qualification mode, you drag them backward. If they're not ready and you pitch, you lose them.

The goal is for both to move forward together — same pace, same frequency. That only happens when you're genuinely tracking where they are in real time and adjusting. Not running a process. Responding to a person.

"They'll get to a place where they can buy — but you have to be there with them. Not ahead. Not behind."

Key Principles

Fluffy questions yield fluffy responses. Vague answers are a signal that your question wasn't specific enough. Ask again. Define the word they used. Don't move on with data you can't actually use.

Statements try to convince. Questions create understanding. Don't build authority by telling them things. Ask questions that lead them to see the gap themselves. When they say their own reasons out loud, they believe it.

If you're handling objections all day, your discovery is bad. Objections are just the cost of not building enough clarity earlier. The best reps barely handle objections because the discovery already addressed everything.

Every indecision is rooted in confusion. Every decision is rooted in clarity. Your one job is to give them enough clarity that the decision becomes obvious. That only happens when you've actually understood what they need.

Never agree with limiting beliefs. Probe them. If they say something that blocks the sale, don't nod. Ask why. Make them articulate it. They'll often dismantle it themselves.

Action Items

  • On every call this week: when a prospect gives a vague answer, ask one more level of depth before moving on. Practice going from L1 to L2 to L3 on at least one thing per call.
  • When a prospect says they're doing fine or "just looking" — ask directly: "If things are going that well, what made you want to hop on this call?" Don't skip this question.
  • Eliminate statements you make to build credibility. Turn them into questions. If you catch yourself saying "I know that works" — stop. Reframe: "Out of everything you've tried, why do you think that never worked?"
  • When a prospect says something that limits the conversation (deleted Facebook, doesn't want social media) — don't agree. Say "why?" and let them explain.
  • Match your energy to the demographic. With established, older business owners: calm, direct, short questions. Save the warmth for after you've earned it.
  • Review the Brian call a second time now that you have the coaching. Look specifically for the L1 moments you accepted and where the Level 2 question should have gone.
