Needs, Values, and Decisions

People don't buy what they need. They buy what they value. These are different things — and confusing them is one of the most common reasons a technically good pitch doesn't close.

Needs vs. Wants vs. Values — The Hierarchy

Needs

A need is something that is objectively absent or deficient. A rep who's closing at 15% when they're capable of 30% needs better close rate skills. That's a need. Needs are often obvious from the outside, but they don't drive buying decisions on their own.

Wants

A want is a consciously desired outcome. "I want to be making $30K a month." It's the articulated version of the need — what the person says they're after. Wants matter, but they're often surface-level. The prospect says they want income but what they actually want goes deeper.

Values

A value is what the desire is actually in service of. The rep who wants $30K months may be driven by freedom (values autonomy), by proving something (values significance), or by providing for their family (values security). The value is the deepest layer. It's also the one that closes deals.

Pitch to the need: weak. Pitch to the want: better. Pitch to the value: the thing that actually moves people.

Why People Buy What They Value, Not What They Need

People spend money on things they emotionally prioritize, not things they rationally require. A rep might need better objection handling — but if they don't value their own growth as a closer, they won't pay for coaching. A different rep might not technically need the program right now — but because they value being elite, they're in.

This is why pitching need ("you need this because your close rate is low") rarely closes, but pitching value ("the version of you that closes at 30% lives in a completely different reality — and you clearly care about getting there") moves people.

How to Surface Values in Discovery

Values don't show up when you ask "what do you need?" They show up when you ask why something matters. The follow-up question is everything.

  • ✓
    "What would it actually mean to you to hit that number?" — Not how they'd spend it. What it would mean. The answer reveals the value underneath the want.
  • ✓
    "What's the real cost of staying where you are?" — Surfaces the values at stake if nothing changes. People move away from loss faster than toward gain when the right value is threatened.
  • ✓
    "Who else does this matter for?" — If family or others appear, you've found a social value. These are often stronger motivators than personal ones.
  • ✓
    "What would be different about your life — not just your income?" — Forces the prospect to articulate the lifestyle, identity, or relational change they're actually seeking. That's the value.

Pitching to Values vs. Pitching to Features

ScenarioPitching FeaturesPitching to Values
Prospect values freedom "You get 7 group sessions per week and weekly 1-on-1 coaching." "This is the skill set that lets you work 4 days a week and still outperform someone working 6. That's the freedom side of this."
Prospect values significance "We've coached 2,100+ reps." "The people who come out of this program become the closers that other reps ask for advice. That reputation is real — and it's earned here."
Prospect values security "There's a money-back guarantee." "The guarantee exists because we know what this does. But more than the guarantee — the skill you walk away with can never be taken from you. That's security that doesn't disappear when an offer does."
Prospect values growth "The curriculum covers psychology, identity, and technique." "If you're the kind of person who actually wants to understand why you do what you do on calls — not just the tactics — this is built for you. It goes places most training won't touch."

Call Exchange — Need vs. Value Discovery

VERSION 1 — Rep pitches need, prospect doesn't feel moved
Rep
"Based on what you're describing, you need to work on your post-pitch frame and how you handle the first objection. That's what's costing you deals."
Correct diagnosis. But the rep pitched to the gap (need) not to why closing that gap matters to this person. Response will be mild.
Prospect
"Yeah, I'm sure that's true. I'll think about it."
VERSION 2 — Rep surfaces the value first, then pitches to it
Rep
"You mentioned earlier that you want your wife to be able to quit her job. What does that mean to you — like why does that matter so much?"
Going under the want to find the value.
Prospect
"She's been working since before we had kids. I just want her to have the choice. She deserves that."
Rep
"That's what we're actually working toward. The close rate isn't the point — it's what it unlocks for her. The rep who closes at 30% can give her that choice. That rep is on the other side of this. What's in the way of you being him?"
Pitched directly to the value. The prospect is now emotionally engaged in the decision in a completely different way. The close is already half-made.
