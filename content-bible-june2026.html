Straight From JJ — Source Material

Signature Stories — Ready to Film
The $36,000 sale he lost by going back to the script
First ten minutes she was open, emotional, giving him everything unprompted. He got tactical — went back to the framework because he didn't trust what he was seeing — and felt her pull back into her head in real time. "The process is a floor, not a ceiling. When someone is already above it and you drag them back down, you've just de-sold them."
Kevin — the student who never once mentioned commission
Older guy, first calls on a new offer. Asked what brings him down after a bad call and he didn't mention money once — looked at a bad call the way a mechanic looks at a broken engine. Curiosity instead of shame. That's genuinely rare, and it's the thing that separates reps who move fast from reps who stay stuck.
Spencer — the closer whose biggest skill was silence
Reviewing calls for a financial advisory team — a tech issue came up mid-call and Spencer just waited. Didn't fill it, didn't apologize for it. "That silence was authority you cannot fake. You either have peace with yourself or you don't, and it shows up in the quiet moments."
The 10% closer who never ran his closing sequence — for months
Talented door-to-door rep, knew something was off, kept looking for the answer everywhere except the one place it actually was. He'd been running his full pitch for months and never once ran the actual close. That's what happens when the problem is internal — you search for it everywhere else.
Hormozi told him to shut down B2C — he was right
Not something JJ saw coming. Rebuilding Sales.io from scratch afterward taught him more than anything he could have planned for. A real story about being inside the room instead of watching it from outside — and what that access actually teaches you.
Hot Takes — Industry Critique
Scripts are the destination, not the starting point
The whole industry runs on flowcharts — if they say this, you say that. It trains reps to listen for triggers instead of listening to people. Great pattern recognition, terrible at actual conversation. The moment a prospect says something they didn't prepare for, they freeze.
Accountability without diagnosis makes reps worse
"You're not closing because you're not doing enough calls" — maybe. Or maybe they're walking into every call from scarcity and no amount of volume fixes that. Nobody wants to sell identity work because you can't put it in a module and give it a clever name. So they sell activity instead.
Most sales trainers have never been in a real high-ticket conversation
Most people selling sales training got a few clips from one or two real $30-50K calls and built an entire brand on it. The courses are recycled — NEPQ is pacing and leading with extra steps. 40 coaches teaching the same script and calling it their proprietary method. Same thing, different colored box.
The less you try to close, the more you close
The industry is built on pressure — build momentum, overcome every objection, make them feel the pain until they move. The best sales JJ has ever made didn't feel like selling. The prospect sold themselves; his job was to reflect their reality back clearly, then get out of the way.

The Objection Reframe — "I Can't Afford It"
What they say: "I need to think about it" / "I can't afford it"

The word underneath it is afford. They say it like a fact — it's not, it's a decision disguised as a math problem. When someone says they can't afford something, what they're usually saying is: "I don't fully believe the gap between where I am and where I could be is real enough to justify this."

The money usually exists. The certainty doesn't.

What Resonance Actually Sounds Like
After a talk or session"I've been trying to figure out how to say that for two years and you just said it in one sentence."
Quoting it back word for word"That thing you said about the person who needs the sale the most." / "When you said you'd rather lose the sale than lose the frame."
The strongest signal"I had a call exactly like that last week." — when what you said becomes the container for someone else's experience, that's when it actually landed.
