"I've Tried Things Like This Before" — Historical Identity Objection

This objection sounds like product skepticism. It's actually about the prospect's history with themselves — and that's a completely different problem to solve.

What This Objection Actually Is

When a prospect says "I've tried things like this before and it didn't work," they are making an identity statement about their own history. The statement is: "I am someone who has tried and not succeeded."

The surface objection is about your program. The real objection is about them. They've compiled evidence that investing in this category of thing leads to disappointment — and they're applying that evidence to you before you've even had a chance to differentiate.

Why This Is a Historical Identity Objection

The prospect isn't just telling you about past programs. They're telling you about past versions of themselves. "I tried and it didn't work" carries a subtext of "I tried and I didn't work." They may not be aware of this — but it's there. And it makes this objection resistant to logical rebuttal, because the logic they're defending is emotional.

"You cannot argue someone out of their own history. But you can help them see that their history isn't the only possible future."

— JJ Hernandez

Why Arguing With Past Experience Always Fails

The moment you try to prove that your program is better than what they've tried before, you've made a critical error: you've positioned the conversation as your program vs. their experience. Their experience wins every time. It's personal. It's lived. It's real to them in a way that your pitch can never be.

When a rep says "but we're totally different" after someone says they've been burned before, the prospect hears: "Ignore your own experience and trust me instead." That is not a persuasive argument. It is a red flag.

What the Rep Does Wrong What Actually Works
Immediately defends the program: "We're different though." Validates the experience first: "That makes total sense. Tell me more about what happened."
Lists differentiators before understanding what failed. Asks what made the past experience not work — and uses the answer to differentiate.
Gets defensive about being compared to other programs. Welcomes the comparison — shows curiosity, not insecurity.
Talks about the program before talking about the person. Leads with understanding the prospect's past experience so the differentiation is precise.

The Move — Validate, Then Separate

01
Step One
Validate the Past Experience
Before you say anything about your program, acknowledge what they went through. Not in a corporate "I'm so sorry to hear that" way — genuinely. "That's a real thing — there's a lot of stuff in this space that overpromises. Tell me what that was like."
Why it works: Validation drops the prospect's guard. They're expecting you to defend yourself. When you don't — when you actually ask about their experience with curiosity — the dynamic shifts. You become the first person in this conversation who isn't selling past their pain.
02
Step Two
Ask What Made Those Experiences Not Work
The key question: "What made those experiences not work for you?" This question does two things simultaneously: it gets you the actual failure points from their past, and it gives you a specific differentiation target. Instead of defending your program generically, you get to address the exact thing that failed them before.
Prospect might say: "It was all generic content, no personal attention." Now you know exactly what to differentiate on. You're not defending against a vague comparison — you're addressing the specific failure they named.
03
Step Three
Separate This From That — Precisely
Now differentiate — but using their words, not yours. "The thing you described — generic content with no feedback loop — is exactly what this isn't. The whole model is built around personal attention because we saw what happened when that element was missing." You're not saying "we're different." You're showing them the specific difference that addresses the specific failure they described.
The key: never separate generically. "We're unique" means nothing. "The thing that failed you in that experience is the thing we built this around solving" is surgical and credible.

Call Exchange — Right vs. Wrong Response

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Defends the Program Immediately
Prospect
"I've actually invested in programs like this before and honestly didn't see the results I was promised. So I'm pretty skeptical."
Rep
"Yeah we hear that a lot — there's definitely a lot of bad programs out there. But we're different. We have a money-back guarantee, the coaching is highly personalized, and JJ has worked with Iman Gadzhi's team so the credibility is real. We get actual results."
Immediately pivoted to defending. Didn't ask about the past experience. Didn't validate. The prospect now has to evaluate a list of claims against their lived experience. Their experience wins.
Prospect
"Yeah... I've heard that before too though. I'm just not sure."
Exact outcome: the defense sounds identical to what every other program said before the prospect was disappointed.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Validates and Separates Precisely
Prospect
"I've actually invested in programs like this before and honestly didn't see the results I was promised. So I'm pretty skeptical."
Rep
"That's completely fair. And honestly — there's a lot in this space that deserves to be skeptical of. What were those experiences like? What made them not work for you?"
Validates. Doesn't defend. Asks about the past experience specifically. The prospect was expecting a pitch. They got curiosity instead.
Prospect
"It was basically a course with a bunch of videos. Once I paid I never heard from anyone. And the tactics were very scripted — like it didn't account for how actual conversations go."
Rep
"Okay — I want to be specific with you because that matters. What you described — a course model where you pay and disappear, scripted tactics that break down in real conversations — that's actually the opposite of how this is structured. There are no scripts here. You get weekly 1-on-1 coaching, group call reviews of your actual calls, and a community where you have direct access to coaches. The people who built this program built it because they were frustrated by exactly what you described."
Differentiates using their exact words. Not generic. Not defensive. Addresses the specific failures they named. The contrast is clear without dismissing their experience.
