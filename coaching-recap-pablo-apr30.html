Your Pattern — What We Identified

The most important thing from today had nothing to do with Abdul. It was watching yourself in real time and seeing the habit that's running underneath your calls.

"You're trying to win by likability — not authority. Those are two completely different things."

The tell? When Abdul asked the dumb certificate question, instead of putting it back on him, you offered to walk him through the modules — something you know you're not supposed to do. You did it to win him over. That's the pattern: when there's tension or disengagement, you reach for charm and accommodation instead of holding your frame.

The Pattern (Old)
When a prospect gets weird, goes vague, or disengages — accommodate them. Show more. Explain more. Be more likable. Try to earn them back through effort and generosity.

Result: you give away control, they sense it, and the frame collapses.
Authority (New Standard)
When a prospect gets weird — hold the frame. Play dumb. Put it back on them. Let the silence sit. You don't chase. You don't explain again. You lead.

Result: they feel your certainty, and certainty is what people actually buy.

Likability gets you liked. Followability gets you paid. People don't write $7,500 checks to someone they like — they write them to someone they trust and believe in. Those are built through authority, not charm.

What JJ Taught — 6 Principles

Here's everything we covered, distilled. These aren't tips — they're the foundation of how you need to think on every call going forward.

Principle 01
Start with "Why are you here?"
When a prospect seems unengaged, checked out, or unclear on why they're even on the call — ask it directly, early. "Why are you here?" cuts through the noise immediately. If they can't answer it clearly, nothing else matters. You can't sell someone who doesn't know what they want. This is your first line of defense against wasting your own time.
Principle 02
Never let a vague answer slide
Every time you accept a surface-level answer, you're agreeing to work with incomplete information. Dig until it's real. Use their language back at them. Ask "what do you mean by that?" or "what does that actually look like for you?" Vague answers are a signal — either they're not ready, or they haven't thought about it. Either way, you need to know which one it is.
Principle 03
Own the first 2-3 minutes completely
The frame gets set in the opening minutes and it almost never changes. Everything is in your control early — or nothing is later. No rambling. No letting them cut you off. No vague openers. You set the tone. If they interrupt or go sideways in the first two minutes, pause it. Address it. Get back on track. You're leading this.
Principle 04
Life scripts — you teach people how to treat you
The moment you let bad behavior happen once, you've written a script for how that person interacts with you. Abdul answered his phone. You let it go. The script was written. It's not about being aggressive — it's about addressing it cleanly the first time. One clear, calm response sets the tone. Letting it slide trains them that it's acceptable.
Principle 05
Authority ≠ confrontation
A lot of people confuse these two. Confrontation is reactive and emotional. Authority is calm and certain. You don't need to raise your voice or get aggressive to assert authority. You need composure. You need to be the most settled person in the room. When Abdul asked the certificate question, the authoritative move was to pause, play dumb, and put it back on him — not to explain again or show him the modules. Calmness IS the power.
Principle 06
Ditch the script — you need your instincts back
Following a script mechanically robs you of your ability to be present. When you're running a checklist in your head, you stop reading the person in front of you. The goal is to remove the script so you can actually focus on them. The curriculum will give you a framework, not a script. The framework gives you direction — your instincts navigate within it. You can't develop those instincts while hiding behind a script.
The Move — When They Ask a Dumb Question

Abdul asked "do I get a certificate?" after a full 7-minute presentation. Here's exactly how to handle it:

Don't do this
Explain it again. Pull up the modules. Show him more. Try to win him back. "I know, it's a coaching program, let me show you what's inside..."
Do this instead
Play dumb. Put it back on him.

"Was the presentation not clear? I mean, we just went through all of that — were you listening? I can run it back if you need, but I mean, I'm a little confused here. Am I misunderstanding something?"

Make him feel like the one who's off, not you. Because he is.

You're not crazy. He wasn't paying attention. You don't apologize for the presentation — you hold your frame and let him figure out that he's the one who missed something.
