Elicitation

Seeing behind the mask. Most prospects present a surface version of their situation. Your job is to draw out what's actually real — without making them feel interrogated or exposed.

What Elicitation Is

Elicitation is the art of drawing out what's real beneath what's stated. It's not interrogation. It's not manipulation. It's skilled curiosity — asking questions that create safety and invite depth, so the prospect can get to the truth of their situation without feeling forced.

Most reps operate on the surface version. They hear "I'm looking to increase my income" and pitch to that. Elite reps hear that and ask one more question — and find out the real driver: the debt, the shame, the relationship pressure, the private goal. That's what the pitch should actually address.

Why the surface version isn't enough

Prospects don't always know what they really want. Or they know but don't feel safe enough to say it yet. Or they've presented a cleaned-up version because they're not sure how much to trust you. Elicitation creates the conditions for the real thing to come out — and the real thing is always more compelling to pitch to than the stated version.

Surface Version vs. Real Version

Examples

Surface: "I want to make more money." Real: "I'm ashamed that my younger brother is out-earning me and I can't stop thinking about it."

Surface: "I feel like I'm not growing in my current role." Real: "I've been on the same offer for two years, I know I should have moved by now, and I'm starting to wonder if I'm just not good enough."

Surface: "I want to improve my sales skills." Real: "Every call I feel like I'm performing and I hate it. I want to be the version of me that just talks to people naturally and still closes."

You can't pitch to the surface version and expect a deep close. The surface version is intellectual. The real version is emotional. Only the emotional version creates the felt urgency to act.

Elicitation Questions That Don't Feel Like Interrogation

  • ✓
    "What does that mean for you — like really?" — The casual "really" invites depth without sounding clinical. It signals you want to understand, not just collect data.
  • ✓
    "Tell me more about that." — The simplest elicitation tool that exists. Don't rush past it. When a prospect mentions something real, "tell me more about that" opens the door without any agenda attached.
  • ✓
    "What's the part that bothers you most?" — Helps you find the sharpest pain point. Not just what's wrong but what hurts the most — that's the lever.
  • ✓
    "What's that been like?" — Invites feeling-language without asking for it directly. Naturally surfaces the emotional reality behind the stated situation.
  • ✓
    "If you're being completely honest, what's really holding you back?" — Permission-based. "Being completely honest" signals safety and signals that you can handle the real answer. Often surfaces the thing they've been holding back.
  • ✗
    Don't use: "Why haven't you fixed this already?" — sounds judgmental. "How long has this been a problem?" — sounds like a script. "Is money a concern?" before trust is built — sounds presumptuous and kills the safe space.

Holding Space for the Real Answer

The most common mistake

A rep asks a good elicitation question. The prospect pauses, starts to go somewhere real, and the rep jumps in before they finish — with a solution, with a reframe, with empathy statements that feel performative. Every time you interrupt the process, you push the prospect back to the surface.

The practice is simple and hard: ask the question. Then shut up. Hold the space. Even if there are eight seconds of silence. The real answer needs room to come out. When you fill the silence, you take that room away.

When the real thing surfaces

When a prospect gets to something true — you'll feel it. The pace slows. The language changes. Sometimes there's a pause before they say it. When that happens: don't pivot immediately to your pitch. Acknowledge it first. "I hear that. That sounds like it's been sitting with you for a while." Then, and only then, do you move.

Call Exchange — Surface vs. Real Discovery

VERSION 1 — Rep stays on the surface, pitches to the stated version
Prospect
"I'm at a point where I just want to grow, you know? I feel like I'm capable of more."
Rep
"Totally. That's exactly who this is built for — people who are ready to take it to the next level. We'll help you close more, earn more, and build the skills that get you there."
Pitched to "capable of more" and "want to grow." True but shallow. The prospect said something vague and the rep matched it with something vague. No real connection was made.
VERSION 2 — Rep elicits, finds the real thing, pitches to it
Prospect
"I'm at a point where I just want to grow, you know? I feel like I'm capable of more."
Rep
"What does 'capable of more' feel like from the inside? Like what's the gap you're experiencing?"
Elicitation. Not accepting the surface answer. Inviting the real one.
Prospect
"Honestly, I get on calls and I know I'm not fully present. I'm in my head. And I know when I'm in flow I can close anything, but I can't access that consistently."
Rep
"That's the real thing. And that's exactly what we work on — not the tactics, but the state. Because the version of you in flow isn't a mystery — it's just you without the mental interference. That's what this is built to clear. What would it mean to close from that state consistently, not just occasionally?"
Pitched to the real thing. The prospect feels seen at a level that changes the conversation entirely.
