Social (S) Type

Closing the community-driven, family-first prospect. The S type doesn't buy for themselves. They buy for the people around them — and if the decision feels like it's being taken away from those people, you've lost them.

What the S Type Cares About

The S type cares about belonging, safety, and impact on the people they love. They want to know who else is in the room, whether they'll be supported, and whether this decision makes sense for their family — not just for them.

They're warm and easy to connect with, which can make reps think they're close to a yes when they're actually waiting for something different: permission to trust this. That permission comes from the community, from other people's experiences, and from feeling genuinely understood.

The fear underneath the S type

S types have a deep fear of making a decision that lets people down — financially, relationally, or socially. Spending $7,500 on a program feels like a risk not just to them but to their family. That fear is real and it has to be honored, not dismissed. The rep who skips past it loses. The rep who acknowledges it and helps them see the decision as protecting their family, not risking them, wins.

How the S Type Makes Decisions

Consensus and safety

S types rarely make major decisions alone. They're processing not just whether they want something but whether the people around them will support it. "I need to talk to my wife/partner" is not a stall for most S types — it's a real part of their decision process. Fighting it damages the relationship. Including it in the conversation closes the deal.

They're also risk-averse. They need to believe the downside is manageable before they can feel excited about the upside. Guarantee language, community evidence, and testimonials from people in similar situations all reduce the perceived risk and lower the barrier to a yes.

What Kills the Call with an S Type

Pressure or urgency tactics. Scarcity, countdown clocks, "this is only available today" — all of these signal to an S type that you don't care about them, you care about the transaction. Their walls go up and they emotionally check out while continuing to nod politely.

Isolating the decision. "This is your choice, you don't need anyone's approval" might feel empowering to other types. To an S type it's threatening. They're not looking for permission to decide alone — they're looking for a framework that makes the decision safe for everyone involved.

Ignoring the family mention. If an S type brings up their partner, kids, or family, they're telling you something important. Skipping past it to continue your pitch communicates that you weren't listening — which destroys trust with someone whose entire buying decision is built on trust.

What Closes an S Type

  • ✓
    Bring the community into the pitch. "There are 80 people in this program right now who are doing exactly what you're trying to do." The S type wants to know they won't be alone in this. Show them the room before you ask them to step into it.
  • ✓
    Reframe the investment as protection. "The risk isn't spending the $7,500 — the risk is staying at $6K months and watching time pass while nothing changes. What does that do to your family?" Make the decision to invest feel like the safe choice, not the scary one.
  • ✓
    Reference others in their situation. "I had a guy last cohort — similar situation, spouse was hesitant, two kids — he came in and by month two his wife was telling him it was the best decision he'd made." Social proof from people in identical circumstances is the highest-value evidence for an S type.
  • ✓
    Include the partner in the solution. If they bring up their spouse, don't try to close around them. "What would your wife need to see or understand to feel comfortable with this? Let's make sure we cover that." This is not a stall — it's the close.

Call Exchange — Right vs. Wrong with an S Type

VERSION 1 — Rep misses the family signal, pushes urgency
Prospect
"I really like this. I just want to make sure I talk to my wife about it before I commit."
Rep
"Totally get it — the thing is, we're actually closing enrollment this week and I'd hate for you to miss the cohort. This is really your decision to make."
Artificial urgency. Isolated the decision from the partner. S type now feels pressured and dismissed. They will not buy today.
Prospect
"Yeah... I think I just need to check in with her first. Can I call you back?"
VERSION 2 — Rep brings the partner into the conversation
Prospect
"I really like this. I just want to make sure I talk to my wife about it before I commit."
Rep
"That makes complete sense — this is a real investment and she should be part of it. What's her main concern likely to be? Is it the money, the time commitment, or just not knowing enough about what this actually is?"
Normalized the inclusion of the partner. Moved toward understanding her concerns so they can be addressed. S type feels heard and supported, not managed.
Prospect
"Probably the money — she's seen me try things before that didn't pan out."
Rep
"Okay. So what she needs to see is that this has a clear risk structure. Here's what I'd want her to know: we have a money-back guarantee. If you don't make your investment back in 90 days, we refund you. That's not a sales line — it's in the agreement. How would she feel about that?"
Addressed the real concern. Spoke directly to the partner's likely objection. S type now has something to bring home that makes the decision safe for the family.
