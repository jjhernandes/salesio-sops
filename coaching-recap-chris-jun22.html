Opening Lesson
There Is No Neutral Influence
In every single conversation you have, the other person will leave better or worse after speaking to you. There is no in-between. No neutral. This is true on sales calls, in your personal life, with your roommates, with your girlfriend — every interaction either moves people up or moves them down.

When you understand this, your intention shifts. You stop just going through the motions and start consciously deciding: what state do I want to leave this person in?
"It's my job to make sure that you feel as clear as possible on your intentions, your goals, where you're going, what you're doing. There should be very, very clear expectations and very clear feelings."
Call Review — David's Pitch
Logic Signs Off on the Emotional Contract
David's call came up — his pitch felt very logical and reason-based. The issue wasn't that he was wrong, it's that he was pitching before the emotional contract was signed.

The framework: People make decisions emotionally and justify them logically. Logic doesn't close — it confirms. By the time you pitch the price, they should already have said yes internally. Logic is the final signature on a contract that emotion already drafted.

David gets uncomfortable on the close — not because he's operating at a lower consciousness, but because the close feels unfamiliar. Things aren't hard, they're unfamiliar.
"You're never gonna propose to a woman you don't know is gonna say yes. Why would you pitch if you don't know they're gonna say yes?"

State Management — What Moves Chris
The Good Day / Bad Day Pattern
Chris's girlfriend pointed it out: he can't have a good day if he feels like he didn't have a good workday. Not tied to money — tied to performance and hitting inputs.

Root of it: he's gauging the quality of his day by whether he hit a set number of reach-outs and inputs. When external circumstances block those inputs, it affects his whole state — even when it wasn't his fault.
Moves Him Up
Hitting his input targets
Feeling like he emptied the bucket. Waking up and starting strong. Being in a space he controls. Faith — removing shame from the equation. Girlfriend's presence.
Moves Him Down
Missing daily input goals
External circumstances disrupting a planned day. Unrealistic standards he sets for himself. Low-energy roommate situation creating psychological drag in his own home.
"What you're going through is essentially that you're looking for a feeling of fulfillment. But you can create and optimize for that feeling if you know what it is that makes you feel that way."
Framework — Clarity Systems
Engineer the Feeling Instead of Chasing It
The problem isn't Chris's drive — it's that he hasn't named specifically what creates his optimal state. He's going off a general feeling of "fulfilled day" without knowing exactly what produces it.

JJ's anchor points for reference: Waking up early. Working out early. Going to bed slightly hungry. Knowing the bank account is trending up. Running. Clarity that he's progressing — physically, financially, directionally.

The goal: identify Chris's specific anchor points so he can engineer them into his days intentionally — not just hope they happen.
"People always know what their perfect day looks like. And when I ask them why they're not doing most of it — they go quiet. You can control this."
Integrity angle from church: Entrepreneurs over-commit and use external factors as excuses for why they didn't follow through. Living with integrity means only committing to what you'll actually do — and doing it.
Story — The $50K/Month Student
If You Can't Name It, You'll Chase It Forever
JJ told the story of a student making $50K/month who kept saying he needed to "prove them wrong." When JJ pushed to name who exactly — the student couldn't answer. He was romanticizing the idea of proving something to someone that didn't exist.

The lesson for Chris: if you can't describe the feeling you're chasing, you'll be chasing it until you're 80. Money won't give you fulfillment. JJ made $196K in a single day — still felt the same. The fulfillment comes from knowing what creates it, then doing those things consistently.
"If you can't describe that feeling, we don't know where we're going. We don't know what we're looking for. You'll be chasing that forever."

Direct Coaching Feedback
Give Less Context — Your Point Lands Faster Than You Think
JJ noticed and named it directly: Chris articulates what he wants to say very well and speaks clearly — but he adds more context than needed to be understood. JJ said he understood the point roughly 10 seconds before Chris finished explaining it.

This is something Chris does to build connection — but it has the opposite effect over time. When you say less, people value what you say more. On sales calls specifically, this will make his delivery significantly stronger.
"You articulate what you want to say very well. Cut back some of what you say — and on calls, you'll be so much stronger in your delivery. In real life, people will value your word more."
Lesson 1 Takeaway
The Three Pillars of Success in Sales — and Life
Chris correctly identified the three pillars from Lesson 1: self-mastery, communication, observation. JJ confirmed — these are the root of all success regardless of industry.
Self-Mastery
You know what you need to do and you do it. You keep your word to yourself. You're a master of your own state and behavior.
Communication
You say what needs to be said, clearly, at the right time. Observing you're gonna be late and not communicating it breaks trust immediately.
Observation
You're reading the room, reading the person, reading the situation. You see what others miss. This is what separates good from elite.
Moving company example: arrived 18 minutes late, didn't call Chris — but called his assistant to check. That's low self-mastery, low communication, low observation. All three failed at once.
Lesson 3 — Identity Types
The Three Selling Identities — Which Is Most Common
Chris flagged that community identity will be the hardest for him to sell to. JJ's response: community identity is actually the least common. Don't stress about it.
Most Common
Historical identity — everyone has a version of "back in my day." Past self, past glory, past relationship, old physical peak. It lingers in almost every person you'll talk to.
Also Common
Role/position identity — CEOs who think they're king, founders who need to feel respected. Very common in business-to-business conversations.
Community identity (PTA, group affiliations, tribes) is rare in the sales reps and offer owners Chris will be speaking to. Don't spend mental energy there.
