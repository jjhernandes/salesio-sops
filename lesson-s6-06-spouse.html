"I Need to Talk to My Spouse" — Permission vs. Decision

This objection lives in two completely different worlds. In one, it's legitimate — the spouse has a real stake. In the other, it's an exit strategy dressed as a relationship obligation. You have to know which one you're in.

Permission vs. Decision — The Core Distinction

There are two versions of "I need to talk to my spouse." One is real — the prospect genuinely makes financial decisions jointly and their partner has a legitimate role in this. The other is a stall — the prospect is not ready to decide and they're using their spouse as the reason so it doesn't feel like rejection.

The wrong response is the same to both: immediately say "Of course, no problem" and schedule a follow-up. This works for neither. If it's real, you've left the spouse with zero information and a skeptical prospect who couldn't close the pitch. If it's a stall, you've given the stall a calendar date and nothing will change.

How to Read Which One It Is

Real: The prospect mentioned their spouse earlier in the conversation. They talk about finances as "we" decisions. They're not uncomfortable saying this — they state it plainly and genuinely seem to want to include them.

Stall: The spouse appears for the first time at the moment of close. The prospect becomes slightly evasive when pressed. They use vague language ("I just kind of want to make sure she's okay with it") rather than genuine inclusion language.

When It's Real — Include the Spouse, Don't Exclude Them

The Principle

If the spouse is a real stakeholder, trying to close without them is a mistake. Even if you "win" the conversation and the prospect commits, the spouse's objections have not been addressed. Post-close doubts will spike. Buyer's remorse is highly likely. The program may never start.

The move when it's real: Get the spouse on a call. "I completely respect that — honestly, that's how it should work for a decision this size. Would it make sense to get them on a quick call together? I want them to have the same picture you have, because right now they're working with whatever you're able to relay, and that's not quite the same."

What You're Not Doing

You are not trying to "sell the spouse." You're making sure the spouse has accurate information to make a good decision. This is genuine — and the prospect can feel the difference. The rep who is eager to get the spouse on a call to close them is identifiable and repellent. The rep who wants to make sure the whole family has what they need to decide well is trusted.

When It's a Stall — Surface What's Underneath

The stall version of this objection is dangerous because it sounds completely reasonable. Of course you'd want your spouse's input. No rep wants to look like they're disrespecting someone's relationship. So most reps concede immediately — even when the objection is a smokescreen.

The Test Question

The cleanest way to surface whether it's real or a stall is this question — asked calmly, with no pressure in the tone:

"I totally understand. Quick question — if your spouse is fully on board, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"

If the answer is a clear yes, the spouse is the real concern and you can move toward including them. If the answer is hesitant, vague, or pivots to something else ("well I'd still want to think about..."), the spouse isn't the issue. Something else is. Surface it.

What to Do With a Hesitant Answer

"That's helpful — sounds like there might be something else you're weighing too. What is it? I'd rather we talk through it now than have you go into a conversation with your spouse without having resolved what's on your own mind."

What Not to Do

  • ✗
    Don't immediately concede and schedule a follow-up. The follow-up call will not happen unless you've resolved what's actually underneath. A calendar date is not a commitment when the decision is still unresolved.
  • ✗
    Don't try to close around the spouse. "Well, you know yourself better than anyone — you could make this decision independently." This is disrespectful of their relationship and they'll feel it. It also creates a post-close problem when the spouse finds out.
  • ✗
    Don't send a follow-up email with more info and hope it works out. The spouse has not seen the full conversation. A summary email does not replicate the emotional arc of the call. It gives them enough information to say no and not enough to say yes.
  • ✓
    Do test the objection with the question, then act based on what it reveals. Real concern: include the spouse. Stall: surface the real objection. Either way, you're in the right conversation.

Call Exchange — Testing the Objection

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Immediately Concedes
Prospect
"I think I'd need to run this by my wife before making a decision like this."
Rep
"Of course, absolutely — that makes total sense for something this size. I'll send you some info you can share with her. Want to set up a follow-up call later this week?"
Accepted the objection without testing it. Doesn't know if it's real or a stall. The follow-up call will likely not happen. The "info to share" will not close the spouse.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Tests the Objection
Prospect
"I think I'd need to run this by my wife before making a decision like this."
Rep
"That makes sense. Quick question — if your wife is fully on board, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"
The test question. Calm, no pressure. Just checking whether the spouse is the real issue.
Prospect
"Yeah, I mean — yeah. If she's okay with it I'd be in."
Rep
"Perfect. Then the best move is to get her on a quick call together — even 20 minutes. She'll have questions I can address directly, and honestly you don't want to be in a position of trying to summarize a 45-minute conversation. What does your schedule look like this week? I can find a time that works for both of you."
The objection was real. The right move is to include the spouse — not sell around her. This is respectful, practical, and it closes more deals than any other approach to this objection.
VERSION 3 — Stall Version — Surfacing the Real Objection
Prospect
"I think I'd need to run this by my wife before making a decision like this."
Rep
"That makes sense. Quick question — if your wife is fully on board, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"
Prospect
"I mean... yeah, probably. But I'd still want to think about it more myself first."
The stall version. The spouse isn't the real issue — the prospect hasn't decided yet.
Rep
"That's helpful. So there's something on your end that's still open — separate from your wife. What is it? I'd rather we sort that out now."
Cuts through the spouse objection to the real one. Doesn't force anything — just creates space for the real conversation to happen.
