Step 6 — Consequence

The cost of staying the same. Most reps skip this step and wonder why prospects can "think about it" forever. This is why.

Why Most Reps Skip This Step

It feels uncomfortable. Surfacing the cost of inaction feels like you're pressing on a bruise — and reps who haven't built the confidence to sit with that discomfort skip past it into the pitch instead.

That's a critical mistake. Without consequence, the prospect can walk away from the call feeling good about the conversation, mildly interested in the solution, and completely uncompelled to act. They'll "think about it" for two weeks and then ghost you.

The Psychology

Humans are loss averse. The pain of potential loss is roughly twice as motivating as the pleasure of equivalent gain. Step 5 built the pleasure side — the vivid, desirable future. Step 6 builds the loss side — what it actually costs to not move toward that future.

Without Step 6, you have a one-sided emotional equation. The prospect wants the future you described, but they don't feel the weight of what staying put costs them. That weight is what creates genuine urgency — not manufactured pressure tactics.

What Staying the Same Actually Costs

Most prospects have normalized their situation. They've been at the same income level for months or years. The pain is present but dull — they've adapted to it. Your job in Step 6 is to un-numb that pain by making it specific and forward-looking.

The question isn't "how bad is it right now?" The question is: "What does it cost if this is still your situation six months from now?"

Categories of Cost

Financial cost: every month at $5K instead of $15K is $10K they didn't earn. Over six months, that's $60K. That's not abstract — that's real.

Identity cost: what does it cost them to still be in the same place, still unable to point to the thing that made the difference? That feeling compounds.

Relationship cost: the pressure it puts on people they care about. The conversations they're avoiding. The things they've promised that they can't deliver.

Time cost: every month you delay learning this is a month you're on the wrong trajectory. Skill compounding works in both directions.

How to Surface Cost Without Being Manipulative

The Distinction

Manipulation is inventing a cost or exaggerating it to create fear. That's not what this step is. The cost is real — you're just asking the prospect to reckon with it honestly instead of glossing over it.

You're not creating the consequence. It's already there. You're asking a question that helps the prospect see it clearly for themselves. That's honest, and it's useful to them even if they don't buy.

The consequence question: "If nothing changes — if you're still in the same spot six months from now — what does that actually cost you? Not just financially, but across the board."

After they answer, don't move fast. Let the answer sit for a beat. Don't immediately transition to the pitch. The prospect just said something real. Give them a moment to hear themselves. That silence after the consequence answer is where urgency is born.

Call Exchange — Skipped vs. Completed Consequence

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Skips Consequence
Prospect
"Yeah, I want to be at $20K a month. I want to be out of survival mode."
Rep
"I love that. Let me tell you what we do and how we'd help you get there..."
No consequence. The prospect is mildly inspired by the future picture but hasn't reckoned with the cost of not pursuing it. The pitch will generate interest. The close will hit "I need to think about it" — because there's nothing pushing the decision.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Completes Consequence
Prospect
"Yeah, I want to be at $20K a month. I want to be out of survival mode."
Rep
"I want to ask you something before we get into anything on our side. If nothing changes — if you're still in the same spot six months from now — what does that cost you? Not just financially."
Prospect
"Honestly... it costs me my confidence. I'm already feeling like something's wrong with me. And my girlfriend is patient but I can tell she's worried. And every month I don't figure it out, I lose more of my belief that I actually can."
Rep
[pause] "So it's not just the money — it's who you become if this keeps going. That's real. How long are you willing to let that run?"
Now the prospect has named what's actually at stake. The conversation has weight. The pitch that follows will land because it's walking into a real, emotionally felt problem — not a casual interest. Close rate goes up significantly when this step is done right.

The Bridge Into Step 7

After the Consequence Answer

Once the prospect has named the cost, acknowledge it briefly — not with hollow empathy, but with honest recognition. Then bridge to the transition. The call is about to shift from understanding to presenting, and that shift needs to be earned, not assumed.

A simple bridge: "I appreciate you being that honest. Based on everything you've just told me — I actually think I can help with this. Can I tell you what we do and you can tell me if it fits?"
