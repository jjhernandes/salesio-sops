Coaching Moments

What was worked through

01
Closing
The closing sequence breakthrough
Chris was sitting at a 10% close rate in door-to-door. He optimized every other lever — built 4 avatar-specific opening scripts, pushed his show rate to 76%, understood qualification cold. But close rate didn't move.
After going through the closing sequence this week (money aside → what do you like → why → handle logistics → handle authority), he went back through every lost deal he could remember. The pattern was obvious: they weren't saying no. They were saying "not yet" — and he never helped them make a decision. He handled pre-close, never the close.
"They weren't saying they didn't want to buy. They were just saying now seems like a busy time. I never helped them make a decision — and that was my final job."
Chris Byrd
He also noted: from a pre-close standpoint, he had it. People loved him, trusted him, were ready to buy from him. The sequence — specifically working through the onion of circumstances, other people, and then the prospect's own decision — is the piece that was missing.
02
Interviews
Drew at Ryan Pineda — what went wrong
Chris had an interview with Drew (Ryan Pineda's team). He spent the call asking Drew about the offer structure — dream outcome, perceived likelihood of achievement, hidden costs. He was demonstrating business understanding.
The miss: He wasn't selling himself. He was analyzing their business without ever connecting his experience to what they need. Drew came back later: "Come back in 3 months when you have an offer. It's experience for us." Chris was confused — he thought showing depth of knowledge was the move.
After going through Jackson's job hiring module, Chris saw it: he never aligned his experience to their avatar. He was speaking business-owner to business-owner. He was showing he gets the offer. But he never said "here's why I'm the person you hire."
"I should have taken the time to align how I understood their avatar with my experience. I did not do that."
Chris Byrd
JJ's note: It's worth texting Drew back — "What did you mean by no experience? I want to make sure I understand." Push for clarity. It might not be what you think.
03
Loom + Positioning
How you frame your background determines your status
Chris asked why JJ said not to mention "outbound sales" in a general Loom. He had a chip on his shoulder about it — he felt it represented what he worked for, the grind, the dog mentality.
JJ played an example Loom (Nora Campbell): "I started my career making 100 cold calls a day in New York City selling high-value commercial real estate and PropTech deals. Over 6 years I focused on high-growth Series A and Series B startups…"
The point: She's doing outbound sales. But she's not saying "outbound sales." She's saying 100 cold calls a day, NYC, commercial real estate, PropTech, Series A, B. Every detail elevates her status. People don't even know what Series A means — but it sounds important and they assume she does.
"If you just say outbound sales, people put you in a box. They see it as replaceable. It's not you, it's the industry perception. Frame it with specifics that show you know what you're doing."
JJ Hernandez
The rule: General Loom → just say "sales," let them assume in the positive. Applying for a full-cycle or outbound-specific role → name it, but lead with specific impressive details, not the category. Serving background (over $1M in revenue, part-time) is also worth surfacing if framed right — it's proof of performance, not a lesser credential.
04
Interview Frame
Frame can't be in the conscious mind
Chris asked: "Should I run my sales process through the interview?" JJ's answer cut through the overthinking.
Don't premeditate it. The moment you're strategizing about how to show frame, you've already lost it. Frame has to be internalized, not performed. The interviewer has assumed authority because of their role — all you have to do is make them feel like you're on the same level. If you do it right, they start wondering why you're even applying.
"It's pretty damn simple to just show up as the authority. They can't make you feel like you're below. They should almost feel like you could be above them — and when you do too good of a job, that's when they ask, 'Why is this guy applying here?'"
JJ Hernandez
The frame: You understand what they're talking about. You get what they need. That's all it takes for them to hire you. Don't try to guide the call consciously — just show up as yourself, knowing what they're looking for.
05
Accountability
Chris asked for brutal honesty — JJ answered it directly
Chris asked JJ to always hold him to an unreasonable standard. He said the story where JJ got genuinely irritated — the moving company being late — showed him that JJ actually cares, and that's the kind of coach he wants.
JJ's response was a story: a student came to him after 8 weeks complaining he didn't "fully understand scriptless selling yet." JJ told him that's disrespectful — what took JJ years to learn, the student expected to have in 8 weeks. The student didn't like it. JJ didn't care.
"I don't tolerate anybody's bullshit. I just won't. If you give me a reason to call it out, I will. But I'm not going to look for things just to be like that."
JJ Hernandez
Chris gave JJ permission to hold him to that standard. JJ accepted it.
