Rapid Prospect Profiling

What to know about a prospect before minute five. Not their backstory — their state. Their decision-making style. Their dominant fear. Their deepest driver. These five things change how you pitch everything else.

The 5 Things You Need Before You Pitch Anything

01
Thing One
Their PACE Type
Are they Practical, Action, Social, or Emotional? This determines the register you pitch in. Everything else — how you frame outcomes, how you handle objections, what proof you lean on — shifts based on this. Get it wrong and a technically good pitch lands flat.
Listen for: pace of speech, what they focus on in the first answer, whether they're asking about data or feelings or community or outcomes.
02
Thing Two
Their Primary Driver
What is the thing underneath the stated want? Freedom? Significance? Security? Proving something? The driver is the real "why" — and it's where the emotional close lives. Without it, you're pitching a product. With it, you're pitching a transformation that matters to them specifically.
How to find it: "What would it actually mean to you to get there?" The answer reveals the value underneath.
03
Thing Three
Their Dominant Fear
Not the surface concern — the real fear. Fear of wasting money. Fear of trying again and failing. Fear of what their partner will say. Fear that they're actually not capable. Every major "I need to think about it" has a fear underneath it. Know the fear and you can address it before it becomes the obstacle.
How to find it: "What's the part of this you're most uncertain about?" or "If this didn't work out, what would that mean for you?"
04
Thing Four
Their Decision-Making Style
Do they decide fast and alone, or slow with consensus? Do they need to understand everything first, or do they trust their gut? A fast decision-maker will feel patronized if you over-explain. A slow one will feel pressured if you try to close before they're ready. The close style must match their decision style.
How to find it: listen for whether they're gathering data or checking a gut feeling. Whether they reference others' opinions. Whether they're already looking for permission or still building toward it.
05
Thing Five
Their Current State — Not Just Their Situation
Situation is the facts: current income, years in sales, offer type. State is the energy they're carrying into the call: frustrated, hopeful, burned out, skeptical, excited but guarded. State determines receptivity. A prospect who is burned out needs to be met differently than one who is excited but unsure. Know the state before you pitch.
How to find it: "How are you feeling about where things are right now?" — not the numbers, the feeling. Tone and energy tell you as much as words.

Gathering Them Without It Feeling Like an Intake Form

The wrong approach

Going through five structured questions in order — "What's your current income? What are your goals? What's been holding you back? What do you fear? How do you make decisions?" — sounds like a form being read aloud. Prospects check out. The rapport evaporates. You end up with data but no connection.

The right approach: ask one broad question and listen for all five signals in the answer. "What's been going on for you lately — like what made you want to get on a call?" A good answer tells you the state, hints at the driver, reveals the PACE type through delivery, and often surfaces the dominant fear in what they don't say.

Then follow the thread. One question, one follow-up, one more. Build the profile through conversation, not questionnaire.

The Difference Between Situation and State

Why state matters more

Two reps can have identical situations — same income, same close rate, same years of experience — and be in completely different states. One is hungry and ready to invest. One is burned out and needs to be convinced the effort is worth it. The pitch that closes the first one doesn't even get a hearing from the second.

State shapes what's possible in the conversation. Before you pitch to anyone's situation, you need to know their state. Because the state is the environment your pitch lands in.

Call Exchange — Building the Profile Through Conversation

Rep builds a real-time profile through a single conversation thread
Rep
"Before I tell you anything about the program — what's been going on for you lately? Like what made you decide to get on a call?"
Open question. Listening for PACE type (delivery), state (energy), primary driver (what they lead with), and dominant fear (what's missing or heavy).
Prospect
"Honestly I'm just tired. I've been grinding for eight months and my income hasn't moved. I know I'm capable of closing better but I can't figure out what's blocking me. I feel like I'm doing everything right."
Rep
"State: burned out, frustrated. Primary driver: proving capability. PACE: likely E or P based on 'I feel like.' Dominant fear: that the block is something unfixable about them. Decision style: unclear yet — need one more question." [internal] "What do you mean by 'everything right' — what have you tried so far?"
One follow-up. Not a checklist. Pulling the thread on the most important piece — what "everything right" means to them. This will reveal the gap and confirm or adjust the profile.
Prospect
"I've watched courses, studied call recordings, implemented scripts. I just don't close the way I know I should be able to."
Rep
"The courses and scripts targeted the wrong thing. You've been working on the technique layer when the real issue is the layer underneath it — identity and state. That's why nothing moved. You didn't fail. You worked hard at the wrong thing. That's fixable."
Profile complete enough to pitch. Tailored the diagnosis to their specific state (burned out, self-doubting). Addressed the dominant fear (that they're broken) immediately. Now the pitch has a foundation.
