Step 12 — The Close

They're committing to a decision they already made. Your job is not to push — it's to make it easy for them to land on what they already know.

The Reframe: You're Not Selling a Program

By the time you get to Step 12, a well-run call has one of two situations: the prospect has essentially already decided, or there's one remaining obstacle surfaced in Step 11. The close is not a magic technique you use to get someone over the line — it's the natural conclusion of a conversation where both people have reached understanding.

You are not closing them on a 12-week program. You are helping them commit to a decision about their own life that they've been working toward throughout this call. The program is just the vehicle.

"Connection is what people actually buy."

— JJ Hernandez

What the Close Actually Sounds Like in Scriptless Selling

No Magic Words

The close in this model does not have a secret phrase. What it has is a clear, calm, direct movement toward action — after objections have been addressed and the prospect has confirmed the solution fits. It's simple. It's direct. It doesn't have flair.

The Direct Close

"Based on everything we've talked through — are you ready to move forward?" That's it. No qualification, no escape hatch, no softening. Ask directly. Then hold silence. Let them answer.

The Assumptive Close

"Okay — so the next step is getting you set up. Do you want to do the full $7,500 or does one of the split-pay options work better for you?" This closes by moving to logistics rather than asking yes/no. Works well when the prospect has already said "this sounds right" but hasn't explicitly said yes. Don't use it to bypass real objections — use it when you're reading that the decision is already made and they're waiting for you to lead.

When to Close — The Signal, Not the Step Number

The Signal

You close when: all objections have been addressed, the prospect has confirmed the solution fits (Step 9), and you can feel the energy shift from evaluating to agreeing. That might happen at Step 12 of the framework. It might happen earlier. The framework tells you the minimum — not the maximum.

If you're on Step 12 and it doesn't feel like the conversation has resolved — don't close. Go back. Something in discovery, objection handling, or confirm fit is still open. Closing into unresolved doubt never works and usually damages the relationship.

Signs the Prospect Is Ready

They start asking operational questions ("how does onboarding work?" "when would I start?"). Their energy shifts — they go from evaluating your answers to leaning in. They re-reference something from discovery positively: "Yeah and you said the job board has weekly postings, that's actually really helpful." These are green lights. The decision is being made in real time.

Checklist — Close Readiness

  • ✓
    Prospect confirmed the solution fits (Step 9 completed)
  • ✓
    Price has been stated clearly (Step 10 completed)
  • ✓
    All stated objections have been addressed (Step 11 completed)
  • ✓
    Prospect energy has shifted — they're engaging, not evaluating
  • ✗
    Do not close if one active objection remains unaddressed — go back to Step 11
  • ✗
    Do not close if you feel uncertain or rushed — your energy transfers to them

Call Exchange — Forced vs. Natural Close

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Forces the Close
Prospect
"I mean, it sounds good but I'm still a little on the fence."
Rep
"I totally get it — but honestly I think you should just go for it. Worst case, there's a money-back guarantee. And honestly people who wait usually wish they started sooner. So what do you say — you want to lock it in today?"
Prospect said they're on the fence. Rep pushed past it. "Just go for it" and "people who wait" are pressure tactics that acknowledge the prospect's hesitation without resolving it. The prospect hasn't been helped — they've been nudged. This doesn't close well.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Closes Naturally
Prospect
"I mean, it sounds good but I'm still a little on the fence."
Rep
"What part of the fence? Like — is something still unresolved, or is it more the discomfort of making a real decision?"
Goes back to discovery instead of pushing forward. Distinguishes between a real concern and decision anxiety.
Prospect
"Honestly I think I'm just scared I'll invest and not follow through."
Rep
"That's the most honest thing you've said. Let me ask you something — is the fear that you can't, or that you might not? Because those are different things."
Prospect
"That I might not... I know I can."
Rep
"Then the decision's already made. You know what you need to do. How do you want to handle the payment?"
The close came from resolving the actual objection — self-trust — and then moving cleanly to logistics. The prospect led themselves to yes. The rep just held the space for it to happen.
