Lesson 6 · Section 4

Possibility Priming

Building belief that change is achievable for them specifically. The hidden objection that kills closes without ever showing up by name.

The Hidden Objection

Many prospects do not close because they do not believe the change is possible for them.

Not in theory. They understand the change is theoretically possible — they have seen testimonials, they have heard the success stories. What they do not believe is that it is possible for someone in their specific situation, with their specific background, their specific obstacles.

They will not tell you this. It would require more vulnerability than most people bring to a sales call. Instead they say "let me think about it." Or they ask about the price — not because the price is the real issue but because they need a logical container for a feeling they cannot name.

Possibility priming addresses this before it becomes an objection at the close. You bring belief into the conversation during discovery, through the right questions and the right social proof, so the prospect arrives at the pitch already believing change is possible for them.

If you wait until the close to deal with this, you are already behind.

Identity-Matched Social Proof

Generic Proof Does Not Work

Most reps use testimonials like wallpaper. "We've helped 2,100 reps." "Our students go from X to Y." These numbers are impressive but they are not personal. The prospect's internal response is "that's great for those people." It does not crack the belief that change might not be possible for them specifically.

Identity-Matched Proof Works

Identity-matched social proof puts a specific person in front of the prospect who shares their specific situation. Not just "a rep who was struggling" — but "a rep who was a year into sales, making $6K a month, had tried other training programs that did not work, and had a family depending on them." The closer the identity match, the more the prospect's internal resistance dissolves.

When the prospect hears about someone who was exactly like them and got to where they want to be, the belief barrier cracks. Not because you told them it was possible — because they saw someone like them do it.

You have to know your student stories at this level of specificity. Vague testimonials do not prime possibility. Specific, identity-detailed stories do. Build a mental library of your students organized by their starting situation, not just their result. When you meet a prospect who matches a specific student profile, deploy that story precisely.

The Possibility Question

After deploying identity-matched social proof, always follow with:

"What would it mean for you if that was actually achievable in your situation?"

This question does two things simultaneously. It invites the prospect to imagine the future state as real and achievable. And it requires them to articulate what it would mean — which activates the desire state at the same time as the possibility state.

Why the Wording Matters

"What would it mean for you if that was actually achievable" — the word "actually" is doing real work here. It acknowledges that the prospect might not fully believe it yet. It gives them permission to entertain the belief without committing to it. That permission is what allows the belief to start forming.

If you ask "isn't that great?" you get "yeah that's great." If you ask "what would it mean for you if that was actually achievable?" you get something real and personal and emotionally loaded.

Signals That Possibility Is Missing

  • ✗
    "Yeah but those people probably had an advantage I don't have." Classic possibility deflection. They are comparing themselves negatively to the success stories and concluding the results are not reproducible in their situation.
  • ✗
    "I've tried things before and they didn't work." This is past experience creating a belief ceiling. The prospect has collapsed the possibility of future success based on past failure. They need to see that their past results were circumstantial, not predictive.
  • ✗
    "That sounds good but I'm not sure it's for someone at my level." Direct statement of possibility absence. They are telling you they do not believe it applies to them.
  • ✗
    Flat affect when hearing results. They acknowledge the results without any emotional response. No excitement, no surprise, no curiosity. This means the results are not landing as personally relevant. Possibility is not active.
How to Respond to Each Signal

When you hear any of these signals during discovery, stop and prime. Do not wait to address it at the close. Deploy an identity-matched story immediately, then ask the possibility question. Address the specific belief barrier directly: "The person I'm thinking of actually had the same concern — here's what was different about the approach."

Call Exchange — Possibility Priming in Action

Rep Identifying and Addressing a Missing Possibility State
Prospect
"I've heard about programs like this before. I just always wonder — are the results real? Or is it like, the people who are already good at sales who get results?"
Possibility state is missing. The prospect is questioning whether results are achievable for someone who is not already strong.
Rep
"That's actually the most important question to ask. Let me tell you about a specific situation. We had a guy — Marcus — who came in at 18 months in sales, was making about $5K a month, had already tried two other programs that were basically scripted approaches that didn't fit how he thinks. He did not come in as someone who was already good. He came in stuck and frustrated. By month two he was at $22K. Not because he had some hidden advantage — because the approach we use is built for people who are stuck, not people who are already winning."
Identity-matched story with high specificity. Starting situation matches the prospect's situation. Not a generic success story.
Rep
"What would it mean for you if that kind of result was actually achievable in your situation?"
The possibility question. Asked immediately after the story. Inviting the prospect to step into the belief.
Prospect
"If that's real? That changes everything. That's exactly the kind of thing I've been looking for. I just didn't know if it was actually possible for someone starting where I'm at."
Possibility state is now active. The prospect has stepped into the belief. The pitch lands on a completely different mind than it did three exchanges ago.
