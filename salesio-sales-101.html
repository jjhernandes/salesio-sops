What Is Sales, Really?

Forget whatever image you have of a sales person. The slick talker, the pushy guy on the phone, the person who won't take no for an answer. That's not what this is, and it's not what works.

Sales is human. It's a conversation where someone has a problem, you might have something that solves it, and your job is to figure out honestly whether that's true, then help them make a clear decision. That's it. Nobody's tricking anybody. The best closers don't sound like closers. They sound like someone who actually cares whether this is right for you.

People buy based on emotions and compensate logically. Someone doesn't sign up for a coaching program because of a spreadsheet. They sign up because something in their life isn't working and they can feel it, and then they use logic to justify the decision after the fact. Your job in a sales call is to understand that feeling first. Everything else comes after.

A Call, Step by Step

Every sales call, no matter what's being sold, moves through roughly the same shape. Here's the plain-English version. The full framework (Section 3 in the Training Bible) breaks this into twelve steps. This is just the map before you get the detail.

1
The Intro
The first couple minutes. You're setting the tone, building a little rapport, and telling them how the call is going to go.
2
Discovery
This is the one everyone asks about. Discovery just means asking real questions to understand what's actually going on with this person before you say a word about what you're selling. What do they want? What's stopping them? Why does it matter to them? You're not pitching yet. You're listening.
3
The Pitch
Now, and only now, you present the offer, built around exactly what they told you in discovery. A good pitch feels like it was written for that one person, because it was.
4
Objections
They hesitate. "I need to think about it." "It's a lot of money." "Let me talk to my partner." These aren't rejections, they're usually just the real concern showing up late. You work through it together.
5
The Close
You ask for the decision. Directly. Not hinting, not hoping they bring it up first. A "yes" and a signature, or an honest "no." Either one is a clean close.

The Glossary

Words you're going to hear constantly starting Week 1. Bookmark this section.

Lead
Someone who might be a fit for what you're selling, but hasn't talked to anyone yet. Just a name and some contact info at this stage.
Prospect
A lead who's now actually on a call with you. The moment they pick up, they upgrade from "lead" to "prospect."
Setter
The person whose job is booking the call: reaching out, qualifying interest, and getting it on the calendar for a closer.
Closer
The person who runs the actual sales call and gets the prospect to a real decision. This is usually the role people mean when they say "sales rep" in a high-ticket setting.
Show / No-Show
Did the prospect actually show up to the call they booked? A lot of booked calls never show. Tracking this is how you know if your setting process is healthy.
Qualifying
Figuring out, usually early in discovery, whether this person is actually a real fit: do they have the budget, the need, and the timing to move forward. Not everyone who shows up is qualified.
Discovery
The part of the call where you ask questions to genuinely understand the prospect before you pitch anything. Covered in full in Section 4 of the Training Bible.
The Pitch
Presenting the offer. What it is, what it costs, why it fits what they told you in discovery. Covered in Section 5.
Objection
A reason someone gives for not moving forward right now: price, timing, needing to check with someone else. Almost never the real, full reason. Covered in Section 6.
Frame
Who's actually in control of the conversation and its energy. Holding your frame means staying grounded and certain even when a prospect pushes back or gets tense. Covered in Section 9. You'll hear "hold your frame" a lot.
Close / Closing
Directly asking for the decision and getting a real answer, yes or no. "Closing" the deal means they said yes and it's done.
Won
The deal closed. They said yes and paid (or committed to a payment plan).
Follow-Up
Reaching back out to a prospect after a call that didn't close on the spot. A huge number of real closes happen on the follow-up, not the first call.
The Offer
Whatever you're actually selling: the specific product, program, or deal, at a specific price.
Cash Collected (CC)
Money that's actually landed in the account today. Different from the total deal size if someone's on a payment plan.
Revenue Generated
The full value of a deal, even if it's being paid off over time rather than all at once.
Show Rate
The percentage of booked calls where the prospect actually shows up.
Close Rate
The percentage of calls you actually take that turn into a sale. This is the number you'll track constantly, including in your own Call Tracker.

Applying for Your First Job: The Loom

Most companies on the job platform want a short Loom video with your application, not just a resume. If you've never done one, that's normal — here's exactly what a good one looks like, even if you have zero real calls under your belt yet.

🎥
Why a Loom instead of a resumeA hiring manager can't hear tone, energy, or how you carry yourself from a PDF. In a role where your voice and presence are the job, a 60-90 second video tells them more in one take than a resume tells them in ten minutes of reading.
1
Open with who you are and what you want — 10 seconds
Name, and the role you're applying for. Say it like you already believe you're a fit. "Hey, I'm [name], applying for the [role] position." No throat-clearing, no "um so basically." Start.
2
What you've actually done — 20-30 seconds
If you've closed deals, lead with the number. If you haven't yet, don't apologize for it — frame the training itself as the proof. "I'm currently in the Sales.io Mastermind, training in discovery, objection handling, and call psychology. I've run mock calls every week and my close rate on those has been climbing." That's a real, specific, honest sentence — not a script, not a lie.
3
Why you specifically — 15-20 seconds
Don't say "I'm a hard worker" — everyone says that. Say something true and specific about how you actually operate. "I'm coachable — when something's not working I fix it fast instead of getting defensive about it." One real trait beats three generic ones.
4
Close it like a call — 10 seconds
Direct ask, no hedging. "I'd love the chance to get on a call and show you what I can do." Then stop talking. The same rule from Section 3 applies here: after you ask, be quiet.
Framing things in your benefit
No experience isn't a weakness — vague experience is
"I don't have experience" kills applications. "I'm in active training and I track my own numbers every week" doesn't. Same person, same stage — one sounds unfinished, the other sounds like someone already building a habit of accountability.
Talk about reps, not just results
If you don't have a big number to lead with yet, lead with volume and consistency instead: how many mock calls you've done, what you've changed based on coaching feedback. That's a real signal to a hiring manager — it shows you improve.
Common mistakes
✕
Reading off a scriptIt's audible, and it undercuts the entire point of a video application in a communication-based role. Know your 4 beats, then talk — don't recite.
✕
Going over 90 secondsHiring managers are watching a stack of these. Long doesn't read as thorough, it reads as unaware of the assignment.
✕
Apologizing for being new"Sorry, I don't have much experience yet" is the one line that will cost you the callback. Say what you do have — training, reps, coachability — and stop there.
