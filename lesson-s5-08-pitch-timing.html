Pitch Timing

The pitch ends when they start asking "how do I get started?" — not when you've covered everything. Knowing when to stop is the skill most reps never develop.

Shorter Closes More

The data on pitch length is consistent and counterintuitive: shorter pitches close at higher rates. Not because prospects need less information, but because the right information in the right amount creates clarity, and clarity creates decisions.

Longer pitches introduce more variables. More variables create more potential friction points. More friction points create more hesitation. The rep who pitches 10 minutes isn't being thorough — they're inviting objections they didn't need to handle.

The Target Range

For a well-run discovery that surfaces the 3-4 key points, the pitch should run 3-7 minutes maximum. This includes the Mirror Recap, the feature-to-pain connections, check-ins, and any social proof deployed. If you're consistently going past 7 minutes, you're covering things the prospect didn't ask for.

Elite reps regularly close in under 5 minutes of pitch time — not because they rushed, but because they were so precise in discovery that the pitch was almost obvious.

The Rule

When the Pitch Ends

The pitch ends when the prospect starts asking forward-looking questions — "What does enrollment look like?" "How does it work?" "What happens after I sign up?" Those questions mean the pitch has done its job. The prospect has mentally moved to the next step.

At that moment, stop pitching. If you add another feature, you introduce a new variable at the exact moment they were about to make a decision. You're extending the pitch past the close.

The biggest timing error in pitching is not going long — it's not recognizing the signal to stop. Reps run their entire pitch plan even when the prospect has already sold themselves. The prospect who asked "what does enrollment look like?" at minute four is ready. Going to minute eight means you talked them out of a decision they'd already made.

Covering Everything Is Not the Goal

A lot of reps feel like they owe the prospect a complete walkthrough of the offer. They don't. The prospect is not buying a complete walkthrough — they're buying a solution to their specific problem. When that solution is clear to them, the pitch is done. Covering everything else is satisfying for the rep, not for the prospect.

Signs You've Gone Too Long

These are the real-time signals that your pitch has crossed from persuasive to exhausting.

  • ✗
    The prospect goes quiet. Early in the pitch, a quiet prospect is thinking. Late in the pitch, a quiet prospect has checked out. If you've been talking for five minutes and you're getting minimal response, you've gone past the window where they were actively engaged.
  • ✗
    One-word responses. "Yep." "Sure." "Okay." These are not agreement — they're endurance. The prospect is waiting for you to stop. They were with you for the first three points. The subsequent points are things they're weathering, not receiving.
  • ✗
    "That sounds good" with no energy. Flat delivery of a positive phrase is a warning sign. Real enthusiasm sounds different — there's forward lean in the voice, specificity, follow-up. A flat "that sounds good" is a social grace, not a buying signal.
  • ✗
    They redirect to logistics before the pitch is done. "How long does this take?" "What are the hours?" "Is this all on Zoom?" These questions during a pitch — not after — signal that they've mentally exited the decision-making mode and are now trying to find reasons not to commit.
  • ✗
    You're repeating yourself. If you notice you're re-emphasizing a point you already made, the pitch has run out of new content and you're filling time. Stop. Ask a check-in question and see where the prospect actually is.

How to Recover When You've Pitched Too Long

Stop. Don't Finish.

The instinct when you realize you've gone too long is to wrap up the remaining points quickly — get through it and then transition. Don't. Every additional point after the prospect has checked out makes recovery harder. Stop mid-pitch if needed.

Situation Recovery Move
Prospect has gone quiet for 30+ seconds while you're pitching Stop. "Let me pause here — how are we doing? Is this landing in a way that feels relevant to what you're dealing with?"
Getting one-word "yep / okay / sure" responses for the last two minutes Stop pitching entirely. "Before I keep going — which of the pieces we've covered so far feels most relevant to your situation?" Re-engage them actively.
Prospect says "that sounds good" flatly mid-pitch Take the opening: "Good — that's the most important piece. Let me ask — based on what we've covered, where are you at?" Move toward the close rather than continuing the pitch.
You catch yourself repeating a point you already made Acknowledge it internally, stop, and check in: "How does all of this land for you overall?" Transition to dialogue, not more monologue.

Timing Benchmarks by Call Type

Well-Run Discovery (30-40 min)

If discovery went deep and all four states are active, the pitch should run 3-5 minutes. The Mirror Recap takes 60 seconds. Each feature-to-pain point takes 30-60 seconds with a check-in. Three points, one social proof story if needed, clean transition to close. Under 7 minutes total is achievable and powerful.

Shorter Discovery (Under 20 min)

If discovery was cut short or the prospect was brief in their answers, the pitch will feel thin. Resist the urge to fill the time with features. Instead, use early pitch check-ins to surface missing information: "Does that feel like the part that's most relevant to your situation?" Their response will tell you what's actually missing. Discovery can happen inside the pitch — but only through check-ins, not through covering more features.

The "thorough rep" who pitches for 12 minutes rarely outcloses the "precise rep" who pitches for 4. If you're consistently running long pitches, review your discovery quality first. Long pitches are almost always a symptom of vague discovery — the rep doesn't know which 3-4 things matter, so they cover everything and hope. Fix the discovery. The pitch timing fixes itself.

Call Exchange — Pitching Past the Close

VERSION 1 — Rep Pitches Past the Signal
Rep
"...and the 1-on-1 coaching is where you'll really see the close rate change happen, because we're going directly into your specific call patterns. Does that make sense?"
Prospect
"Yeah — what does enrollment actually look like?"
Forward-looking question. The close signal. The pitch is done.
Rep
"Great — and actually before I get into that, let me also touch on the group calls because I think that's another really important piece. You get seven group calls a week covering tonality, setting, psychology, B2B strategy, and then call review on Fridays which is where reps submit their calls for live feedback..."
Rep missed the signal. Keeps pitching. The prospect asked to move forward and the rep pulled them back into the pitch. This is how a ready buyer becomes a "let me think about it."
Prospect
"Yeah okay, that all sounds great. I'd probably want to look everything over and maybe chat with my wife."
They were ready. The extended pitch gave them time to cool and find reasons to pause. "Talk to my wife" wasn't a concern two minutes ago.
VERSION 2 — Rep Reads the Signal and Stops
Rep
"...and the 1-on-1 coaching is where that ceiling gets broken — specific to your calls, not generic. Does that feel like the missing piece?"
Prospect
"Yeah — what does enrollment actually look like?"
Close signal. Rep recognizes it.
Rep
"Simple. We get you started today, you get immediate access to the platform and your first 1-on-1 is scheduled within 48 hours. Investment is $7,500 — there are also split pay options if that works better. What works for you?"
Pitch stopped. Clean transition to enrollment. The prospect asked to buy — the rep let them. No more features, no more stories, no more time for the prospect to cool down.
Prospect
"What does the split pay look like?"
Logistics question about payment. They're buying. The close is in progress.
