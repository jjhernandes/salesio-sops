Peer Review Sessions

How to give feedback that actually changes behavior — and how to receive it without getting defensive. Done right, peer review is one of the fastest growth tools in the program.

The Rules of Useful Feedback

Most peer feedback is useless. Not because people don't care — because they don't know how to give it. Vague, personal, or opinion-based feedback produces nothing. Specific, behavioral, observable feedback produces change.

Every piece of feedback in a peer review session must pass three tests: specific, behavioral, non-personal. If it fails any one of those tests, it doesn't get said.

Specific

Not "your discovery felt weak." That's vague. "At the 7-minute mark, after they said their main challenge was inconsistency, you moved on to the next question without going deeper. That was the moment to ask 'what does that inconsistency actually cost you?'" That's specific. Specific feedback can be acted on. Vague feedback gets nodded at and forgotten.

Behavioral

Describe what happened, not what it means about the person. "You sped up your pace after the price objection" is behavioral. "You seemed nervous" is interpretive. Behavioral feedback points to a thing the rep can change. Interpretive feedback makes them self-conscious about a personality trait.

Non-Personal

The feedback is about the call, not the rep. Not "you're too agreeable" — that's a character judgment. "On this call, when they said they'd done other programs, you validated their concern before understanding it. That gave the objection more weight than it deserved." That's about a behavior on a specific call. That can be changed.

What Useful Feedback Sounds Like vs. What Doesn't

Not Useful Useful
"Your energy was kind of low on this one." "Between minutes 4 and 9, your pace was slower than your opening. The prospect's energy mirrored yours and the engagement dropped."
"I think you need to work on building more rapport." "You transitioned to pitch at minute 11. They hadn't told you their biggest pain point yet. The pitch wasn't connected to anything they said."
"You gave in too easily." "When they said 'I need to think about it,' you said 'totally understand' within 1 second and started scheduling a follow-up. You didn't ask what they were weighing."
"You were pretty good at objection handling I thought." "On the price objection at minute 22, you used three questions to understand what was behind it before offering any response. That's a Score 3 on objection handling."

The Format — Play, Pause, One Observation

How a Peer Review Session Runs

The rep plays a segment of their call — 3 to 5 minutes. When the segment ends, the reviewer gives exactly one specific, behavioral, non-personal observation. Not a list. One thing. The rep notes it, responds briefly, and then the session moves to the next segment or the next call.

One observation per segment is not a limitation — it's the discipline. When reviewers try to give five observations, most of them land as noise. One thing, clearly stated, applied to the next call. That's the output.

01
Step
Rep chooses the segment
The rep picks the 3-5 minute segment they want reviewed. Not the best part of their call — the part they're uncertain about, or the specific moment they want outside eyes on. "I want to hear what you noticed between minutes 8 and 13 — that's where I think I lost it."
02
Step
Both parties listen together
Reviewer and rep listen to the segment at the same time. Reviewer takes timestamped notes. Rep listens without defending anything in their head — just observing alongside the reviewer.
03
Step
Reviewer gives one observation
Specific, behavioral, non-personal. Timestamp included. One thing. The rep does not respond with "yeah but" or "I know, but the prospect was..." — they write it down. Questions for clarification are allowed. Defense is not.
04
Step
Rep states what they'll change
After receiving the observation, the rep states — out loud — the specific thing they're changing on the next call. Not "I'll keep that in mind." The thing. "Next call: when they give me the first answer on why they're here, I'm going to ask one more layer before moving on." Said out loud in front of the reviewer. That's the commitment.
Example Peer Review — One Observation Given and Received
Reviewer
"At minute 9:22 — right after they said they'd been in sales for two years and still weren't consistent — you said 'okay, got it' and moved to the next question. That was the moment to go deeper. What did 'not consistent' mean for them specifically? You had the pain right there and you moved past it."
Specific. Behavioral. Timestamp included. One thing.
Rep
"Yeah — I noticed that too when I reviewed it. I felt them open up there and I rushed past it. Next call: when they give me a pain word like 'inconsistent' or 'stuck,' I'm asking what that actually looks like in their day before I move on."
Received it. Didn't defend. Committed to a specific change out loud.

Receiving Feedback — Why It's Harder Than Giving It

Receiving feedback is harder than giving it because it requires you to hold two things simultaneously: staying open and not agreeing with everything. The defensive rep rejects all feedback. The overly agreeable rep says yes to everything, changes nothing, and repeats the same patterns.

The productive middle: receive it fully, without dismissing it, and then decide what's true after the session — not during it.

The No-Defense Rule

During the review, you do not defend your choices. Not "I did that because the prospect was about to hang up." Not "I know, but in that moment..." You listen. You write it down. You ask for clarification if needed. Defense comes after the session, in your own review, when you decide if the feedback is accurate. During the session: receive it.

  • ✓
    Write every piece of feedback down. Even if you don't agree with it. Writing forces you to receive it rather than dismiss it.
  • ✓
    Ask for specifics if the feedback is vague. "Can you point me to the exact timestamp?" This trains your reviewers to give better feedback and helps you understand it more precisely.
  • ✗
    Don't explain why you did it. Context-explaining is defense with a softer label. It signals that you're protecting your ego rather than extracting the insight.
  • ✗
    Don't evaluate feedback in real time. "I'm not sure I agree with that" during the session shuts the reviewer down and blocks your own learning. Evaluate it after. During the session: take it in.

Session Structure

Recommended Format

45-60 minutes per session. Two reps. Each rep brings one call. Each call gets 20-25 minutes. Within that time: 2-3 segments, one observation per segment. The last 5 minutes of each call's review is the rep stating their committed change out loud.

Choosing Segments

Don't pick the best moments to show off. Pick the moments you're uncertain about — the ones where you felt something go sideways, where you made a choice you're not sure about, where you lost momentum and want to understand why. The goal is not to impress your peer. The goal is to extract an insight.

Frequency matters. Peer review works when it's regular. One session every two weeks minimum. Ideally weekly. The rep who does peer review monthly is getting a fraction of the benefit of the rep who does it weekly. Calendar it. Protect the time.
