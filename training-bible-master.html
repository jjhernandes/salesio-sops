Identity & Inner Game

The best closers don't sound like closers. ↗ 04 The Internal Narrator There are two conversations on every call. The one you're having with the prospect — and the one you're having with yourself. Only one of them is helpful. ↗ 05 Archetype Activation Copying the top closer on your team is the fastest way to become a mediocre version of them. Find your archetype — it's more powerful than any script. ↗ 06 Behavioral Magnets What you radiate is not what you intend to radiate. The gap between those two things is exactly where most reps lose deals. ↗ 07 Composure Masterclass Staying grounded under pressure is not a personality trait. It's a skill. And it's the one that separates closers from salespeople. ↗ 08 Confidence vs. Certainty Confidence is something you perform. Certainty is something you are. Only one of them transfers to the prospect. ↗ 09 Frames & the Ego Your frame doesn't break because you used the wrong words. It breaks because of what you believe about yourself. ↗ 10 Limiting Beliefs & The Governors Something invisible is capping your income. It's not your close rate. It's not your offer. It's a belief running below conscious thought. ↗ 11 The Emotional Assessment Scale You can't change where you're operating from until you know where you are. Most reps think they're operating higher than they actually are. ↗ 12 Self-Brainwashing Passive programming has been running your identity your whole life. They're the internal architecture that every technique, every framework, every close is built on top of. Section 02
PACE — Human Profiling
People don't buy the same way. PACE gives you a map for how each personality type processes decisions and what they need to move.
01 PACE Personality System Scripts fail because they assume every prospect is the same person. They're not. PACE gives you a framework for reading who's in front of you and adapting in real time. ↗ 02 Identifying PACE Type in the First 5 Minutes You don't need 20 minutes of rapport-building to know who you're talking to. The signals are there from the first words. Your job is to listen for them. ↗ 03 Practical (P) Type Closing the analytical, skeptical prospect. The P type doesn't need to feel good about the decision. They need to know it's the right decision. Those are different things. ↗ 04 Action (A) Type Closing the competitive, impatient prospect. The A type doesn't want to be understood. They want to win. Get to the outcome fast or get out of the way. ↗ 05 Social (S) Type Closing the community-driven, family-first prospect. The S type doesn't buy for themselves. They buy for the people around them — and if the decision feels like it's being taken away from those people, you've lost them. ↗ 06 Emotional (E) Type Closing the feeling-led, story-driven prospect. The E type decides with their body before their brain. If your pitch doesn't create a felt experience of the future, no amount of data will close them. ↗ 07 The Decision Map How people actually decide to buy — not the version sales training pretends. The real architecture of a buying decision is emotional first, always. Logic comes after to justify what the body already chose. ↗ 08 Needs, Values, and Decisions People don't buy what they need. They buy what they value. These are different things — and confusing them is one of the most common reasons a technically good pitch doesn't close. ↗ 09 The Three Identity Types Every prospect is navigating three versions of themselves on a call. The rep who understands which one is driving the conversation can pitch to the deepest possible level of motivation. ↗ 10 Secondary Needs & Neurochemicals What drives behavior below the surface isn't logic — it's chemistry. The four neurochemicals that govern a prospect's decision-making are running on every call. Most reps don't know it. Elite reps work with it. ↗ 11 Elicitation Seeing behind the mask. Most prospects present a surface version of their situation. Your job is to draw out what's actually real — without making them feel interrogated or exposed. ↗ 12 Listening Between the Lines What isn't being said is almost always more important than what is. The gap between a prospect's words and their meaning is where the real close happens — if you know how to hear it. ↗ 13 Rapid Prospect Profiling What to know about a prospect before minute five. Not their backstory — their state. Their decision-making style. Their dominant fear. Their deepest driver. These five things change how you pitch everything else. ↗ 14 Pre-Call Profiling Most reps skim the questionnaire. Elite reps read it like a novel. The emotional signals are there before the call even starts — word choice, emphasis, what they avoided saying. The rep who reads these walks into the first minute with a hypothesis. Everyone else walks in cold. ↗
Section 03
The 12-Step Call Framework
The call has a structure. Every step exists for a reason — from the open to the close, and why each phase sets up the next.
01 The 12-Step Framework A framework is the floor, not the ceiling. It's what great reps build on — not what they hide behind. Understand why it exists before you execute it. ↗ 02 Step 1 — Intro The first 60 seconds set the entire tone of the call. Most reps spend those seconds performing. That's the trap. ↗ 03 Step 2 — Current Reality Surface to depth inquiry. Most reps accept the first answer. Elite reps know the first answer is never the real one. ↗ 04 Step 3 — Problem Awareness Finding what's actually in the way. Not the category of problem — the specific thing. There's a difference, and it determines whether your pitch lands. ↗ 05 Step 4 — Solution Awareness "What would need to be true?" Get the prospect to define their own solution criteria before you ever open your mouth about what you offer. ↗ 06 Step 5 — Future Pace The specific life they actually want. "More money" is not a future. A vivid, emotional, concrete picture of what better looks like — that's what closes deals. ↗ 07 Step 6 — Consequence The cost of staying the same. Most reps skip this step and wonder why prospects can "think about it" forever. This is why. ↗ 08 Step 7 — Transition to Pitch Earn the right before you present. The shift from discovery to pitch is the most delicate moment on the call — and most reps fumble it. ↗ 09 Step 8 — The Pitch Connect every feature to what they said. Nothing goes in the pitch that wasn't in their discovery. Nothing. ↗ 10 Step 9 — Confirm Fit The verbal commitment before price. You don't reveal the number until they've told you it sounds like what they need. Skipping this creates objections that don't have to exist. ↗ 11 Step 10 — Price Reveal Say it once. Hold the silence. Everything you do after stating the price — before the prospect speaks — makes it worse. ↗ 12 Step 11 — Objection Handling Objections are questions, not rejections. The rep who argues with an objection always loses — because they just turned a question into a debate. ↗ 13 Step 12 — The Close They're committing to a decision they already made. Your job is not to push — it's to make it easy for them to land on what they already know. ↗ 14 The Full Call Architecture Timing, flow, and talk ratios. When you know how time should be distributed on a well-run call, you can diagnose exactly where your calls are going wrong. ↗ 15 Call Mapping Exercise Pull a real call from this week. Map it against the 12 steps. Be honest. This is the fastest way to find exactly where your calls break down. ↗
Section 04
Discovery Mastery
Discovery is where deals are won or lost. How to ask questions that create self-awareness in the prospect, not just collect information.
01 Why Discovery Is Where the Sale Is Won or Lost 80% of sales are won or lost in discovery. Not the pitch. Not the close. What you do in the first half of the call determines everything that follows. ↗ 02 The Pain Ladder Surface situation to emotional core. Each level is one question deeper than the last. Most reps stop at level two. Closes happen at level four. ↗ 03 The Four States What must be active before you pitch. Pitching before all four are present creates resistance. Your job in discovery is to activate each one. ↗ 04 Dissatisfaction Priming Making the gap feel real. Dissatisfaction is not about making someone feel bad. It is about making the distance between where they are and where they want to be impossible to ignore. ↗ 05 Desire Activation The Future Self Projection Technique. Getting the prospect to speak AS their future self — not about it. The difference between those two things is everything. ↗ 06 Possibility Priming Building belief that change is achievable for them specifically. The hidden objection that kills closes without ever showing up by name. ↗ 07 Attention Engineering What you make vivid gets bought. The brain does not purchase what it hears — it purchases what it pictures. Specificity is the tool. ↗ 08 The Reflect-Before-You-Ask Protocol The habit most reps do not have. Pause before the next question. Reflect what you heard. Then ask. That sequence changes everything about how the prospect experiences the conversation. ↗ 09 Intentional Silence The most underused tool in sales. Silence is not dead air. It is pressure on the prospect to go deeper. Most reps fill it because they are uncomfortable. That comfort costs them the close. ↗ 10 "Tell Me More About That" Going deeper without interrogating. Four words that open every door. The most versatile tool in discovery and the one most underused by people who think they already know how to ask questions. ↗ 11 Cognitive Dissonance in Discovery Widening the identity-reality gap. The discomfort of holding two conflicting beliefs simultaneously is one of the most powerful buying motivators. Your job is to surface it, not create it. ↗ 12 Discovery Mistakes What kills the close before the pitch starts. These are not pitch problems or close problems. They are discovery problems that show up as close problems. Know which ones you are making. ↗ 13 The Early Objection Surface Finding core resistance in discovery. Objections that show up at the close were almost always visible in discovery. The rep who learns to see and address them early never gets blindsided at the close. ↗ 14 Active Listening Repeat their life better than they told it. That's the skill. When you can do that, prospects feel more understood in 30 minutes with you than they have with most people in years. ↗
Section 05
The Pitch
The pitch is not a feature dump. How to present based on what the prospect told you — TikTok Pitch, Heaven and Hell, making the offer feel inevitable.
01 The TikTok Pitch Only hit the 3-4 things that matter to them. Everything else is noise. The pitch should feel like a mirror, not a menu. ↗ 02 The Heaven and Hell Framework Hell to Heaven. Gap to Bridge. The most persuasive structure in sales is the emotional narrative the prospect already carries. You just have to use it. ↗ 03 Do Not Feature Dump Every feature you mention that doesn't connect to something they said is a gap in relevance. Enough gaps and you've lost them — even if the offer is perfect for them. ↗ 04 Pitch Check-Ins Most reps pitch in a monologue. The prospect drifts at minute two and never comes back. Check-ins are how you keep them in the conversation instead of just listening to it. ↗ 05 The Mirror Recap Before you present a single feature, reflect their life back at them. The pitch should feel like a mirror, not a menu. This is what makes the mirror literal. ↗ 06 Social Proof Architecture Generic testimonials don't close deals. Identity-matched proof does. The prospect needs to see themselves in the story, not just the result. ↗ 07 The Scriptless Pitch Scriptless doesn't mean unprepared. It means internalized. The pitch comes from what they said — which is exactly why it can't be written in advance. ↗ 08 Pitch Timing The pitch ends when they start asking "how do I get started?" — not when you've covered everything. Knowing when to stop is the skill most reps never develop. ↗ 09 When to Use Screen Share Screen share is for proof, not for pitching. Use it to punctuate the conversation with something they can see. Don't let it replace the conversation itself. ↗ 10 The Inevitable Pitch By the time you present, the prospect already knows what you're going to say and why it makes sense. That's not luck. That's what a complete discovery looks like. ↗
Section 06
Objection Handling
Objections are signals about where the prospect's identity got stuck. The real structure of every objection and how to handle each one.
01 The Meta-Rule — Why You Get Objections and How to Stop Getting Them Most objection handling training teaches you how to recover from objections. This lesson teaches you why they form in the first place — and how great reps prevent most of them before they exist. ↗ 02 The Emotional Contract — Diagnosing Before Price Price objections almost never happen because of price. They happen because the prospect hasn't internally agreed that this is right for them yet. The emotional contract is what changes that. ↗ 03 The Four Identity Barriers — What Every Objection Is Really About Every objection a prospect raises traces back to how they see themselves. Not the offer. Not the price. Them. Understanding identity is how you stop arguing with objections and start dissolving them. ↗ 04 "I Need to Think About It" — The Specificity Challenge "I need to think about it" is the most common stall in sales — and the most mishandled. Most reps either concede immediately or push back too hard. Neither works. Specificity is the move. ↗ 05 "I Don't Have the Money" — What's Actually Being Said Money objections are almost never about money. They are signals about certainty or priority dressed in financial language. The rep who understands this distinction closes deals others walk away from. ↗ 06 "I Need to Talk to My Spouse" — Permission vs. Decision This objection lives in two completely different worlds. In one, it's legitimate — the spouse has a real stake. In the other, it's an exit strategy dressed as a relationship obligation. You have to know which one you're in. ↗ 07 "Is This Worth It?" — The Three Doors Framework When a prospect asks whether this is worth it, they're actually asking one of three completely different questions. The rep who gives the same answer every time is wrong twice out of three. ↗ 08 "I've Tried Things Like This Before" — Historical Identity Objection This objection sounds like product skepticism. It's actually about the prospect's history with themselves — and that's a completely different problem to solve. ↗ 09 "What If It Doesn't Work?" — The Fear of Failure Objection The prospect who asks "what if it doesn't work?" is not being pessimistic. They've already invested emotionally — they want this to work. The fear is of disappointment, not of failure itself. ↗ 10 Energy Shift Detection — "I Feel Like the Energy Shifted. Am I Wrong?" The most dangerous thing that happens on a call is invisible — the moment the prospect disconnects emotionally. Most reps miss it entirely. Elite reps catch it in real time and address it directly. ↗ 11 The Cost Reframe — "Is It Truly Impossible, or Not Fully Confident Yet?" Most money objections are certainty problems wearing financial language. The cost reframe separates the two — and it does it in a single question that changes everything. ↗ 12 Pattern Interrupting Stalls Before They Happen Most reps wait for stalls to arrive, then try to recover. Elite reps see them coming and change the direction before the stall has time to form. ↗ 13 Handling Buyer's Remorse The sale doesn't end when they say yes. The 24-48 hours after the close is when doubt peaks. What you do at the moment of close determines whether the commitment holds. ↗
Section 07
Sales Psychology
How people actually make decisions — anchoring, cognitive dissonance, loss aversion, social proof at the identity level.
01 How Buying Decisions Actually Happen System 1 vs. System 2. Understanding which brain makes the call — and which one writes the justification after the fact — changes everything about how you sell. ↗ 02 The Halo Effect Your first 90 seconds determine the lens through which the prospect experiences everything else on the call. Get the opening right and the whole call is easier. Get it wrong and you're fighting uphill the entire time. ↗ 03 Anchoring The first number stated in any negotiation becomes the reference point for everything that follows. Whoever sets the anchor owns the frame. Don't let that be the prospect. ↗ 04 Pre-Suasion Influence doesn't begin when you pitch. It begins the moment the prospect's attention is directed — before you've said a word about your offer. What they're thinking about when they first hear you determines how they hear you. ↗ 05 Priming Questions Questions don't just gather information. They activate emotional states. The right question sequence gets the prospect into the buying state before you've said anything about your offer. ↗ 06 Cognitive Dissonance The identity-reality gap is the most powerful buying driver in existence. When what someone believes about themselves doesn't match what their results show, the discomfort is unbearable — and they'll move to resolve it. ↗ 07 Identity Consistency People act in ways consistent with what they've said about themselves. Elicit the statement in discovery. Use it at the close. The most powerful close you'll ever run is the prospect's own words. ↗ 08 Future Self Projection Talking about the future creates distance. Speaking from the future creates urgency. Get the prospect to describe their life as if it already happened — and watch everything change. ↗ 09 Loss Aversion Kahneman's research is clear: losses feel roughly 2.5x more painful than equivalent gains feel good. Selling what someone will gain is half as powerful as helping them see what they're already losing. ↗ 10 Loss Framing Every benefit you pitch can be rewritten as a prevented loss. This lesson is the practical exercise: take each core benefit of Sales.io and flip the frame. Loss language hits harder — always. ↗ 11 Legitimate Urgency Manufactured urgency is dead. Prospects can feel it. The urgency that actually closes deals comes from their own timeline, their own goals, and their own cost of delay — not from "only 2 spots left." ↗ 12 The Regret Minimization Frame Short-term comfort versus long-term identity. This frame doesn't close people — it gets them to close themselves by asking the right question about who they want to be looking back. ↗ 13 Reciprocity When you genuinely give — value, insight, your real attention — people feel a natural pull to give back. The best closers don't manufacture this. They create it by being the most valuable person in the room before they ask for anything. ↗ 14 Social Proof at the Identity Level Generic testimonials say "it works." Identity-matched proof says "it works for people like you." Those are two different conversations with two very different effects on a close. ↗ 15 Contagious Emotion Your emotional state transfers to the prospect. Not through what you say — through how you show up. Your state is your most powerful persuasion tool and the one most reps have no control over. ↗ 16 The Structure of Influence Every principle in S7 exists as one layer in a larger structure. When they stack correctly, the call doesn't feel like persuasion — it feels like the natural conclusion of a genuinely helpful conversation. ↗
