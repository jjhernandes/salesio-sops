Managing a Bad Streak

Bad streaks are normal. Every rep has them. What kills reps is not the streak itself — it's what they do in response to it. Here's how to reset without spiraling.

Bad Streaks Are Normal

The Reality Check

Every rep who has ever been good at sales has had a stretch where nothing closed. Three calls in a row that didn't go your way. A week where every prospect seemed unqualified or checked out. A month where you couldn't get your state right no matter what you did.

This is not a sign that you've peaked or that sales isn't for you. It's a sign that you're human and operating in a game that has variance in it. The variable you can control is what you do during the streak — not whether the streak happens.

The dangerous story: "I used to be good at this but now I'm off." This story is a trap. It uses past performance as proof that current performance is a deviation from your true level. The truth is: both your peak and your trough are part of your range. The goal is to raise the floor, not to explain why you're not at the ceiling.

The only meaningful question during a streak is: am I doing the right things at a high standard? If yes, the results will come. If no, fix the things — but don't treat results as the measure of whether you're a good rep. Results with variance are not a reliable measure of anything in the short term.

The Spiral — How It Actually Works

The streak isn't what kills you. The spiral is. Understand the mechanism so you can break it before it runs.

01
Stage 1
The First Loss
A call doesn't go well. Normal. No rep closes every call. But you add a little extra meaning to this one — "that was different," "something is off." You carry a trace of that into the next call without realizing it.
02
Stage 2
The Second Loss Confirms the Story
Now there's a pattern. Your brain, which is a pattern-recognition machine, is now operating under the hypothesis "I'm in a slump." You enter the next call slightly tighter. Slightly less certain. Slightly more attached to the outcome because you need it to prove the story wrong.
03
Stage 3
The Third Loss — The Spiral Locks In
The story is now confirmed. "I'm definitely in a slump." You now bring a compromised state to every call. Tightened energy, outcome-attached, slightly desperate. And here's the cruel part: that state produces worse calls, which produce more losses, which confirm the story further. The spiral feeds itself.
The spiral is not a sales problem. It's a psychology problem. The prospect didn't change — you did. And you can change back. But not by trying harder.
04
Stage 4
Overcorrection
Desperate to break the streak, reps try harder. They push more on closes. They add more urgency. They start using tactics they wouldn't normally use. Every one of these moves makes the situation worse because they all signal neediness. The prospect reads it. And the results get worse.

The Reset Protocol — After 3 Consecutive Losses

What to Do (It's Not "Try Harder")

After three consecutive calls that don't close, do not get on another call until you've run this protocol. Attempting to force your way out of a spiral by taking more calls in the same state produces more data confirming the story. Pause first. Reset. Then dial.

  • ✓
    Listen to a call you're proud of. Pull up a recording of a call that went well — one where you were genuinely in your state, the prospect opened up, and the conversation flowed. Not to imitate it — to remind your nervous system what your actual level feels like. This breaks the "I'm off" story faster than anything else.
  • ✓
    Run the data. Pull your last 20 calls and look at the numbers honestly. Is your close rate actually below your average? Or are you in a normal variance window and catastrophizing it? Often the streak feels worse than the data shows. Let the data decide if this is a real problem or a perceived one.
  • ✓
    Identify the actual breakdown — if there is one. Listen to the three recent calls. Was there a technical breakdown — a step you're skipping, a question you're not asking? Or was it a state issue? If it's technical, fix the specific thing. If it's state, do the identity anchoring steps below before your next call.
  • ✓
    Call your coach. Not to vent — to get an honest outside read on what's happening. Sometimes you're too close to your own calls to see the pattern. Your coach sees it. Use that resource. It's what they're there for.

Identity Anchoring During a Streak

Who You Are Is Not What Your Numbers Say Right Now

A bad streak does not change who you are as a rep. It changes what your results say for a limited window. Those are different things. Reps who collapse during streaks are the ones who have built their identity on their results — and when results dip, the identity wobbles, which makes the results worse.

The rep with a stable identity can take three losses in a row and show up to the fourth call at the same level they would have after three wins. That stability is the competitive advantage.

Self-Talk During a Bad Streak — Two Versions
Rep (Spiral)
"I don't know what's wrong with me. I used to be able to close these. I'm probably losing my edge. Maybe I need to change my approach completely. If I don't close this next one I'm going to have a real problem."
Identity tied to results. Every loss confirms a story about who they are. Next call is now compromised before it starts.
Rep (Anchored)
"Three in a row that didn't close. Let me look at what actually happened. My state has been lower than usual this week — I've been tired and I've been checking my numbers before calls. I'm going to fix those two things and see where I am in another week. My 90-day average is still solid. This is variance, not a trend."
Diagnostic, not catastrophic. Identity is stable. The data is the reference point, not the story. Next call is not compromised.

"Your identity should be built on what you do, not what your results say. Do the right things. The results follow — sometimes with a delay."

— JJ Hernandez
