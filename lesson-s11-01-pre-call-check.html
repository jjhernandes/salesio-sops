The 2-Minute Pre-Call Check

Your state is your strategy. Who you are when you dial is the most important variable on the call. This protocol makes sure you show up as the right version of yourself every single time.

What the Pre-Call Check Is

The Protocol

Two minutes. Before every call. Not ten minutes, not a full ritual — two minutes to run a quick internal audit and confirm you're actually ready for the person about to pick up the phone.

Most reps finish one call and immediately dial the next. They carry the energy of the last conversation directly into this one — the lost deal, the no-show, the difficult objection — and the prospect has no idea what happened before them. But they feel it. That residue is costing you closes you should be winning.

This check is not about hyping yourself up. It's not a motivation ritual. It's a calibration — a quick read on where you are right now and whether that state serves the next person on the call.

If you're calibrated, you dial. If you're not, you take 60 more seconds and run the reset. Then you dial. That's the whole protocol.

Why reps skip it: They tell themselves they don't have time. The reality is they don't have time not to. A 2-minute check prevents a 60-minute call from going sideways. The math is obvious. The discipline is what separates the reps who do it from the reps who stay inconsistent.

The 5 Questions

Run through these before every call. Honest answers only — this doesn't work if you tell yourself what you want to hear.

01
Question One
What level am I at right now?
On a 1-10 scale, where are you? Not where you want to be — where you actually are. If you're honest and the number is below a 7, something needs to shift before you dial. You cannot run a high-level conversation from a 4.
Below a 7 means you run the reset protocol before dialing. Non-negotiable. A 6 feels close enough but it isn't.
02
Question Two
What do I know about this person?
30 seconds of context before you dial. What's their situation? What did they come in saying? Job, income range, stated goal. You should never dial someone you haven't looked at. Even a quick CRM scan counts. You can't be genuinely curious about someone you know nothing about.
If you know nothing about them, spend 60 seconds in Close before the call. Don't show up blank. Blank is disrespectful to the call and to the prospect.
03
Question Three
What am I genuinely curious about?
Name one real thing you want to understand about this person. Not a discovery question from a playbook — an actual thing. "I want to know why they haven't pulled the trigger yet" or "I want to understand what's kept them stuck." Genuine curiosity changes how you listen and how the conversation feels to them.
If you can't name one real curiosity, you're about to pitch at them instead of have a conversation with them. Pitching at people doesn't close. Conversations do.
04
Question Four
What do I want for them?
Not what do you want from them — what do you want for them. Do you genuinely want this person to get clarity on whether this is the right move? Or are you here to close a deal? These are different orientations and the prospect can feel both of them. One builds trust, the other erodes it.
Correct answer: "I want this person to leave clearer than they arrived." If your honest answer is "I want to hit my number," run the reset before dialing.
05
Question Five
Am I okay if this doesn't close today?
This is the hardest one to answer honestly. If the deal doesn't happen on this call, are you actually okay? Not "yeah sure" okay — genuinely, operationally okay. If you're not, that need will show up on the call in your pace, your tone, and your responses to friction. And it will cost you the deal.
The goal is non-attachment, not indifference. You care about the outcome — you just don't need it. The moment you need it, you've already lost leverage.

The Reset Protocol

When Your Answers Aren't Where They Need to Be

If any of the five questions surfaces a problem — your state is low, you're not curious, you need this close — you don't push through it. You take 60 additional seconds and run the reset. This is how you stop carrying yesterday's losses into today's opportunities.

  • ✓
    Physical state first. Stand up. Take three slow breaths from your abdomen, not your chest. Roll your shoulders back and down. Your physical posture directly affects your mental state. Do the physical piece before anything mental.
  • ✓
    Release the last call. If you're carrying residue from a difficult conversation, name it and set it down. "That call didn't go how I wanted. It's over. This is a completely different person in a completely different situation." That's it. Don't process the last call now — just release it.
  • ✓
    Anchor to your intention. Say this out loud if you can: "My job is to find out what's true for this person and help them make the right decision." This single sentence resets your orientation from closer to advisor. Advisors don't need the sale — they need the right answer for the person in front of them.
  • ✓
    Find one genuine curiosity. Pull up something you're actually curious about before you dial. Even one question. Curiosity is the fastest bridge from anxious and outcome-attached to present and effective.

If after the reset you're still not in a state that serves the prospect: Consider whether you need a longer break. A call taken in the wrong state produces outcomes worse than rescheduling. Know this. Use it. Rescheduling one call to protect your state on the next five is the right call.

The Bottom Line

The pre-call check is not a nicety. It's not a soft skill. It's the most direct lever you have over your close rate that isn't dependent on what the prospect says or does. You control your state. Most reps act like they don't. That's the gap.

Two minutes. Every call. No exceptions.
