Setter Objections

"I'm not interested" is not a no. It's a "not yet." The setter who understands this books calls that setters who fold never reach.

"I'm Not Interested" Is Not a No

What the Objection Actually Means

"I'm not interested" in the first 30 seconds of a cold interaction does not mean the person has evaluated what you're offering and decided it's not for them. It means you haven't broken the pattern yet, or you triggered their sales guard by moving too fast.

It's a reflexive response, not a considered decision. The setter who treats it as a final no is not getting rejected — they're folding before the real conversation even started.

The principle that applies here is the same one that applies everywhere in Sales.io: you don't need to fight objections, you need to get curious about them.

When someone says "I'm not interested," they're usually saying one of four things: "I don't understand what you're offering," "I've been burned by this before," "I don't think this applies to me," or "I don't trust you yet." None of those are a closed door. All of them are a conversation.

The Top 5 Setter Objections

Objection Response Approach
"I'm not interested." Validate + get curious. "Fair enough — out of curiosity, is it that the timing's off or that sales training specifically isn't something you're looking at right now?" Surfaces the real reason.
"I already have a coach / program." Don't compete. Get curious. "Got it — how's that going for you so far? What's it doing for your close rate?" If it's working, disengage gracefully. If there are gaps, they'll tell you.
"I don't have the money right now." Don't push. Qualify timeline. "Understood — is the plan to revisit this once you're in a better place financially, or is the growth piece not a priority right now at all?" Separates the broke-but-motivated from the genuinely not interested.
"I'm not looking for anything right now." "Makes sense — are you in a good spot with your close rate or are you still working through things?" Moves the conversation to their reality, not your pitch.
"Send me more info." (Brush-off) Decline the redirect. "I could do that, but honestly it won't give you what the conversation would. What would make a 20-minute call worth your time?" Forces them to either engage or end it clean.

The Pattern — Validate, Curiosity, Reframe

Why This Pattern Works

Validate: you're not arguing with their objection. You're acknowledging it as real, which lowers their defenses because they feel heard. Curiosity: a genuine question moves the conversation from sales script to actual dialogue. Reframe: once they've revealed what the real concern is, you can address it directly — not with a rehearsed answer, but with something that actually fits what they just told you.

01
Step 1
Validate
Don't fight the objection. Acknowledge it without flinching. A setter who immediately pushes against an objection signals that they were expecting agreement and weren't ready for resistance. Validation signals you're unfazed.
"That makes sense." / "Fair enough." / "Understood." — short, genuine, not a set-up line.
02
Step 2
Curiosity Question
Ask a question that reveals what's actually behind the objection. Not a rhetorical question designed to make them feel silly — a real one you'd genuinely want the answer to. The question should feel like the natural next thing a person with no agenda would ask.
"Out of curiosity — is it that the timing's off, or that this kind of investment specifically isn't on your radar right now?"
03
Step 3
Reframe
Use what they told you in step 2. Don't use a pre-scripted response — respond to their actual answer. If they said timing, address timing. If they said trust, address trust. The reframe works because it's specific to them, not generic.
If they said "timing" — "Got it. What would need to change for timing to be right?" If they said "I've tried programs before" — "That's fair. What made those not work for you?"

When to Push and When to Let Go

Not every objection should be worked. The setter's job is to find real opportunities, not convert every contact into a booking. Wasting time on someone who is genuinely not a fit or genuinely not ready hurts your numbers the same way bad bookings do — it consumes time that could have gone to real leads.

Push When:

The objection is reflexive, not considered. They said "not interested" in under 10 seconds. They're engaged but hesitant. The real objection is different from the stated one. They revealed a pain point that connects to what you offer. They have the markers of a qualified prospect.

Let Go When:

They've clarified after two questions and it's genuinely not a fit. They're pre-revenue with no near-term path. They're in financial distress. They've said no twice with specific reasoning, not reflexive deflection. Pushing past this damages the brand and wastes your time.

The graceful exit is also a skill. When you do let go, do it on your terms — not theirs. "Understood — look, if anything changes on your end, I'll leave it open. Take care." Clean, professional, no resentment. They might come back. They definitely won't if you burned it.

Exchange — Setter Who Folds vs. Setter Who Holds

VERSION 1 — Setter Who Folds
Prospect
"I'm not really interested, thanks."
Setter
"Oh okay, no problem! Sorry for bothering you. Have a great day!"
Immediate capitulation. Apologizes for the call. This wasn't a no — it was a reflex. The setter read it as a closed door and exited. Real lead lost.
VERSION 2 — Setter Who Holds
Prospect
"I'm not really interested, thanks."
Setter
"Fair enough." [pause] "Out of curiosity — is it that the timing's off, or that sales training specifically isn't something you're looking at right now?"
Validates without caving. Curiosity question pulls back the curtain on the real objection. Calm and unhurried.
Prospect
"Honestly I've done a few programs and they never really moved the needle. I don't want to waste money again."
Real objection revealed: past failure, not current disinterest. This is a workable conversation.
Setter
"That's fair — and I get why that would make you cautious. What was it about those programs that didn't work? Was it the material, the structure, or just nothing stuck?"
Doesn't defend other programs. Doesn't pivot to the pitch. Gets the specific failure point — which will become the anchor for what makes this different.
Prospect
"It was all theory. No one worked with me on actual calls. I needed someone to watch me and tell me what I was doing wrong."
Setter
"That's the exact gap we fill. Every coach here is actively closing — not teaching from slides. Call review is a core part of the program. I'm not saying book something today, but it'd be worth a conversation to see if this is actually different for you."
Specific reframe using their words. Not overselling. Offers a low-stakes next step. This lead almost walked. Now it's a booking.
