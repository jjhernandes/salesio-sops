Operational
Drag

Performance gap metric — cash per call · closer benchmarking · scaling decisions
Operational Drag tells you exactly how much money you are gaining or losing on every single booked call by having a specific closer handle it — compared to your best closers.
The Formula — Three Steps
01
Cash Per Booked Call (for each closer)
Cash Collected ÷ Booked Appointments
Run this for every closer individually. This is the core unit — how much revenue one call in their hands actually produces.
02
Set the Benchmark
(Top 3 Cash/Call) ÷ 3 = Benchmark
Average the cash-per-call of your top three closers. This is the standard every other closer gets measured against — not a theoretical ceiling, a real internal number.
03
Calculate Operational Drag
Closer's Cash/Call − Benchmark = Drag
Positive = they're above the benchmark (pulling the team up). Negative = every call routed to them loses that dollar amount vs. what your top closers would have produced.

Important — Segment by Calendar Type
Not all booked calls are equal. Most teams run a main calendar (primary leads, higher intent, better show rates) and a backup calendar (overflow, lower intent, follow-up leads). A closer who only works the backup calendar will have a structurally lower cash-per-call — that's the lead quality, not their skill.

Before calculating drag, always segment closers by which calendar they work. Compare main-calendar closers against other main-calendar closers. Compare backup-calendar closers against each other. Never mix them — the number becomes meaningless and unfair.

= 1 gallon of fuel

Cash per booked call
= miles per gallon
In Sales
Operational drag
= MPG below your 3 most efficient cars
You pay the same price for every gallon of fuel regardless of which car you put it in. The question is how far each car takes you. A closer with high drag isn't just underperforming — they're actively burning expensive fuel at a worse rate than your best cars.
What You Do With This Number
Route calls intentionally. If a closer has severe drag, reduce their call volume until the root cause is addressed. Every call is a finite resource — stop burning it at a discount.
Set a minimum standard for new hires. Define the drag threshold a new closer must hit within their first 30 days. If they can't reach it, you know early.
Make coaching decisions data-driven. Drag tells you who needs attention. Pair with call review to find why — is it offer rate, objection handling, frame control, or conviction?
Make scaling decisions clean. Before hiring a new closer, know your benchmark. A new hire at or above benchmark adds capacity. Below benchmark adds drag. Know which one you're buying.
Track monthly, not weekly. Single-week variance is noise. Monthly drag is signal. Small sample sizes punish closers who hit a bad stretch — give the number time to stabilize before acting on it.
