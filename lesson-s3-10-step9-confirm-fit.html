Step 9 — Confirm Fit

The verbal commitment before price. You don't reveal the number until they've told you it sounds like what they need. Skipping this creates objections that don't have to exist.

Why You Need Verbal Buy-In Before Price

Price hits differently depending on context. If a prospect hears $7,500 before they've agreed that the solution fits — price is the first thing they're evaluating. Their first thought is "is this worth it?" — and they don't yet have enough buy-in to answer that with a yes.

If they hear $7,500 after they've told you "yeah, this sounds like exactly what I need" — price is secondary. They're now evaluating logistics, not fit. That's a fundamentally easier conversation.

The Confirm Fit Step Creates a Checkpoint

Before you move to price, you need a clear signal that the prospect believes the solution addresses their situation. If that signal isn't there, you are not ready to reveal price. You either go back to the pitch and find what's missing, or you ask a clarifying question to surface the gap.

The Confirm Fit Question

"Based on what you just heard — does this sound like what you were actually looking for?"

Variations

"Does this answer the thing you said was missing?" — good when the discovery surfaced a very specific gap.

"Is this the type of thing that would actually move the needle for you — or does something feel off?" — invites honest feedback and surfaces objections before price.

"Based on what I walked you through, does this feel like it's pointing at the right things?" — good when the prospect needs to trust the direction before the financial conversation.

Ask one confirm fit question and wait for the answer. Don't add qualifiers. Don't say "based on what I just showed you, and I know we covered a lot, but does this feel like it addresses the core thing you mentioned?" — that's hedging. Ask clean, then hold silence until they respond.

What to Do When the Answer Is No

Go Back, Not Forward

If the prospect says "I'm not sure" or "it sounds okay but..." — do not push forward to price. That "but" is telling you something is unresolved. Going to price over unresolved doubt is how you generate the objections that feel impossible to handle — because they're actually real.

The Recovery Question

"What part doesn't feel like it fits?" — surfaces the gap so you can address it directly. Maybe you covered something they don't care about and missed something they do. Maybe there's a feature of the program they haven't heard yet that closes the gap. Maybe the fit genuinely isn't there — and knowing now is better than pushing past it.

The rep who pushes past a "no" on confirm fit to get to price is the rep who creates the harshest objections. "I need to think about it" and "It's too expensive" at the price reveal are almost always unresolved fit concerns. The prospect hasn't said it's not for them — they just haven't said it is. Price before buy-in = resistance.

Why This Eliminates Most Post-Price Objections

Without Confirm FitWith Confirm Fit
Price hits a prospect who hasn't agreed the solution fits Price hits a prospect who just told you "yes, this sounds like what I need"
"Is this worth it?" is the first question in their mind — no answer yet "Is this logistically possible for me?" is the question — they've already decided it's worth it

Call Exchange — Skipped vs. Completed Confirm Fit

VERSION 1 — No Confirm Fit
Rep
"...and the guarantee means you either get the results or your money back. So with all of that — the program is $7,500."
Prospect
"Hmm. That's a lot. I'd have to think about it."
Rep
"Totally, I understand — we also have payment plans..."
The price hit a prospect who hadn't confirmed the solution fit. "Think about it" is not a price objection — it's a fit objection. They're not sure this is the right thing. Payment plans don't solve that. The close is already in trouble.
VERSION 2 — Confirm Fit First
Rep
"...and the guarantee means you either get the results or your money back. Before I go into the investment — does what I just described sound like what you were actually looking for?"
Prospect
"Yeah — honestly, yeah. The 1-on-1 call review thing is exactly what I've been missing. And the job board is huge for me."
Rep
"Good. So investment-wise — to join the mastermind it's $7,500."
Price follows buy-in. The prospect has said yes to the solution before hearing the number. They're now evaluating whether they can make it work — not whether it's the right thing. This is a much more manageable conversation.
