The Breakthrough — What Actually Changed

You named it yourself. You went back and watched your calls from last week versus this week and saw a stark difference. Here's what shifted.

The old way — checklist mode
You were gathering pain but had no plan for what to do with it. Discovery was a checkbox — ask the questions, get the answers, move on. The pain never made it into the pitch. You were running the process but not using what the prospect gave you.

"I was getting the pain and didn't know what to do with it because I was just doing a checklist."
The new way — gap selling, pitch reordered
You identified the one thing they actually care about — job stacking — and led with that. Everything else became a bonus. Not ignored, just not the main event.

"I know the only thing you care about is job stacking, so let me show you how we do that here. And as a bonus, not only do you get that — you also get these things."

The gap is now real. Current state → desired state → you bridge it. That's what's closing.
"Every time I get to a new level, I have to master it. Get to that level and master it. But it's good."

That's the right mindset. The reps compound. The next level will feel the same way — and you'll master that too.

Call Review — The Special Snowflake

You pulled up a live call — financial planner/analyst, $130K base, leaving his job via a voluntary separation program, interested in stacking but skeptical it works for someone like him. Follow-up Thursday. Here's what JJ saw.

Who this prospect is
Classic special snowflake syndrome. He believes his situation is uniquely complex — that the normal rules don't apply to him. He's had 15+ interviews, lost 2 final rounds, zero offers. Still thinks he's above average and that the market just doesn't appreciate him yet.

He has a massive ego wrapped in intellectual language. He overcomplicated a simple question about how many interviews he's had. He asked to speak to a career coach before committing — not because he needed information, but because he wanted someone to reassure him that he's special enough for this to work.

You already identified the problem: you felt defensive at times — trying to prove the product could work for him instead of putting it back on him. That's the pattern to fix before Thursday.

"You gotta make him humble himself throughout the whole call — or put his money where his mouth is and let his ego pay for it. There's no in-between."
The Moves — What to Do on Thursday

Two things JJ called out that would have changed the trajectory of this call. Apply them on the follow-up.

Miss #1 — "Go into your thing"
When the prospect said "go into your thing" — he was putting you in the same box as every other sales rep. Most reps hear that and just start pitching. Don't.

The move: pause. Then — "Before I do, you said you don't know if stacking is even possible for you. What did you mean by that?" Now you're not the sales guy. You're the one who actually heard him.
Miss #2 — The AI consulting gig
He mentioned he "thinks" he landed an AI consulting gig. That phrase — thinks — is an escape hatch he'll use to avoid committing. You should have gone there first.

"What do you mean you think you landed it? What does that look like?" Understand exactly what that is before moving forward. If he's using it as a reason not to invest, you need to know that now, not after the pitch.
The ego move — humble him, don't validate him
He's a financial planner at a startup. Not a C-suite exec. When he plays the "my situation is uniquely complex" card — flip it.

"I mean, you're not, like, a director or anything — so honestly your profile isn't that unusual. We've had people in your exact position do this." Make it a non-issue. Take away the specialness. Either he proves you wrong or he drops the frame.

JJ's preferred approach: crush the ego, let it come crawling back. Don't validate it. Don't play the nice guy. It's harder but it's cleaner.
The pause — keep doing it
You were already using this and it was working. He got uncomfortable, started filling the silence, even called it out directly: "You pausing, man, I know you got more questions." That's exactly what you want. He was closing himself.

Don't change this. The pause is working.
Don't do this
Prove it to him. Pull up testimonials to convince him. Try to find the one piece of evidence that makes his objection disappear. That's defensive. It confirms he's holding the frame, not you.
Do this instead
Put it back on him. "I hear you. What specifically makes you think it won't work for you?" Let him answer. Then use his own words against the objection. You don't prove — you ask.
