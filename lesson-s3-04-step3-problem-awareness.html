Step 3 — Problem Awareness

Finding what's actually in the way. Not the category of problem — the specific thing. There's a difference, and it determines whether your pitch lands.

The Stated Problem vs. The Real Problem

What Prospects Tell You

"I need to make more money." "I'm not consistent." "I don't know how to handle objections." These are categories. They are not problems. A category is a direction to look in — it's not specific enough to build a solution on.

The real problem is one level deeper. It's the specific thing happening in their calls, in their mindset, or in their situation that produces the category-level result. A rep who "doesn't handle objections well" might have composure issues, or might be pitching before understanding, or might be targeting the wrong prospects. Those are different problems with different solutions.

Your job is not to accept the category. Your job is to get to the specific thing — the actual mechanism that's keeping them stuck. That's what creates a targeted pitch, and that's what creates real close urgency.

Getting to the Specific Thing

The problem with accepting a category: you end up pitching a broad solution to a vague problem. The prospect hears it and thinks, "yeah that sounds generally helpful" — but they don't feel the urgency of "this solves the exact thing that's been holding me back." That's the gap between interest and a close.

Category (Accept This)Specific (Find This)
"I'm not closing consistently" "I get to the end of calls and can't hold my frame when they push back on price — I end up discounting or scheduling a follow-up instead of closing"
"I need better skills" "I've been using scripts but they make me sound robotic and prospects can feel it — I lose them during the pitch every time"
"I'm not making enough money" "I've been closing $4-6K for eight months — I know it's a mental block but I don't know how to get past it"

Why Problem Clarity Creates Close Urgency

The Mechanism

When a prospect articulates their specific problem out loud — in detail, with context — something happens: they hear themselves. They feel the weight of the thing they just described. That feeling is what drives the decision to change.

You cannot manufacture that feeling for them. You can only create the conditions for them to access it themselves. That's what this step does. You ask the right questions, they speak the problem into existence, and the urgency becomes theirs — not something you imposed on them.

"People buy based on emotions and compensate logically."

— JJ Hernandez
The Rep Who Finds the Real Problem

When you've found the specific thing, the rest of the call clicks. Your pitch isn't a general presentation — it's a targeted response to something the prospect just told you they're dealing with. They recognize themselves in what you say. That recognition is trust, and trust is what closes deals.

The Questions That Surface the Real Problem

  • ✓
    "What specifically happens in those moments?" — moves from a general statement ("I lose deals") to a specific scene (what was happening on the call when it fell apart).
  • ✓
    "What do you think is actually behind that?" — invites the prospect to diagnose themselves. They usually know more than they've said. This question surfaces it.
  • ✓
    "Have you tried to fix it? What happened?" — prior attempts reveal what they've already ruled out and where the real sticking point is. A prospect who's tried scripts and failed has a different problem than one who's never invested in their skills.
  • ✓
    "If you had to put your finger on the one thing — what is it?" — forces specificity. Prospects often don't get this question so they'll pause. Let them. What comes out is usually the real thing.

Call Exchange — Category vs. Specific

REP WHO ACCEPTS THE CATEGORY
Prospect
"I just feel like I can't handle objections. Every time someone pushes back I just kind of freeze."
Rep
"Yeah objection handling is huge. That's actually one of the things we focus on a lot in the program. We go deep on all the major objections — price, timing, spouse..."
Took "objections" as the problem and started pitching to the category. Didn't find out what specifically triggers the freeze, when it happens, what the rep does in that moment. The pitch is generic. The prospect isn't feeling understood — they're hearing a brochure.
REP WHO FINDS THE SPECIFIC THING
Prospect
"I just feel like I can't handle objections. Every time someone pushes back I just kind of freeze."
Rep
"What specifically triggers the freeze? Like what does the prospect say right before you feel it?"
Prospect
"It's usually price. They say 'that's too expensive' and I just... I don't know what to say that doesn't feel pushy."
Rep
"What do you end up doing in that moment?"
Prospect
"Honestly I usually offer a discount or tell them to think about it. Which I know is wrong but I panic."
Rep
"So you know exactly what's happening — the frame breaks under price pressure and you pull back before they've actually said no. How long has that been the pattern?"
Now the rep knows: it's not "objection handling" — it's frame collapse specifically at price, leading to pre-emptive concessions. That's a very specific problem with a very specific solution. The pitch will be surgical.
