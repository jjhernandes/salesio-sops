The Gaps — Enterprise B2B Lens

1. Pitches before understanding the business situation High

In 4 of 6 calls, Matt opens with a company overview before the prospect has shared anything meaningful about their situation. In enterprise B2B, this is costly — not because you need to close faster, but because a generic pitch doesn't help you qualify. You end up having a good conversation with someone who may not be the right fit, or may not be the right stakeholder. Discovery first gives you the information to tailor the pitch, ask sharper follow-up questions, and surface the real buying situation.

Afra call, 0:54 — "So I'll jump in, give you a bit of background on us..." — 4 minutes in before a single question. Javed call — same pattern, even though Javed already knew the service from Mayur. The Campspot call is the exception and it's the best-structured call of the batch.
2. Informational questions only — no business impact questions High

Matt asks the right operational questions — who are your competitors, what does your acquisition look like, what keywords are you targeting. These are useful. But he never goes a layer deeper into business impact: What happens to the business if this doesn't get solved? What's the internal pressure behind this initiative? Is there a timeline, a launch, a board expectation driving this? In enterprise sales, understanding that context determines whether this is a real priority or a nice-to-have — and that changes everything about how to run the cycle.

Podium call — Lindsey mentioned a research study coming out in the next few months and a new program launch. Those are time-sensitive business events. Matt acknowledged them but never asked: "What happens if the PR around that study doesn't land? What's the cost of low visibility at launch?" That question would have told him exactly how urgent this is.
3. Same pitch on every call — doesn't adjust to what was shared High

The core pitch is nearly identical across all 6 calls: 75,000 publications, tier-1 placements, Forbes/Wired/Fortune, editorial content, guaranteed placements, link back to site. This is fine as a foundation. The issue is it doesn't shift after discovery. After Conner explains he's in cannabis B2B SaaS focused on SEO and GEO, the pitch should name the publications that matter in that space and describe what results look like for a company at that stage. It doesn't. In enterprise, the pitch needs to feel like it was built for them specifically.

Conner call — after learning he's in cannabis cultivation B2B SaaS, Matt's pitch still mentions Forbes, Wired, Fortune without any cannabis or ag-tech publication specifics. Conner asked good questions about niche publications — Matt addressed it briefly but never made the connection concrete.
4. Gatekeepers aren't being identified or routed around High

In enterprise and mid-market, the person who books the discovery call is often not the final decision maker. That's not a problem — it's the reality of the buying process. The problem is not identifying it early and planning accordingly. When Afra said she'd need to take everything to her CEO, the right move was to understand what the CEO cares about, what would make this an easy yes internally, and how to either get the CEO on a call or arm Afra with the right materials to champion it. Instead Matt gave Afra the full pitch and hoped she'd relay it accurately.

Afra call — "I want to discuss all of this with my CEO." The right follow-up: "What does your CEO typically need to see to feel good about a decision like this? And is there a version of this conversation we could set up together so he can get his questions answered directly?"
5. Channel partner opportunity missed with Kate High

Kate Gundry at Pluck PR is not a direct client — she's a potential channel partner. She runs a 12-person boutique firm, she already identified Pearl and YouNow as perfect fits for SwayyEm's service, and she essentially pitched the value proposition back to Matt during the call. In enterprise B2B, agency-to-agency relationships are one of the highest-leverage acquisition channels because one partner can bring multiple clients with zero additional CAC. Matt already does 25% of his business this way — but he treated Kate like a prospect rather than a potential source of 3–5 clients. The good news: it's closing. The gap is that this happened by accident, not by design.

Kate, mid-call: "I almost want to say to them, try this for this, and see how much further it goes." She's describing being a referral source. The right pivot: "We actually work with agencies in a white-label or referral model — about 25% of our business. If Pearl and YouNow are good fits, how do you typically handle bringing in outside partners for your clients?"
6. Next steps are set but lack mutual success criteria Medium

Scheduling a follow-up and sending materials is the right move in enterprise — no issue there. The gap is that the next steps are one-sided. Matt commits to delivering something (analysis, deck, examples) but doesn't get the prospect to commit to what they'll bring to the next call or what would need to be true for them to be ready to move forward. That one question changes how engaged the prospect shows up to call two.

Campspot call — Matt committed to pulling a competitive gap analysis. Good. But before hanging up: "When we come back with that analysis — if it shows the gap you're describing between you and Firefly and ResNexus, what would you need to see to start building a business case internally?" That answer tells him exactly how to frame the follow-up.
7. Social proof doesn't match company size or industry Medium

Visa, Binance, SAP, and Pegasystems are strong names. But dropping them into conversations with a cannabis SaaS startup, an ed-tech company, or a boutique PR agency creates distance rather than credibility. The subtext a prospect hears is "this was built for companies 10x our size." Enterprise buyers want to know you've done this for companies in their situation, not just that you've done it for the biggest logos in the world. Case studies and references should mirror who's in the room.

Javed call — Matt opened with SAP, Pegasystems, Visa, Binance. Javed is a Series A company. Those names don't make him feel understood — they raise the question of whether SwayyEm's relationships and content approach will work for a company at his stage.

What to Improve

Fix 1: Lead with discovery — understand before presenting

The first part of a discovery call should be exactly that: discovery. Understand their current situation, what they've tried, what's driving the initiative, and who's involved before presenting anything. The pitch then becomes a direct response to what they said, not a presentation that happens to them.

"Before I walk you through what we do — help me understand your situation first. What are you currently doing to get visibility and drive inbound, and what's not working the way you want it to?"
Fix 2: Ask business impact questions, not just operational ones

Operational questions (competitors, channels, keywords) tell you what to say. Business impact questions tell you whether this is a priority and what's driving it. In enterprise, you need to know if there's a board deadline, a launch, a competitive threat, or internal pressure — because those are what move timelines.

"You mentioned you want to build visibility around this research study — what happens if that doesn't get picked up broadly when it drops? What does low visibility at that moment cost the business?"  /  "Is there a specific timeline or internal event driving this initiative, or is this more of an ongoing priority?"
Fix 3: Tailor the pitch to what they just told you

After discovery, connect their situation directly to what you offer — in their words. Don't run the standard 5-minute pitch. Reference what they said. Name the specific publications or outcomes that are relevant to their industry and company stage. The pitch should feel built for this specific person, not pulled from a deck.

"Based on what you shared — you're a new entity in the cannabis B2B space, focused on SEO and GEO, no existing visibility — here's specifically what we'd do for you and why it fits that situation..." [then tailor from there]
Fix 4: Identify the buying process early

In enterprise and mid-market, the discovery call almost always involves someone who isn't the final decision maker. That's fine — but figure it out in the first few minutes and plan accordingly. Either get the decision maker on the call, or understand what they need to say yes so the internal champion can build the case properly.

"Who else typically gets involved in a decision like this — is it just you, or does your CEO or a finance team need to be part of the conversation? I want to make sure the right people have what they need when it gets to that stage."
Fix 5: Recognize channel partners and open that conversation directly

When an agency or consultancy shows up on a call — especially one that mentions multiple clients who could benefit — pivot the conversation. Don't pitch them as a direct client. Ask how they handle gaps in their offering, whether they've brought in outside partners before, and what a referral or white-label arrangement would look like for their firm. One agency partner can be worth 3–5 clients.

"Actually — about 25% of our business is agency-to-agency, either white-label or referral. Given what you're describing with Pearl and YouNow, it sounds like this comes up more than once for your clients. How do you typically handle situations where a client needs something outside your core?"
Fix 6: Set next steps with mutual commitments, not just deliverables

Scheduling a follow-up and sending materials is the right enterprise move. The upgrade is getting the prospect to commit to something too — what they'll bring to the next call, what questions they'll have answered internally, or what would need to be true for them to be ready to move forward after the analysis. This separates engaged prospects from ones who are just taking your call.

"I'll pull together the competitive gap analysis before we reconnect. On your end — if the data shows the gap we're describing, who else on your team would need to be part of the conversation about moving forward? Let's make sure the right people are on the next call."
Fix 7: Match social proof to company stage and industry

Keep Visa and Binance in the back pocket. Lead with references that mirror who's in the room — similar company size, industry, or growth stage. If you don't have an exact match, use the category: "We work with a lot of B2B SaaS companies at your stage..." That lands closer to home than enterprise logos that can feel out of reach.

Instead of leading with Visa/Binance — "We work with a lot of B2B SaaS companies that are in a similar position — established product, strong team, but not yet in the public conversation the way their competitors are. That's usually where we see the biggest impact."

Priority Order

  • 1 — Lead with discovery every call. Understand before presenting. This is the root of most other gaps.
  • 2 — Add business impact questions. Know why this matters to the business, not just what they're doing today. That's what separates a real priority from a nice-to-have.
  • 3 — Tailor the pitch to what was shared. Same presentation every call means it's not a pitch — it's a deck.
  • 4 — Identify the buying process in the first few minutes. Find the decision maker. Either get them on the call or arm the champion properly.
  • 5 — Systematize the channel partner play. Kate happened by accident. Build a profile for agency/partner prospects and run a deliberate conversation with them.
  • 6 — Add mutual commitment to next steps. What does the prospect agree to bring to the next call? That question surfaces engagement level before you invest more time.
  • 7 — Lead with relevant social proof. Mirror the room — company size, industry, growth stage — before dropping enterprise logos.

What's Actually Working — Don't Change This

Matt's tone is right for enterprise. He's not pushy, he doesn't create artificial urgency, and he doesn't rush the sale — all of that is correct for this market. The Javed close, the Pluck PR relationship, and the Payaza and RAAPID deals are proof that the product sells and Matt can close. The competitive gap analysis offer is a strong differentiator and should be used on every eligible call. The improvements here are about going deeper, not moving faster.

Bottom Line

Matt has a good process for enterprise B2B — he's building follow-ups, delivering value before asking for anything, and closing when the timing is right. The gap is in discovery depth. He understands what companies are doing but rarely understands why they're doing it, what's at stake if they don't solve it, or who actually makes the decision. Getting clearer on those three things earlier in the call would improve qualification, tighten the follow-up, and surface the real buying timeline — without changing the pace or feel of the sales process at all.
