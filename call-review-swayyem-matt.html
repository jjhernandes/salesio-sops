Sales context: This is enterprise and mid-market B2B. The goal of call one is not to close — it's to qualify the opportunity, understand the business problem, get the right people in the room, and earn a concrete next step. Multi-call cycles are normal. Sending materials, scheduling analysis calls, and building internal champions are all part of the process. This review is graded on that standard, not on B2C single-call close metrics.

The Two Roles

Matt — Founder / Opener

  • Owns: Discovery calls (call 1)
  • Qualifies the opportunity and the fit
  • Uses founder credibility to open doors at the right level
  • Surfaces business situation, pain, timeline, and stakeholders
  • Determines if the deal is worth advancing
  • Hands off a clean brief to Hannah for call 2
  • Stays available for executive-level escalations

Hannah — Closer / Business Development

  • Owns: Everything from qualified opportunity to signed contract
  • Runs the follow-up call with the analysis or proposal
  • Builds the business case with the prospect
  • Handles the buying process, stakeholder navigation, and objections
  • Manages pricing conversations and contract terms
  • Drives toward a decision and signs the deal

The Handoff — What Matt Owes Hannah Before Call 2

  • Business situation — what they're doing now and where it's breaking down
  • Business impact — what's at stake if this doesn't get solved
  • Decision maker — who signs, who influences, who can kill it
  • Timeline — is there a real deadline or is this exploratory
  • Budget — confirmed or needs to be built internally
  • Fit assessment — is this a qualified opportunity worth Hannah's time

Matt  = discovery call gaps    Hannah  = closing motion gaps    Both  = structural / process gaps

1. Pitches before understanding the business situation Matt

Matt's job on call one is to qualify, not pitch. When he opens with the company overview, he loses the ability to tailor anything to the prospect's actual situation — and ends up having a good conversation with someone who might not be the right fit or the right stakeholder. Discovery first gives him the information he needs to decide whether to invest more time and to hand Hannah a clean brief.

Afra call, 0:54 — "So I'll jump in, give you a bit of background on us..." — 4 minutes before a single question. Javed call — same pattern even though Javed already knew the service. The Campspot call is the exception and it's the strongest of the batch.
2. Not surfacing business impact — leaving Hannah with incomplete intel Matt

Matt asks good operational questions — competitors, channels, keywords. These are useful. But he rarely gets to business impact: what's at stake if this doesn't get solved, what's driving the initiative internally, whether there's a deadline or event creating pressure. This is the most important intel for Hannah to walk into call two with. Without it, she's presenting a proposal without knowing what the prospect actually needs to justify the decision internally.

Podium call — Lindsey mentioned a research study launching soon and a new program announcement. Matt acknowledged both but never asked what happens if those don't get visibility. That's a real business consequence — and it's exactly what Hannah would use to anchor the proposal.
3. Decision maker and buying process not identified Matt

Matt rarely asks who makes the final call, what the internal approval process looks like, or whether there's a budget holder who needs to be involved. This leaves Hannah going into call two blind on the org chart. In enterprise B2B, knowing the buying committee — who decides, who influences, who can kill it — is essential to structuring the follow-up correctly. Matt's job is to surface this in call one so Hannah isn't discovering it mid-proposal.

Afra call — "I want to discuss all of this with my CEO." Matt continued pitching instead of asking: "What does your CEO typically need to see? Is there a version of this where we get him on a call so he can ask his questions directly?" That question shapes everything about how Hannah would structure the next step.
4. No handoff brief from Matt to Hannah before call 2 Both

Across these calls, Hannah is introduced as "head of business development" but is mostly silent. If she's the closer, she needs a structured handoff brief from Matt before she runs call two — what the prospect said, what pain was surfaced, who's in the buying process, what the timeline looks like, and what the prospect needs to see to move forward. Without that brief, she's starting from scratch on a deal Matt already opened.

Campspot, Podium, Addium — all moving to follow-up calls. Hannah should be running those. Matt should have debriefed her with: situation, pain, stakeholders, timeline, and the one thing that needs to be answered to advance the deal.
5. Follow-up calls should be Hannah's — not Matt running everything end to end Both

Right now Matt is doing both jobs: opening and closing. In a two-person sales structure, that's unsustainable at scale and means neither job gets done at its best. The competitive gap analysis calls, the proposal walkthroughs, the pricing and contract conversations — those belong to Hannah. Matt's time is best spent opening new relationships, especially at the founder or executive level where his credibility as a 15-year founder is the asset.

Campspot — Matt committed to delivering the competitive analysis and reconnecting May 6. That's a Hannah call. She runs the analysis walkthrough, builds the business case, and drives toward a decision. Matt stays available if the CEO needs to get involved.
6. Hannah needs a closing playbook for call 2+ Hannah

Hannah's closing calls need a clear structure: recap what was shared in call one, present the analysis or proposal, address the business case they need to make internally, handle objections, and advance to a next step with a specific commitment. Each call should end with the prospect either moving to a contract or clearly stating what the remaining obstacle is. Right now there's no documented playbook for what Hannah runs on call 2 and beyond.

With Addium, Podium, and Campspot all heading into follow-ups, Hannah needs a clear structure for each: what she's presenting, what questions she's asking, and what outcome she's driving toward before she hangs up.
7. Channel partner opportunities need to be recognized faster Matt

When an agency owner shows up, it's a different conversation — they're not a buyer, they're a potential source of multiple clients. Matt already does 25% of his business agency-to-agency. He should recognize that profile in the first few minutes and pivot to a partner conversation rather than a standard discovery call. The Kate deal is closing, but it happened by feel rather than by design.

Kate, mid-call: "I almost want to say to them, try this for this, and see how much further it goes." She's describing being a referral source. The right pivot: "We actually do 25% of our business through agency partnerships — white-label or referral. Given what you're describing, how do you typically handle bringing in outside partners for your clients?"
8. Pitch doesn't tailor to what was shared in discovery Matt

When Matt does pitch, it's the same pitch on every call. After Conner explains his cannabis SaaS situation, the pitch should reflect what that means specifically. After Lindsey describes a research study with a launch deadline, the pitch should be anchored to that. The pitch is a tool to advance qualification — and it works best when it mirrors what the prospect just said.

Conner call — after learning he's in cannabis B2B SaaS focused on SEO and GEO, Matt's pitch still runs through Forbes, Wired, Fortune generically. Conner asked about niche publications — that was the cue to get specific and show you understand his space.
9. Social proof doesn't match who's in the room Matt

Visa, Binance, SAP, and Pegasystems are strong names but create distance with smaller or mid-market companies. The subtext a Series A founder hears is "this was built for companies 10x our size." Lead with references that mirror the company stage and industry. If exact matches don't exist, use the category — "B2B SaaS companies at your stage, pre-brand awareness" — rather than the biggest logos in the portfolio.

Javed call — Matt opened with SAP, Pegasystems, Visa, Binance. Javed is a Series A company. A more effective opener would reference companies at a similar growth stage and show what those outcomes looked like specifically for them.

What to Improve — By Role

Matt's improvements are about running a better discovery call. Hannah's improvements are about building a closing motion. Both need a structural handoff process between them.

Matt — Discovery Call Improvements

1. Lead with discovery — understand before presenting

The first 5–7 minutes should be questions, not a company overview. Understand their situation, what they've tried, and what's driving the conversation before the pitch starts. The pitch is then a direct response to what they said — not a presentation that happens to them.

"Before I walk you through what we do — help me understand your situation first. What are you currently doing to build visibility and drive inbound, and where is it falling short?"
2. Ask business impact questions — not just operational ones

Operational questions (competitors, keywords, channels) tell you what to say. Business impact questions tell you whether this is a real priority and what's at stake. Matt needs to surface the "why now" and "what happens if this doesn't get solved" so Hannah has the ammunition to build the business case on call two.

"You mentioned you want visibility around the research study launch — what does it cost the business if that doesn't get picked up when it drops?"  /  "Is there a specific event or timeline driving this, or is this more of an ongoing priority?"
3. Identify the decision maker and buying process

Before the call ends, Matt should know: who makes the final call, who else is involved, and what the internal process looks like. This tells Hannah who to get in the room on call two and how to structure the proposal.

"Who else typically gets involved in a decision like this — is it just you, or is there a CEO or finance team that needs to weigh in? I want to make sure the right people are on the follow-up so we don't have to do this twice."
4. Tailor the pitch to what they just told you

When Matt does pitch, it should mirror what the prospect shared in discovery. Reference their industry, their specific situation, and the outcome that matters to them. Generic pitch = generic interest.

"Based on what you shared — new brand, cannabis B2B SaaS, SEO and GEO focused with no existing visibility — here's specifically how we'd approach this for you..." [tailor from their words, not from the standard deck]
5. Recognize agency/partner profiles and open that conversation

When an agency shows up — especially one that mentions multiple clients who could fit — pivot from direct sale to partner conversation in the first few minutes. Matt already does 25% of his business this way. Make it deliberate.

"About 25% of our business is agency-to-agency — white-label or referral. If this fits multiple clients of yours, how do you typically handle bringing in outside partners? That might be a more interesting conversation than pitching you directly."
6. Use relevant social proof — match company stage and industry

Keep Visa and Binance in the back pocket. Lead with references that mirror who's in the room. If an exact match doesn't exist, use the company stage or situation — "B2B SaaS companies at a similar stage, trying to get into the public conversation for the first time."

"We work with a lot of companies in your position — strong product, established team, but not yet appearing in the conversations your ICPs are having in search and AI. That gap is usually where we drive the biggest impact early."

Hannah — Closing Motion to Build

1. Build a standard call 2 structure

Every follow-up call Hannah runs should have the same skeleton: recap what was shared in call one (show you listened), present the analysis or proposal anchored to their specific situation, build the internal business case with them, handle objections, and land on a clear next step with a specific commitment. Structure removes friction and keeps deals moving.

"Before I walk you through the analysis — I want to make sure I'm framing this correctly. You mentioned [X] in our last call. Is that still the priority, or has anything shifted since then?"
2. Present the analysis as a business case, not a report

The competitive gap analysis is a strong asset. Hannah should present it in the language of business outcomes — what the gap is costing them, what closing it would mean in concrete terms, and what the minimum viable campaign looks like to get there. The analysis is the proof; the business case is the close.

"Here's what we found. Your top two competitors are dominating [X] and [Y] in search and AI results right now. Estimating conservatively, that's [N] monthly searches where your brand isn't appearing at all. Here's what a 6-month campaign would look like to close that gap specifically."
3. Surface and handle objections directly

In enterprise, objections in call two are usually about internal justification — budget approval, stakeholder alignment, ROI evidence, or timing. Hannah's job is to surface the real blocker, not just the stated one, and work through it with the prospect rather than around it. "Who else needs to be convinced of this, and what do they need to see?" is a useful question at this stage.

"What's the main question you'd need answered before moving forward — is it the budget, the ROI model, or getting another stakeholder comfortable with it? I want to make sure we're solving the right problem on this call."
4. End every call with a specific next step and a committed date

Call two should end with either a signed agreement, a clear path to one, or an explicit statement of what's blocking it. "I'll think about it" is not a next step. Hannah should get a specific answer to what happens next and when, before hanging up.

"Based on what we covered today — what's the next step on your end? And what date can we put on the calendar to reconnect so this doesn't lose momentum?" [If they're not ready: "What's the one thing that needs to be answered before you can move forward?"]

Structural — Both

Build the handoff brief — Matt to Hannah after every discovery call

After every call one, Matt should complete a short brief for Hannah: business situation, pain and business impact, decision maker and buying process, timeline, and his overall qualification read. This gives Hannah everything she needs to run a sharp follow-up without starting from scratch.

Handoff brief (after every discovery call):

Company / Contact:
Situation: [what they're doing now and where it's failing]
Business impact: [what's at stake if unsolved]
Decision maker: [who signs, who influences]
Timeline: [real deadline or exploratory]
Budget: [confirmed / needs to be built internally / unknown]
Qualified: [yes / no / needs more info]
Next step: [what Hannah is presenting on call 2]

What's Working — Don't Change This

Matt's tone is right for enterprise — relaxed, credible, not pushy. The competitive gap analysis as a follow-up offer is smart and should be used consistently. The Javed close, Pluck PR, Payaza, and RAAPID deals confirm the product sells and the team can close when the process is working. The improvements here are about deepening discovery and splitting the workload properly — not changing the pace or feel of the sales process.

Bottom Line

The biggest structural issue is that Matt is doing both jobs — opening and closing — without a clean handoff to Hannah in between. That's unsustainable at scale and means Hannah isn't getting the intel she needs to run sharp follow-up calls. Matt's discovery calls need to go deeper on business impact, decision maker, and timeline — not to close faster, but to hand Hannah a brief she can actually use. Once that handoff is working, the two-person structure becomes the asset it's supposed to be.
