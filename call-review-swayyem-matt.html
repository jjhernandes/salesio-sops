The 9 Gaps

1. Pitch before discovery High

In 4 of 6 calls, Matt opens with a company monologue before the prospect has said anything meaningful. The pitch can't be tailored to a situation that hasn't been shared yet. The prospect ends up hearing a generic presentation instead of feeling like the solution was built for them. The Campspot and Pluck PR calls are exceptions — and they're noticeably better for it.

Afra call, 0:54 — "So I'll jump in, give you a bit of background on us..." — 4 minutes of pitch before a single question. Javed call, 1:14 — same pattern, even though Javed already knew the product from Mayur.
2. No pain or consequence questions High

Matt asks informational questions — competitors, traffic sources, customer acquisition — but never digs into pain. He never asks: What have you tried? What happened? What's it costing you to not solve this? Without pain, there's no urgency, and the prospect leaves the call interested but not compelled. Across all 6 calls, pain was zero'd in on zero times.

Conner said "we need brand visibility for this new entity." Matt moved straight to pitch. The real question: what happens to Addium's ability to raise or attract partners if no one knows it exists? That question never got asked.
3. Exclusivity is completely wasted High

"We only take one client per industry niche" is mentioned once across all 6 calls — as a casual reassurance answer to Afra's question about competitors. This is their single biggest urgency lever and it's being treated like a footnote. Every interested prospect should hear this before the call ends, framed proactively: if a competitor moves first, the space is gone.

Afra call, 7:42 — said once in passing, never returned to. Javed, Conner, Dakota, Kate — never mentioned. In a 15-minute call with a prospect showing real interest, this should come up before the next steps.
4. Budget objections accepted without a fight High

When Dakota said "I don't think we could make it work this year — maybe Q4," Matt said "yeah, sure." That's revenue walking out the door. The right response is to understand what's actually blocking it: Is it truly budget? Is it the inability to track ROI? Is it competing priorities? Each of those has a different response. Accepting "maybe Q4" without a single follow-up question is a soft pass dressed up as a courtesy.

Dakota call — "This may be something we'd want to revisit in Q4." Matt: "Yeah, sure." No pushback, no ROI reframe, no urgency. A better response: "What would need to change about the business case for this to make sense this quarter rather than Q4?"
5. Agency partner opportunity completely missed High

Kate Gundry at Pluck PR is not a client — she's a potential channel partner. She represents 10–12 clients, runs a boutique firm of 12 people, identified 2–3 clients herself that are perfect fits (Pearl, YouNow), and basically pitched SwayyEm's value back to Matt. He treated her like any other prospect. A partner conversation could yield 3 clients through one relationship and zero acquisition cost.

Kate, mid-call: "I almost want to say to them, try this for this, and see how much further it goes." She is selling herself on becoming a referral source. Matt never asked: "Does Pluck PR ever bring in outside agencies for clients? What would that partnership look like?"
6. Warm leads treated the same as cold High

Javed came in pre-qualified by Mayur — he'd already read the deck, looked at the pricing page, and had specific questions about packages and payment terms. He was ready to move. Matt still opened with the standard company pitch. The result: Javed closed himself. That's fine, but a better approach would have been to skip the pitch, confirm what Mayur had covered, and spend those 15 minutes getting Javed to a larger package or a signed agreement on the call.

Javed call — "He forwarded your conversation thread to me, and that's why I decided I should book this call to understand more in detail." This is a warm handoff. The right opening: "Great — so you've seen the deck. What questions came up that you want to dig into?"
7. Generic pitch — doesn't change with context Medium

The core pitch is nearly identical across all 6 calls: 75,000 publications, tier-1 placements, Forbes/Wired/Fortune, editorial content, guaranteed placements, link back to site. After Conner explains he's in cannabis B2B SaaS, the pitch doesn't shift to cannabis-specific publications or outcomes. After Afra says they do zero marketing at all, the pitch doesn't adjust for a company with no existing funnel. Prospects can tell when a pitch wasn't built for them.

Conner call — after learning the focus is cannabis cultivation software, Matt's pitch still references Forbes, Wired, Fortune. He never named a single relevant publication for the cannabis or ag-tech space.
8. Passive close on every call Medium

"I'll shoot over an invite" is not a close. It's a handoff. Five of six calls end with Matt promising to send something — a deck, an invite, an NDA. The prospect leaves with no skin in the game and no real commitment made. Before hanging up, Matt should get a verbal answer to: "If we come back with X on our next call, is this something you're ready to move on?" That answer changes how the next call is prepared.

Conner — "I'd love to see the deck and shop it around." Matt: "Cool. Yeah..." — Conner just said he's going to show it to competitors. That's the moment to respond, not to let it pass.
9. Social proof doesn't match the room Lower

Visa and Binance are dropped as name-drops across multiple calls regardless of who's in the room. For a cannabis SaaS startup (Conner) or an ed-tech company (Podium), those names create distance rather than credibility. The prospect thinks "that's great for enterprise brands — we're not that." Social proof should feel like a mirror: "We've worked with companies in your space doing exactly what you're trying to do."

Javed call — Matt mentions SAP, Pegasystems, Visa, Binance upfront. Javed is a Series A startup. Those references don't make him feel understood — they make the service feel built for someone else.

What to Fix and How

Fix 1: Flip the opening — discovery first, every call

Don't offer any background on the company until you understand the prospect's situation. The first 3–5 minutes should be questions. Then your pitch is a response to what they said, not a prepared presentation that happens to them.

"Before I give you any background on us, I'd love to understand where you're at. What's working for you right now in terms of getting in front of new clients online — and where are the gaps?" [Then listen. Build the pitch from their words.]
Fix 2: Ask the pain question — then dig on consequences

Informational discovery tells you what to say. Pain discovery tells you why they'll buy. Get to the cost of the problem. Lost deals, time wasted on bad outreach, competitors gaining ground, a CEO who can't get coverage. That's what creates urgency — not a list of your features.

"You mentioned you've tried PR before and didn't get pickup — what happened from there? What did that cost you in time and opportunity? What does it mean for the business if you don't crack this in the next 6 months?"
Fix 3: Use exclusivity as a close — every call, proactively

This is the most underused weapon across all 6 calls. Once a prospect shows real interest, bring it up before the call ends — not as an answer to their question, but as a proactive statement. This is the urgency lever that changes "I'll think about it" to "let's move."

"One thing worth knowing before we wrap — we only work with one client per industry category. So if a competitor books a call next week and moves forward, your space is taken. I'm not saying that to pressure you, but it's real. If this looks like a fit, it's worth not sitting on it."
Fix 4: Fight the budget objection before accepting it

When a prospect says "maybe later" or "let me check the budget," that's a soft no — not a closed door. Dig before accepting it. Find out if it's truly budget, or if it's uncertainty about ROI, internal approval friction, or competing priorities. Each of those has a different answer.

"Before we push this to Q4 — help me understand. Is it the budget itself, or is it more about not having a clear ROI story to take internally? Because if it's the latter, that's actually what our follow-up analysis is designed to solve."
Fix 5: Recognize agency partners and pivot the conversation

When an agency owner shows up, the conversation structure is totally different. They're not a buyer — they're a potential channel. The goal is to understand how they handle gaps in their offering, and whether a referral or white-label arrangement makes sense. Matt works with agencies (25% of business) — he should say so and open that door.

"Actually, about 25% of our business comes from PR agencies who either white-label our service or refer clients who need this. Given what you're describing with Pearl and YouNow — how does Pluck typically handle situations where a client needs something outside your core?"
Fix 6: Treat warm leads differently — skip the pitch, go deeper

When a prospect comes in pre-qualified, the pitch is already done. Use the call to uncover what questions they still have, confirm fit, and push for a larger commitment or a same-call next step. The warm lead already believes — now help them decide.

"Sounds like Mayur already walked you through the core of what we do — so let me skip the background and just ask: what questions came up that you want to dig into? And what would need to be true for you to say yes to the 6-month versus the pilot?"
Fix 7: Tailor the pitch to what they just told you

After discovery, connect what the prospect said directly to what you offer. Don't run the standard pitch. Restate their situation in their words, then show how the solution maps to it. The pitch should feel like it was written for this person specifically.

"So based on what you shared — you're a new brand, no existing visibility, in a niche where trade pubs don't move the needle — what we'd actually do for you is [specific approach]. Here's why that matters for your situation specifically..."
Fix 8: Don't leave without a real commitment

Before hanging up, get an answer to one of these: "What would you need to see on our next call to be ready to move forward?" or "Is the budget something you'd have access to this quarter if the analysis looks right?" That answer either advances the sale or surfaces the real objection — both are useful.

"Before we wrap — I want to make sure our next call is worth both our time. If we come back with the competitive analysis and it shows a real gap between you and [competitor], is this something you'd be ready to move on this quarter?"

Priority Order

  • 1 — Stop pitching before discovering. Root cause of most other issues. Nothing else improves until this changes.
  • 2 — Ask pain and consequence questions. Surface questions = surface interest. Pain = urgency and real reasons to buy.
  • 3 — Use exclusivity proactively on every call. It's your best urgency lever. It's been used once across 6 calls. That has to change.
  • 4 — Fight the budget objection. "Maybe Q4" is a soft no. Dig into what's actually blocking it before accepting it.
  • 5 — Build an agency partner channel starting with Kate. One conversation with Kate could yield 2–3 clients. Follow up with a partner proposal, not a sales deck.
  • 6 — Treat warm leads differently. When Mayur pre-qualifies someone, skip the pitch and go straight to commitment.
  • 7 — Tailor the pitch to what they said in discovery. Same pitch every call means it's not a pitch — it's a presentation.
  • 8 — Leave every call with a real commitment, not a promise to send something.
  • 9 — Match social proof to the room. Visa and Binance don't resonate with a cannabis startup or a boutique PR agency.

The Kate Opportunity — Don't Let This One Slide

Kate Gundry is the most valuable contact in this batch and the call that needs the most urgent follow-up — not with a standard sales deck, but with a partner proposal. She runs a 12-person boutique firm with 10–12 clients. She identified Pearl and YouNow on her own as perfect fits. She's been in PR for 16 years and understands exactly what SwayyEm does and why it's different. She needs a white-label or referral arrangement, not a pitch. One properly structured follow-up here could be worth $16–24K/month in revenue through a single relationship.

Bottom Line

Matt knows his product, he's easy to talk to, and he does the prep work — those are real assets. The issue is he's running a presentation, not a sales call. The prospect drives the conversation, reveals objections on their own terms, and often closes themselves (Javed) or doesn't close at all (Dakota, Afra). The fix isn't more product knowledge — it's slowing down, staying in discovery longer, asking the uncomfortable follow-up question, and building urgency from the prospect's own words before the pitch ever starts. That shift changes the conversion rate more than anything else on this list.
