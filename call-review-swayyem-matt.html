Matt — What You Do as the Founder

Why your approach works

You built this company over 15 years. When you get on a call, that credibility walks in before you say a word. Prospects give you more latitude because they are talking to the person who built the product and the relationships. You can open with a pitch and still get a warm response. You can take a relaxed, conversational approach and close a pilot in one call because your track record does a lot of the work. That is not a technique. It is the result of who you are in this space, and it is important to understand that because the approach Hannah needs to take is going to look different.

What is working

  • Opening with the company story and letting credibility carry it
  • Relaxed, peer-level tone with enterprise contacts
  • Pre-call research building trust fast (Campspot)
  • Competitive gap analysis as a follow-up offer
  • Closing Javed on a pilot in one call on a warm lead
  • Comfortable with agency owners and founders at a peer level
  • Deep product knowledge on every call

Where your calls have gaps

  • Discovery is mostly operational, not business impact
  • Decision maker and buying process rarely identified
  • Pitch does not shift based on what the prospect shared
  • Social proof does not match company size or stage
  • Kate call: partner opportunity was not opened deliberately
  • Afra call: gatekeeper not identified or routed around

What to take away, Matt

Your results are strong and the product is closing. The one thing that would make your calls sharper is going deeper on business impact. You ask great operational questions but rarely get to what is at stake for the business if this does not get solved. That context is what separates a real priority from a nice-to-have, and it is what makes follow-up conversations much easier to close. The Campspot call is your best model. Pre-research, discovery first, gap analysis as the value-add for the next step. That is the template.

Hannah — Running These Calls Going Forward

What your approach needs to look like

You do not have 15 years of founder credibility walking into the room with you, and that is fine. It just means your process needs to be more deliberate. Where Matt can open with a pitch and rely on his story to carry the room, you need to earn the right to pitch by asking the right questions first. Where Matt closes a warm lead in one call because the prospect already trusts the founder, you need a tighter structure to get to the same outcome. The good news is that structure is learnable and once it is built, it works on every call regardless of who the prospect is.

On Discovery Calls

Lead with questions, not the pitch

The first 5 to 7 minutes of every call should be you asking questions, not presenting. Understand their current situation before you say anything about SwayyEm. This does two things: the prospect feels heard, and you get the information you need to make the pitch specific to their situation rather than generic.

"Before I walk you through what we do, help me understand where you are right now. What are you currently doing to build visibility and get in front of new clients, and where is it falling short?"
Get to business impact, not just operations

Asking about competitors, channels, and keywords tells you what to say. Asking about business impact tells you why they will buy. You need to know what happens to the business if this problem does not get solved, whether there is a deadline or launch creating pressure, and whether this is a genuine priority or something they are exploring. Those answers determine how you run the rest of the cycle.

"You mentioned you want visibility around this product launch. What happens if that launch does not get picked up publicly? What is at stake for the business?" / "Is there a specific timeline driving this, or is it more of an ongoing priority?"
Find out who makes the decision

Before the call ends, you need to know who signs, who influences, and whether the person you are talking to can actually say yes. Map this out on every call. It tells you who needs to be on the next call and how to structure the proposal so it speaks to the right people.

"Who else typically gets involved in a decision like this? Is it just you, or does your CEO or finance team need to weigh in? I want to make sure the right people are on our next call."
Tailor the pitch to what they just told you

After discovery, connect what they said directly to what you offer. Reference their specific situation, their industry, the problem they just described. Do not run the standard pitch. The prospect should feel like the solution was built for their situation, not pulled from a deck. Generic pitch equals generic interest.

"Based on what you shared, here is specifically what we would do for a company in your situation..." [build from their exact words]
Use social proof that matches who is in the room

Dropping Visa and Binance can create distance with a Series A startup or a mid-market SaaS company. The prospect hears "built for companies 10 times our size." Lead with companies at a similar stage and situation. If you do not have an exact match, use the category and the situation instead of the logo.

"We work with a lot of B2B companies at your stage, strong product but not yet showing up in the conversations your buyers are having. That gap is usually where we see the biggest early impact."
Recognize agency partners and run a different conversation

When an agency or consultancy gets on a call with you, they are not a direct buyer. They are a potential source of multiple clients. About 25% of SwayyEm's business comes through agency partnerships. When you see this profile, open the partner conversation early and deliberately. Do not wait for them to bring it up.

"About 25% of our business comes through agency partnerships, white-label or referral. Given what you are describing with your clients, how do you typically handle situations where a client needs something outside your core?"

On Follow-Up and Closing Calls

Run every follow-up with a clear structure

Every follow-up call should follow the same skeleton: recap what was shared on the first call to show you listened, present the analysis or proposal tied to their specific situation, build the business case with them, handle objections, and close on a committed next step before you hang up. Structure keeps deals moving and signals professionalism without the founder name behind it.

"Before I walk you through the analysis, I want to make sure I am framing this correctly. You mentioned [X] last time. Is that still the main priority, or has anything shifted?"
Present the analysis as a business case, not a report

The competitive gap analysis is a strong tool. Present it in business outcome terms: what the gap is costing them right now, what closing it would look like, and what the minimum campaign looks like to get there. The analysis is the proof. The business case is what gets the internal approval to move forward.

"Here is what we found. Your top competitors are showing up consistently for the terms your buyers are searching. That is qualified attention going to them instead of you every month. Here is what a 6-month campaign would look like to change that specifically."
Surface the real objection and handle it directly

In enterprise sales, the objection on call two is usually about internal justification: budget approval, getting another stakeholder comfortable, or needing more evidence. Find the real blocker and work through it, not around it. Asking who else needs to be convinced and what they need to see is often the most useful question at this stage.

"What is the main thing standing between where we are now and a decision? Is it the budget, getting someone else comfortable, or something else? I want to make sure we are solving the right problem on this call."
End every call with a committed next step

Every call should end with either a decision or a specific next step with a date both sides agree to. Before you hang up, get an answer to what happens next and by when. If they are not ready, find out what the one remaining obstacle is. "I will think about it" is not a next step.

"Based on what we covered today, what is the next step on your end? What date can we put on the calendar so this does not lose momentum?" / "What is the one thing that needs to be answered before you can move forward?"
