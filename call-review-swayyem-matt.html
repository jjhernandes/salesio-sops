Why Matt's Approach Works for Him

The Founder Advantage

Matt built this company over 15 years. When he gets on a call, that fact is doing a lot of the work before he says a word. Prospects give him more latitude because they are talking to the person who built the product and the relationships. He can open with a pitch and still get a warm response. He can take a relaxed, conversational approach and close a pilot in one call because his credibility walks in ahead of him. That is not a sales technique you can copy. It is the result of who he is in this space.

What Matt does that works

  • Opens with the company story and lets credibility do the work
  • Relaxed, peer-level tone with enterprise contacts
  • Pre-call research builds instant trust (Campspot)
  • Competitive gap analysis as a follow-up offer
  • Closes Javed on a pilot in one call on a warm lead
  • Comfortable talking to agency owners and founders directly
  • Handles tough product questions without hesitation

Where Matt's calls have gaps

  • Discovery is mostly operational, not business impact
  • Decision maker and buying process rarely identified
  • Pitch does not shift based on what was shared
  • Social proof does not match company size or stage
  • Kate call: did not recognize the partner opportunity by design
  • Afra call: did not route around the gatekeeper

What Hannah Needs to Do Differently

Taking Over These Calls

Hannah does not have 15 years of founder credibility walking into the room with her. That is not a knock on her ability. It just means the approach has to be more deliberate. Where Matt can open with a pitch and rely on his story to carry the room, Hannah needs to earn the right to pitch by asking the right questions first. Where Matt closes a warm lead in one call because the prospect trusts the founder, Hannah needs a tighter process to get to the same outcome. The good news is that process is learnable and consistent. Once it is built, it works on every call regardless of who is running it.

Discovery Calls

Lead with questions, not the pitch

Matt can open with the company story and get away with it because his name and tenure carry weight. Hannah needs to flip this. The first 5 to 7 minutes should be questions about the prospect's current situation before she says anything about SwayyEm. This does two things: it makes the prospect feel heard, and it gives Hannah the information she needs to make the pitch specific to them.

"Before I walk you through what we do, help me understand where you are right now. What are you currently doing to build visibility and get in front of new clients, and where is it falling short?"
Get to business impact, not just operations

Matt asks good operational questions: who are your competitors, what channels are you using. Hannah should go one level deeper and get to business impact. What happens to the company if this problem does not get solved? Is there a deadline, a launch, a competitive threat that is creating pressure? Those answers are what determine whether this is a real priority or a nice-to-have, and they are what make the follow-up proposal land.

"You mentioned you want visibility around this product launch. What happens to the business if that launch does not get picked up publicly? What is at stake?" / "Is there a specific timeline or internal event driving this, or is this more of an ongoing priority?"
Identify who makes the decision

Hannah needs to know before the call ends who signs, who influences, and whether the person she is talking to can actually say yes. Matt can sometimes skip this because his name gets him escalated quickly. Hannah needs to map this out deliberately so she knows how to structure the next step and whether she needs to get additional stakeholders in the room.

"Who else typically gets involved in a decision like this? Is it just you, or does your CEO or finance team need to be part of the conversation? I want to make sure the right people are on our next call."
Tailor the pitch to what they just told you

Matt runs a version of the same pitch on most calls and it works because his delivery and credibility fill the gaps. Hannah needs the pitch to feel specific to each prospect. After discovery, reference what they said, name the situations that match their industry, and connect the solution directly to the problem they just described. Generic pitch equals generic interest.

"Based on what you shared, here is specifically what we would do for a company in your situation..." [then build from their exact words, not from a standard script]
Use relevant social proof, not the biggest logos

Matt dropping Visa and Binance works because the founder of the company is saying it. Coming from a rep, it can actually create distance. Prospects at a Series A startup or a mid-market SaaS company hear "built for companies 10 times our size." Hannah should lead with companies at a similar stage and situation, or use the category if specific names are not available.

"We work with a lot of B2B companies at your stage, strong product but not yet showing up in the conversations your buyers are having. That gap is usually where we see the biggest early impact."
Spot agency partners and run a different conversation

When an agency or consultancy shows up on a call, they are not a direct buyer. They are a potential source of multiple clients. Matt figured this out naturally with Kate, and it is closing. Hannah needs to recognize this profile earlier and pivot the conversation intentionally. One agency partner can bring in three to five clients with no additional outreach cost.

"About 25% of our business comes through agency partnerships, white-label or referral. Given what you are describing with your clients, how do you typically handle situations where a client needs something outside your core?"

Follow-Up and Closing Calls

Run every follow-up call with a clear structure

Every call two should follow the same skeleton: recap what was shared on call one to show you listened, present the analysis or proposal tied to their specific situation, build the business case they need to make internally, handle objections, and close on a specific next step with a committed date. Structure keeps deals moving and shows professionalism without the founder name behind it.

"Before I walk you through the analysis, I want to make sure I am framing this correctly for your situation. You mentioned [X] last time. Is that still the main priority, or has anything shifted?"
Present the analysis as a business case, not a report

The competitive gap analysis is a strong tool. Hannah should present it in business outcome terms: what the gap is costing them right now, what closing it would look like, and what the minimum campaign looks like to get there. The analysis is the proof. The business case is what gets the internal approval.

"Here is what we found. Your top competitors are showing up consistently for the terms your buyers are searching. Conservatively, that is several hundred qualified searches a month where your brand is not in the conversation. Here is what a 6-month campaign would look like to change that specifically."
Surface and handle objections directly

In enterprise sales, the objection on call two is usually about internal justification: budget approval, getting another stakeholder comfortable, or needing more evidence. Hannah's job is to find the real blocker and work through it, not around it. "Who else needs to be convinced, and what do they need to see?" is often the most useful question at this stage.

"What is the main thing standing between where we are now and a decision? Is it the budget, getting someone else comfortable with it, or something else? I want to make sure we are working on the right problem."
End every call with a committed next step

Every call should end with either a decision or a specific next step with a date both sides agree to. "I will think about it" is not a next step. Before hanging up, Hannah needs an answer to what happens next and by when. If the prospect is not ready, she needs to know what the one remaining obstacle is.

"Based on what we covered today, what is the next step on your end? And what date can we put on the calendar so this does not lose momentum?" / "What is the one thing that needs to be answered before you can move forward?"

What to Focus On

Matt — What Worked and What to Learn From

  • The Campspot call is the closest to the right model: pre-call research, discovery before pitch, gap analysis as the follow-up offer. That is the template.
  • The competitive gap analysis is a strong differentiator. It should be offered on every eligible call where the prospect cannot justify cost without seeing the data first.
  • The one consistent gap across all calls: business impact questions. Operational questions qualify the company. Impact questions qualify the urgency and give the follow-up a real anchor.
  • The partner opportunity with Kate worked but happened by feel. Recognizing that profile early and opening the partner conversation deliberately is a repeatable play.

Hannah

  • Discovery first on every call. Questions before pitch. No exceptions.
  • Find the business impact, not just the operations. What is at stake if this does not get solved?
  • Know who makes the decision before the call ends. Map the buying committee.
  • Make the pitch specific to what they just told you. Generic pitch equals generic interest.
  • Follow-up calls: recap, present the business case, handle the real objection, commit to a next step before hanging up.
  • When an agency owner shows up, ask about the partner model early. That is a different conversation than a standard discovery call.
  • Lead with social proof that matches company stage. Save the enterprise logos for when they fit.

Bottom Line

Matt's approach works because 15 years of founder credibility is doing a lot of the work. The deals closing right now are proof of that. Hannah taking over these calls is the right move for scale, and she has the skills to do it well. The difference is that she needs to earn the trust Matt walks in with. That means better discovery, sharper questions, more deliberate process. Not more pressure. The goal is the same. The path to get there looks slightly different.
