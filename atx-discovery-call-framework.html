Before You Start

How to Use This Framework

This is not a script. It is a sequence. Know it well enough that you do not need to read it — so when the prospect goes off in a direction, you can meet them there and still get back on track.

The one thing that changes everything on Call 1: stop trying to explain the product and start trying to understand the person. The three calls reviewed all had the same gap — Arun moved to education too quickly before the prospect felt fully heard. Everything below is designed to fix that.
What Call 1 is for
Understanding their situation fully
Surfacing the real pain behind their interest
Qualifying capital, structure, and timeline
Building enough trust to earn a second call
Knowing exactly which deal to present on Call 2
What Call 1 is NOT for
Walking through the full product in detail
Presenting specific deals or yields
Talking about your own portfolio
Overcoming every objection
Closing anything
Step 1 · 3–4 min

Open & Frame

The frame you set in the first 90 seconds determines whether this call feels like a consultation or a sales pitch. Set it deliberately.

3 – 4 minutes
What the calls revealed: Arun opened all three calls with a company description. It was professional, but it immediately positioned him as a presenter rather than a consultant. The prospect sat back and listened instead of leaning in. A stronger opening flips that dynamic.
Current opening (all three calls)

"I'm one of the Senior Investment Consultants here at the organisation. What we do essentially is government-funded investments — supported living, social housing, children care homes, elderly care..."

Improved opening — frame first, then context

"[Name], good to connect. Before I tell you anything about what we do, I want to spend most of this call understanding your situation. I've got a few specific questions about your background, what you're looking for, and what has and hasn't worked so far. If what you describe lines up with what we work on, I'll set up a second call and walk you through a specific opportunity tailored to exactly what you've told me. If it doesn't line up, I'll tell you that straight. Does that work for you?"

Why this works: It removes pressure immediately. It positions you as a specialist who qualifies clients rather than a salesperson chasing anyone. It sets the expectation that Call 2 will be personalised — which builds anticipation. And it gets a verbal yes before you've said anything about the product.
After they confirm — one-line context

Only then, give a single sentence of context: "Just so you know where I'm sitting — we specialise in government-funded property investment. Think commercial real estate, but with government-backed income and no landlord duties. That's all I'll say about it for now — I'd rather hear about you first."

Experienced investor track (Jason's call): When the prospect already has supported living or social housing in their portfolio, skip the education framing entirely. Open with what you already know: "From what you told me, you've been in this space and you know how it works. So rather than walking through the basics, let me ask a few sharper questions — and then I'll show you what I have that I think could fit you specifically." These prospects do not need convincing the sector works. They need evidence that this specific deal and provider are sound.
Step 2 · 5–7 min

Quick Qualification

Get the hard facts early — not to screen people out harshly, but so you don't spend 30 minutes with someone who cannot move forward. Ask naturally, as part of a conversation.

5 – 7 minutes
From the calls: The capital question was asked late in all three conversations (20+ minutes in). By then, time had been spent building context for someone whose situation might not have been right. Ask this earlier — but warm it up first with one background question.
The three things you need to know
Capital available — cash ready to move, not contingent on a sale or raise
Decision maker — are they the one who decides, or is a partner/adviser involved
Genuine intent — actively looking, not just browsing
Sequence — run in this order

"Tell me a bit about your background — are you currently invested in property, or is this a new area for you?"

After they answer

"And what made this a priority now — what's the trigger that got you looking at this specifically?"

Capital — ask it clean, not awkwardly

"Before I go any further — the investments we work with are cash purchases. There's no mortgage product available for this asset class, so it does require capital that's ready to move. Is that where you are, or is there a bridging piece involved?"

If they hesitate on capital: "That's not me trying to put a number on you — I just want to make sure I'm not walking you through something that isn't the right fit for your position right now. Even knowing roughly the range helps me point you to the right opportunity."
Structure question — ask this if capital is confirmed

"Are you looking to purchase in a personal name, limited company, or do you have a family investment structure in place?" — This opens the tax structuring conversation naturally, which is ATX's biggest differentiator and often the one that surprises experienced investors most.

Ask about bad past experiences — this is gold for the presentation. Jason had a provider hand back 10 properties with 12 months left on the contract, then ignore his calls, then take legal action. That single experience shapes everything about what he needs to feel confident. If you know this before Call 2, you know exactly which part of the operator due diligence to lead with.
Lesson from Kevin: Kevin had a FIC already set up — Arun noticed it and it became a strong moment of trust. From now on, always ask about structure early. It shows sophistication and often reveals whether the tax conversation is worth having on this call or saved for Call 2.
Step 3 · 10–12 min

Discover

This is the most important part of the call. The information you get here is what you build the entire Call 2 presentation around. Take notes. Use their exact words when you echo back.

10 – 12 minutes
The pattern from all three calls: Every prospect had a clear frustration they were ready to share — Syed hated management hassle and non-ownership deals, Kevin hated not being in control of his money, VJ had tenants ignoring court orders and costs eating into returns. None of them needed to be convinced there was a problem. They needed to feel that Arun understood their specific problem. Ask, listen, and echo back. That is what builds trust with analytical investors.
1

Current situation

"Walk me through your current property portfolio — what you own, how it's structured, and how it's performing."

Let them talk. Do not interrupt to explain something or offer a comparison. Just listen.

2

What has frustrated them

"What's been the main frustration with the way things are set up right now? Not just the obvious stuff — what specifically is draining your time or eating into your returns?"

This is where the real pain comes out. Syed mentioned religion-based objections to interest. Kevin mentioned not being in control. VJ mentioned tenants ignoring court orders. These are the things that matter — not generic BTL complaints.

3

What they have tried

"Have you looked at this kind of investment before, or spoken to anyone else in this space? How did that go?"

This tells you their baseline of knowledge, what has let them down, and what objections are already formed. Syed had tried three different companies and was frustrated by the loan-style products with no ownership. Knowing that before the pitch means you lead with ownership immediately on Call 2.

4

Their actual goal

"What does the end goal look like for you? Not just a return number — what does this money need to do for your life?"

VJ said £20K/month passive income and something to pass to his children. Kevin said passive income while he travels and semi-retires. Syed wanted hands-off income while he travels between UK and Pakistan. These are different goals that require different framing on Call 2.

5

Timeline and urgency

"Is this something you're actively looking to move on in the next few weeks, or more of a longer research phase?"

Do not manufacture urgency. Find out if it is real. If the capital is sitting in stocks or accounts doing nothing useful, that is real urgency. If they are in the middle of a sale or raise, it is not. Know the difference before Call 2.

After discovery, echo it back before moving on: "Let me just make sure I've got this right — you've been in property since [X], your main frustration is [their words], the goal is [their goal], and the timing is [their timeline]. Have I got that right?" This single move builds more trust than anything you could say about the product.
Step 4 · 4–5 min

Build the Problem

Only do this if they have BTL or residential property. Use their specific frustrations, not a generic list. The goal is not to educate them — it's to confirm that what they already know is actually as bad as they think.

4 – 5 minutes
From the calls: All three prospects already knew BTL was broken. Arun listed the problems accurately but generically. The stronger move is to reference what they specifically said and confirm it — then add the data they might not have.
Mirror their pain, then add the data

"You mentioned [their specific frustration — e.g. 'tenants ignoring court orders' / 'management eating into returns' / 'not wanting to be dependent on an individual tenant']. That's not bad luck — that's the direction the market has been moving for the last five years. Section 21 is gone. Four months notice before you can sell. EPC upgrades mandatory by 2028. Two-month rolling contracts. The investors who are building serious wealth right now have moved away from that model. That's the whole reason this conversation is happening."

Keep this brief. You are not delivering a lecture — you are validating what they already know and creating the contrast that makes the alternative feel necessary. Two minutes maximum. Then move to credibility.
Step 5 · 3–4 min

Credibility & Trust

These are analytical buyers. They are checking whether you are real before they agree to a second call. Give them what they need to feel safe — without overwhelming them with information.

3 – 4 minutes
From the calls: VJ looked up ATX Global on Companies House mid-call and got confused by the brand structure. Kevin wanted to understand exactly who pays him and whether there is a middleman. Syed needed to understand the ownership model. All three prospects had a version of "is this real?" — address it proactively, briefly, and move on.
The credibility bridge — say this once, cleanly

"Before I say anything else — you will want to do your own due diligence on us, and you should. Our main company is ATX Global. We trade as Assisted Living Investments. You can find us on Companies House, our Google page lists our office on Great Portland Street in London, and I will send you all of that after this call. We have placed over £162 million in investments across 176+ properties. 96% of our clients reinvest. Our own capital is in this sector alongside our clients — every deal we bring you is one we would put our own money into."

The payment structure — address it before they ask

"One thing people always want to understand early: we are a brokerage. We get paid by the seller. We do not take a fee from you in any way. The contract you sign is directly between you and the housing association — they pay you directly. We are out of the relationship once the deal completes. You will never be dependent on us for your income."

How ATX earns — answer this before they ask

"One thing I always like to be upfront about: we are a brokerage. We earn a percentage from the seller — typically 2 to 4% of the sale price. We do not charge you anything. Your capital goes through your solicitor directly, never through us. This came up in four separate calls — Kevin, VJ, Jason all asked a version of it. Answer it once, early, and move on."

Lesson from VJ: When he searched Companies House mid-call, it created confusion and almost derailed the conversation. Proactively giving the correct name (Assisted Living Investments, registered as ATX Global) and acknowledging the brand structure removes that moment entirely.
Step 6 · 3–4 min

Book Call 2

This is the only close on this call. Get the calendar invite confirmed before they leave the Zoom. A prospect who leaves Call 1 without a booked Call 2 is a prospect you are following up cold.

3 – 4 minutes
From the calls: Syed's Call 2 was booked, but Arun had to leave for another call and the booking felt rushed. Kevin's was smooth. VJ's was good. The pattern: when Arun set up the second call as "education, not a commitment," the prospect relaxed and booked easily. Keep that framing.
Transition to booking

"Based on everything you've told me — [brief echo: their capital, their goal, their situation] — I know exactly which opportunity I want to walk you through. I'm going to build a presentation specifically around your situation, not a generic deck. The second call is about 45 minutes. It's not a call to move forward — it's purely so you have everything you need to make an informed decision. What does your calendar look like in the next two to three days?"

Do not leave it open. Give two specific options: "I've got Thursday at 10 or Friday at 2 — which works better for you?" Giving options closes faster than asking an open question.
If they want to "think about it" before booking

"Totally fine. What's the one thing you need to think through? If it's about the deal itself — that's exactly what the second call is for. If there's something about the process or our company, I'd rather answer it now than have it sit as an unanswered question."

After they confirm — follow-up message same day

Send a short WhatsApp or email: "[Name] — great to connect. Our call is confirmed for [date/time]. I'll be putting together a specific presentation based on what you shared — [one line summary of their situation]. Talk soon."

Post-Call 2 Email Checklist (from Jason's call)

Jason's action items were clear but almost missed because they piled up at the end of the call. Build this email before you hang up, not after. Send within 2 hours.

Property addresses for their preferred location
Full financial breakdown — purchase price, annual rent, monthly rent, net yield, CPI clause
Operator accounts (Nurture Housing or relevant provider)
Housing and Planning Act 2016 legislation link
Developer info (Gleeson or relevant builder — Companies House, listed status)
One-paragraph summary of the deal and why it fits their situation
Suggested next call date and time — already agreed on the call
Objection Handles

Common Objections — From These Calls

Every objection below appeared in at least one of the three recorded calls. These are not hypotheticals.

"I couldn't find much about your company on Companies House."
"Fair question — our legal entity is ATX Global Ltd, but we trade as Assisted Living Investments and Supported Living Investments. We also have a brand called One Touch. I know it sounds like a lot — I'll send you the Companies House link, our website, and our Google business page right after this call so you can verify everything independently. We're based in Great Portland Street in Central London."

From VJ's call — he looked it up mid-call and got confused. Get ahead of it.
"The other companies I spoke to just offered loan-style products with no ownership."
"That is completely different to what we do. What you're describing is a bond or loan note — you give money, you get a fixed return, and you have no asset. We are talking about property ownership. Your name is on the title deed. It sits on your balance sheet. You can sell it, refinance it, or pass it on. The contract is between you and the housing association. We are just the broker."

From Syed's call — he had seen three different loan-style products and was frustrated. This distinction closed his objection immediately.
"The rent looks too high. £2,800 a month on a £260k property doesn't feel real."
"I understand why it feels that way — it's a very different model to standard residential. In Supported Living, the government pays twice: a care package and accommodation costs separately. The operator receives what are called exempt rents, which sit well above standard market rates. That's why the yield is 13% rather than 4%. It's not too good to be true — it's a different sector with a different funding mechanism. I'll show you exactly how the money flows on our next call."

From Kevin's call — he questioned the £2,800 figure directly. Explaining exempt rents resolved it.
"What if the housing association goes bust?"
"That's the most important question you can ask, and I'd rather you asked it now. The short answer: the government has built a legal mechanism specifically for this scenario. It's called the Housing and Planning Act 2016. A housing association cannot be wound up without 28 days' notice to the Regulator of Social Housing. Another provider steps in. The income continues. I'll send you the full legislation link, and I'll walk you through three real cases where this happened — and every time, the income was protected. This sector has been tested. It has got stronger each time."

From Kevin's call — asked directly and well. This answer converted the biggest risk into a demonstration of sector resilience.
"I'm not sure if this aligns with my religion." (Sharia compliance)
"I work with a significant number of Muslim investors and this comes up regularly. The key distinction is ownership — this is a property you own outright, not a loan or bond structure. You are receiving rental income from a tenant, which is halal. The refinancing option involves interest, but that's entirely optional — a large number of our clients purchase cash and never refinance. If you do want to explore Sharia-compliant finance on the back end, we work with Riyad Bank and Gatehouse Bank, both of whom have Islamic mortgage products for specialist assets."

From Syed's call — addressed it naturally. Having the bank names ready builds trust immediately.
"I'm looking at several options — stocks, Airbnb, other property. This is just one of them."
"That's sensible — diversification is how serious investors think. I'm not here to tell you this is the only thing worth considering. What I can do is give you enough information on the second call that you're comparing it on a level playing field. One thing worth thinking about: the goal you mentioned — [their stated goal] — which of those other options actually delivers that without requiring your ongoing time? That's the real question."

From Syed's call — he was comparing five options. Do not compete with the other options directly. Bring the conversation back to their stated goal and let the contrast do the work.
"Can you guarantee the rent?"
"I'll be straight with you — 'guaranteed' is the most misused word in this industry. What I can tell you is that the income is contractual, backed by organisations funded by central government and local authorities, many of them PLCs doing hundreds of millions a year. That is not the same as one individual tenant paying from their personal income. Is it risk-free? No. Nothing is. But the risk profile is fundamentally different from what most people understand as 'property investment risk.' I'll show you exactly what those risks look like on our next call — and what legislation protects you."

From Kevin's call — Arun handled this well. This version sharpens it further.
Lessons from the Calls

What the Transcripts Showed

Patterns across Syed, Kevin, and VJ — what worked, what to sharpen, and the specific moments that made or lost ground.

All three calls Working well

Arun's rapport is strong. He quickly picks up on personal context — Syed's Eid, Kevin's son, VJ's background — and mirrors it naturally. These moments land because they are genuine. Keep doing this. Analytical investors trust people before they trust products.

All three calls Needs sharpening

The opening is a company description, not a frame. All three calls started with Arun explaining ATX Global before asking a single question. The prospect immediately goes into passive listening mode. Flip this — set the agenda, get a yes, then ask first.

Syed's call Strong moment

When Syed expressed concern about interest and religious compliance, Arun acknowledged it immediately and with confidence — "I work with a tremendous amount of Muslim clients." This built instant trust. The follow-up on Riyad Bank and Gatehouse Bank was exactly right.

"As a Muslim, you know, it does not align with my religion as well... I don't want to just give cash and get more cash in return. That's a prohibited contract." — Arun handled this correctly. Formalise this answer so it's consistent every time.
Syed's call Missed opportunity

The call ended abruptly because Arun had another call to jump to. The booking was made but felt rushed. Syed had a question at the end that never got answered. Never let the booking be the thing that gets compressed — if you are running short on time, say so early and restructure the call around getting to a booking before you run out of time.

Kevin's call Strong moment

Kevin asked directly what happens if the housing association goes bust. Arun's answer — Housing and Planning Act 2016, the government intervention mechanism, mentioning BlackRock — was the best answer in any of the three calls. It turned the biggest risk into a demonstration of why the sector is safe. This answer should be in every Call 1.

"The government's put it in place to protect themselves." — Sharp framing. Keep it.
Kevin's call Needs sharpening

Arun mentioned his own portfolio yield ("I've got around 20-22% yield on my deals") unprompted. It was meant to build credibility but came across as a comparison that confused rather than reassured Kevin. Arun's personal portfolio strategy is not the point — Kevin's return is. Keep the conversation on the prospect.

Kevin's call Key observation

Kevin told Arun exactly how to close him: "If you spell it out — here's what it would cost you to do it yourself, here's the refurb, here's the stamp duty, here's what you'd be all-in for — and then show me the premium is actually small, I'm happy to move forward." Arun confirmed he would do this. The Call 2 presentation framework should always include this cost breakdown comparison.

VJ's call Strong moment

Arun introduced the FIC structure (Family Investment Company) naturally after discovering VJ had a limited company portfolio and children he wanted to pass assets to. This was the highest-value moment in VJ's call — it differentiated ATX from every other conversation VJ had had, and VJ immediately engaged.

VJ's call Needs sharpening

VJ looked up ATX Global on Companies House mid-call and found the brand structure confusing (ATX Global Ltd / ATX Global Ventures / Assisted Living Investments / One Touch). This created a trust wobble. The explanation was honest but reactive. Proactively name the legal entity and trading names before anyone has to search — it removes the wobble entirely.

"So what is this ATX Global then?" — This question should never be asked. Answer it before they have to.
All four calls Consistent gap

The business impact question was never asked. Nobody was asked "if nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does that cost you?" All four prospects had real urgency — Syed's capital sitting in stocks for 12 months, Kevin's millions in accounts not generating returns, VJ's portfolio generating less each year, Jason's empty property costing him cash flow. That urgency existed. It was just never surfaced explicitly. Ask the cost of inaction on every call.

Jason's call Strong moment

Arun opened by recapping what he knew from the previous conversation ("you're an experienced investor, supported living, 15% minimum yield — is there any leeway?"). This is exactly right for a warm second-touch call. It signals preparation, saves time, and moves directly to what matters. On any call with a returning or pre-qualified prospect, always open with a recap and a confirming question, not a company description.

Jason's call Needs sharpening

Arun did not have the deal document ready — "I literally just got it maybe about an hour ago" — and the numbers on screen were from an old email and explicitly wrong. Jason was a serious investor who makes fast decisions and has a son in legal who will review everything. Walking into that call with inaccurate numbers on screen damages credibility in exactly the audience where credibility matters most. Never present a deal live before the document is confirmed accurate.

"These numbers are not correct, but I would show you here." — Do not say this on a call. Fix the document first.
Jason's call Strong moment

When Jason asked why the properties are not mortgageable, Arun gave a clear, honest two-part answer: politics (lenders do not want the publicity of repossessing vulnerable tenants) and investment value (sold above vacant value, so standard LTV products do not work cleanly). This is one of the best explanations in all four calls. It is honest, logical, and shows depth of knowledge. Every person in the pipeline will ask this question at some point — have this answer ready.

Jason's call Key behaviour to replicate

When Jason mentioned his empty HMO that he cannot fill, Arun immediately offered a practical solution — approach the local council for emergency and temporary accommodation on a nightly rate. This had nothing to do with the deal being discussed. But it was genuinely useful advice, and Jason noticed. Giving value outside the deal builds more trust than anything in the pitch. When a prospect mentions a problem, solve it if you can — even if it is not what you are selling.

Jason's call Key observation

Jason said "I always make a quick decision — yes, no, or whatever." This is a high-velocity prospect. When someone signals they decide fast, your job changes: stop managing their journey and start removing blockers. Jason needed addresses, financials, Nurture accounts, legislation links, and Gleeson information. All of those were promised but none were ready on the call. For fast-moving prospects, the deal pack needs to exist before the call — not be assembled after it.

Jason's call Pattern across all calls

Jason asked "where does your company earn from this?" near the end of the call. Kevin asked about the middleman structure. VJ asked about the contract flow. Syed needed to understand the ownership vs loan distinction. In every single call, the prospect needed to understand that ATX is a brokerage paid by the seller, that the contract is direct between them and the housing association, and that their capital never goes through ATX. This is not a question to wait for — it is a statement to make proactively in the first five minutes of every call.
