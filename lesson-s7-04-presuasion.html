Pre-Suasion

Influence doesn't begin when you pitch. It begins the moment the prospect's attention is directed — before you've said a word about your offer. What they're thinking about when they first hear you determines how they hear you.

What Pre-Suasion Is

Robert Cialdini — Pre-Suasion (2016)

In his follow-up to Influence, Cialdini introduced a concept that changes how you think about the entire structure of a sales call. Pre-suasion is the practice of directing attention in a way that makes the prospect more receptive to a specific message before that message is delivered.

The central insight: what people are thinking about at the moment of a pitch shapes how they receive that pitch. Get them thinking about safety and they'll evaluate your offer through a safety lens. Get them thinking about ambition and they'll evaluate it through an ambition lens. The frame is set before the pitch.

Pre-suasion is not manipulation. It's attention architecture. You decide which part of the prospect's experience you want activated before you speak to the thing your offer addresses. Every rep does this accidentally. Elite reps do it on purpose.

Where Pre-Suasion Lives on a Call

The Pre-Call Frame

The confirmation message you send before the call. The subject line. Whether you include anything substantive or just logistics. What the prospect reads in the 30 minutes before they get on with you sets their initial attention state. Most reps ignore this entirely.

A pre-call message that says "Looking forward to our call — I had a chance to review what you shared and I have some thoughts" primes the prospect to expect someone prepared and thoughtful. That's pre-suasion before the call even starts.

The Opening Questions

The first questions you ask direct attention. "Tell me about your background in sales" activates a different mental state than "When you think about where you want to be in your career, what does that actually look like?" The second question activates ambition and future-orientation — exactly the state you want someone in before hearing about a program that can accelerate their career.

The Discovery Sequence

The order of your discovery questions is pre-suasion architecture. Asking about pain before asking about goals means the prospect is primed by discomfort when they hear about solutions. Asking about the future first, then the gap between now and then, means they're in aspirational mode before the pain surfaces. Different orderings produce different emotional states at the moment of the pitch.

Engineering the Attention State

You Want Them Feeling Pre-Suasion Move
Frustrated by current reality Ask them to describe the pattern — not just the number. "Walk me through what a typical week looks like right now."
Excited about their future "Paint me a picture — what does your career look like 18 months from now if everything goes right?"
Aware of the cost of staying put "How long have you been in this spot? What's that cost you over that time?"
Confident in your credibility Name a specific result from someone in a similar situation before you pitch. Not a list — one vivid story.

The Pre-Suasion Call Architecture

01
Pre-Call
Confirmation Message
Send a brief, specific message that signals you prepared. One thing you noticed about their situation. This primes them to expect a different kind of conversation — not a sales process, but a genuine assessment.
02
Opening (0-3 min)
Activate the Ambitious Self
Your first question should activate the prospect's aspirational identity — who they want to be, not what's going wrong. Get them speaking from ambition first. That's the lens they'll evaluate your program through.
"Before we get into anything specific — what's the version of your career that you actually believe is possible for you?"
03
Discovery (3-20 min)
Surface the Gap
Now bring in the current reality. They're speaking from their ambitious self — now explore why they're not there yet. The frustration lands against the backdrop of the aspiration they just articulated. The gap feels real.
04
Pre-Pitch (before you present)
One Vivid Story
Before you present your program, name one person from a similar starting point with a specific outcome. Not "we have hundreds of success stories" — one person, one starting point, one result. That's the mental state you want them in when the pitch starts: "someone like me already did this."
"Before I walk you through what this actually looks like, let me tell you about someone who came in at almost exactly where you are..."

Call Exchange — Pre-Suasion in Opening

Opening Without Pre-Suasion Architecture
Rep
"Alright, so tell me a bit about your background and where you're at in sales right now."
Generic opener. Activates "current situation" frame — which is often frustration, confusion, or uncertainty. They begin the call in their pain state.
Opening WITH Pre-Suasion — Ambition First
Rep
"I want to start with something that most people skip. What does the career you actually believe is possible for you look like? Not the conservative version — the real one."
Activates aspiration and ambition. Prospect begins the call from their best self — the person who would make a bold investment decision. Everything from here is filtered through that lens.
Prospect
"Honestly? I think I could be making $30-40K a month in the next couple years. I know that sounds ambitious but I genuinely believe it."
Rep
"It doesn't sound ambitious — it sounds accurate. Tell me what's standing between where you are now and that number."
Now the pain surfaces — but it's framed against the aspiration they just voiced. The gap between $30-40K and their current reality is the pain. Much more motivating than starting with the pain cold.
