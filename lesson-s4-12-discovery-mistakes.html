Discovery Mistakes

What kills the close before the pitch starts. These are not pitch problems or close problems. They are discovery problems that show up as close problems. Know which ones you are making.

The Setup

Most reps who struggle to close believe they have a closing problem. What they actually have is a discovery problem. The close is too late to fix what went wrong in discovery. By the time you pitch, the decision has largely been shaped by what happened in the first half of the call.

These are the seven most common discovery mistakes. Each one kills the close in a specific way. Learn to recognize them in your own calls. One or two of these is almost certainly the reason your close rate is where it is.

The Seven Mistakes — and the Fix

The Mistake What It Looks Like The Fix
Accepting surface answers Prospect says "I want to make more money" and the rep builds the pitch on that Always go one level deeper. "What does more money actually mean for your life?" Surface answers are placeholders. The real answer is one question further.
Rushing to the pitch Rep asks 2-3 questions and then transitions to the offer before all four states are active Do not pitch until you can confirm dissatisfaction, desire, possibility, and attention are all present. The pitch is earned by the discovery, not scheduled after a fixed number of questions.
Asking leading questions "That must be really frustrating, right?" or "So you want to close at a higher rate, obviously?" Ask open questions. Let the prospect define their own experience. Leading questions produce the answers you want, not the truth. "How does that sit with you?" not "that's frustrating, right?"
Telling instead of asking Rep describes the prospect's pain back to them rather than letting the prospect articulate it Dissatisfaction that the prospect articulates is 10x more powerful than dissatisfaction you describe. Ask the question. Hold the silence. Let them say it.
Not following emotional threads Prospect mentions something with weight — "it's been hard" — and the rep moves to the next planned question Every emotional signal is a door. Stop. Go through it. "Tell me more about that." The close is almost always behind one of these doors that most reps walk past.
Moving on before getting specifics Prospect gives a vague answer and the rep accepts it and continues: "Got it. So tell me..." Vague pain does not create urgency. Specific pain does. When an answer is vague, go specific: "Walk me through a specific example of when that happened." Stay until it is real.
Not confirming understanding Rep moves to the pitch without reflecting back what they heard or confirming they understood correctly Before pitching, do a brief reflection: "Let me make sure I understand where you're at..." This signals listening, catches misunderstandings before the pitch, and creates a moment of connection that primes the prospect for what comes next.

How to Diagnose Which Mistake You Are Making

Review Your Own Calls

Pull the last five calls where the prospect said "let me think about it" or did not close. Listen to the discovery section. Not the close — the discovery. Ask yourself: did I follow the emotional threads? Did I accept surface answers and move on? Did I transition to the pitch before dissatisfaction was fully built? Did I tell them their pain or help them articulate it?

The pattern will be there. Usually it is one or two consistent mistakes across all five calls. That consistency is actually good news — it means the fix is specific and learnable.

The Fastest Indicator

If your close rate is below 20% and your offer is genuinely good, you have a discovery problem. If prospects consistently seem interested during the call but do not close, you have a discovery problem. If you get a lot of "I need to think about it" objections, you have a discovery problem. The close does not fix these — better discovery does.

Call Exchange — Multiple Discovery Mistakes in One Call

Rep Making Four Common Mistakes in Discovery
Rep
"So what's bringing you to this call today — what are you looking to achieve?"
Prospect
"I want to get better at sales, make more money, have more freedom. You know, the usual."
Rep
"Got it, totally. And you're probably pretty frustrated with where things are at, right? Like it sounds like you've been grinding without seeing the results."
Mistake 1: Accepted surface answer. Mistake 2: Asked a leading question ("you're probably pretty frustrated, right?"). The rep is telling the prospect their emotion instead of letting the prospect describe it.
Prospect
"Yeah, I guess you could say that. It's been a journey."
Rep
"Totally, it can be. Well, what we do here at Sales.io is specifically designed to help reps in your situation. Let me walk you through what we offer — we have a 12-week program that..."
Mistake 3: Rushed to the pitch. Two exchanges into discovery. None of the four states are active. Mistake 4: Did not follow the emotional thread in "it's been a journey" — that phrase was a door. This close will almost certainly end in "let me think about it."
