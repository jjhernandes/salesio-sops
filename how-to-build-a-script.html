Sales.io — Script Building SOP

Build a script
that actually converts.

This is not a script. This is a system for building one. The difference between a rep who closes at 10% and one who closes at 30% is not what they say. It is the structure underneath the conversation and the identity behind the delivery. This SOP builds the structure.

"Everyone else teaches you what to say. We teach you how to think. The script is a thinking tool — not a crutch."

Before You Build

Read this first. A script built on the wrong foundation will cost you deals, not close them. Before you write a single question, lock in three things: what you are selling, who you are selling it to, and what promise brought them to the call. Every phase below is built on those three anchors. Skip prep and the whole conversation falls apart.

The 3 Anchors — Lock these before anything else
Offer
What exactly are you selling — deliverables, timeline, result promised.
Avatar
Who is on the call, what world they live in, what pain they carry in.
Promise
What the marketing said. What they expect based on how they got here.
Problem Buckets
The 3 to 5 real pain points your avatar universally runs into.
The Phase Map

Eleven phases. Every call moves through all of them in order. The script you build is the customized version of this structure for your specific offer and avatar.

1 — Open the Frame
2 — Map Their Reality
3 — Surface the Pain
4 — Shatter the Pattern
5 — Lock the Vision
6 — Qualify the Conviction
7 — Teach Through Insight
8 — Anchor Their Words
9 — Show the Distance
10 — Paint the Cost
11 — Bridge + Close
Phase 1
Phase 1
Open the Frame
Purpose: You own the call from the first sentence or they do. This phase sets the tone, confirms context, and disarms defensiveness before it builds.
Your Starter Line
"Hey [Name] — [Rep name] here from [Company]. You booked in because of [specific reason or context they gave]. Before we get into it — still good on time?"
Customized Opener Questions — Write 3 to 5 for your offer
What's the main thing that pushed you to book this call today specifically?
Walk me through where you're at right now — what does your situation look like?
What are you currently doing in [their area], and what's feeling off about it?
When you saw [ad / content / post] — what about it made you stop?
Build rule: Write one specific opener for your offer that mentions exactly why they are on the call. Vague openers bleed authority. Never open cold.
Phase 2
Phase 2
Map Their Reality
Purpose: Understand what they are working with before you work with them. No assumptions. Get the lay of the land. If you skip this, your pitch will be about the wrong things.
Baseline Questions — Customize these to your offer's world
How are you currently handling [the core process your offer solves]?
What have you already tried to fix this? What happened?
What is actually working right now — and what feels like it's just a band-aid?
Have you invested in anything to solve this before? What was that experience like?
Are you actively looking to make a move on this or still figuring out if it's a priority?
Build rule: This phase is data collection. Do not pitch. Do not react emotionally to what they say. Just map. The information you gather here runs every phase after it.
Phase 3
Phase 3
Surface the Pain
Purpose: Pull the real problem to the surface. Most prospects walk in with a stated problem. That is almost never the actual problem. This phase gets underneath it.
Pain Extraction Questions — Write these for your specific problem buckets
What does this actually cost you when [specific problem] happens? Time, money, opportunities?
How long have you been dealing with this — and what have you already tried?
What does a bad week look like when this isn't working?
If you had to put a number on what this problem has cost you in the last 6 months, what would you say?
What does [the absence of the solution] do to [their goal, team, income, life]?
Build rule: You need the prospect to hear themselves describe their own pain out loud. That self-diagnosis is worth more than any pitch you will ever give. Design questions that make them say it — not you.
Phase 4
Phase 4
Shatter the Pattern
Purpose: Break the belief that what they have been doing will eventually work. Until you do this, they see no reason to change. This is where cognitive dissonance starts.
Pattern Break Frame
"Most people in your position try [common shortcut or workaround specific to your niche]. The problem with that approach is [why it fails]. Sound familiar?"
Pattern Break Questions — Build 2 to 3 specific to your offer's most common myths
Have you noticed that [common approach] tends to [create a secondary problem]? What's been your experience with that?
What made you try [what they have been doing] — and at what point did you realize it was not moving the needle?
If more of the same approach was going to solve this, would it have worked by now?
Build rule: Name the exact thing your avatar tries before they find you. Then show them why it fails without attacking their intelligence. The goal is gentle disruption — not "you've been doing it wrong."
Phase 5
Phase 5
Lock the Vision
Purpose: Get them emotionally invested in a specific future state. This is Heaven in the Heaven/Hell framework. Make them feel what winning actually looks like before you present anything.
Vision Questions — Pull the future out of them in their own words
If this was completely solved six months from now, what specifically would be different?
What does winning actually look like in your world — not generally, specifically?
Beyond the numbers — what would it feel like to have this handled?
What opens up for you when [their core problem] is no longer a thing?

"The vision has to be theirs. If they describe the future in their own words, they own it. If you describe it for them, they are just listening to you talk."

Build rule: Write vision questions that force specificity. "Better" is not a vision. "Making $22K last month instead of $9K" is. Push until you get the real picture — then use their words back to them in Phase 8.
Phase 6
Phase 6
Qualify the Conviction
Purpose: Confirm they have the intent, capacity, and urgency to actually buy. If any of these are missing, do not present. Qualify hard here so you do not waste time closing the wrong person.
Conviction Qualifier Questions — Build to match your offer's price point and buyer type
Have you invested in something like this before — and if so, what was that experience?
Who else is in the decision with you on this — or is this your call to make?
How important is solving this right now on a scale of 1 to 10 — and what makes it that number?
Is there a timeline you're working with, or is this more of a "whenever" situation?
If today you found something that you genuinely believed would solve this — what would stop you from moving on it?
Build rule: Budget qualification should come through behavior, not direct questions. Past investment patterns tell you more than "can you afford it" ever will. Design around that.
Phase 7
Phase 7
Teach Through Insight
Purpose: Create the gap between what they know and what is actually true. One well-placed insight that reframes their situation builds more trust than five minutes of rapport-building.
Insight Questions — Design around your niche's most common blind spots
Are you familiar with [industry insight, best practice, or common risk] — and how it shows up in situations like yours?
One thing that most people do not realize about [their situation] is [insight]. Have you run into that?
A pattern we see across everyone in [their position] is [pattern]. Does that match what you are experiencing?
Build rule: Pick one insight your best prospects always respond to. Make it specific to their world. The goal is not to overwhelm them with information — it is to make them feel like you understand their situation better than they do.
Phase 8
Phase 8
Anchor Their Words
Purpose: Tie everything they said back to where you are going. Use their language, not yours. This is where you convert a good conversation into forward pressure — without pressure.
Anchor Frame
"Earlier you mentioned [exact thing they said] — that matters because [why it connects to your solution]. Is that still the thing that would make this worth it for you?"
Anchor Questions — Pull directly from what they said in Phases 3 and 5
You mentioned [exact pain or goal they named] earlier — would a solution that specifically addresses that change things for you?
When you said [their words] — what did you mean by that exactly? I want to make sure I'm connecting the right things.
Based on what you've shared, the core of what you're trying to solve is [summarize]. Does that feel accurate?
Build rule: You cannot script exact anchor lines in advance because they come from the conversation. What you script is the structure: "You said [X] — would [Y] solve that?" Train yourself to listen for the anchors in real time.
Phase 9
Phase 9
Show the Distance
Purpose: Make the gap between where they are and where they want to be impossible to ignore. This is the setup for Phase 10. No gap, no urgency.
Gap Questions — Build specific to your offer's outcome vs. their current state
On a scale of 1 to 10, how close does your current approach get you to [the vision they described in Phase 5]?
What's the actual distance between where you are now and where you said you want to be in six months?
If you stay on the current path — the same tools, the same approach — how long before you get there? Realistically.
Build rule: Do not answer the gap question for them. Ask it, then stay quiet. The discomfort of realizing the distance themselves is the thing that creates urgency. You filling the silence kills it.
Phase 10
Phase 10
Paint the Cost
Purpose: This is Hell in the Heaven/Hell framework. Make inaction feel more expensive than action. Do not threaten — let them arrive at the cost themselves.
Cost Questions — Make them calculate the cost of staying stuck
If nothing changes over the next 12 months, what does that actually look like for you?
What has staying in this situation already cost you — in time, money, or opportunity?
Is this a problem that gets easier on its own, or does it compound the longer you leave it?
What does another year of [their main pain] cost you at the number you put on it earlier?

"People do not buy solutions. They buy their way out of pain they can no longer justify staying in."

Build rule: Phase 10 only works if Phase 3 and Phase 5 were done right. If you did not get deep on the pain and the vision, the cost feels abstract. The cost has to be connected to their specific life — not a generic consequence.
Phase 11
Phase 11
Bridge + Close
Purpose: Position the offer as the only logical next step based on everything they just said. Then close with confidence — not desperation.
Presentation Structure — Build your 3 pillars here
Pillar 1: [What makes getting started seamless] — "We handle [specific onboarding thing] so you are not starting from scratch."
Pillar 2: [What makes it built for them specifically] — "This is not a template. We build around [their specific situation or niche]."
Pillar 3: [What makes the result inevitable] — "We stay in it until [specific outcome]. Not set and forget."
Close Lines — One of these. Then silence.
"Based on everything you've shared today — does this feel like the right move to get you from [where they are] to [what they described]?"
"You came in today because [reason they gave]. From what we've talked through — does this solve that for you?"
"Is there anything that would stop this from being the right step for you right now?"
Build rule: If objections come up here, they are almost always about pain that was not surfaced deeply enough in Phases 3 and 10. Do not try to overcome with logic. Loop back: "Tell me more about that — what specifically is the concern?"
The 5-Step Build Process

You have the 11 phases above. Now here is the actual process for building your version of this for a specific offer.

Step 1 of 5
Lock your foundations before you write anything
Write out your offer in one sentence: what it is, who it is for, and what result it creates.
Write out your avatar's top 3 to 5 problem buckets — the things they universally come in carrying.
Identify the marketing promise that brought them to the call — and make sure Phase 1 of your script matches it.
If you cannot clearly describe all three in writing, you are not ready to build a script yet. Start here.
Step 2 of 5
Build a frame-setting opener that earns the right to ask questions
Write one specific opener that names who you are, why they are here, and confirms time.
Write 3 to 5 opening questions that start the conversation without overwhelming or pitching.
Your opener should make them feel like they are in good hands — not being sold to.
Test it out loud. If it sounds like a script when you say it, rewrite it until it does not.
Step 3 of 5
Build the pain stack — Reality, Pattern Break, and Cost in sequence
Write baseline questions for Phase 2 that map their current setup — specific to your offer's world.
Write pain extraction questions for Phase 3 that get underneath the stated problem to the real one.
Write 2 to 3 pattern break frames for Phase 4 — name the exact thing your avatar tries before they find you, then show why it does not work.
Build cost questions for Phase 10 that make them calculate what staying stuck actually costs — in real numbers, not feelings.
Step 4 of 5
Build the vision and gap — Heaven, then the distance to it
Write 4 vision questions for Phase 5 that pull out a specific, emotional picture of winning — in their words.
Write 3 gap questions for Phase 9 that force them to measure the distance between where they are and where they described wanting to be.
Note: you cannot write the anchor lines for Phase 8 in advance — they come from what the prospect says live. What you build is the structural frame: "You said [X] — does this solve that?"
Heaven and Hell only work together. If one is weak, the other loses its power.
Step 5 of 5
Build the bridge and close — then drill it until it is automatic
Write your 3 presentation pillars for Phase 11 — ease of start, built for them, result guaranteed.
Choose one close line and commit to it. Reps who bounce between close lines signal indecision. Pick one that fits your voice and burn it in.
Build out 3 to 5 objection scenarios with response frameworks — not scripts, frameworks. You want to know how to think about each one, not what to recite.
Run the full script on a practice call before you put it on a live prospect. Hear yourself say every phase out loud. Fix what sounds wrong. The script is never done — it evolves with your calls.
Non-Negotiables
Do not pitch before you understand the pain. The script gives you permission to wait. Use it.
Do not skip qualification. A great presentation to the wrong person is a wasted call for both of you.
Use their language in the close, not yours. The anchor in Phase 8 and the close in Phase 11 should sound like things they said, reflected back.
Silence is part of the script. After a gap question, a cost question, or a close line — stop talking. The discomfort you feel in that silence is what moves them.
The script does not replace identity work. A weak identity running a strong script still closes weak. The script handles the structure. You handle the energy behind it.
Update the script based on patterns you see across calls. After every 10 calls, look for the moment people consistently disengage. That is where the script needs work — not the close.

"A script is a thinking tool. Not a crutch. If you need the script to know what to say next, you have not internalized the conversation well enough yet. Drill the phases until you do not need the words — just the structure."
