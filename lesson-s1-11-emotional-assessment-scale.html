The Emotional Assessment Scale

You can't change where you're operating from until you know where you are. Most reps think they're operating higher than they actually are.

The Map of Consciousness

The Framework

This framework comes from David Hawkins' work in "Letting Go." You don't need to be into the spiritual framing to use it. Think of it as a practical map of the emotional states that drive human behavior — specifically behavior on sales calls.

Every state on this map has a specific energy signature. That energy signature predicts how you'll respond to adversity, to objections, to coaching, to a bad week. Most reps are operating from states that have a performance ceiling built in — and they're unaware of it because they've never had a map.

How to Use This

This isn't about labeling yourself permanently. It's about being honest about where you are right now, this week, in this season of your career. The goal is awareness first — because you cannot move from a position you can't see.

Read through the levels. Don't skip to where you want to be. Find where you actually are.

The Levels — Below the Line

Shame / Guilt
Believing you don't deserve success
This is the basement. At this level, you don't believe you deserve the close, the income, or the recognition. Self-sabotage right before the close is the calling card — things go well, and then at the critical moment, something breaks down that shouldn't have. Often invisible: the rep thinks they're having bad luck. They're not. They're executing a belief that success is for other people.
Apathy
"What's the point?"
Low volume. Low energy. Not tracking details. Going through the motions on calls — present in body, absent in mind. Apathy often follows a period of high effort with poor results. The rep stopped caring as a self-protective mechanism. The problem: prospect care and rep energy are directly linked. An apathetic rep creates an apathetic prospect.
Grief
Processing a loss — professional or personal
Lost a big deal. Bad personal news. A relationship fell apart. Whatever the source, grief consumes cognitive and emotional bandwidth. The rep can't be fully present because part of their attention is occupied by something unresolved. Trying to push through grief without acknowledging it usually produces flat, disconnected calls that both parties can feel.
Fear
Afraid of losing the sale, of rejection, of looking stupid
Fear is sneaky — it often masquerades as preparation or caution. The rep who over-prepares because they're terrified of being caught not knowing something. The one who hesitates everywhere on the call because they're afraid of saying the wrong thing. Fear-based selling has a specific texture: tentative, over-cautious, and exhausting to be on the other side of.
Desire
Wanting the sale so badly you can feel it
This is where most struggling reps spend most of their time. Desire is above fear on the map, so it feels like progress — but it's still a push state. You need the close. You want it bad. And the wanting is visible. The prospect feels the weight of your want. Desire produces short bursts of high-energy performance, but it's unstable and always at risk of collapsing into fear or apathy when results don't come fast enough.
Anger
Frustrated with prospects, with the offer, with yourself
Some reps can close from anger for a season — it generates energy and directness. But it's short-fused and reactive. An angry rep gets defensive when challenged, has shorter tolerance for objections, and often pushes rather than pulls. Prospects who are on the fence get pushed away, not closed. And reps operating from anger rarely receive coaching well — the anger defends against input.
Pride
The Most Dangerous Level for a Closer
"I know what I'm doing." Shuts out coaching. Shuts out feedback. Closes to learning. Pride feels like confidence and certainty — but it's neither. A proud rep stops improving because they've decided they don't need to. They get defensive when given feedback, dismissive of new approaches, and start to attribute losses to the prospect or the offer rather than themselves. This is the level JJ has actually removed students from the program for — not because of performance, but because pride makes coaching impossible.

Above the Line

Courage
Willing to be wrong. Willing to try the hard thing.
This is the first level above the line. Courage doesn't mean fearless — it means willing to act despite the fear. The rep at courage will ask the hard question even when it feels uncomfortable. They'll try a new approach even when they might fail. They'll accept coaching even when it stings. Getting to courage is the first real breakthrough in this work.
Neutrality
Things happen. I process them without spiraling.
At neutrality, a lost deal is a lost deal. A coaching note is a coaching note. The rep processes events without amplifying them emotionally. This is the baseline of a grounded rep — not numb, not detached, just steady. Neutrality is what composure looks like from the inside.
Willingness
Active, engaged, learning
The rep at willingness brings genuine energy to the work. Not forced positivity — real engagement. They show up to coaching calls ready to learn. They implement feedback. They're curious about why something didn't work rather than defensive. Willingness is the state where growth compounds fastest.
Reason
Everything is traceable and logical
At reason, a bad week isn't mysterious — it has a cause. The rep at reason reviews their calls, identifies the pattern, makes the adjustment. A lost deal isn't a referendum on their identity — it's data. Reason removes the emotional charge from outcomes and replaces it with analytical clarity. The rep becomes their own best coach.
Acceptance
Things are as they are. No fight, no inflation.
Acceptance is not resignation. It's peace with what is, which frees up enormous energy. The rep at acceptance isn't fighting reality — the prospect who wasn't ready, the deal that wasn't right, the week that didn't go as planned. They see clearly, without the distortion of needing it to be different. From this ground, their responses are clean and accurate.

The Peak

Love
Genuine care for the person in front of you
Not performed. Not a technique. Real care for what this decision means for the person you're talking to — their life, their income, their family, their trajectory. When you're operating from love, you're not trying to close them. You're trying to help them make the right decision. The paradox: that's exactly what closes them. Prospects feel the difference instantly. This is what people mean when they say a conversation felt different, felt honest, felt like the rep actually gave a damn. They did. That's the peak — and it's accessible to anyone willing to get out of their own way.

Where Most Sales Reps Operate

The honest answer: Desire, Anger, and Pride.

These three levels are where the bulk of sales culture lives. Desire because the income potential of this space is real and people want it badly. Anger because grinding without results creates frustration. Pride because the culture of sales performance rewards arrogance and punishes vulnerability.

These are all push mechanisms. They generate short-term energy but have a ceiling. You can get to $30-50K/month from desire and anger. Some reps do. But then it plateaus — or collapses. The energy isn't sustainable, and the identity running underneath it isn't sturdy enough to handle the weight of real scale.

And when the $30K month happens from desire, it doesn't feel like anything. Because desire is never satisfied — the moment the result arrives, desire moves the target. That emptiness is a data point: you're operating below the line, even when winning.

How to Know Where You Are Right Now

The Diagnostic

Don't ask "what level am I at in general?" Ask: how are you responding to things right now, this week?

How do you respond to adversity — a bad call, a lost deal, a tough week? Frustration and blaming = anger. Shutdown and disconnection = apathy or fear. Over-efforting from a place of panic = desire. Defensive when coached = pride. Curious and steady = above the line.

How do you respond to coaching? Do you receive it cleanly and implement it? Or does feedback feel like criticism even when it isn't? The more defended you are against feedback, the lower on the scale you're likely operating.

How do you respond to a good week? Does the high feel balanced and grounded? Or does it spike into overconfidence and "I figured it out" energy that sets you up for the next crash? That spike is desire/pride — still below the line.

The Self-Assessment Prompt

Sit with this honestly: if the next five calls you take all end in no, what will your internal state look like by call five? Will you be curious and grounded (above the line) — or frustrated, withdrawn, or pushing harder in a way that signals desperation? Your honest answer locates you on the map.

Raising Your Level

  • Your environment. The people around you set the baseline of what's normal. You will drift toward the average emotional level of your closest environment. This is not motivational advice — it's an actual mechanism. Put yourself around people operating above the line.
  • Genuine care for what you do. Not performed passion — actual belief that this matters. When you care about the outcome for the person in front of you, the emotional state that arises naturally is above the line. Purpose pulls you up.
  • Quality of inputs. What you consume daily is programming. Comparison content, news, social media conflict, complaint — these drop your level. Calls, coaching, things that build your understanding — these raise it.
  • Service. Helping other people — genuinely, without transaction — raises your level faster than almost anything else. Within the Sales.io community, being the person who shows up for others lifts you above the push states quickly.
  • Discipline and honesty with yourself. Doing what you said you'd do, even when it's uncomfortable. Not lying to yourself about where you are or what's actually happening. These structural practices anchor you above the line.
  • Comparison drops you. Neediness drops you. Resentment about other people's results drops you. Dishonesty with yourself — pretending you're at reason when you're operating from anger — drops you and makes the climb harder because you can't fix what you won't see.

Call Exchange — Same Objection, Three Levels

Prospect says: "I've tried programs before. They promised results and didn't deliver."

OPERATING FROM PRIDE
Rep
"Yeah, those programs are garbage. This is completely different. We actually get results — I mean, our track record speaks for itself. Two thousand one hundred students, basically zero refunds. Those other programs don't have anything close to what we do."
Dismissive of the prospect's experience. The "I know better" energy of pride is present throughout. No genuine curiosity about what actually happened — just defense of position. Prospect doesn't feel heard. They feel argued with.
OPERATING FROM COURAGE
Rep
[pause] "That's a real concern. I'm not going to tell you we're automatically different — what made those programs not work for you? Like specifically, what happened?"
Courage is willing to be uncertain. The rep doesn't claim they're definitely better — they get curious. They ask the uncomfortable follow-up instead of deflecting into a defense. The prospect's experience is taken seriously.
Prospect
"There was just no personal attention. It was all pre-recorded content and a Facebook group."
Rep
"Okay — that actually tells me a lot. What would real personal attention have looked like for you?"
Still curious. Not rushing to make the sale. Courage asks the next question even when the close is in sight.
OPERATING FROM LOVE
Rep
[longer pause] "That's frustrating — and honestly, that experience is part of why I do this. Tell me what happened. What did you go in hoping for?"
Love is curious about the person, not the sale. The "why I do this" isn't a pitch line — it's genuine context for why the rep cares about getting this right. The prospect feels something different. Not a technique. Not a process. Someone who actually wants to understand.
Prospect
"I just wanted someone who actually looked at what I was doing and told me what to fix. Not generic modules."
Rep
"That's exactly what we're built for — and I want to be honest with you. If after we talk today this doesn't feel like the right fit for where you are, I'll tell you that too. What I want is for you to make the right decision, not just a decision. Can I show you how the coaching actually works?"
From love, the rep can offer genuine reassurance — including the reassurance that they're willing to lose the sale to serve the person. That paradox is the most credible thing a rep can say. And it only sounds real when it's actually true. This is what closes from the peak.
