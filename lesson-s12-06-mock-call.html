The Mock Call Protocol

Most mock calls don't improve performance because they're run wrong. Here's exactly how to set them up, run them, and review them so that every mock translates to real call improvement.

Mock Calls That Work vs. Mock Calls That Don't

Fake Mock Call Real Mock Call
Easy prospect profile — cooperative, agreeable, no hard objections. Realistic profile built on actual rep weaknesses. Includes known pressure points.
Coach goes easy because the rep is new or they want to be encouraging. Coach escalates difficulty systematically. Comfortable rep is a learning rep that isn't actually being challenged.
Not recorded. No review afterward. Recorded every time. Reviewed within 24 hours using the 5-Point Framework.
Feedback is vague. "That was pretty good, work on your close." Feedback is specific, timestamped, one observation per segment.
Run once in a while when someone has time. Minimum 2 per week in the first 90 days. Scheduled, not optional.

A mock call that isn't realistic is not practice — it's theater. If the "prospect" never pushes back, never challenges your credibility, never says "I need to think about it" and means it — you're not building anything. You're just performing. The real calls will be harder than that, and you'll be unprepared.

The Mock Call Setup

Step 1 — Build the Prospect Profile

Before the mock call, the coach creates a prospect profile. This is not random. It's based on the rep's known weaknesses. If the rep struggles with discovery, the profile includes a prospect who gives surface answers and resists going deeper. If the rep loses frame under price pressure, the profile includes a budget-conscious prospect who challenges the price.

The profile includes: background, pain point, objection type, and at which point in the call the prospect should escalate resistance.

Step 2 — Set the Recording

Record every mock call. No exceptions. Zoom, Loom, any platform that captures both video and audio. The recording is half the value of the mock call. Without it, you're practicing blind.

Step 3 — Treat It Like a Real Call

Rep does their pre-call preparation. No joking around before it starts. The coach gets into character — they are the prospect from the moment "start" is called. Both parties maintain the fiction for the full call. Breaking character to coach mid-call removes the realistic pressure and kills the value.

No softball scenarios. The point of the mock call is to create the same pressure as a real call in a safe environment. Safe means you can make mistakes without losing a real deal. It does not mean easy. The harder the mock, the more useful the transfer to real calls.

The Coach Role — Escalating Difficulty

The Coach's Job

The coach's job is not to play a pleasant prospect. The coach's job is to find the rep's ceiling and push into it. If the rep handles a surface objection well, the coach escalates — adds more skepticism, asks harder follow-up questions, challenges credibility. The goal is to find the moment the rep breaks and make that moment happen during practice, not on a real call.

L1
Level 1
Engaged but Cautious
Prospect is interested, asks questions, gives real answers in discovery. Mentions they've "tried other programs" once. Agrees to the price without major resistance. This level tests basic structure and discovery depth.
L2
Level 2
One Hard Objection
Prospect gives surface answers in discovery until pushed. One firm objection during close — "I need to think about it" or "the price is more than I expected." Coach holds the objection for at least 30 seconds to test whether the rep rushes or gets curious.
L3
Level 3
Credibility Challenge + Price
Prospect questions how long the rep has been doing this, references a competitor, and then objects to the price. Tests composure under credibility attack and ability to hold frame through a two-stage objection sequence.
L4
Level 4
Closed Off + Multiple Objections
Prospect gives one-word answers in early discovery. Multiple objections stacked at close. Tests whether the rep can open a closed prospect and maintain composure through sustained resistance.
L5
Level 5
Full Gauntlet
Hostile at opener, closed in discovery, credibility challenge mid-call, price objection, "I need to talk to my partner," and one more unknown variable the coach adds at their discretion. The full gauntlet. If the rep can hold frame and stay curious through this, real calls feel manageable.

Recording and Reviewing — The Compound Effect

The mock call is 50% of the value. The review is the other 50%. A mock call without a review is a slightly more realistic conversation. A mock call with a review is a targeted training intervention that directly improves your next real call.

Review the Mock Using the 5-Point Framework

Same framework as a real call. Opener, Discovery, Pitch, Objection Handling, Close. Score each point 1-3. Identify the lowest score. That's what the next mock targets. Run the next mock specifically against that weakness.

  • ✓
    Minimum 2 mock calls per week in the first 90 days. This is not optional. It's the difference between reps who plateau and reps who break through. Two mocks a week, reviewed with the 5-Point Framework, applied to real calls.
  • ✓
    Start at Level 2 difficulty minimum. Level 1 is for day one of a brand-new rep only. Anyone who has been on at least 10 real calls should start at Level 2 and move up based on performance.
  • ✓
    Apply one thing per mock to the next real call. The transfer is mandatory. What did you learn? What are you changing? State it out loud after the review. Do it on the next call.
  • ✗
    Don't debrief mid-call. Coach stays in character until the call ends. Post-call debrief happens after a 5-minute break. Breaking the mock to coach kills the flow and realism that makes the practice valuable.
