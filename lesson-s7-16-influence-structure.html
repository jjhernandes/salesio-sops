The Structure of Influence

Every principle in S7 exists as one layer in a larger structure. When they stack correctly, the call doesn't feel like persuasion — it feels like the natural conclusion of a genuinely helpful conversation.

How It All Fits Together

Most reps who study influence try to use one technique at a time — anchoring here, loss framing there, social proof somewhere else. The techniques land weakly because they're deployed in isolation, without the architecture that makes them work together.

The structure is not a checklist. It's a sequence where each principle sets up the next. Miss a layer and the ones above it sit on a weak foundation.

1
Pre-Call — Engineering the Conditions
Before the call starts
Contagious emotion: Your state is set before they pick up. Genuine conviction, not performed enthusiasm.
Pre-suasion: What you send pre-call, how you confirm, what you prime them to think about — all of it shapes reception before minute one.
Halo effect: The first 90 seconds create the frame everything else is experienced through. Calm, unhurried, grounded.
2
Discovery — Opening the Conditions
First 30-40 minutes
Priming questions: Discovery questions that prime the emotional state you need for the close.
Cognitive dissonance: Surface the gap between their identity and their reality. The discomfort creates urgency.
Future self projection: Get them speaking AS their future self — present tense, specific, vivid.
Loss aversion: Surface the cost of inaction. Not manufactured — their actual cost, in their words.
3
Pitch — Activating the Decision
The 10-20 minute pitch window
Anchoring: Anchor high before price. The transformation value, the cost of inaction, identity-level social proof.
Identity-level social proof: Deploy the story that matches their exact starting point and doubt.
Loss framing: Every benefit expressed as what they'll stop losing, not just what they'll gain.
Reciprocity: The quality of the discovery conversation itself — the value you've already given — is the most genuine form of reciprocity.
4
Close — Completing the Decision
The final 5-15 minutes
Identity consistency: Use the identity statements they made in discovery: "You said you're serious about this — this is that decision."
Legitimate urgency: Their timeline, their stated goal date, their cost of delay — not manufactured pressure.
Regret minimization: "A year from now, in the same spot — what would that feel like?" Only when it fits the call naturally.

The Mastery Test

The mastery test is not "can you name all 15 principles?" It's this: Can you run a call where every principle is active and it still sounds like a completely natural conversation? No seams. No "okay now I'm going to use loss framing." Just a genuine conversation where the principles are running in the background, invisible.

The danger of knowing too much too soon: Reps who learn these principles sometimes over-index. Every sentence becomes calculated. The prospect feels studied, not understood. Internalization, not deployment — that's the target.

"Everyone else teaches you what to say. We teach you how to think."

When the structure of influence is fully internalized, the techniques dissolve into the conversation. You're not running anchoring — you're just naturally establishing value before price because you understand why that matters. You're not consciously deploying cognitive dissonance — you're genuinely curious about the gap between who they are and where they are.

That's when the close rate compounds. Not when you know more techniques. When the techniques become invisible because they've become how you think.
