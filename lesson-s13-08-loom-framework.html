The 60-Second Loom Framework

Every application Loom follows the same four-part structure. Master this and every video you send is a sharp, confident, targeted pitch — not a nervous ramble.

The Four-Part Structure

60 seconds. Four parts. No filler. This is not a casual video — it is a compressed sales call where you are the product and the company is the prospect. Every second carries weight.

The structure: Hook, Credibility, Value Prop, CTA. In that order. Every time.

01
0:00 — 0:12
Hook — Why This Company
Open with something specific about this company. Not "I saw your posting and I'm interested." Reference something real: their product, their founder's content, a result a customer shared publicly, something you found in your research. This signals immediately that you are not sending a mass application. You chose them. That is rare.
"I watched your founder's breakdown of the psychology behind high-ticket closers last week and it matched exactly how I think about the sale. That's why I reached out specifically."
02
0:12 — 0:28
Credibility — Your Track Record
Give them one or two real, specific data points about your performance. Deal size you've closed. Close rate. Offer you worked on. Revenue generated. Named companies if relevant. Not a resume reading — two sentences that paint a picture of a rep who has produced results. If you are early in your career, cite what you can: a Sales.io mock call close rate, a previous role result, or a specific coaching skill you have been developing.
"I've closed $420K over the last 8 months on a B2C coaching offer at a 17% close rate. Before that, I did two years in agency sales where I consistently hit 110-130% of quota."
03
0:28 — 0:50
Value Prop — What You Bring to Their Specific Offer
Connect your experience to their specific offer. Why does your background make you a fit for what they are selling? What about their customer avatar lines up with prospects you have closed before? What specific skill or experience translates directly to their context? Make this connection explicitly. Do not leave them guessing.
"Your offer is in the mindset and transformation space, which is where I do my best work — customers buying for identity change respond well to the psychology-first approach I use, and your curriculum seems built around that same idea."
04
0:50 — 1:00
CTA — What You Want to Happen Next
Ask for a specific next step. Not "let me know if you're interested." Not "feel free to reach out." A confident, specific CTA: "I'd love 20 minutes to ask you a few questions about the offer structure and the pipeline. Are you open to a quick call this week?" The rep who asks directly for what they want is the rep who closes.
"I'd love a 20-minute discovery call with you to see if this is a mutual fit. I've got openings Thursday and Friday this week."

What Not to Say in Your Loom

Do not open with your name and why you are excited. "Hi, my name is [Name] and I am really excited to apply for this role" wastes the first 10 seconds and signals you are reading from a script. The hook is your opener. Get into it immediately.

Do not say "I am a hard worker" or "I am passionate about sales." These phrases mean nothing. Everyone says them. Replace them with a specific result. "I am a hard worker" becomes "I ran 22 calls last week and closed 4." Evidence replaces adjectives.

Do not go over 75 seconds. The 60-second constraint forces you to be specific. When reps remove the constraint, they ramble. A 3-minute Loom full of qualifications tells the operator you cannot be concise. That is a problem in sales, where every word on a call matters.

Do not use the same Loom for multiple companies. If your hook references "your company," but the word "your" does not correspond to anything specific, the operator hears a template. Templates do not get discovery calls.

Example Script Outline

This is a fill-in-the-blank outline, not a word-for-word script. Adapt every line to the specific company. Natural beats rehearsed every time.

The 60-Second Template
[0:00] "I came across [specific thing about the company — a piece of content, a result, their offer page]. That's what made me reach out directly."
One sentence. Specific. Shows you did the work.
[0:12] "Quick background on me: I've been in high-ticket sales for [X time], most recently on [offer type/vertical] closing [deal size] at [close rate or result]. Before that, [one other relevant data point]."
Two sentences. No resume list. Just the numbers that matter.
[0:28] "The reason I think there could be a strong fit here is [connection between your background and their specific offer, customer, or sales environment]."
This is the most important part. If you cannot make this connection specifically, go back to your research.
[0:50] "I'd love to schedule 20 minutes to ask you a few questions about the role and see if this is a mutual fit. I'm available [specific days]. Let me know."
Direct. Confident. Leaves no ambiguity about what you want next.

Record It, Watch It Once, Send It

The Process

Record the Loom. Watch it exactly once. If you stumbled badly in the first 5 seconds, re-record once. If it is an otherwise good take with a minor imperfection, send it. Perfection is not the goal. Authenticity and specificity are the goal. A rep who waited two hours to send the perfect Loom still loses to the rep who sent a solid one same day.

How Many Takes Is Too Many

Two. Three maximum if the first two were genuinely off. After that, you are in your head and the video will sound like it. What kills a Loom faster than a stumble is the flat, over-practiced energy of a rep who has recorded the same script 11 times and sounds robotic. Record it fresh. Send it.
