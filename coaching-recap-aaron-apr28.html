What is Working

Your opening frame is clean and professional

You set the tone immediately — open dialogue, no slides, we'll see if there's a fit. Steve relaxed into that. He came in as a skeptical buyer and you disarmed that in the first 60 seconds. That is a skill. Keep that exact opening on every call.

Discovery was solid and you listened well

You uncovered the E2 visa situation, the son's role, Steve's timeline shifting to "not this year," the capital sitting in the stock market, and his core criteria: ROI under a year, not wanting to be a slave to the business. You also found out about BNB Authority and exactly why Steve didn't move forward with them — the numbers didn't add up and the cleaning cost didn't make sense. That competitive intel shaped how you positioned Vacarya later. That is good salesmanship.

The three-part model explanation was clear

Find, launch, operate. You walked Steve through each piece without getting technical or overwhelming him. He said "yeah, that makes sense" at every checkpoint. The operational system explanation — the SOPs, WhatsApp support, virtual assistant option — landed well for someone who specifically said he does not want to be a slave to the business. You matched the pitch to what he told you mattered.

The real data slide was the right move

The Kansas City property doing $40,838 in revenue with $18,332 in profit is a real number from a real client. You didn't just project — you showed it. And you stayed conservative when presenting it: "I don't like blowing smoke up the chimney." Steve heard that and trusted it. That restraint actually built more confidence than a bigger number would have.

The regulation objection was handled well

Steve asked about changing STR laws in Florida and Texas — a real concern. You had a real answer: multifamily commercial buildings are zoned differently from residential, and if regulations ever do shift, you get a hotel license. You referenced Philadelphia as a real example. Steve visibly relaxed after that answer. That is what objection handling looks like when you actually know your product.

The E2 close at the end was smart

Bringing up the E2 success rate in the final minutes — 100% success, mostly Canadian clients, access to your E2 lawyer — reactivated the original reason Steve got on the call. He said "that's really helpful, for sure." That was a good instinct. The timing was late but it reinforced the decision to come back for the follow-up.

Where to Sharpen

1. Steve asked "so what do we do?" and you did not close

At 38:54, after you handled the cleaner objection and explained the virtual assistant option, Steve said: "So how do we, what do we do?" That is a prospect asking you to close them. That is not a common moment. Most closers would give anything for a prospect to say that on a first call. Instead of presenting a next step toward payment, you said "so Steve, I guess you tell me, my friend" — and handed the decision back to him. Steve then filled the silence with "I need to talk to my son first." The son had not been a real gatekeeper until that moment. You created the opening for that objection by not closing when you had the green light.

Steve at 38:54: "So how do we, what do we do?" — This is the close. The response should have been: "I'd love to get you started. Let's look at what that looks like for you today."
2. The son's role was never clearly mapped in discovery

Steve mentioned his son early — the E2, the electrician path, the visa situation. But you never established whether Steve needed his son's approval to make this financial decision. Steve said "I have money. I want to do something with it." That is an independent buyer. His son was context, not a co-decision maker. By not clarifying this in discovery, you left the door open for the son to become a gatekeeper at the end of the call. When Steve brought him up after the close attempt, you accepted it rather than addressing it: "I can do this on my own. He doesn't have to be interested in it." Steve said that himself. That was your signal to anchor back to his authority and close.

Discovery question to add: "Is this a decision you're making on your own, or would your son need to be part of the decision before you moved forward?"
3. "I need to think about it" was not addressed

When Steve said "I have to talk to my son first. I have to think about it for a bit," you moved straight to booking the follow-up. You did not ask what specifically he needed to think about. "I need to think about it" and "I need to talk to my son" are two different things — and neither was explored. What is the one thing that would make him comfortable moving forward? You do not know. That question would either surface the real objection and let you handle it now, or it would confirm there is none and give Steve a reason to decide today.

"Totally, I hear you. What specifically is the thing you want to think through? I want to make sure I've actually answered everything on my end."
4. The pipeline has too many steps to the close now

The current path to closed is: (1) follow-up call with son, (2) client reference call, (3) close. That is three more conversations before Steve spends money. Each additional step gives him more time to talk himself out of it, find another competitor, or just go quiet. The client reference in particular was added as a formal step when it should have been offered as a confidence tool that runs in parallel, not as a required checkpoint. The goal of the follow-up call with the son should be to close, not to set up another call.

Reframe the follow-up: get the son on the call, close them both. Offer the client reference as something that can happen this week, not as a gate before the decision.

Three Things to Do on the Follow-Up

1. Open the follow-up by anchoring Steve's decision authority

Before the son says anything, establish that Steve is the one making this decision. Do not let the son accidentally become a veto. Steve has the capital, Steve did the research, Steve got on the call. The son is a stakeholder, not the decision maker.

"Steve, before I bring your son up to speed, I just want to make sure I'm clear — this is your capital and your investment, right? Your son is part of the bigger picture with the E2, but the decision to get started is yours."
2. Close on the follow-up call — do not book another one

The follow-up is not a second discovery call. Walk the son through the model, answer his questions, and then ask for the decision. Steve is already at an 8-9. You are not starting over. You are finishing what you started.

"Based on everything Steve and I talked through last week, and now that you've had a chance to hear it — does this make sense to you both? Is there anything standing in the way of getting started?"
3. Use the E2 angle as a closing tool, not just context

Steve is putting money in the stock market while it crashes and worrying about his son's visa options. This business solves both problems. The $25K goes toward the E2 application. The portfolio generates income. The son can be the one running it. You have a perfect close sitting right there — use it explicitly.

"Think about what you told me at the start — you want options for your son and you want your money working for you. This does both. The capital you invest goes toward the E2. The business gives your son something to run when he's ready. The income starts coming in now. There is no version of this where you lose."

What to keep doing

Your discovery is strong, your frame is professional, and your product knowledge is solid. Steve trusted you. He said "I like your job." That is a prospect who is sold on the person, not just the product. The call was genuinely good. The only thing that got in the way of a same-call close was not pulling the trigger when Steve handed you the green light. Everything else — the rapport, the model explanation, the data, the objection handling — was working.

Bottom Line

Aaron, Steve was ready. He had the money, he liked the model, he said the price "wasn't bad," and he literally asked what the next step was. The close was there. The lesson from this call is simple: when a prospect asks you what to do next, tell them. Don't hand the decision back. Don't wait. Walk them to the close. Go into Friday's call with one goal — get the decision. Not another follow-up. Not a client reference scheduled for later. The decision.
