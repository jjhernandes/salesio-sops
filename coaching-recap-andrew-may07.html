What We Covered

Topic

Pitching first on DMVs

Andrew asked whether it's okay to pitch before doing discovery, since his DMV calls require giving context before he can ask questions. JJ confirmed: giving context first is fine when necessary — the issue isn't the order, it's reading where the buyer is and not dragging the process when they don't need it.

Topic

Sales is about versatility and awareness, not process

Andrew's calls are short and surface level because his buyers are sophisticated. JJ reframed this as a strength — the problem isn't the lack of a deeper framework, it's not reading buyer intention in real time. Some buyers come in already sold. Going deeper with them creates resistance, not rapport.

"It's more about navigating and knowing where they are on that buying scale than it is anything else."

Key Issue

Oversharing creates doubt

JJ pointed to specific examples from Andrew's recorded calls — sharing unnecessary context (iCloud email issues, technical problems) that didn't benefit the conversation. With high-level buyers, extra information doesn't build trust. It raises questions about whether the company has its act together.

"People will assume in the positive with the less they know. The less you say, the more they fill in the gaps in your favor."

Example: being 2 minutes late. Say "appreciate your patience" — not "I spilled coffee on myself and my day's been a mess." The second version gives away your frame before the call even starts.

Key Issue

Don't de-sell opt-in calls

For booked calendar calls — these people came in with intention. Asking too many questions or adding unnecessary context makes them think more, not feel more confident. The job is to confirm they're clear, close loops on anything that might create resistance, and get out of the way.

"Everything that slows down a sale is rooted in confusion. Everything that speeds up a sale is rooted in clarity."

Framework

Clarity before price

Before setting a price expectation with anyone who seems uncertain, make sure they clearly understand what they're buying. Check in the same way JJ checks in during coaching — "does that make sense?" — and wait for a genuine "yes," not a polite one. Only drop the number once they're nodding, not still processing.

If you sense uncertainty in their tone, call it: "I sense some uncertainty — what needs to be more clear?" This shows you care and keeps them from no-showing instead of just telling you the truth.

Soft Spot

Sounding young on calls

Andrew's age only shows when he says too much. Short, confident responses — "okay, that makes sense, why are you doing that now?" — project more authority than longer explanations. Less words, more control.

Key Principles from This Call

Clarity sells, confusion kills. Everything that slows down a sale is rooted in confusion. Get them crystal clear before anything else.

Less context, more intention. Oversharing doesn't build trust with sophisticated buyers. It plants doubt. Say less, let them assume positive.

Read the buying scale. Some people come in sold. Don't talk them out of it. Know where they are and respond to that, not to a script.

Words = authority. Fewer words, delivered with confidence, project more authority than more words delivered nervously.

Action Items

  • Stop sharing operational context on calls (email issues, tech problems). If something's wrong, just fix it quietly and move on.
  • Before dropping price on any uncertain prospect, check for genuine understanding first — "does that make sense / is that clear?" — wait for real buy-in.
  • If you hear uncertainty in someone's tone, name it directly: "I'm sensing some uncertainty — what needs to be more clear?"
  • Practice awareness outside calls. In everyday conversations with your girlfriend, friends, anyone — when you notice they're unclear or not tracking, call it out. Build the muscle.
  • When dialing, remove visual distractions. Turn away from the screen, close your eyes if needed — put full attention on their voice and tone.
  • Take 2-3 seconds before responding on anything unclear. If you can't answer it in your head, ask for clarification instead of guessing.

Context

Andrew is a setter working opt-in calendar calls and DMVs for an AI offer (~$25K). His calls run 6-7 minutes and are intentionally short. His buyers are high earners ($250K-$1M/year). He's graduating high school today and moving to Miami in late August.

JJ's overall read: Andrew isn't doing anything that ruins calls. The gaps are subtle — extra context that creates doubt, not reading uncertainty in tone fast enough, and not knowing when a buyer is already sold. No major process overhaul needed. Just sharper awareness.
