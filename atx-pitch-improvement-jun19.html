Foundation

The Core Principle

Rule #1
Discovery is more important than the pitch. You curate the pitch around the discovery.
If you don't know exactly what they need, want, and understand — your pitch will always fall flat. Not because you didn't listen, but because you didn't ask the questions that yield the answers you need.
Rule #2
"How much money am I going to make?" is what they actually care about.
Lead with the result. The education, the legal framework, the provider story — that's all to build reassurance and confidence around the result. Don't bury the number.
Rule #3
Skip what's already been covered. Don't repeat what they already know.
If something was established in discovery, skip it in the pitch. Jump to the next thing. The pitch structure is a guide — not a script to read top to bottom every time.
Before The Call

Pre-Call Nurture Sequence

After discovery and before the presentation call — send this. The goal is to get them to the pitch already warm, already curious, already sold on the concept. So you're closing, not educating from scratch.

Step 1
Right After Discovery
  • Thank them for their time
  • Confirm next call date/time
  • Tell them you're sending something to watch before the call
Step 2
Send the Video
  • 6–8 minute video max
  • Covers the supported living model simply
  • Includes 1–3 example deals (not 5, not 10)
  • Shows the money flow visual
  • Ends with social proof / results
Step 3
Frame The Next Call
  • "Watch this before we jump on"
  • "Anything I don't answer in the video, we'll cover live"
  • "We'll also walk through a live deal together"
Why This Works

They're showing up to the pitch call already knowing the model. You're not educating from zero. The call becomes a conversation about their specific situation and a specific deal — not a seminar. They're primed to say yes, not catching up.

Don't Do This

Don't send 5+ deals. It's like a restaurant menu with 80 items — overwhelming, not impressive. Send 1–3 examples that show range. Let the live deal on the call do the heavy lifting.

The Pitch

Revised Pitch Structure — Under 15 Min

This is the sequence when they already watched the pre-call video. Skip sections they already understand. Hit what matters to them based on what came out of discovery.

Time Section What to Cover When to Skip
0–2 min Quick Reset Recap what you learned in discovery. "Based on what you told me, you're looking for X. So I want to walk you through something that specifically addresses that." Then set the structure: "Here's what we're going to cover today — three things." Never skip this. It anchors everything.
2–5 min The Deal — Result First Open with the specific deal you're showing them. Property address, price, yield, monthly income. Show the numbers immediately. "This is what it looks like." Pull up the deal sheet only — clean, one view. Don't skip. This is what they care about.
5–9 min How It Works Now explain the model only enough to support the deal. The government money flow visual here. Keep it to 2–3 points max. If they watched the video, this is a 60-second confirmation, not a lesson. Shorten if they already understand the model from the video.
9–12 min Risk + Protection Story Proactively address the provider risk — don't wait for the objection. Walk through: provider fails → government problem → legal framework activates → specialist steps in → you still get paid. Reference real cases (1CIC 2024, 2012, 2007). Then the big players: BlackRock, Aviva, USS. Cut to 2 min if they seem confident. If they're skeptical, give it full time.
12–14 min Long-Term Picture Refinance strategy (50% LTV pull), exit options, capital growth (Savills projection). Show the 10-year cash flow increase. Keep it brief — most don't need all of this. Let them ask. Skip if they're clearly ready to move. Save for questions.
14–15 min Next Steps "Simple next step: £5k reservation, signed reservation form. 28 days to exchange. You own the asset, you start getting paid." Shut up after that. Never skip this.
Key Insight

You don't need to run every section every time. The table above is a mental map, not a checklist. What came up in discovery tells you which sections matter most to this person. Use the structure to stay organized — not to deliver a presentation.

Screen Sharing

Screen Sharing Rules

The visual assets are strong. The problem is sharing the screen too much, too early, and moving around too fast. Here's when to share and when to stay off.

1
Only share when it adds clarity a verbal explanation can't
The money flow diagram (government → business → investor) is the clearest visual in your arsenal. That earns screen share time. Bullet point slides and document navigation do not.
2
One view at a time — never scroll while talking
When you share and move around the screen, their brain is trying to track two things at once: what you're saying and what you're showing. You lose them. Show one thing. Explain it. Stop sharing. Continue talking.
3
The deal sheet gets its own moment
When you pull up the deal — that's a dedicated screen share. Show the property. Show the numbers. Let them sit with it. Don't immediately switch to something else. Silence while they look at the deal is fine.
4
Objections: pull up the specific slide, nothing else
If they ask "what happens if the provider goes bust?" — pull up the provider failure flow and walk through it step by step. Don't share the whole deck. One slide. One objection. Done.
5
Default position: camera on, screen off
Most of the pitch should be face to face, not slides. The more they look at your screen, the less they're feeling the relationship. Connection is what people actually buy.
Objections

Objection Handling

These are the most common objections in this pitch. Don't wait for them — address them proactively at the right moment in the flow.

"What happens if the provider goes bust?"
Walk through the failure sequence: Provider fails → government faces two problems (residents at risk + emergency housing cost at £250M-500M/yr) → Housing and Planning Act activates → MySham steps in → investors continue to get paid. Reference: 1CIC 2024, same structure in 2012 and 2007. Then anchor with the big players: BlackRock, Canada Life, Aviva, USS.
Pull up: Provider Failure Flow visual
"The return isn't guaranteed right?"
Be upfront about it — don't hedge. "The rent isn't guaranteed in the way a bank account is guaranteed. What you have is government-funded income backed by legislation, long-term contract, and the covenant strength of businesses with tens of millions under management. That's a very different risk profile than buy-to-let." Then compare: BTL at 6% yield vs this at 13% net.
No visual needed — verbal framing is stronger here
"I can only pull 50% equity on refinance"
Acknowledge it. "That's correct — and it's worth knowing upfront. 50% LTV. But here's what that actually looks like: you pull out half your capital three months in, and you're still sitting at 18.7% cash-on-cash return. Most buy-to-let investors would love that number." Show the cash-on-cash return calculation.
Pull up: Cash-on-cash return comparison table
"Is Hull actually a good location?"
Pull up the Savills capital growth projection. "Hull's going through a massive regeneration. Savills — one of the biggest property research firms in the UK — projects just under 30% capital growth over 5 years. That's not us saying it." Then show the property and floor plan briefly.
Pull up: Savills projection + property location
"I need to think about it" / "Send me more information"
Don't send a pack of documents. Ask: "Of course — what specifically do you want to think through? Is it the return, the risk side, or just the timing?" Get to the real objection. Most "I need to think" responses are "I'm not convinced on one thing yet."

Discovery

Discovery — What You're Listening For

Your pitch should be shaped entirely by what you pull out in discovery. These are the answers you need before you ever open a presentation.

What to Find Out

Their current portfolio situation — Do they have buy-to-let already? How fed up are they with it? Have they experienced Section 21 issues?

Their primary goal — Cash flow, capital growth, passive income, or exit? Not everyone wants the same thing from the same deal.

Their knowledge level — Have they heard of supported living? How much do they know about the model? This tells you how much education you need in the pitch vs. how much you can skip.

Their capital position — Can they move at £150k+? Are they buying cash or do they need finance? This changes which sections of the pitch matter most.

Their biggest fear — What would stop them? If they say "I'm worried about the tenant going bust" — you know to spend more time on the protection story.

Their timeline — Are they looking to invest this quarter or "eventually"? If eventually, they're not ready for the close. Find out why and address it.

The Fitness Coach Analogy (JJ)

"She was by far one of the best coaches I'd ever seen — but she just pitched everything she could do. She'd have charged whatever she wanted if she had found out what that specific person needed. Information shared without context doesn't land. The same pitch repeated to different people with different needs will always underperform."

The Frame Shift

After discovery, get them to articulate why they want to work with you — not the other way around. "Based on what you've told me, I don't think every product we have is right for you — but this one might be." Let them tell you why it fits. That's the close doing itself.

Summary — What Changes Tomorrow

1. Pre-call video goes out after every discovery call. 6–8 min, covers the model, shows 1–3 example deals.

2. Open every pitch call with the deal — numbers first. Don't build to it. Lead with it.

3. Screen share is surgical. Money flow visual. Deal sheet. Specific objection slides. Nothing else.

4. Skip what discovery already covered. Don't repeat the problem if they know the problem. Jump to the asset.
