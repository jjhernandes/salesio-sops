Lesson 3 · Section 11

No Neutral Influence

You're moving them up or pulling them down. There is no neutral on a sales call. Your state transfers — either you elevate the prospect or you depress them. Learn which one you're doing.

There Is No Neutral

Every human interaction has an emotional direction. Two people get on a call and one of them is going to move the other — up in energy, clarity, and certainty, or down into confusion, pressure, and guardedness.

Most reps assume they're neutral when they're not performing at their best. They think "I wasn't at my best today but I wasn't bad either." That's not how influence works. A flat, disengaged, outcome-attached rep does not produce a neutral prospect. They produce a depressed one.

Why This Matters

The prospect's emotional state at the end of the call is what they buy or don't buy from. Not the features. Not the price. Their state. A prospect who feels more certain, more hopeful, and more clear about their situation is a prospect who buys. A prospect who feels confused, pressured, or guarded doesn't buy — and doesn't refer people either.

Your state sets their state. You don't get to opt out of that dynamic. You just get to decide whether you're doing it consciously or unconsciously.

The dangerous assumption: "I'm a professional — I keep my energy neutral on calls so the prospect can decide freely." Wrong. Neutral energy from a rep reads as low energy. Low energy depresses the prospect's state. There is no benevolent neutral. You are either a lift or a drag.

What Elevating Looks Like

The Elevated Prospect

After talking to you, they feel more certain about their situation than they did before. They're clearer on what's been blocking them. They have more hope about what's possible. They feel genuinely heard — not sold to, not manipulated, not rushed.

You don't have to manufacture this. It happens naturally when your state is genuinely high and your orientation is genuinely toward them. You can't fake it long-term. The only sustainable way to elevate a prospect is to actually be elevated yourself and actually care about them.

  • ✓
    Their pace slows down and opens up. When a prospect starts the call guarded and clipped, then gradually becomes more expansive and honest, you're elevating them. They're relaxing into the conversation because you're giving them something safe to move toward.
  • ✓
    They start telling you things they didn't plan to tell you. The real stuff — the fear about making another bad investment, the embarrassment about where they are, the thing they haven't admitted to themselves. That's the conversation under the conversation. When it surfaces, you're operating at an elevating level.
  • ✓
    Their certainty increases. Not because you hammered them with value props — because you helped them get clear on their own situation. A genuinely clear prospect, who actually needs what you're offering, will close themselves. Your job is to get them clear.
  • ✓
    They thank you for the conversation regardless of the outcome. Even if they don't buy, an elevated prospect feels grateful for the time. That's how you know you served the conversation and not just your pipeline.

What Depressing Looks Like

The depressed prospect closes their access to you. They become more guarded as the call progresses, not less. Their answers get shorter. They hedge more. They're not thinking through their situation — they're managing you. That's a signal that your energy is a drag, not a lift.

Rep Behavior Effect on Prospect State
Rushing through discovery to get to pitch Prospect feels unheard. Guardedness increases. They're preparing objections instead of opening up.
Filling every silence immediately Prospect can't think. Anxiety in the rep transmits as anxiety in the conversation. The call feels pressured.
Over-explaining or restating after each point Prospect loses confidence in the rep. The rep sounds uncertain. If the rep doesn't believe it, why should they?
Reacting defensively to objections Prospect now knows they've hit a nerve. They double down on the objection or disengage entirely.
Bringing up price or urgency without setup Prospect feels manipulated. Trust breaks. Anything said after that has lower credibility.

Audit Your Last 5 Calls

The Exercise

Pull up your last five call recordings and ask one question about each: did the prospect become more open or more closed over the course of that conversation? Not whether you closed them — whether their state elevated or declined from minute 5 to minute 45.

If most of your prospects are ending calls more guarded than they started, that's a state problem, not a technique problem. No script fixes a depressing state.

01
Marker
Did their answer length increase or decrease?
Prospects who are being elevated give longer, more honest answers as the call progresses. Prospects being depressed give shorter, more guarded answers. Listen for this pattern in your recordings.
02
Marker
Did they volunteer information or just answer questions?
An elevated prospect starts adding context without being asked — they're in the conversation, not just responding to it. A depressed prospect responds only to what you ask and nothing more. They're protecting themselves.
03
Marker
How did the call end emotionally?
Not whether they bought — how did they sound at the end? Energized and grateful, or tired and relieved it was over? The emotional exit of the call tells you more about your impact than any metric.

"Connection is what people actually buy. Not programs. Not results. The feeling that this person gets it and genuinely wants this for me."

— JJ Hernandez

The rep who elevates every prospect they talk to — regardless of whether the deal closes — builds a reputation that compounds over time. Referrals come from people who felt elevated, not people who were sold. Every call is a chance to send someone away better than they arrived. That's the standard.
