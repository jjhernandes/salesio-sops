Sales Interview 101

How to walk into a closer or setter interview, hold your frame, and leave them wanting to hire you. Everything in here applies whether it's a Zoom screen share with a sales director or a multi-step hiring process at a big team.

Section 01

The Frame

The biggest mistake people make going into a sales interview is treating it like a job application. It's not. It's a sales call where the product is you.

And the same rules apply. The person who needs it the most will come across as desperate. They can feel it before you say a word. When you walk in needing this specific role to survive, that energy leaks into everything — how fast you talk, how much you agree with everything they say, how you handle a tough question.

The frame you want to walk in with: you're looking for the right fit. You're evaluating them the same way they're evaluating you. That's not arrogance, that's just true. You're about to invest your time, your energy, and your reputation into someone else's offer. You have every right to be selective.

The shift: Go from "I hope they pick me" to "I hope this is the right room." That one change in how you walk in will change everything about how you come across.
Section 02

Before You Get On

You should know three things before you hit accept on that Zoom link.

Know the offer. Not just what it is — who it's for, what transformation it promises, what the prospect is actually afraid of. If you don't know who's buying this and why, you're not ready. Go find a VSL, a sales page, a podcast episode. Whatever they have publicly. Understand it.

Know your numbers. If you have them, bring them. Close rate, average ticket, total revenue generated. If you don't have them yet, be honest and direct about where you are and why you're ready. Confidence in your starting point is better than vague promises about your potential.

Have one specific question ready. Something that shows you actually did your homework. Not "what does the role look like" — something specific to their offer, their market, their team. That question does more than your resume.

Section 03

How to Show Up

Slow down. The faster you talk, the more nervous you look. Composure is slow. Take your time with answers. A brief pause before you respond reads as confidence, not hesitation.

Don't fill silence. If you finish answering and they don't respond immediately, let it sit. The reflex to keep talking to fill dead air is the same thing that kills sales calls. Silence is fine. Let it be.

Answer the question and stop. Over-explaining is a tell. Give the answer, give one example if it helps, and stop. If they want more they'll ask.

Have opinions. If you agree with everything they say, you have no frame. You're auditioning instead of having a conversation. If they say something you see differently, you can say so respectfully. That's not a risk. Agreeing with everything is the risk.

Watch for this: The interview version of the needy closer is the candidate who laughs at everything, agrees with everything, and ends every sentence going up in pitch like a question. It signals you need their approval. Don't be that person.
Starting From Zero

If You Have Little or No Experience

No track record is scary going into an interview. But here's what most new reps don't understand: the companies that hire new closers aren't looking for a finished product. They're looking for the right raw material. Coachable, hungry, and willing to do the work.

The problem isn't that you don't have experience. The problem is when you act like that's a bigger deal than it is. The minute you apologize for where you are, you've already lost the frame.

The reframe: Experience means you've closed deals. It doesn't mean you're good. And no experience means no bad habits either. Own that.

What to build before the interview

01
Do mock calls. Even ten recorded practice calls gives you something real to reference. You don't need paid experience — you need reps.
02
Study the offer deeply. Know what it promises, who it's for, and what objections their buyers typically have. This matters more than your resume.
03
Know exactly what training you've done. Sales.io, a course, a mentor, anything. Name it specifically. "I've been training" means nothing. "I've been doing X, which taught me Y" is something.
04
Have a real answer to "why sales." Not "I'm a people person." The actual reason you chose this career. That answer tells them who you are faster than any number does.

How to answer the hard questions without numbers

Q
How many calls have you closed?

Be honest and redirect. Don't hedge — state it directly and then give them something real: "I don't have closed calls on a high-ticket offer yet. What I have is [training background], [X mock calls completed], and a clear picture of where I need to get to. I'm not looking for easy — I'm looking for the right place to build."

That's not weakness. That's someone who knows what they're doing and where they're going. Most interviewers respect it more than someone who can't explain their own numbers.

Q
Why should we hire someone with no experience over a proven closer?

Don't get defensive. Answer it directly. "A proven closer with bad habits is harder to coach than someone who's built on the right foundation from the start. I'm not bringing in bad patterns. I'm coming in ready to learn this offer the right way."

If they push back on this, that's actually useful information about how they think about training and development.

Q
Have you done any sales before?

Anything counts. Retail, customer service, convincing a friend to do something, negotiating anything — all of it involves influence. Don't dismiss it. "I haven't closed high-ticket remotely yet, but I've [short example]. What I learned from it was [specific insight about people or buying behavior]."

The insight at the end is what matters. It shows you're already thinking about sales at the right level.

What kills your shot as a new rep: Desperation. If you come across like you need this specific job to survive, they'll sense it. Apply to multiple offers at once. Go into each one genuinely okay walking away. That's not a tactic — it's what actually keeps you in the right frame.
Lead With
+Coachability — and show it, don't say it
+Specific training you've done and what you took from it
+Honest framing of where you are and where you're going
+Questions that show you understand their offer
+Energy and certainty — hunger without desperation
Avoid
-Apologizing for your starting point
-Overpromising — "I'll be your best closer in 30 days"
-Saying you're a "fast learner" with nothing to back it up
-Vague answers about why you want to do sales
-Treating this like your only shot — it rarely is
Section 04

Common Questions and How to Answer Them

Q
Tell me about yourself.

Keep it short and relevant. Where you've been, what you've learned, where you're going. Two minutes max. They don't need your life story — they need to know if you can sell and if you're coachable. End with why you're looking at this type of role right now.

The mistake: going chronological from the beginning and giving them your full resume out loud. They can read. Tell them the story behind the story.

Q
Why do you want to work here?

Be specific. Generic answers — "I love sales, I'm driven, I want to grow" — are disqualifying. Reference something real about their offer, their market, or what you've seen of their team. If you can't be specific, you didn't do your homework and they'll know it.

Q
What are your numbers? What's your close rate?

If you have numbers, give them. Be specific and honest — don't inflate them. Specific numbers that aren't perfect are more credible than vague claims that sound great.

If you're new and don't have numbers yet: own it directly. "I'm coming in without a track record on a high-ticket offer. What I can tell you is what I've been building and why I'm confident in my ability to learn this fast." Then give them something real — door-to-door experience, any sales context, mock calls, the training you've done.

Don't fake it. They're going to find out. Honesty with confidence lands better than inflated numbers that fall apart under one follow-up question.

Q
How do you handle objections?

Don't name a method. Don't say NEPQ. Don't recite a framework. Talk about what you actually do — which is try to understand what's behind what they're saying. "I need to think about it" isn't an objection about time. "I can't afford it" isn't always about money. The real question is what are they actually afraid of.

Give one real example if you have it. "Someone told me they needed to think about it, I asked what specifically felt unresolved, and we found out the real issue was..." That kind of answer shows you can actually do it, not just describe it.

Q
What's your sales philosophy?

This is your moment. Say something real, not something you read in a course. If you've been through Sales.io training, you have a real answer here. Talk about what you actually believe about why people buy and how you think about conversations. Be specific and be willing to defend it.

The wrong answer is any sentence that starts with "I believe in building rapport and trust and then..." That's the generic version of this answer and it tells them nothing about you.

Q
Where do you see yourself in 5 years?

Be honest. If you want to be running your own business one day, say that — the right offer owner respects ambition. If you want to be a top closer building long-term equity in a company, say that. A straight answer that's real is better than the corporate version of this answer every time.

Q
Do you have any questions for us?

Yes. Always. See the next section.

Section 05

What to Ask Them

The questions you ask tell them more about you than most of your answers. Ask things that show you actually understand what it takes to close at a high level. Not "what does the role look like." You want to know the real stuff.

01
What's the average close rate on this offer right now, and what does your top closer do differently from the average one?
02
What does lead quality look like? Where are most of these prospects in terms of awareness when they get on a call?
03
What does onboarding look like for a new closer? How long before I'm expected to be at full capacity?
04
What's the main reason closers don't work out on this offer?
05
How do you handle call reviews and coaching? Is there a feedback process in place?
06
What does compensation look like and how is it structured? (Ask this toward the end, not first.)
Section 06

Closing the Interview

Before you get off the call, ask this:

"Based on our conversation, is there anything that would give you hesitation about moving forward with me?"

Most candidates never ask this. It shows confidence. It also gives you a chance to handle any real concern right there instead of losing the opportunity on a follow-up you never get.

If they say no, great. Then ask what the next step looks like and when you can expect to hear back. Pin it down. A vague "we'll be in touch" is not a next step.

If they surface a concern, handle it the same way you'd handle an objection on a sales call. Understand it, address it directly, and move forward.

Section 07

Red Flags to Watch For

Not every offer is worth your time. If you see these in the interview, pay attention.

!
They can't tell you their average close rate or ticket size. If they don't know their own numbers, the offer has problems.
!
Vague about compensation. "Unlimited earning potential" with no specific structure. Every serious offer can tell you exactly what their top closer makes.
!
No onboarding process. They expect you to just figure it out. That means you're going to burn through leads while you learn their offer on your own.
!
They can't explain why closers don't work out. Either they haven't thought about it or the real answer is something they don't want to say.
!
High pressure to start immediately with no ramp time. They need a body on the phone more than they need the right person on the phone.
Section 08

What Not to Do

Do
+Take your time with answers
+Be honest about where you are
+Ask specific questions about the offer
+Have an opinion and be willing to share it
+Close the interview by asking about hesitations
+Pin down the next step before you leave
+Let silence sit when it comes
Don't
-Name-drop methods like NEPQ as your answer
-Inflate your numbers
-Ask about compensation first
-Agree with everything they say
-Over-explain your answers
-Say "I'm very coachable" — show it instead
-Leave without knowing what happens next
