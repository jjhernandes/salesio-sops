Weak Questions vs. Presupposition Rewrites

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. Every question creates conditions for the answer you get. Weak questions create weak answers. Presupposition rewrites create forward-moving conversations.

The Principle

What Makes a Question Weak

A weak question invites a yes/no answer, creates optionality the prospect shouldn't have at that stage, or opens a door to a frame that works against the conversation. Most weak questions are lazy habits — things reps say because they're comfortable, not because they move the conversation forward.

The pattern of a weak question: it puts the prospect in a position to easily say no or retreat. "Would you be interested in...?" "Could you see yourself...?" "Is that something you'd want?" These all have trap doors.

Every question should create the conditions for the answer you need. That doesn't mean manipulating the prospect. It means asking in a way that moves the conversation toward clarity, not toward exits.

The Pattern: Diagnose, Then Rewrite

For each weak question below, note what's wrong with it before reading the rewrite. The habit to build is recognizing weak framing in real time — then swapping it without stopping the flow of the call.

Full Comparison Table — 12 Rewrites

Weak Question What's Wrong With It Presupposition Rewrite
"Would you be interested in improving your close rate?" Offers a yes/no. Assumes they might not want this obvious thing. Sounds like a survey opener. "What's been the main thing holding your close rate back?"
"Is now a good time for you?" Invites a "no." Creates an easy escape at the top of the call before any value is established. "You blocked off this time — walk me through where you're at and what made you want to do this call."
"Do you think you could afford this?" Frames the prospect as potentially unable. Embarrassing question that signals low confidence in your offer. "When it comes to the investment, what matters most to you — structure, return timeline, or flexibility?"
"Could you see this working for you?" Places the burden of proof on the prospect's imagination. Invites doubt. "What would need to be in place for this to be a clear decision for you?"
"Do you want to think about it?" Conceding the close before being asked to. Signals you expected a "no." "What specifically are you still working through?"
"Does that make sense?" Constant use signals insecurity. Turns pitch into a quiz. Breaks the flow. [Say the thing. Hold silence. Let them respond.]
"Would you like to join?" Timid close. Sounds like you're hoping. No certainty in it. "Based on everything we've talked about, the next step is getting you started. Which payment option works best for you?"
"Is there anything holding you back?" Invites a laundry list of objections all at once. Bad timing and bad framing. "What's the one thing that, if it were resolved, would make this a clear yes for you?"
"If you had to guess, what would you say?" Unnecessary hedge. Makes you sound uncertain about your own question. "What's your gut telling you right now?"
"Can I ask you something?" Permission-seeking before a question is a composure leak. Just ask the question. [Ask the question directly.]
"What's your budget?" Premature. Puts the focus on cost before value is established. Invites anchoring below your price. "When you've made investments like this in the past, what made them feel worth it?"
"Do you have any questions for me?" End-of-call cop-out. Hands control back to the prospect and breaks the close frame. "What's the most important thing you want to be clear on before we move forward?"

Building the Habit

The Real-Time Test

Before you ask a question on a call, run it through this filter: does this question give the prospect an easy door to retreat through? If yes, reframe it before you speak. The pause it takes is worth it.

The hardest ones to kill: "Does that make sense?" and "Is that okay?" Both signal insecurity. Both can be eliminated completely. Let your statements land. Trust the silence that follows.

Drill

Pull your last 3 call recordings. Write down every question you asked. Flag every weak one. Rewrite each using the patterns from this lesson. Then drill the rewrites in mock calls until they're automatic.

This is not a vocabulary exercise. It's a frame-control exercise. The questions you ask are the call you're running.
