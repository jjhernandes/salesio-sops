Core Rule
Never pitch just to pitch. You only present once you have earned the right to present.

A strong close is built in discovery. If discovery is weak, objections become harder, the pitch feels forced, and the prospect will not feel clear enough to decide.

The emotional contract is signed before the pitch ever starts. By the time you transition, they should already feel like they want help — your only job is to show them you're the right person to provide it.
V2 Change — Core Rule

Added the "emotional contract" line. V1 explained the rule logically but didn't name what you're actually building toward. The emotional contract is the concept that ties everything together — if prospects reach Section 11 and suddenly go cold, it's because the contract wasn't fully signed in Sections 2–7. Naming it here primes David to think about it on every call.

S1

Intro: Respect First, Likability Later

Set the frame. Get permission. Establish who's running the call.
Script
Hey [NAME], good to connect. You booked this call about fixing your outbound system — do you still have the full 45 minutes?
Awesome. Here's how this works: I'm going to ask you some fairly in-depth questions to understand exactly where you're at and what's getting in the way. From there, I'll tell you what I think the real problem is — and if it makes sense for both of us, I'll show you exactly how I can help you fix it. Sound good?
V2 Change — Intro

"How this works" — V1 said "I'll ask a few in-depth questions." V2 says "here's how this works." Small word swap, but it shifts the frame from David asking permission to David running a structured process. He's already in control before the first question.

"If it makes sense for both of us" — V1 said "if it makes sense, I'll show you how I can help." V2 adds "for both of us." This is a detachment signal. It tells the prospect David isn't just trying to close them — he has criteria too. That raises the perceived value before a single question is asked.

Key Principle

Tone here is calm and certain. Not warm, not eager. Think doctor, not vendor. The prospect booked the call — you don't need to sell them on showing up. They're already there.

S2

Current Reality

Surface the situation. Get numbers. Understand volume, results, and timeline.
Script
1.What are you trying to sell, and who are you selling it to?
2.How long have you been doing outbound for this offer?
3.Walk me through what you're currently doing — what does your outbound actually look like day to day?
4.How many DMs are you sending a week, roughly?
5.Out of those, how many replies are you getting? And how many of those turn into actual conversations?
6.What's working right now, even a little bit?
7.And what's clearly not working?
8.What are you currently making from this offer — what does a good month look like?
V2 Change — Current Reality

Expanded from 3 questions to 8. V1 had "What are you currently doing," "what's working," and "what's not working." That's surface-level. You need volume numbers (Q4, Q5) because those become your reference point later when you calculate the cost of their skill gap. If they're sending 200 DMs a week and converting 0, that's a very different conversation than 20 DMs and 0 converts.

Added income question (Q8). "What does a good month look like?" surfaces their baseline revenue without making it feel like a financial audit. This number gets used in Future Pace when you ask what they want to be making — the gap between those two numbers is your leverage.

Split "working/not working" into two separate questions (Q6, Q7). In V1 they were combined with a parenthetical. Keeping them separate gives you cleaner answers and avoids them rushing past the "working" part to get to complaints.

S3

Problem Awareness + DM Review

Make them see the gap themselves. Don't tell them what's wrong — guide them to name it.
Script
1.What do you feel is the biggest issue with where things are right now?
2.What have you already tried to fix it?
3.Why do you think that hasn't worked?
→ Pull up their sent DMs / screen share
4.Walk me through your thinking with this message — what were you going for here?
Let them explain first. You're listening for what they think the goal is vs. what's actually happening.
5.When you read it back now, what do you think the person on the other end felt when they got this?
Most people pause here. That pause is the moment of self-awareness. Let it breathe.
6.Can I show you what I'm seeing? [Share the 4 keys — walk through specifically what's off and why]
7.Does that land? Do you see how there's a gap between what you intended and what they actually received?
→ End screen share
8.What do you feel that gap is actually costing you right now — in conversations, in revenue?
9.How long has this been going on?
V2 Change — Problem Awareness

Flipped the DM review order. V1 went: screen share → "walk me through your thought" → then David tells them what's wrong. V2 goes: screen share → "walk me through your thinking" → "when you read it back, what do you think they felt?" → then David shows them. This is critical. When someone names their own problem, they own it. When David names it first, it can feel like a critique. The self-awareness question (Q5) creates the "oh" moment before the expert commentary.

"What were you going for here?" replaced "walk me through your thought with this text." Same intent, more natural language.

Added pause instruction after Q5. The pause is the conversion moment in this section. V1 had no note about it. Most reps talk through silences — this annotation tells David to hold it.

Key Principle

The DM review is your unfair advantage. Nobody else does this on an intro call. It makes the problem visible and concrete in a way that abstract questions never can. Don't rush it. This is where you earn authority.

S4

Solution Awareness

Surface what they already know they need — without telling them first.
Script
1.What do you feel is missing from your current approach?
2.You've been trying to fix this on your own for a while now — what's made it hard to figure out?
3.Considering you haven't solved it yet, how do you actually know what the right solution would look like?
4.If you could wave a wand and have one thing in place that you currently don't — what would it be?
5.What would need to be true for you to feel fully confident moving forward with something new?
V2 Change — Solution Awareness

Added Q2 and Q4. Q2 — "What's made it hard to figure out?" — surfaces the self-doubt or knowledge gap that keeps them stuck. This is important because it directly seeds why expert help matters. Q4 — the wand question — gets them to articulate the solution in their own words before David presents anything. If they say "I need someone to just show me what's wrong with my messages in real time," David's job in the pitch just got significantly easier.

Q3 is your sharpest question in this section. "Considering you haven't solved it yet, how do you know what the right solution would even look like?" V1 had this but didn't set it up. V2 flows into it from Q2 so it doesn't feel like a confrontation — it feels like a natural next question.

S5

Future Pace

Get them living in the outcome before you show them the path.
Script
1.If this was fully solved in the next three to six months — what would your revenue actually look like?
2.Why that number specifically? What does hitting that mean for you?
3.What would that allow you to do that you can't do right now?
4.What would change about how you show up — how you feel about yourself, your business, your day?
5.What would make solving this fully worth it to you?
V2 Change — Future Pace

Added Q2 — "Why that number specifically?" V1 asked for the number and moved on. This follow-up turns a dollar amount into a story. When they explain why $10K/month matters, they reveal the real motivation — the thing that's actually driving this call. That's your anchor for the close later. Reference their reason, not just their number.

Q4 was sharpened. V1 said "What do you feel that would change about how you see yourself?" V2 expands it to include "your business, your day." This opens the emotional range and usually produces a richer answer, especially for people who are identity-driven.

Key Principle

Go slow here. Don't rush to the next question the second they answer. Reflect what they say back before you move on. "So you said hitting $8K would mean you could quit the part-time job — I want to make sure I heard that right." That reflection signals you're actually listening, not just running a script.

S6

Consequence / Cost of Inaction

Make the cost of staying the same feel more real than the cost of change.
Script
1.You mentioned this has been going on for [X months/time]. What has it already cost you — not just in the future, but right now?
2.What happens if this stays exactly the same for the next three to six months?
3.Personally — not just in business — what does that cost you?
4.If you looked at yourself in the mirror six months from now and nothing had changed — what would you feel?
5.Is staying where you are actually an option for you?
V2 Change — Consequence

Added "not just in the future, but right now" to Q1. V1 only asked about the future cost. The past cost is equally powerful — they've already paid it. "What has this already cost you?" makes the loss feel real and accumulated, not hypothetical. Loss aversion research shows people are more moved by what they've already lost than what they might lose.

Q3 was restructured. V1 said "Personally, what does that cost you?" directly after the future question. V2 separates it with "not just in business" — this signals you're asking about something deeper than revenue, which usually produces a more honest and emotionally significant answer.

Q4 adjusted. V1 said "in the mirror, how would you feel about yourself?" V2 says "six months from now, nothing had changed — what would you feel?" Adding the time frame makes it more vivid and specific.

Key Principle

Q5 — "Is staying where you are actually an option?" — is the most important question in this section. Pause after you ask it. You want them to answer it out loud. If they say "no," they've just committed to change before you've pitched anything. The sale is already 80% done.

S7

Committing to Coaching

The most underbuilt section in V1. This is where the emotional contract gets signed.
Script
1.Based on everything you've just told me — why do you think you haven't been able to solve this on your own yet?
2.What's it actually like trying to figure this out through trial and error — what does that process feel like?
3.If you had someone who had already done what you're trying to do, and they were looking at your work with you on a weekly basis — how much faster do you think you'd move?
4.Why specifically — what would be different about that kind of support?
5.What would you need to see from that kind of relationship to know it was actually working?
V2 Change — Committing to Coaching

Expanded from 2 questions to 5. This was the weakest section in V1. The two original questions were valid but thin. This section is where the prospect argues themselves into wanting coaching — that argument needs to be fully developed before you transition to pitch. If they haven't made the case in their own words, price will feel like the obstacle.

Q1 flips the attribution. "Why do you think you haven't been able to solve this on your own?" makes them say, in their own words, why solo effort isn't enough. This is not a leading question — it's an open one. Let them answer it fully.

Q2 is new — the emotional texture question. "What's it actually like trying to figure this out through trial and error?" Most reps skip this entirely. The answer to this question is usually the most emotionally honest thing said on the entire call. Use it at the close.

Q3 plants the specific image of mentorship without naming it as the product yet. "Someone looking at your work with you weekly" is a description of Pillar 2 — but they're endorsing it before they know it's for sale.

Key Principle

Do not transition to pitch until Section 7 is fully developed. If the answers here are short or half-hearted, go deeper with "tell me more about that" or "what do you mean by that specifically?" A rushed Section 7 creates a cold Section 11.

S8

Transition to Pitch

Mirror their situation back using their exact words — then earn the right to present.
Script
Alright. Based on what you've shared — you're sending outbound daily, you know the offer is strong, but the messaging isn't converting the way it should. You said it's been going on for [X months], it's already cost you [what they said], and staying where you are isn't actually an option for you.
I think this is a strong match. Let me walk you through exactly how I help people fix this.
V2 Change — Transition

Made the mirror specific. V1 said: "The main thing I'm hearing is that PROBLEM is stopping you from CONSEQUENCE." That's a template. V2 mirrors their actual situation: the offer is working, the messaging is the gap, it's been going on a specific amount of time, and staying isn't an option. Fill this in with exactly what they said. Use their words, not summaries of their words.

Removed "I think this could be a really strong match." "Could be" hedges. V2 says "I think this is a strong match." Small word but it signals more certainty. You don't need their permission to be confident.

Key Principle

The mirror should be so accurate that they nod or say "yeah, exactly" before you start the pitch. If it lands, the pitch isn't a sales presentation — it's a delivery. They already said yes to the need. You're just showing them what solving it looks like.

S9

Pitch

Three pillars. Connect each one to what they specifically said. Check in after each.
Script
When you join, it's six months. Here's what that includes:
Pillar 1 — Fully tailored outbound scripts for your specific niche and offer.
You mentioned you've been sending messages that don't feel right but you're not sure why. This removes that entirely — you'll know exactly what to say, why it works, and how to adjust it for different conversations.
Check-in: Does that make sense for where you're at right now?
Pillar 2 — Two weekly one-on-one calls where we review your actual conversations and break your specific bottleneck in real time.
You said trying to figure this out through trial and error has been exhausting. This replaces that entirely. We're looking at your real DMs every week — not theory, not frameworks in a vacuum. Your conversations, your results.
Check-in: Can you see why that would move you faster than anything you've tried on your own?
Pillar 3 — My personal number. Text or call me anytime you're stuck between sessions.
Progress doesn't wait for the next scheduled call. When you're in a conversation that's going sideways and you need to know what to say next — I'm available.
Check-in: Does that feel like the kind of access that would actually make a difference?
V2 Change — Pitch

Added connection sentences to each pillar. V1 stated each pillar and asked if they could see how it would help. V2 connects each pillar to something they specifically said in discovery. Pillar 1 references their DM confusion. Pillar 2 references their frustration with trial and error. This turns the pitch from a product list into a direct response to their situation.

Rewrote the check-in questions. V1's check-ins were slightly awkward ("You see how that would help you know exactly what to say always?"). V2 makes them feel like natural conversation: "Does that make sense for where you're at?" "Can you see why that would move you faster?" These invite a yes without pushing for one.

Pillar 3 expanded. V1 said "my personal number so you can text or call anytime." V2 adds "Progress doesn't wait for the next scheduled call." This one sentence makes the access feel like a real benefit instead of a feature on a list.

S10

Confirm Fit

Get a verbal commitment to the solution before you reveal price.
Script
1.Overall — is it clear how those three things together: custom scripts, weekly one-on-ones, and personal access to me would help you get to [THEIR SPECIFIC GOAL]?
2.Why specifically? Walk me through that.
V2 Change — Confirm Fit

Minor — filled in their specific goal. V1 said "THEIR ULTIMATE GOAL" as a placeholder. It's worth noting: when you ask this in real life, use the exact dollar amount or outcome they named in Future Pace. "Would help you get to $8K a month so you can leave the job" is a hundred times more powerful than "help you reach your goal."

"Walk me through that" sharpened from "Why specifically, though?" Same intent, slightly more natural. The goal here is to get them to verbalize their own reasons for wanting this — which makes the transition to price feel like a logical next step, not a pressure point.

Key Principle

Do not skip this section and go straight to price. Section 10 is the verbal handshake before the number lands. If they can clearly articulate why the three pillars would help them, you've reduced the price objection by 70% before it ever comes up.

11

Price Reveal

Say it once. Say it with certainty. Then stop talking.
Script
The investment for the six-month program is $3,000 to get started, and $3,000 once you hit $5,000 in revenue.
— Hold silence. Wait for them to respond. —
V2 Change — Price Reveal

Removed "Are you more of an Amex or Visa kind of guy?" V1 ended with this assumptive close line. The intent is correct — skip the "are you ready" question and go straight to payment. But the line itself can feel abrupt and slightly gimmicky for a coaching sale where you've just spent 40 minutes building deep trust. Save this for shorter-cycle, lower-ticket offers. For a $6K coaching program, the silence after the number is the close. Let it breathe.

Added the silence instruction explicitly. V1 had no mention of what to do after the price reveal. This is one of the most important moments in the entire call. The person who speaks first after the price is said loses frame. Hold it. Let them react.

The milestone pricing structure is strong — keep it. "$3K to start, $3K when you hit $5K" is a trust signal. It means David only gets fully paid when they get results. That reduces the perceived risk before the prospect even raises an objection.

—

Objection Handling

Stay calm. Get curious. Resolve — don't redirect.
If the energy shifts
"[Name], I feel like the energy shifted a little. Am I wrong, or is there something I'm not seeing?"
If they say money
"I hear you. Is it truly that the money is impossible — or is it more that you're not fully confident yet that this will work for you?"
[If they say confidence:] "What would you need to see to feel confident? Let's talk through that."
[If it truly is money:] "Okay. Is there a version of getting started that would work for you, or do we need to look at timing?"
If they say they need to think about it
"Of course. What specifically do you feel like you need to think through? Let's just talk about it now."
If they say they need to talk to a spouse or partner
"That makes sense. What do you think their main concern would be? Let's make sure you have everything you need to have that conversation."
Then: "Personally — setting them aside for a second — is this something you want to do?"
V2 Change — Objection Handling

Added two new objection paths. V1 covered energy shift, money, and think-about-it. V2 adds the spouse/partner objection and the branch paths inside the money objection (is it truly impossible, or is it confidence?). The money objection branches are critical because the wrong response to each is different. If they don't have the money at all, talking about confidence doesn't help. If it's a confidence issue, talking about payment plans doesn't help either.

Added resolution branches inside "think about it." "Let's just talk about it now" is the move. In V1, the line ended after "What specifically do you feel like you need to think through?" V2 adds "Let's just talk about it now." This is a gentle frame reassertion — the answer isn't to give them time, it's to remove the thing blocking them.

Key Principle

The objection stage should feel like home. If it feels urgent or pressured, the emotional contract wasn't fully signed before pitch. Stay calm, stay curious. A prospect who feels chased will pull back. A prospect who feels understood will move forward.

12

Close

Help them commit to the decision they already said they wanted.
Script
"Based on what you told me, this is exactly the problem you said you wanted to solve. You said [their specific cost of inaction]. You said staying where you are isn't actually an option. And you said getting to [their specific goal] would mean [their specific why]."
"The only thing left is getting started. Let's take care of that now."
V2 Change — Close

Made the callback specific. V1 said "this is the exact problem you said you wanted to solve." V2 says "you said [their specific cost of inaction]" and "you said getting to [their specific goal] would mean [their specific why]." The close should reference the three most emotionally significant things they said on the call. This isn't manipulation — it's reminding them of their own words and their own decision.

Left the core principle from V1 untouched. "They are not just committing to the program. They are committing to the new version of themselves." This is correct and important. That's what the close is about.

Key Principle

After "let's take care of that now" — silence. Do not add another sentence. Do not soften it. Do not explain anything further. They have enough information. Give them space to say yes.
