Pre-Call Profiling

Most reps skim the questionnaire. Elite reps read it like a novel. The emotional signals are there before the call even starts — word choice, emphasis, what they avoided saying. The rep who reads these walks into the first minute with a hypothesis. Everyone else walks in cold.

Why Most Reps Miss Everything in the Questionnaire

The skim trap

Reps skim for facts: age, income, years in sales, offer they're working. These go into a mental box labeled "situation." Then they get on the call and start from zero, asking the same questions that are already answered in the form — which wastes time, bores the prospect, and signals that you didn't bother to prepare.

The questionnaire isn't a fact sheet. It's a window into how someone thinks, what matters to them, what they're afraid of, and what kind of conversation they're ready to have. You just have to know how to read it.

The cold start problem. Walking into a call without having read the questionnaire properly means you're spending the first 5-10 minutes gathering information the prospect has already given you. Every minute of that is a minute you're not building trust. The prospect can sense when a rep hasn't done their homework — and it tells them how much you care.

What to Look For — Beyond the Facts

  • ✓
    Word choice. How did they describe their situation? Clinical and detached ("looking to optimize my performance") vs. raw and honest ("I'm exhausted and I feel like I'm failing"). The language tells you the emotional temperature before you've said hello.
  • ✓
    What they emphasized. If someone writes three sentences about money and one sentence about the actual skill they want to develop, money is the real driver. Emphasis is intention. Where they put energy in the form is where they put energy in their life.
  • ✓
    What they avoided or were vague about. "How long have you been in sales?" — "A few years." Why not the number? Either they don't want to seem inexperienced or they don't want to seem like they've been at it too long without results. The vagueness is a signal. What they hedge around is often where the real tension is.
  • ✓
    The ask at the end. Most questionnaires have a "why are you reaching out now?" or "what do you hope to get from this?" field. Read this carefully. It tells you what they're consciously hoping for — which is often one layer above what they actually need.
  • ✓
    Length and thoroughness of their answers. A prospect who writes paragraphs in every field is Emotional or Social — they want to be known before they're sold. A prospect who writes single sentences is Practical or Action — they want efficiency, not explanation. Match the depth of preparation to what they've shown you they're comfortable with.
  • ✗
    Don't just check the income box. The income number is the least useful piece of data in the form. It tells you where someone is. It tells you almost nothing about where they want to go, why they want to get there, or what's stopped them.

Building Your Opening Hypothesis Before the Call Starts

After reading the questionnaire properly, you should be able to write three sentences: who this person is, what they actually want, and what's most likely in the way.

This is your opening hypothesis. It's not a script — it's a starting orientation. You walk into the call knowing roughly where you're going and what to listen for. You still let the call tell you what's true. But you start ahead of where most reps start.

Example hypothesis from a questionnaire

Prospect wrote: "I've been in high-ticket sales for 2 years. I'm doing okay but I know I'm leaving money on the table. My close rate is around 20% and I want to be at 30%+. I've tried a few things but haven't found anything that really clicked."

Hypothesis: This is a P or A type — clinical language, specific numbers, focused on performance metric. Primary driver is likely significance or freedom. The "haven't found anything that clicked" is a historical identity flag — probably some skepticism toward coaching. Open with specificity and earn trust through clarity before getting into transformation language.

What the pre-call is really about

Pre-call profiling isn't a research exercise. It's a preparation of your mindset and your orientation. When you walk into a call knowing who you're talking to — even roughly — you're already more present, more curious, and more useful. The prospect can feel that. And it changes what's possible in the conversation before you've said anything.

Call Exchange — Skimmed Prep vs. Real Prep

VERSION 1 — Rep skimmed, starts cold
Rep
"Hey Sarah, great to connect. Tell me a little bit about yourself and what you're working on."
Generic opener. Sarah already told you all of this in the form. She knows you didn't read it. The tone of the call is set — this feels like a transactional process, not a conversation.
Prospect
"Sure, so I'm in high-ticket sales, been doing it about two years, I'm doing okay but I know I can do better..."
VERSION 2 — Rep prepared, opens with what they read
Rep
"Hey Sarah — I read through what you wrote. You mentioned you've tried a few things and nothing's really clicked. I'm curious what 'not clicking' feels like from where you're sitting. What's been missing?"
Started from the form. Named something specific. Asked a depth question immediately. Sarah knows this rep did their homework and the conversation is already different.
Prospect
"Honestly, I've done the tactical stuff and I know it. I just can't seem to stay consistent. One week I'm great, next week I'm in my head."
Rep
"That's not a skill gap — that's a state gap. The skill is there. What's inconsistent is the internal environment you bring to calls. That's actually the more interesting problem to solve. Tell me more about what 'in your head' looks like — what are you thinking when it happens?"
Already past the surface level in the first three exchanges because the prep gave the rep a starting point. This is the compounding advantage of real pre-call profiling.
