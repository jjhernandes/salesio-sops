Check-In

Chris opened with: "I feel clear." The North Star exercise from last week worked. He's now looking at things through JJ's lens — understanding what's bringing him up and down, and seeing things differently.

He shared something personal about his arc. At 15–17, he went through mental health challenges and family difficulties. He found Eric Thomas and Tony Robbins, which gave him a new lens — "you can do this, you're good enough" — when nobody in his environment was saying that. The gift: it renewed his mind. The curse: he was in an environment that contradicted everything he was learning, and Tony Robbins' "change your state instantly" framework masked the fact that deeper limiting beliefs weren't being addressed yet.

Over time, he shifted to running on negative fuel — Hormozi-style, "if it's the right thing to do, just do it." Useful fuel. Gets things done.

The Shift

Chris is now consciously choosing to step back into operating from a higher state. He said: "The quality of how you perceive everything is going to affect everything drastically." He's not abandoning push motivation — he's adding pull motivation on top of it and targeting Willingness and above as his new operating floor.

JJ's Framing

Push motivation is real and not wrong. Michael Jordan, Kobe, Hormozi — all ran on anger/desire at their peak. The pull (operating from Love and Reason) develops over time through experience, not by snapping your fingers. Something shifts — a life event, a realization — and then you can't operate the old way anymore. That's a natural evolution, not a failure of the previous approach.

What Brings Him Down

Chris got clear on this since last week. He hadn't had a precise answer before — now he does.

The Trigger

"If I'm going through a day and I feel like I'm overwhelmed, or like I can't achieve everything in the day — that's what draws me down. And then I'll fall into a place of hesitation."

Not money. Not comparison. Overwhelm → hesitation. When the day feels like too much to complete, he stalls. The response is to fall into a when-then fallacy.

When-Then Fallacy

Chris identified this pattern himself this week. He'll set a goal, then lock himself into conditional thinking: "When I do this, then I'll do this... when this happens, then this will happen." It's a delay mechanism dressed as a plan. The North Star exercise broke this — it forces the question: what's the best next step? Not "what needs to be true first."

Anti-Fragility System

JJ introduced this framework — originally from a mentor who worked with Elon Musk, taught inside SpaceX. It used to be in the curriculum, now delivered 1-on-1 because the conceptual guidance makes it land better.

Core purpose: Make you less fragile when things don't go perfectly. Most people set a goal, assume success, and when failure happens, take it personally. This system pre-loads failure into the plan so it stops being threatening.

Step 1
Desired Outcome
Define the specific goal. Not vague — specific. What does the win look like?
→
Step 2
Failure Envisioned
Pre-mortem. What's the most likely version of "not quite there"? Not zero — just short of the outcome.
→
Step 3
Process
Move with all available inputs until you hit a breaking point.
Resources Initiation Speed
→
Step 4
Failure Confirmed
Hit the breaking point. Evaluate what happened. Iterate and go back to Step 1 with new information.
Applied to Chris — Landing a Role

Desired outcome: Land a role within the next month that fits his $160K/year earnings target.

Failure envisioned: Getting stuck in the interview stage, or getting an offer that doesn't align with earnings goals.

Process: Use Sales.io resources (Loom framework, interview prep, job board). Take initiative. Move fast — apply to as many aligned opportunities as possible.

Breaking point / failure confirmed: If after a round of applications he hasn't landed something, assess what broke down and iterate — adjust approach, not identity.

Chris connected this to Charlie Munger's inversion model ("what's everything that could go wrong?"). JJ affirmed the comparison — same core logic, but the Anti-Fragility System makes it actionable with a clear loop.

Goals & Vehicle

  • North Star target: $160,000/year
  • Path: Land a closing role on a high-ticket or strong-commission offer — let the vehicle determine the mechanics
  • Aspirational math Chris ran: 240 opportunities/month × 30% close × $1,200 commission = significant monthly earnings. JJ's note: the math varies wildly by company — don't fixate on it. The vehicle will dictate the math, not the other way around.
JJ's Framing on Vehicle Selection

The vehicle matters more than the math. JJ knows people doing $100K/month with 40 reps and people doing the same with 2. Don't over-engineer the numbers before landing — the interview process will reveal what's real. Chris has sales experience. JJ isn't worried about him landing; the skill is there. Week 3 will cover how to apply, what to look for, and how to evaluate opportunities correctly.

Why He Hasn't Applied Yet

Chris was honest: it's fear-based. He wanted to have the right perspective and supporting framework before going out. JJ's take: that's fine for week 1, but it's also the when-then fallacy in action. "When I have enough preparation, then I'll apply." The Anti-Fragility System exists exactly for this — apply, hit a breaking point, iterate. You don't need to be perfect before initiating.

Chris's Core Variable

"With me, it's always going to come down to belief. You can't sell something you don't believe in." JJ agreed 100%. For Chris, this means the right role isn't just about money or company size — it has to be something he can genuinely stand behind. That belief piece will be the multiplier when everything else is in place.
