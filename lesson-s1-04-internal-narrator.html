The Internal Narrator

There are two conversations on every call. The one you're having with the prospect — and the one you're having with yourself. Only one of them is helpful.

What the Internal Narrator Is

It's the running commentary in your head during a call. Constant, unsolicited, mostly unhelpful. Every rep has it. Most are partially living there while trying to be present on the call. It sounds like this:

Common Narrator Lines
  • "That was a bad answer. Why did I say it like that?"
  • "They're not going to buy. I can already tell."
  • "I should have said X instead of Y back there."
  • "They went quiet. That's a bad sign."
  • "I need to wrap this up — I'm losing them."
  • "Why did I ask that? That was a dumb question."
  • "Okay, here comes the price. Don't mess this up."

While you're inside that commentary, you are not on the call. You're not listening to the prospect. You're not picking up on what they're actually saying or feeling. You're in a separate conversation — with yourself — that is competing for the same cognitive bandwidth as the call.

What It Costs You

Lag. You're half a beat behind because part of your attention is inside the narrator. Prospects feel this — it shows up as slight disconnection, like you're not fully listening. And you aren't.

When someone's not fully listening to us, we feel it. We don't always name it. But we feel it. We become slightly more guarded. We give shorter answers. We stop volunteering information. We start filtering. The prospect doesn't go cold because the offer isn't good enough. They go cold because the rep stopped being present.

The internal narrator is a presence thief. Every second you spend in it is a second you're not with the person who is deciding whether to trust you.

The Self-Fulfilling Loop

The narrator doesn't just cost you presence. It actively creates the outcomes it predicts. "They're not going to buy" isn't a neutral observation — it's a directive that changes your behavior.

1
The Prediction
"They're not going to buy."
The narrator makes a call based on incomplete data — a pause, a short answer, a slightly flat response — and declares the outcome. This happens midway through the call, sometimes in the first five minutes.
2
The Shift in Behavior
You stop being genuinely curious. You start selling.
Once the narrator decides it's lost, your behavior changes. You stop asking questions that might get real answers — because what's the point? You start pitching harder, trying to recover what you've already decided is gone.
Rep (internally): "They're checked out. I need to close this fast." Rep (on call): suddenly speeds up, goes into pitch mode, stops listening.
3
The Prospect Feels It
The energy shift registers immediately.
You went from present and curious to selling and urgent. The prospect feels the shift. Something changed. They don't know what, but the call just got more pressured. They pull back further.
4
The Confirmed Loss
"I told you so."
They don't buy. The narrator logs it as a correct prediction. It wasn't. It was a self-created outcome. The narrator predicted it and then manufactured the conditions for it to come true. You didn't lose the deal because they weren't going to buy. You lost it because you believed they weren't going to buy.

When It's Loudest

Silence on the Call

The narrator hates silence. The moment a prospect goes quiet — even for 3 seconds to think — the narrator starts filling the void: "They're not interested." "I said something wrong." "Should I say something?" The silence is almost always the prospect processing. The narrator is almost always wrong about what it means.

Inner monologue: "They went quiet. Did I lose them? Should I keep talking? Say something. Say something."

An Unexpected Question

When a prospect asks something you didn't prep for, the narrator spikes. This is especially common in reps with a humiliation wound. The question triggers a fear response, and suddenly half the brain is managing anxiety about the answer while the other half is trying to formulate one.

Inner monologue: "Oh no, I don't know that. What do I say? Don't look stupid. Say something confident. Are they judging me right now?"

When an Objection Comes Up

Objections activate the narrator in reps who take them personally. "It's too expensive" lands as a verdict, not a question. The narrator interprets it as rejection before the rep has even responded.

Inner monologue: "Here we go. They're going to say no. I knew it. How do I save this? Just stay calm. Don't panic. You're panicking."

Right Before the Price Reveal

The peak moment for most reps. The narrator builds anticipation of rejection before the number is even out. Some reps flinch slightly before saying the price — voice dropping, pace changing — because the narrator has already catastrophized the reaction.

Inner monologue: "Okay. Here it comes. Please don't react badly. Please don't make me feel like the number is wrong. Just say it. Say it normally. Don't flinch."

After What Felt Like a Misstep

Said something awkward? The narrator won't let it go. It loops the moment on repeat while you're supposed to be listening to the next thing they say. Classic presence killer — you're half-a-minute behind because the narrator is still auditing the last 30 seconds.

Inner monologue: "Why did I say that. That was weird. Do they think I'm weird now? Just move forward. Why can't I just move forward?"

What Quiets It

Genuine curiosity about the prospect. Not performed interest — actual interest in the specific person in front of you. When you're genuinely trying to understand someone, your attention goes to them. The narrator goes quiet not because you forced it out, but because something more interesting showed up — the actual human on the other side of the call.

This is why prep matters. When you know something real about the prospect before the call, you walk in with something genuine to be curious about. That head start pushes the narrator to the margins before the call even begins.

The narrator doesn't have room when you're fully present. It's not a willpower problem. It's an attention problem. Direct your attention to them — genuinely, completely — and the narrator loses its grip.

You'll also notice: reps who love selling don't have loud narrators. They're too interested in the conversation to narrate it. The narrator is loudest in reps who are performing. Because when you're performing, you're watching yourself perform. When you're genuinely engaged, you're not watching yourself at all.

The Drill

Presence Counter — One Drill Per Call

On your next call: every time you catch the narrator running, put one finger down. At the end of the call, count how many times you caught it. Don't judge it. Just count it.

Most reps doing this for the first time: 15–25 catches per call. Elite reps who've been working on this: 2–5. The gap between those numbers is your growth edge as a rep right now. Not your script. Not your objection handling. Your ability to stay present for the full length of a call.

The goal isn't zero. The goal is to notice faster. Because the moment you catch the narrator, you're back. You're present again. The catch is the skill. The faster you catch it, the less damage it does. Train the catch. The silence will take care of itself.

Two Versions of the Same Moment

The moment: prospect hesitates after you ask a qualifying question. Three seconds of silence.

Version A — Rep inside their head: narrator running hot
Rep
"What would it mean for you to be making $20k months consistently?"
Prospect
"..."
Narrator (rep's head): "They stopped talking. That's bad. Did I ask that wrong? Should I rephrase? They're losing interest. Say something. Fill it. Fill the silence."
Rep
"I mean — like, whether that's freedom, or just proving something to yourself, or being able to provide for your family — whatever it means to you personally, you know?"
Narrator won. Filled the silence before the prospect could think. Just talked the prospect out of their own answer. The most powerful data on the call — what they were about to say — is now gone.
Version B — Rep fully present: narrator quiet, attention on the prospect
Rep
"What would it mean for you to be making $20k months consistently?"
Prospect
"..."
Rep notices the silence. Stays present. Watches the prospect's face. Genuinely curious about what's going through their head. Three seconds. Four. Five. Comfortable.
Prospect
"Honestly... it would mean I could stop stressing about money every single month. My wife and I fight about it more than we should. I just want that weight off."
That answer is the close. The real reason. The thing they've never said out loud on a sales call before. It only came because the rep let the silence live. The narrator would have killed it.

Three seconds of silence. That's the difference between a surface answer and the real one. The narrator makes you fill it. Presence lets it breathe. Train the patience. The prospect will always fill the silence — and what they fill it with is the most useful thing they'll say on the call.
