Framework Review — What Was There and What Changes

Ryan's Previous Framework (JP Agin — Enterprise Closing Framework)

Ryan came in with a framework from his last mentor built on JP Agin's structure. Sections: Intro/Rapport → Problem Stage → Rational Question → Pre-handle Objection → Ideal Criteria → Desired State → Consequence → Presentation. JJ reviewed it live and identified the structural issues below.

What's Getting Replaced
Problems with the Old Framework
  • Rapport section: "Can you hear me okay? How's it going?" — surface, forgettable, everyone says it
  • "Potentially getting help" — generic opener, zero differentiation
  • Weak discovery questions: "What do you do for work? Do you enjoy it?" — if they say yes, you're stuck
  • No awareness-stage thinking — framework treats all buyers the same
  • "Rational question" emphasis — JJ couldn't see the purpose; felt like a tactic without a why
  • Doesn't build emotional leverage before pitching
What Replaces It
JJ's Direction for Ryan
  • Trust is built through deep questions — not the first 30 seconds
  • Open by referencing their application: show you actually read it
  • Ask for context, not scripted questions: "Give me context on what's going on"
  • Two-track framework: one for problem-aware buyers, one for solution-aware
  • Core structure: Current State → Gap → Future State. That's it.
  • Elicit emotion through intentional questions — not by asking how things make them feel

The simplest version of the framework: Current state → problem awareness → solution awareness → future state → pitch. JJ called out that building the gap between current state and future state is the whole job. Everything else is just stages of that process.

The Five Awareness Stages — Ryan's Buyers

JJ walked Ryan through the five stages every prospect moves through. Ryan's buyers typically come in at Stage 2 or Stage 3 — a mix of problem-aware and solution-aware. The framework JJ is building will have two tracks to handle both.

The Two-Track Plan JJ Is Building

Track 1 is for buyers at Stage 2 — problem-aware, little solution awareness. These buyers need to feel the pain more deeply before they can see the gap. Track 2 is for Stage 3 — solution-aware buyers who need differentiation before anything else. JJ promised one or two framework variations, with language cues so Ryan can identify which track he's on early in the call without boxing the prospect in.

How to Open the Call

The Principle

Rapport is not built in the first 30 seconds. It's built through the quality of your questions. Don't open trying to be likable — open showing you did the work. Reference what they put in the application. That one move signals: I paid attention, you matter, this isn't a script.

"I took a look at your application — seemed like you needed help with X. I have some notes, but I don't want anything to get lost, so give me some context on what's going on."

For B2C Buyers New to Sales Calls

You can break the fourth wall slightly — frame it so they know the call is different from what they expect. JJ's version: "If you've been on a call like this before, you already know how it goes. I'm going to take it a lot slower — really understand you and where you're at before anything else. And depending on what I see, I'll break down how we can help and what it would look like. No rush to get there."

"Potentially getting help" — cut it. Every rep on every offer says it. It's filler. Reference the application specifically, or reference what they said they were struggling with. Make the first sentence prove you were paying attention.

Ryan's Self-Identified Weakness

Discovery depth. Ryan knows his discovery is the leak. He doesn't sit in the pocket long enough — he pitches before he's built enough leverage. When objections hit late, there's nothing to go back to because the discovery didn't create real emotional stakes. He's not pitching too late — he's consistently pitching too early before the prospect actually feels the gap.

The Chain Reaction

Weak discovery → not enough pain surface → pitches early → objections hit with no emotional foundation → objection handling feels like arguing → no close. The fix isn't objection handling — the fix is going deeper before the pitch. Everything downstream improves when the discovery creates real leverage.

What JJ Flagged Directly

Questions like "do you enjoy your job?" create a trap — if the prospect says yes, there's nowhere to go. The questions have to pull out doubt and dissatisfaction in a way that doesn't let the prospect deflect with a positive answer. Get specific. Get to the number. Get to the timeline. Get to what they've tried and why it failed.

JJ's Coaching Angle This Session

Ryan knows his craft is there. The framework he came in with had real bones — problem stage, current state, desired state. The issue isn't the structure. It's that the questions inside that structure are too weak to build real emotion, and the offer context (BizOp, ages 17-60, dissatisfaction-driven) requires a different kind of depth than what JP Agin's framework was designed for.

JJ's push this session was to stop using a framework that wasn't built for Ryan's buyer type and start building one from scratch around his specific ICP. The new framework will be lighter on structure and heavier on awareness-reading — so Ryan knows when to push deeper and when to move on based on what the prospect is actually saying, not where he is in the sequence.
