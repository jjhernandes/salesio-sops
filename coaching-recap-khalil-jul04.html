JJ's approach: treat Jess and Micah through the same evaluation process. Two types of closers — skill-based (technically trained) and conviction-based (deep belief in the offer). Ideally you want both. A skilled closer and a conviction closer feeding off each other is the goal.

Interview & Roleplay Framework
  • Round 1 — Energy pulse: do they connect as humans? If it feels stale, don't continue
  • Round 2 — Go deeper on anything unclear, check for culture/personality fit, is this someone you want to nurture or someone ready now?
  • Round 3 — Roleplay: intro + 5-min discovery. Have Micah play the prospect. After: debrief both on a 1-10 scale. Ask why that number. Listen for self-awareness vs. surface-level critique
What to Look For
  • Green flag: leads with respect, not likability — earns authority in the first 3 minutes without trying to befriend the prospect
  • Green flag: self-awareness. They know what they missed (didn't find the pain, surface-level discovery) vs. blaming mechanics (tonality, stuttering)
  • Green flag: composure — you can't tell they're nervous, even if they are
  • Red flag: "it was my tonality" — surface-level self-critique means they don't understand the real problem
  • Red flag: losing control of the frame in the first 2-3 minutes — if they allow interruptions or bad behavior early, it will continue throughout
Key Point
Reps will never sell exactly like Khalil — he's the founder, that's not replicable. The goal is not to clone him. It's to give them enough ammunition to close with confidence in their own style, inside the same call environment: calm, trusting, vulnerable. Never tense. Never scripted feeling.

Why Humans Buy — 5 Core Reasons
  • To escape pain
  • To reach a future version of themselves
  • To protect or reinforce who they believe they are
  • Because they trust the person in front of them
  • Because they can't unsee the gap
Teaching Point — Cognitive Dissonance
The gap between who they say they are and what the evidence proves creates real anxiety — not manufactured emotion. When you pull that out through your questions, you become both the authority (you saw what they couldn't) and the savior (you have the solution). That's why they hand over money. The quality of your questions dictates the quality of their responses. Any question you ask tells the prospect more about you than anything you can say directly.
Subjective Language — Always Follow Up

When a prospect says "it's been tough," "things have been rough," or any vague emotional language — always ask what that means to them. "Tough" to one person is losing $1.7M in revenue. To another it's barely getting off the couch. You can't close on a word. You close on what's underneath it.
