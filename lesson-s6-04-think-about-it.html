"I Need to Think About It" — The Specificity Challenge

"I need to think about it" is the most common stall in sales — and the most mishandled. Most reps either concede immediately or push back too hard. Neither works. Specificity is the move.

What "I Need to Think About It" Actually Means

The Translation

"I need to think about it" almost never means "I need more time." It means one of a few very specific things — and the rep's job is to find out which one. Vague thinking-about-it is usually avoidance. Specific thinking-about-it is a real concern you can address.

The prospect isn't telling you they're unsure about your pitch. They're telling you there's something unresolved in their head that they haven't shared with you yet. Your job is to get to what that thing is.

The two wrong responses: Conceding ("Of course, take your time — want to set up a follow-up call?") signals you're fine losing the deal slowly. Pushing ("But what's actually stopping you from deciding today?") signals you need the sale. Both are wrong. Both kill the deal in different ways.

"Vague thinking about it means they're avoiding. Specific thinking about it means there's something real you can actually address."

— JJ Hernandez

The Specificity Challenge

When a prospect says "I need to think about it," the only correct response is to slow down and get specific.

The move: "That makes sense — what specifically are you weighing?"

That's it. Short. Calm. No pressure. You're not fighting the objection. You're asking them to make it concrete. Because once it's concrete, it's addressable. And if they can't make it concrete, that tells you something too.

Why Specificity Works

Vague objections are protective. "I need to think about it" gives the prospect distance. It's an exit door that doesn't feel like an exit door — it sounds reasonable, it sounds mature, and it leaves both parties feeling like nothing went wrong.

When you ask for specificity, you collapse the vague objection. You force the prospect to name the actual thing. And the actual thing is almost always addressable — or reveals that the prospect was never a real buyer, in which case you need to know that now.

The 3 Things They're Usually Thinking About

When a prospect says they need to think about it, there are almost always only three real things underneath. Learn to identify which one fast.

01
The Real Concern
The Money
The investment is a real stretch and they don't know how to say it directly. This gets surfaced when you ask for specificity and they say something about the price, payment plans, or timing with their finances. Addressable — see Lesson 5 and 11 for the money objection framework.
How it sounds after the specificity challenge: "Honestly... it's really the money. I'm not sure how I make it work right now." That's a specific, solvable conversation.
02
The Real Concern
The Risk
They're worried it won't work for them specifically. Not "will this program work" but "will I get results." This is a certainty gap. They need to believe this is actually going to move the needle for their specific situation before they can commit. Addressable — use proof, specifics, and the guarantee.
How it sounds after the specificity challenge: "I guess I'm just not 100% sure this would translate to my specific situation." Now you have something to work with.
03
The Real Concern
Someone Else
There's a spouse, a business partner, or another person in their life whose approval or input they feel they need before deciding. "Think about it" is code for "I need to talk to someone." This is its own objection — see Lesson 6 — but the specificity challenge surfaces it instead of letting it stay hidden until they ghost.
How it sounds after the specificity challenge: "Honestly I'd want to run it by my wife first." Now you can address whether this needs to be a joint conversation — or whether it's a stall.

When They Can't Specify — What That Tells You

The Signal

Sometimes you ask "what specifically are you weighing?" and they can't answer. They fumble. They restate "just... everything." They go vague. This is information. It means one of two things: either the prospect is politely declining and doesn't want to say no directly, or they haven't processed enough to know what they're unsure about.

The Follow-Up

If they can't get specific, try: "I want to help you think this through — when you imagine yourself not moving forward on this today and coming back to this conversation in a month, what would need to be different?" This forces them to project forward and reveals whether there's a real open question or whether the stall is the final answer.

Call Exchange — Right vs. Wrong Response

VERSION 1 — Rep Who Concedes Immediately
Prospect
"I think I need to think about this a little more before making a decision."
Rep
"Of course, totally understand. Do you want to set up a follow-up call for later this week? I can also send you some more information if that helps."
Immediate concession. Zero curiosity. Rep doesn't know what "think about it" means — and chose not to find out. This call will not close. The follow-up will not happen.
VERSION 2 — Rep Who Uses the Specificity Challenge
Prospect
"I think I need to think about this a little more before making a decision."
Rep
"That makes sense. What specifically are you weighing?"
Calm. No pressure. Validates the statement, then asks for specificity. Doesn't fight the stall — makes it concrete.
Prospect
"I guess... honestly it's the money. I know I have it but it's still a lot to commit to at once."
Rep
"That's a real thing and I want to address it. Can I ask — is it that the money doesn't exist, or is it more that committing it all at once feels like a lot?"
Now in the real conversation. The stall has been replaced with a specific concern. The rep is in position to actually resolve it.
Prospect
"More the second one. Like the money is there — I just want to make sure I'm making the right call."
Rep
"So what you need is certainty, not time. Let's figure out what would make this feel certain. What would you need to be true about this for it to be a clear yes?"
Reframes the objection accurately. "Think about it" was actually a certainty gap. Now the conversation is about closing that gap — not scheduling a follow-up that won't happen.
