Red Flags vs. Green Flags

Vetting a company before you apply is not optional. It is the difference between a role that builds your close rate and one that destroys it.

Why Vetting Matters

The Real Risk

The danger is not just wasted time. A bad offer damages your close rate and your confidence simultaneously. You can be technically sound, executing everything correctly, and still close at 6% because the pipeline is cold, the offer is weak, or the product has a reputation problem in the market.

Most reps who quit sales do not quit because they lack skill. They quit after six months on a bad offer with a 7% close rate, believing the problem is them. Your due diligence before you take a role protects your career and your mindset.

Every signal you can gather before the discovery call is leverage. You walk into that conversation already knowing what you are looking for. That confidence reads. The rep who researched walks differently than the rep who just showed up.

Red Flags — Walk Away or Probe Hard

  • ✕
    "Unlimited earning potential" with no specific numbers attached. This phrase is the equivalent of "we'll figure it out." Push for actual commission tiers, actual deal sizes, actual rep earnings. If they cannot give you specifics, that tells you something.
  • ✕
    No documented commission structure. Verbal agreements in sales roles disappear the moment a dispute arises. If there is nothing in writing before you start, protect yourself.
  • ✕
    High rep turnover with no explanation. If multiple reps have come and gone in the last 90 days, ask why. "We just haven't found the right fit yet" is not an answer. High turnover usually means pipeline problems, offer problems, or leadership problems.
  • ✕
    No existing close rate data. If a company cannot tell you what their current close rate is, it means either they are not tracking it (red flag for business maturity) or the number is embarrassing (bigger red flag).
  • ✕
    Hostile Glassdoor or public reviews about rep treatment. One bad review from a disgruntled employee is noise. A pattern of reviews citing late payments, misrepresentation, or hostile management is signal.
  • ✕
    Leadership who cannot answer basic business questions. If the operator does not know their monthly revenue, their customer acquisition cost, or what their best reps are making, that company is not ready to support a high-performing closer.

Green Flags — Signs of a Solid Offer

  • ✓
    Transparent, specific commission tiers. The offer comes with documented commission terms. They know the percentages, when payment hits, and what happens with refunds or chargebacks.
  • ✓
    Documented close rate they are proud to share. A company confident in their offer quality will share close rate data without hesitation. When they lead with it, that is a green flag.
  • ✓
    Reps who have been there 6+ months. Rep tenure is one of the most honest indicators of offer quality. Reps stay when they are earning and when they are treated fairly. Longevity is trust.
  • ✓
    Leadership who knows their numbers cold. When the operator or sales manager can immediately cite average deal size, monthly revenue, close rate, and lead volume, you are talking to someone running a real business.
  • ✓
    Genuine testimonials and a product with real reviews. Product quality affects close rate. A product that customers love creates referrals and reduces objections on the call. Look for evidence the product delivers.

Side by Side — Before You Apply

Category Red Flag Green Flag
Earning potential "Unlimited earning potential" — no specifics Specific tiers: "10% on $5K deals; top reps hit $15K-$20K/mo"
Commission structure Verbal agreement, nothing written, "we'll handle it" Written terms before day one — percentages, timing, chargeback policy
Rep tenure 3 reps in 4 months, high turnover, vague explanations Same reps for 6-12 months; can name them and what they earn
Close rate Can't answer, deflects, or gives a suspiciously high number Specific and reasonable — 12-20% on warm inbound is credible
Leadership Can't answer basic business questions; evasive Knows their numbers cold; responds within 24 hours

How to Find These Signals Before the Discovery Call

Before You Even Reach Out

Run the company through the 10-minute research protocol in lesson 5. LinkedIn for leadership and rep tenure. Glassdoor for culture patterns. Google for product reviews and news. You should know whether a company passes or fails the basic checks before you record your Loom.

During the Discovery Call

Use the questions in lesson 6 to surface these signals in conversation. A confident operator with a solid offer will answer everything without hesitation. Watch for evasion, vagueness, or defensiveness. Those responses are data.

Red flags do not mean walk away immediately. They mean probe deeper. Sometimes what looks like a red flag has a reasonable explanation. But if you probe and still cannot get a straight answer, that is your answer.
