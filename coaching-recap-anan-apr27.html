What Pulls You Below Courage

Two clear patterns came up. Knowing these is the first step to not being controlled by them.

Pattern 1 — Things Outside Your Control
Your dad's retirement next year means the financial support he's been providing — the mortgage, the school fees, the Saudi Arabia costs — will need to be covered by the rest of the family. When you think about your siblings' capacity, your mom, and what the next year might look like, it pulls you into worry and anger. Not because you're weak — because you care and feel responsible.

The key: This doesn't happen for another year. And your siblings' decisions, your brother's capabilities, your father's timeline — none of that is in your sphere of control. The moment you try to carry what isn't yours, you drop below Courage. The moment you return to what you can actually do — you come back up.
Pattern 2 — Decisions Made Without You
When Lana accepts an event without asking you first. When your brother tells you what should be done. When someone makes a move you didn't know was coming. It reads as dismissive or disrespectful — but underneath it, you're the emotional regulator. You need to know what's coming so you can prepare to hold the space for everyone around you.

JJ called it what it actually is: Pride. Not anger. Pride — "my way or the highway." There's nothing wrong with it, but knowing the real name of it changes how you respond to it. It's not about control. It's about feeling unprepared to do your job of keeping things stable.
"Most people operate out of fear and pride. The successful ones get to pride. The very successful ones move past it — to acceptance, reason, and love. Pride gets you to 20–30K. Love gets you to 6 figures, consistently."
The Pride Trap vs. Operating from Love

Pride feels like strength. It drives results. It gets you moving. But it also means you can't let anyone be better than you. You can't let things go. You carry a quiet fear of being inferior, and that fear — even when you can't name it — leaks into every call, every relationship, every decision.

Operating from Pride
"I have to prove I'm better."
Need to control outcomes. Can't admit uncertainty. Holds on to employees out of fear of competition. Reacts when someone gets a step ahead. Makes decisions from a defensive position.
Operating from Love
"There's enough for everyone."
Can let people go graciously. Doesn't see competition. Makes decisions from abundance. Doesn't need to be the best in the room — just needs to do good work. Can't be threatened.

The goal isn't to kill the drive Pride gives you. It's to get above it — where the drive exists without the fear underneath it. That's where the ceiling breaks.
