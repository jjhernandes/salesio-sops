The Full Call Architecture

Timing, flow, and talk ratios. When you know how time should be distributed on a well-run call, you can diagnose exactly where your calls are going wrong.

The Time Breakdown

A full call runs 45-75 minutes. Here's how time is distributed across the three phases on a well-run call.

Why Discovery Takes the Most Time

The discovery phase is where the entire call is built. Everything from the pitch forward depends on what happens in the first 25-35 minutes. Reps who feel like their calls "go long" are almost always spending time in the wrong places — either rushing discovery or over-pitching in the conversion phase.

Why the Close Is Fast

A well-run close takes 3-5 minutes. When discovery was thorough and the pitch was targeted, the close is a formality. If your close is taking 20+ minutes, it means something earlier in the call was incomplete — the close is compensating for missing groundwork.

Talk Ratios — What You Should Be Tracking

The overall talk ratio on a high-performing call is roughly 30% rep / 70% prospect. That number inverts during the pitch phase (Steps 8-9) — but the overall average should stay close to 30/70.

If you pull a transcript and you're talking more than 40% of the time — your discovery is shallow and your pitch is long. Both are symptoms of the same problem: you don't trust the information you have, so you keep presenting instead of asking.

PhaseTarget Talk Ratio (Rep)What It Signals
Discovery (Steps 1–6) 20–30% You're asking, listening, and going deeper — not explaining or pitching
Transition + Pitch (Steps 7–8) 50–65% Rep leads here — but pitch stays targeted and under 10 minutes
Confirm + Price + Objections (Steps 9–11) 30–40% Asking confirm fit, stating price, asking about objections — then listening
Close (Step 12) 20–30% The close is brief and direct — if it's long, go back to objections

What It Means When the Architecture Is Off

Too much time in pitch, not enough in discovery: You're presenting to assumptions. Pitch feels generic. Prospect isn't nodding at the right moments. Close rate drops and objections get harder. The fix: extend discovery by 10 minutes and cut pitch by 5.

Call runs over 90 minutes: Something wasn't resolved cleanly. Either an objection was handled but not closed, or the prospect is in genuine indecision that the call can't solve. At 75+ minutes, call the time: "I want to respect your schedule — let's get clear on where you land."

Call Under 30 Minutes

A call that ends in under 30 minutes almost never closes at a high rate. That's not enough time to complete discovery thoroughly. If your calls are consistently short, you're skipping steps — usually Problem Awareness, Consequence, or both. You're pitching to a surface understanding of the prospect's situation.

Call Exchange — Diagnosing an Off-Architecture Call

CALL REVIEW — Identifying the Flow Violation
Rep
"Walk me through where you are now." [Prospect describes situation — 3 minutes] "Got it. Okay so let me tell you what we do..."
Jumped to pitch after 3 minutes of discovery. No problem awareness. No solution criteria. No future pace. No consequence. The pitch has almost nothing to connect features to. This call runs 35 minutes and ends in "let me think about it."
Rep
After self-review: "I went to pitch at minute 5. I had their income and that they want more. I didn't know what specifically wasn't working, what they needed to change, what better looked like to them, or what another year of this would cost. My pitch was generic because I had generic inputs."
The diagnosis is correct. The fix: commit to 20+ minutes of discovery on the next call before even thinking about the transition. The architecture holds you accountable — if you're pitching at minute 5, you're not running the process.
