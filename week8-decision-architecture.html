How Buying Decisions Actually Happen
30 min · Kahneman · System 1/2 · Core Concept
Lesson Objective
Understand the two systems the brain uses to make decisions — and why almost everything you were taught about sales is aimed at the wrong one. By the end of this lesson, you will never approach a call the same way again.
The Core Model

Daniel Kahneman — a Nobel Prize-winning psychologist — spent his career studying how humans actually make decisions. His most important finding, and the one that changes everything for us in sales, is this: the brain runs two completely different decision-making systems simultaneously. They're called System 1 and System 2.

System 1 — The Decider
Fast. Automatic. Emotional. Pattern-matching. Runs 95% of the time without your awareness. It reads faces, detects threats, makes snap judgments, and decides who to trust within seconds. It makes the buying decision. It cannot be reasoned with because it doesn't use reason — it uses feeling and pattern recognition.
System 2 — The Storyteller
Slow. Deliberate. Analytical. This is the part that can do math, weigh pros and cons, and think about long-term consequences. It feels like the decision-maker. It isn't. It comes in after the decision is made and writes the story about why the decision was rational. The "reasons" a prospect gives you for an objection are almost never the actual reason.
Why This Changes Everything

Think about the last call you lost. You probably had a good pitch. You answered their questions. Maybe you handled their objections. And they still said no, or stalled. Here's why: you won the System 2 argument and lost the System 1 decision.

System 1 had already decided before you said most of what you said. It decided based on how you opened, how calm you were, whether it felt like you were genuinely curious about them or performing curiosity at them, and whether it felt safe to trust you. All of that happened in the first few minutes. Everything after was System 2 constructing reasons to support or contradict what System 1 already felt.

"The prospect who says 'I need to think about it' isn't being logical. Their System 1 didn't feel enough certainty, connection, or desire — so System 2 built a wall."
The Sequence Every Closing Call Actually Runs
01
Prospect joins the call. System 1 immediately begins profiling you — safe or threat? Trustworthy or pitching? Confident or needy? This decision is made in seconds, before you've said anything important.
02
Discovery happens. System 1 is tracking emotional resonance — does this person actually understand my situation? Am I feeling something real, or is this a scripted call? Do I like being in this conversation?
03
The pitch lands. System 1 compares the emotional state of the pitch to its existing trust level and desire level. If both are high, the decision is made. If either is missing, it creates resistance that System 2 will then explain with logic.
04
Objections appear. These are System 2 rationalizations of what System 1 already decided. Fighting the objection with more logic is fighting a story with the wrong weapon. The answer is to go back to what System 1 needs: genuine connection, felt understanding, or activated desire.
05
The close. If System 1 is a yes, the close is almost irrelevant — they're looking for confirmation. If System 1 is a no, no close technique in the world will hold. The sale was won or lost in steps 1-3.
What Earns a System 1 Yes

System 1 is not convinced by information. It's not moved by features. It does not care about your program's curriculum. It responds to three things:

1
Genuine understanding. Not "I hear you" — actually demonstrating that you understand their specific situation better than they do. When you reflect their life back to them with more clarity than they had when they were living it, System 1 says: this person is different.
2
Emotional resonance. The prospect needs to feel something — hope, recognition, urgency, excitement, discomfort about their current reality. Neutral calls don't close. Calls that feel like something do.
3
Trust in you, specifically. Not in the program, not in testimonials — in you, the person on this call. System 1 is deciding: is this someone I would follow? Does this person have my actual best interest in mind, or are they performing that? The answer comes from your state, your calm, your detachment from outcome — not from your words.
The Most Common System 1 Mistakes
01
Treating the call like a presentation. Presenting information to System 2 while System 1 watches and decides whether any of it matters. The closer who creates a feeling outperforms the closer who delivers a flawless pitch every time.
02
Rushing through rapport to get to "the real call." Rapport IS the real call. The first 90 seconds are when System 1 decides whether to trust you. Everything after is downstream of that decision.
03
Fighting objections with logic. System 2 built that objection as a story for a System 1 decision. Adding more logic to a logical story doesn't dissolve it — going back to what System 1 actually needs does.
04
Operating from the wrong state. System 1 reads emotional states before it reads words. A closer who needs the sale radiates anxiety. Anxiety is read by the prospect's System 1 as a threat signal. It doesn't matter what you say after that.

The Halo Effect & Your First 90 Seconds
25 min · Asch · Cialdini · Call Opening
Lesson Objective
Understand why the first 90 seconds of a call determine the filter through which everything that follows is processed — and redesign your opening to earn a System 1 yes before discovery even begins.
The Halo Effect

Solomon Asch's 1946 research showed that a single initial positive trait colors the perception of all subsequent information. People who were described as "intelligent, industrious, impulsive, critical, stubborn, and envious" were rated very differently than those described with the exact same traits — just in reverse order, starting with "envious." The first impression creates a halo. Everything that comes after is seen through it.

📈
Research Finding
Studies on courtroom sentencing show that judges make initial judgments within 3 seconds of seeing a defendant that significantly bias later decisions — even after reviewing identical evidence. Medical studies show that doctors diagnose more accurately when they report liking the patient. The halo effect is not a sales concept. It is a fundamental feature of how human judgment works.

On a sales call, the halo is set in the first 90 seconds. If the prospect's System 1 decides you are trustworthy, calm, and genuinely interested in them — they will filter every subsequent piece of information through that lens. Ambiguous things will be interpreted favorably. Objections will feel smaller. The price will land easier.

If the halo is negative — if they sense performance, anxiety, or agenda — the opposite is true. Every claim you make will feel slightly suspect. Every objection will feel valid. The price will feel too high even if it isn't.

What Creates a Positive Halo Instantly
1
Calm authority, not excited energy. The instinct is to be high-energy and enthusiastic. Resist it. Calm signals that you do this all the time, that you don't need this call to go well, that you're in control. Excitement signals need. Need is a threat signal to System 1.
2
Genuine, specific curiosity. Not "so tell me about yourself" — something that shows you actually read about them before the call. "I saw you mentioned you'd been in roofing for 12 years — what got you looking at remote sales?" Specificity signals investment. Investment signals that you're not going through a routine.
3
A moment of real human connection before the agenda. One genuine exchange — not a performance of warmth. The difference is detectable by System 1. If you're going through a "rapport-building checklist," they feel it. If you're actually momentarily interested in something they said, they feel that too.
4
Framing the call on your terms. The "audit not a sales call" frame works because it positions you as the person running the interaction — not reacting to it. Whoever establishes the frame first is seen as the authority. Authority is a powerful positive halo signal.

Halo Killers — What Destroys the Frame in the First 60 Seconds
01
Opening with "How are you doing today?" It's a script signal. System 1 recognizes it as a sales opening and raises its guard. Skip it or replace it with something real.
02
Over-explaining the program before discovery. "Let me tell you about what we do" before you know anything about them signals that you're there to pitch, not to help. System 1 reads this as agenda over interest.
03
Sounding excited about the call. Genuine enthusiasm for the person is fine. Excitement about your opportunity to pitch them activates their defenses. Stay calm, curious, and in control.

Anchoring — The First Number Owns the Frame
30 min · Kahneman · Cialdini · Contrast Principle · Price Psychology
Lesson Objective
Understand how anchoring biases every price reaction — and learn three specific anchor types you can deploy before your price reveal to make any investment feel accessible, not expensive.
What Anchoring Is

In one of Kahneman's most famous experiments, participants were asked to spin a wheel that was rigged to land on either 10 or 65. They were then asked to estimate the percentage of African nations in the United Nations. The people who spun 65 guessed an average of 45%. The people who spun 10 guessed an average of 25%. A completely irrelevant number — from a rigged wheel — shifted their estimate by 80%.

This is anchoring. The brain uses the first number it encounters as a reference point and evaluates every subsequent number relative to it. This process is automatic, unconscious, and nearly impossible to override even when you know it's happening.

What This Means for Your Price Reveal
If the first number in the prospect's mind when they hear your price is $1,000 (their internal expectation), your $7,500 feels like a lot. If the first number is $25,000 (what they'd pay for a comparable outcome elsewhere), your $7,500 feels like a strategic decision. Same price. Completely different emotional response. The only variable is what number they anchored on first.
The Three Anchor Types — With Scripts
Anchor Type 1: The Cost of Inaction Anchor
Anchor the price against what staying the same is already costing them. This is the most powerful anchor because it reframes the investment as the cheaper option.

Script: "Let me ask you something before we go into the numbers. You've been in this for 3 years at roughly what you're making now. If nothing changes, what does the next 3 years cost you — not just in money, but in time, in what you're not building, in what the gap between where you are and where you want to be actually represents? I want you to have a real number in your head before I tell you what this costs."
Anchor Type 2: The Market Comparison Anchor
Anchor against what comparable outcomes cost in the market — whether that's competitors, coaches, universities, or the cost of trial and error on their own.

Script: "For context — people at this level who go the coaching route typically spend $15,000 to $40,000 for the kind of access and curriculum we're talking about. The programs that get you this level of personalization — real coaches actively closing, not consultants who used to — that's where the market is. I want you to have that context."
Anchor Type 3: The Premium Tier Anchor
If you have a higher tier or a more premium version, present it first. The Contrast Principle (Cialdini) shows that following a high price with a lower one makes the lower feel accessible, not expensive.

Script: "There are two ways people work with us. Some go the full 1-on-1 route — that's $14,000 for individualized access. Most people, especially starting out, come through the mastermind — that's what we're talking about today, at $7,500. It gives you everything except the dedicated 1-on-1 coaching time."
When and How to Deploy Anchors

Anchors work best when they're set naturally, not as a setup. The prospect should not feel like you're "doing a thing" before the price. Deploy anchors as genuine context — because they are. The cost of inaction IS real. The market rates ARE that high. If you believe what you're saying, it sounds like honesty, not technique.

Anchoring Mistakes That Kill the Price Reveal
01
Revealing price with no prior anchor. The prospect fills the anchor void with their own — usually whatever they spent last time on something similar, or whatever number feels "expensive" to them generically. You have no control over that number. Set the anchor first.
02
Making anchors feel like a setup. "Most people pay $50,000 for this kind of result... but for you, today only, it's just $7,500!" That's not an anchor, it's a used car lot. Deliver anchors as honest context, not as theater.
03
Apologizing around the price after setting anchors. "I know it's a big investment, but..." instantly cancels the work your anchors just did. State the price. Stop talking. The silence is doing work — let it.

Attention Engineering — What You Make Vivid Gets Bought
25 min · Cialdini · Pre-Suasion · Discovery Architecture
Lesson Objective
Learn how attention determines perceived importance — and redesign your discovery phase to deliberately engineer which emotional realities are most vivid when the pitch starts.
The Pre-Suasion Principle

Cialdini's research on Pre-Suasion showed that whatever a person is paying attention to immediately before a decision determines the decision. In one study, participants who were first asked about their feelings of love and affection reported higher satisfaction with their cars than those who were first asked about practical transportation needs. The car didn't change. What changed was which filter they were looking through when they evaluated it.

There's a second layer: the brain cannot simultaneously attend to something and dismiss it. Attention is endorsement. If you are holding someone's attention on their pain for 15 minutes, that pain feels significant and real by the time you present a solution. If you spend 3 minutes on pain and jump to features, neither the pain nor the solution registers with the emotional weight that drives decisions.

"A prospect should feel their problem more acutely at minute 20 of discovery than they did at minute 1. If that isn't happening, you are not discovering — you are interviewing."

Notice: the pitch happens after desire and urgency are at their peak — not before. Most closers pitch into a neutral or mildly interested emotional state. Elite closers pitch into desire they engineered through the attention they directed during discovery.

How to Deepen Attention on Pain Without Being Pushy

The instinct when someone says something painful is to move past it quickly — either to the solution or to the next question. Resist this. The moment they reveal something real is the moment to slow down, not speed up. Three techniques:

1
"Tell me more about that." The simplest and most underused phrase in sales. When they say something that carries emotional weight — stop and ask them to go deeper. Most closers move on. The ones who slow down get the real story.
2
Intentional silence. After they share something real, pause. Don't fill the space. They will. What they say in those next 5-10 seconds of silence is almost always more revealing than anything they said before it. Silence signals that you're processing what they said — that it mattered. That creates connection.
3
Reflect before you ask. Before asking the next question, reflect back what they just said — not to summarize, but to show you actually heard it. "So for the last two years, you've essentially been repeating the same month, just with different variables. That's what I'm hearing." When they confirm, they deepen their own awareness of it.
Pain Deepening Sequence Them: "Yeah, I've just been doing the same thing and not really growing."

You: [silence — 4-5 seconds]

"So for [X] years, you've been putting in the work, and the situation is basically the same. The effort has been there — the outcome hasn't moved."

Them: "Yeah, exactly."

"What does that actually feel like at this point? Like, where does that sit with you personally?"

[Now they tell you the real thing — not the situation, the feeling about the situation. That feeling is what you'll reference at the close.]
Attention Engineering Mistakes
01
Rushing through pain to get to the pitch. "Okay, I hear you — here's how we solve that." Every time you rush past pain to get to the pitch, you're pitching into a problem that doesn't feel big enough to justify the investment. Make the problem feel as big as it actually is before you present the solution.
02
Asking the next question too fast. When they say something significant, the next question should not be immediate. Silence, reflection, and follow-up are what turn a data-gathering call into a felt experience. The felt experience is what System 1 votes on.
03
Moving to future pace before pain is fully established. Future pace only creates urgency when it's contrasted against a pain that's vivid and real. If you jump to the dream before the problem has landed emotionally, the gap between them doesn't feel urgent — it just feels nice.

Priming Questions — Activating the Buying State Before the Pitch
35 min · Applied Neuroscience · Integration · Week Capstone
Lesson Objective
Learn what a priming question is, how it activates emotional states before conscious decisions are made, and build a custom priming sequence for your specific offer that you can deploy on your next call.
What Priming Is and Why It Works

Priming is a psychological phenomenon where exposure to one stimulus influences the response to a later stimulus — without the person being aware of the connection. In a famous study, participants asked to think about professors before a general knowledge quiz scored significantly higher than those asked to think about soccer hooligans first. The mental state activated by the first stimulus bled into performance on the second task.

On a sales call, the questions you ask in the first 15-20 minutes prime the emotional state the prospect is in when you start the pitch. Most closers ask questions purely to gather information. Elite closers ask questions that gather information AND prime the desired emotional state simultaneously. These are not two separate things — they're the same question, designed more intentionally.

The Four States You Need Active Before the Pitch
1. Dissatisfaction — their current reality feels insufficient, not just inconvenient
2. Desire — a specific, emotionally real vision of what they actually want
3. Possibility — the belief that change is actually achievable for someone like them
4. Trust — the felt sense that you're the right guide to get them there

If all four are active when you say "let me walk you through what this looks like" — the pitch lands in fertile ground. If any of them are missing, you're pitching uphill.
Priming Questions for Each State
Priming Dissatisfaction — Make the Gap Feel Real
These questions activate awareness of the gap between current reality and desired outcome. They don't create pain — they illuminate pain that already exists.

"If everything stayed exactly the same for the next 12 months — same income, same hours, same trajectory — how does that sit with you?"

"You've been doing this for [X] years. What's the honest answer to why you're not further along than you are?"

"What's the thing about your current situation that, when you're honest with yourself late at night, bothers you the most?"
Priming Desire — Activate the Pull of a Different Future
These questions get the prospect to construct and emotionally occupy a specific, sensory future. The more specific they get, the more real it becomes.

"If this actually worked and everything changed the way you're hoping — not the income number, the life piece — what does a normal Tuesday look like for you?"

"Who's around you when you get to that place? What does it feel like to have that conversation with them?"

"What would you stop doing the moment you didn't have to do it anymore? What's the first thing you'd let go?"
Priming Possibility — Build the Belief That Change Is Real
These questions help the prospect see themselves as someone who could actually achieve the desired outcome — not just someone who wants it.

"When you think about the people you know who've made a similar transition — what do you think made the difference for them vs. the ones who didn't?"

"What's something you've figured out in the past that you didn't think you could — where you surprised yourself?"

"What do you actually think is in the way? Like, the real reason this hasn't happened yet — what is it?"

Note: This last question also surfaces the core objection early — so you can handle it in discovery, not at the close.
Priming Trust — Build the Relationship Before the Pitch
Trust isn't primed with a question — it's primed with how you handle what they say. These aren't questions to ask; they're behaviors to practice during discovery that build the trust state.

Reflect before you ask. Don't jump to the next question. Show you actually processed what they said.

Say the harder truth. If something they say suggests a pattern they're not seeing, name it gently. This is the behavior that separates advisors from salespeople.

Be willing to say this isn't for them. Nothing builds trust faster than someone who prioritizes honesty over closing. If they sense you mean it, System 1 responds: this person is safe.
Putting It Together — A Full Priming Sequence

This is how the four priming states layer together across a discovery call. Notice that each question naturally leads to the next — this isn't a questionnaire, it's a conversation with intentional architecture underneath it.

Full Priming Sequence — Week 8 Integration [After rapport, framing the call]

"Let me start by understanding where you actually are. Not the highlight reel version — the real one. You've been doing [X] for [Y time]. What's actually working, and what's not?"

[They answer. Reflect. Go deeper.]

"You said [their words]. Tell me more about that. What does that actually cost you?"

[They go deeper. Silence. Let it land.]

"Okay. Flip side — if none of that was in the way anymore, and this was exactly what you hoped it would be, what changes first? I mean in your actual life, not just the income."

[They describe their future. Ask follow-ups until it's specific — names, places, feelings, not just numbers.]

"What do you think is actually in the way of that? Like, what's the honest answer to why that's not your life right now?"

[They surface the core objection — usually in disguise as a situation statement. You now have the real problem AND you've heard the core objection before the pitch, so you can handle it inside the pitch, not after.]

[After they've answered all four:]

"Let me make sure I've got this right before I walk you through anything..." [Mirror recap — they confirm. Now pitch into a room full of dissatisfaction, desire, emerging possibility, and trust. That's the buying state.]
Information Gathering (Most Closers)
  • Ask questions to fill a mental form
  • Move through questions at a pace that keeps things comfortable
  • Reach the pitch with a clear picture of the situation
  • Pitch into a neutral, evaluating prospect
  • Rely on the pitch content to create desire
Emotional State Engineering (Elite)
  • Ask questions that gather information AND activate states
  • Slow down at moments of emotional weight
  • Reach the pitch with all four states active
  • Pitch into a prospect already feeling dissatisfaction, desire, possibility, and trust
  • The pitch confirms what they've already decided
