YouTube — the real driver

One video pulled 1.3K views and generated $80K in revenue. Most recent podcast video: 12K views, ~$200K attributed revenue in two months. Long-form is the play. Short-form (reels, TikTok) builds ego, not pipeline.

What Actually Works

Product first. Always. The reason Sales.io scaled was because people got results. Zero refunds. Students renewed. LTV was $30K average. No ad budget can replace a product that delivers.

Content strategy

Start with YouTube. Post once a week. Give real, specific value — not lifestyle content, not top-of-funnel fluff. Call reviews, coaching recordings, raw insights. This is what converts. Consistency compounds.

Program structure (dripped curriculum)

JJ learned structure from Ali (who worked with Elon, runs 80 entrepreneurs, $750K/month). The model: drip modules weekly, each one unlocking the next. 1-on-1 coaching sessions tied directly to each module. Students have to move through it — no skipping, no overwhelm.

Arc: Clarity → Personal Foundations → Rapid Learning Protocol → Call Skills → Influence (behavioral science via Chase Hughes).

Booking model

Book a call directly — not a free community. Free communities require constant activity to stay alive and attract the wrong ICP. If you're charging $5K+, straight to call is cleaner and converts better.

Platform picks
  • Circle over Skool — looks proprietary, white-label feel
  • YouTube over Instagram for revenue
  • Long-form over short-form for conversion
What doesn't scale
  • Free communities without constant activity
  • Top-of-funnel lifestyle content
  • Co-founders without ironclad alignment
  • Big teams (JJ going from 12 → 3 people)

On Co-Founders & Partners

"Every time I've thought about bringing someone on, it almost only did me worse. Because you're always going to go through this battle of what do we want to do — and it's never ultimately your decision."

— JJ
The math problem

When JJ and Alex were doing $60K in revenue, splitting 50/50 meant $30K each. Then the tension: "I think I did more here." When money gets bigger, the split gets messier. It compounds fast.

The loyalty problem

People do what's in their own best interest — not a personal failing, just reality. Even coaches with genuine loyalty will eventually optimize for themselves. Clarity on roles is the only thing that prevents friction, and most partnerships never nail that.

"Unless you literally share a bank account like the Macklin brothers — there are always going to be ebbs and flows. People will do what's selfishly better for them."

— JJ
The credit problem

When you build something worth representing, people attach themselves to it retroactively. Scott called himself co-founder. Coaches who passed through Ashton Hall's team claim they scaled him. Build something real and expect this — but don't let it change your ownership of the story.

Advice to Max (Launching Solo)

  • You already have the hardest thing: real experience, real results. Most people in this space don't. That's your edge.
  • Don't wait to have everything built before you launch. Work expands to fill the time you give it. If you give yourself six weeks, do it in six weeks.
  • Dripping modules means you don't need everything recorded on day one. Record as you go.
  • You don't need someone to do this with you. Solitude isn't weakness — it's control.
  • Start YouTube now. Post once a week. Give real value. Build consistently. That's the whole strategy.
  • Hire an agency to help with YouTube ideas and production when you're ready — but you are the content.
  • Instagram is supplementary. YouTube is where revenue comes from.
  • Focus on the product being bulletproof. Everything else follows from students getting results.

If JJ started over: Post on YouTube once a week. Give good coaching consistently. That's it. Systems and strategy matter more than followers or reach.

The Fear Thread

Both JJ and Max shared the same pattern: they knew they were ready before they acted. Fear of not having enough experience. Fear of going alone. Fear of leaving the salary and the safety net. The universe tends to force the move eventually — Parker going bankrupt, a co-founder leaving, a natural ending.

JJ's take: the fear doesn't go away. You just have to do it anyway. And then you look back and realize the crutch — Alex, the co-founder, the partner — was never what made it work.

"There's this solitude that comes from being independent. And I think if you can master that — it actually becomes your biggest advantage."

— JJ
