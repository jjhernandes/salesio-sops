Calibrated Questions

The words you choose shape the world the prospect experiences on the call. "Why" triggers defensiveness. "How" and "What" invite collaboration. One word changes the entire dynamic.

The Problem With "Why"

"Why" questions demand justification. When you ask someone "Why did you do that?" or "Why haven't you fixed this yet?" their brain interprets it as an accusation. Even when you don't mean it that way, the word itself triggers a defensive state.

In a defensive state, the prospect stops thinking collaboratively. They start building a case for why they're not wrong. You've turned a conversation into a courtroom — and you're the prosecutor.

From Chris Voss — Never Split the Difference

"Why" activates the defensive centers of the brain. "How" and "What" activate the problem-solving centers. The same information comes out of the prospect's mouth, but the internal state they're in when they give it to you is completely different.

A prospect who answers a "how" question is a thinking partner. A prospect who answers a "why" question is a defendant. You cannot close a defendant.

Why vs. How / What — Full Comparison

"Why" Version (Defensive) "How" / "What" Version (Collaborative)
"Why haven't you fixed this yet?" "What's gotten in the way of solving this so far?"
"Why do you think you're stuck?" "What do you think is keeping you at this level?"
"Why did you reach out?" "What made you decide to book this call today?"
"Why is price a concern for you?" "What would make this feel like the right investment?"
"Why do you need to think about it?" "What specifically are you still working through?"
"Why haven't you made a change before now?" "What's been different about the timing now versus six months ago?"
"Why are you skeptical?" "What would need to be true for you to feel confident in this?"
"Why do you feel that way?" "What's driving that for you?"

How Calibrated Questions Guide Without Pushing

A calibrated question moves the conversation in a direction without the prospect feeling pushed. You're not telling them what to think. You're choosing which part of their thinking to activate.

"What would that look like for you six months from now?" activates forward-looking visioning. "What's gotten in the way so far?" activates problem articulation. Both are neutral in tone, but they direct the prospect's attention deliberately.

The Invisible Steering Wheel

A skilled closer uses calibrated questions like a steering wheel — making precise turns without the passenger realizing the car is being driven. The prospect feels like they're thinking freely. They are. You're just choosing the terrain they're thinking on.

The Silence That Follows

A good calibrated question produces silence. The prospect actually has to think. That silence is the sound of the question working. Resist the urge to fill it. Let them work through it. The answer they produce under genuine thinking is the one that's true — and the one you can actually use.

Call Exchange — The Difference in Real Time

VERSION 1 — "Why" Questions (Rep loses the frame)
Rep
"Why haven't you tried to fix your close rate before now?"
Implies negligence. Prospect immediately starts defending themselves.
Prospect
"I mean, I've been busy and I've tried some things. It's not like I've just been sitting here doing nothing."
Rep
"Right no I didn't mean — why do you think those things haven't worked though?"
Another "why." Now prospect is on defense again. The conversation is about justification, not discovery.
VERSION 2 — Calibrated Questions (Rep stays in discovery)
Rep
"What have you tried so far when it comes to improving your close rate?"
Neutral, open. Invites them to share what they've done without implying fault.
Prospect
"I've gone through NEPQ, tried a few scripts — they help a little but nothing's really moved the needle."
Rep
"What do you think has been missing from those approaches for you specifically?"
Now they're diagnosing their own problem. The rep didn't have to say anything — the prospect is selling themselves on what they're missing.
Prospect
"Honestly the deeper psychology stuff. Like I know what to say but I don't know how to actually read people."
