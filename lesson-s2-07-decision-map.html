The Decision Map

How people actually decide to buy — not the version sales training pretends. The real architecture of a buying decision is emotional first, always. Logic comes after to justify what the body already chose.

The Real Buying Decision Architecture

Every buying decision follows the same sequence, regardless of PACE type or price point. The sequence is: emotional trigger → desire state → logical justification → decision.

Most sales training tries to close at the logical justification stage. That's why most sales training produces average results. The decision is made before the logic. The logic is the story the prospect tells themselves to feel okay about something they already want.

Why this matters

If you understand that logic is justification — not the driver — you stop pitching logic to create the desire. You pitch to the emotional trigger first, let the desire state build, and then give them the logic they need to complete the justification loop. This is the actual sequence. Not the one in most sales books.

Emotional Decision First — Always

The neuroscience (simplified)

Decisions are processed in the limbic system — the emotional brain — before the prefrontal cortex (logic brain) even gets involved. By the time someone is consciously weighing pros and cons, the emotional brain has already leaned one direction. The logical evaluation is downstream of an emotional response.

This is not a theory. It's how human brains work. Which means every rep who leads with logic is fighting biology. The reps who close at high rates are, whether they know it or not, working with biology — they trigger the emotion first, then give the brain what it needs to catch up.

"People buy based on emotions and compensate logically."

— JJ Hernandez

The Three Internal Questions Every Prospect Asks

These happen unconsciously, but every prospect runs through them before buying. Your job is to answer all three — in this order.

01
Question One
"Do I trust this person?"
Before a prospect evaluates your offer, they're evaluating you. Credibility, presence, and composure answer this question. If the answer is yes, they'll keep listening. If no, your logic won't land no matter how good it is. This is answered in the first 5-10 minutes — not through what you say, but through how you show up.
Closed by: calm authority, genuine curiosity, not needing the sale. Killed by: desperation, over-justification, rattled energy.
02
Question Two
"Does this actually work for people like me?"
Proof and social validation answer this. The prospect is pattern-matching their situation against evidence that this solution works. They need to see themselves in the success story — the same starting point, the same doubts, the same type of situation. Generic testimonials don't close this. Specific, relevant proof does.
Closed by: specific student examples that mirror their situation. Killed by: vague claims, cherry-picked success stories that don't match their context.
03
Question Three
"Can I justify this decision?"
This is where logic comes in — but as the last step, not the first. The prospect needs something to say to themselves (and their partner, their logical mind, or their bank account) that makes the decision feel rational. ROI math, guarantee structure, and clear outcome commitments answer this. This is not the decision — this is the permission to act on the decision already made.
Closed by: clear ROI, guarantee, specific outcome commitment. Killed by: skipping questions 1 and 2 and jumping straight here.

Why Closing Logic Usually Doesn't Close Anything

The spreadsheet close doesn't work because by the time a rep is running ROI math, either the prospect is already bought in (in which case they don't need the math) or they're not bought in (in which case the math feels like pressure). Logic only lands when questions 1 and 2 are already answered.

The danger of pitching benefits before trust. When a rep jumps straight into program features and benefits without establishing trust and proof first, the prospect hears the logic but it doesn't attach to anything. It's like reading a list of facts about someone you've never met — technically true, but it doesn't move you. Sequence matters. Always close questions 1 and 2 before you pitch the logic of question 3.

Call Exchange — Working the Decision Map vs Skipping It

VERSION 1 — Rep goes straight to logic, skips emotional foundation
Prospect
"Tell me about the program."
Rep
"So it's a 12-week program, $7,500, includes weekly 1-on-1s, seven group sessions per week, access to our job board with 60-plus companies, and a money-back guarantee. Most people see significant income improvement within 60-90 days."
Skipped questions 1 and 2 entirely. Went straight to features and logic. Prospect hasn't been given a reason to care yet. Now they're evaluating rationally with no emotional context — the hardest possible position to close from.
Prospect
"Okay. That's a lot of money. I'll think about it."
VERSION 2 — Rep works the decision map in sequence
Prospect
"Tell me about the program."
Rep
"I will — but first I want to make sure I'm actually telling you the right things. What made you decide to get on a call today?"
Didn't pitch. Turned the conversation toward the emotional driver — question 1 and 2 can't be answered until you know why they're there.
Prospect
"Honestly I've been stuck at the same income level for like eight months and I'm just over it."
Rep
"Eight months is a long time to know you're capable of more and not have it move. What's the cost of another eight months looking the same?"
Building the emotional foundation. Question 1 is being answered through genuine curiosity. Question 2 comes next — after they've articulated the pain and the desire. Only then does logic close.
