Financial Discovery Framework

Built by reverse-engineering a real financial coaching sales call (Kyle Padilla, automatedrev.com — "Brent Bomer's Financial Freedom Application"). This is one of the most skilled numbers-discovery processes on record — worth studying line by line.

What I had to work with: the transcript covers roughly the first 50 minutes of a 122-minute call — the full numbers discovery and most of the psychological discovery phase, but not the price reveal, objection handling, or close. Everything below is built only from what's actually in the transcript. Send the rest and I'll complete the close mechanics.
1

The Numbers Technique

This is the part you specifically asked about. Kyle doesn't just ask "what's your income and debt" — he builds a full P&L with the prospect, live, catching bad data as it comes in.
Tactic 1
Never trust the first number — separate revenue from take-home
Brent opens with "$360,000" as his income. That's business revenue, not personal cash flow. Kyle doesn't build on it until he's stripped out business expenses and taxes to get the real number.
"What's the actual take-home?" → then walks through $2,000/mo expenses + $1,600/mo taxes held out → lands on ~$21–26K/month real.
Tactic 2
Necessities get isolated from debt service — as their own bucket, in a specific order
He explicitly defines the boundary: "necessities before any of the debt payback, the minimums there." This isolates the true cost of living from what's being consumed by debt — which is the number that reveals how much surplus actually exists to attack the problem.
Tactic 3
Build the total out loud, line by line, with a running subtotal
Mortgage → utilities → auto → groceries — each one gets its own question, and after each answer he restates the running total ("okay, that's $9,100... let's round to $10,000"). The prospect is doing the math with him, not just answering into a form. This is also how he catches bad data — Brent's utilities number initially included life insurance, and walking through it line by line surfaced that.
Tactic 4
Debts are itemized by total balance, by category — never by monthly payment
When Brent starts answering with a monthly payment, Kyle stops him immediately: "No, the total of the balances." Monthly payment tells you cash flow. Total balance tells you the real size of the problem and how long payoff actually takes. Credit cards, student loans (split from a loan co-signed for their daughter), personal loans, auto, mortgages — each gets counted separately.
Tactic 5
Assets get the same category-by-category treatment — and he asks why, not just how much
Liquid savings, retirement accounts, and other investments are each asked as distinct line items. He also probes the reasoning behind their allocation (why options trading vs. an ETF) — this isn't just data collection, it's understanding their actual relationship to risk and money.
Tactic 6
The math does the diagnosing — not his opinion
Once the numbers are laid out, Kyle never says "you're bad with money." He does the subtraction out loud ($25K in, ~$10K necessities, the rest going to debt and lifestyle) and lets the gap speak for itself. The prospect arrives at "we don't have a structured plan" on their own.
⚠️
Why this matters for a $7,500 sale: this same discipline — real take-home vs. headline income, true cost of living isolated from debt service, actual liquid capacity — is exactly what determines whether someone can do pay-in-full, a 2-way split, or needs financing. Getting it wrong either kills a real deal or sets up a payment plan that collapses in month two.
2

The Psychology Layer

The numbers build the case. These techniques are what make the prospect feel the case, in their own words, before any pitch happens.
Tactic 7
Ask for the pattern before the point-in-time number
Before any dollar figure, Kyle asks "how long has that been going on?" Learning this is a 30-year cycle — not a one-time setback — raises the emotional stakes far more than any single number could.
Tactic 8
Reflect their own soft language back at them
When Dawn says she "makes it work," Kyle doesn't let it pass — he names what it actually means: "making it work... mostly means just surviving." This uses the prospect's own words to gently dismantle their own excuse, instead of arguing with them.
Tactic 9
Let them name the root cause — never say it first
"What do you think has been missing?" — Brent arrives at "discipline" himself. Kyle never introduces the word. A diagnosis someone gives themselves is one they'll defend; a diagnosis you hand them is one they'll argue with.
Tactic 10
Use an analogy to make them pitch themselves
The "friend who's 300 pounds and needs to lose weight" analogy is the single best move in the call. Kyle asks what that friend should do — the prospects answer "get a trainer" and "accountability" unprompted. He then just draws the parallel to coaching. He never pitches the offer directly; they describe the solution before he's said a word about his program.
Tactic 11
Future-pace before any mention of next steps
Only after the pattern, the real numbers, the emotional cost ("beholden to financial institutions"), and a vivid picture of the win (the round-the-world cruise, the RV, "freedom of choice") does anything resembling a next step get mentioned. The vision gets fully built before the ask.
3

The Framework — 10 Stages

Generalized from what's above. Use this as a numbers-discovery layer inside your existing call — it slots naturally into Current Reality and Problem Awareness on the standard Sales Call Script.
1
Set the Diagnostic Frame
Remove the sales pressure before asking for real numbers

Tell them plainly this part of the call is about understanding their real situation — not a lead-in to a pitch. People give inflated or defensive numbers when they think they're being evaluated for a sale. They give real numbers when they think they're being helped.

💡
Say something close to: "I want to understand your real situation first. Either way, my goal is to leave you better off than when we started this call."
2
Establish the Pattern, Not Just the Snapshot
Ask how long, before you ask how much

"How long has this been the reality?" surfaces whether you're dealing with a one-time setback or a decades-long cycle — and it does more to build urgency than any number that comes later.

3
Get Real Income, Not Headline Income
Strip revenue down to actual take-home before building anything on top of it

If they lead with a business or gross figure, don't build on it. Ask what actually lands in their hands after expenses and taxes. This is the number every other calculation depends on — get it wrong and the whole picture is wrong.

4
Separate Necessities From Everything Else
Build the true cost of living, with debt service explicitly excluded

Ask for housing, utilities, transportation, food — one at a time, out loud — and explicitly exclude any debt payments from this number. State the running total after each answer so they're following the math with you, not just answering questions into a void.

💡
This number is what reveals real surplus — the actual capacity available to solve the problem, separate from what's currently being eaten by debt.
5
Itemize Debt by Total Balance, By Category
Never accept a monthly payment when you asked for a balance

Credit cards, personal loans, student loans, auto, mortgage — each as its own line, each as a total balance. If they answer with a payment amount, stop and clarify immediately. Confusing the two produces a completely wrong picture of the actual problem size.

6
Inventory Assets — and Ask Why
Understand their existing relationship to money, not just the balances

Liquid savings, retirement, other investments — each as its own category. Then ask why it's allocated the way it is. The reasoning tells you more about how they'll behave in a program than the number does.

7
Do the Math Out Loud, Together
Let the gap diagnose the problem — don't diagnose it yourself

Income minus necessities minus debt service. Say it out loud. Don't editorialize. The size of the gap (or the size of what's leaking to undisciplined spending) makes the case without you having to make it.

8
Let Them Name the Real Problem
Ask, don't tell

"What do you think has actually gotten in the way?" A self-given diagnosis gets defended. A diagnosis you hand them gets argued with.

9
Use an Analogy to Let Them Pitch Themselves
Get them to describe the solution before you introduce it

Find an analogy adjacent to their actual problem — physical health, a skill they already respect experts in — and ask what that person in the analogy should do. Let them say "get a coach" or "get accountability" themselves. Then draw the parallel. This is far more persuasive than any pitch line.

10
Future-Pace the Vision Before Any Offer Talk
Build the win in vivid, personal detail before transitioning to the program

Ask what their life actually looks like once this is solved — not abstractly, specifically. What they'd do, how they'd feel, what they'd stop being beholden to. Only once this picture is fully built does it make sense to move toward presenting how you'd get them there.
