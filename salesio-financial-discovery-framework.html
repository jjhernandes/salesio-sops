Financial Discovery Framework

Built by reverse-engineering a real financial coaching sales call (Samuel Leeland, automatedrev.com — "Brooke Bekkedahl's Financial Freedom Application"). A W-2 household doing wealth-building and retirement planning, not debt cleanup — one of the cleanest live-numbers discovery processes on record.

What I had to work with: the transcript covers roughly the first 42 minutes of a 120-minute call — full rapport, the full cash flow and net worth discovery, and the goal-setting phase (retirement number, college savings, tax awareness). It cuts off right as Samuel starts quantifying a margin-recovery example, before the pitch, price reveal, objections, or close. Everything below is built only from what's actually in the transcript. 1

The Numbers Technique

Samuel doesn't just ask "what do you make and what do you owe" — he builds the full picture live with the prospect, then uses one real calculation to turn a vague "are we okay?" into a hard, personal number.
Tactic 1
Disarm the sales frame before a single number is asked for
Before anything financial comes up, Samuel states his objective plainly: "Frankly, we don't need another client... if what you have right now is perfect, God bless, I can lay my head on the pillow." Every number Brooke gives afterward is given to someone positioned as an evaluator of her situation, not a seller of a program.
Tactic 2
Announce the segments out loud, one at a time
"Let's go segment by segment — first cash flow, then investments, then debt management." The prospect always knows what's coming next, which lowers resistance and stops the conversation from feeling like an interrogation.
Tactic 3
Set the target structure before pulling the actuals
Before asking what hits the account monthly, he asks what the ideal structure would be — live off base salary, treat commission as upside. That answer becomes the frame every actual number gets measured against, instead of numbers just sitting there uninterpreted.
Tactic 4
Build the running total live, line by line
Mortgage → student loan → childcare → utilities → groceries → insurance → HOA → phone — each its own question, narrated as a running subtotal. Critical-core expenses get isolated from discretionary spend ("restaurants, coffee, Amazon, fitness") without judgment attached to either bucket.
"$11,100 in critical core expenses" vs. "another $1,000 to $1,300" in discretionary — named separately, not blended.
Tactic 5
Every debt gets a balance AND an interest rate — never just one
Mortgage, student loan, private family loan — each is asked for both figures, because the entire pay-down-vs-invest decision later depends on the rate, not just the size. A 0% loan, a 4% loan, and a 3.5% mortgage all get treated completely differently once the rate is known.
Tactic 6
Normalize with a real comparison before any diagnosis
Right after hearing her numbers, Samuel drops in an anonymized reference point: "I had a conversation with a couple yesterday, $1.3 million in consumer debt... their mortgage is 50% of their income." Brooke's situation instantly reads as strong instead of alarming — which keeps her open instead of defensive for the numbers still to come.
Tactic 7
Calculate the actual finish line, live, in front of them
Instead of "you should save more," he runs the 4% rule out loud using her own target monthly spend: $12,000/month × 300 = $3.6M. "Are we on track" becomes one concrete number she now owns.
Tactic 8
Quantify the cost of the default option — the "devil's number"
Current savings rate (6.7%) vs. recommended (20%) is shown as both a percentage gap AND a time gap — 19 years vs. 14–15 years to the number. Then he quantifies what handing that money to an advisor would cost over the same span: "$666,000 in fees." Doing nothing, or defaulting to an advisor, is reframed as the expensive choice — not the safe one.
⚠️
Why this matters for a $7,500 sale: the same discipline — real net income split from headline income, critical-core isolated from discretionary spend, every debt tied to a rate not just a balance — is exactly what determines whether someone can do pay-in-full, a split, or needs financing. Getting it wrong either kills a real deal or sets up a payment plan that collapses in month two.

The Psychology Layer

The numbers build the case. These techniques are what make the prospect feel the case, in her own words, before any pitch happens.
Tactic 9
Handle an absent decision-maker without losing a beat
When Brooke explains her husband can't make it and that she's the financial decision-maker, Samuel doesn't probe or push back — "Your choice, whatever. Fair enough. It doesn't change too much for me." Instant reassignment of authority, zero friction, momentum never breaks.
Tactic 10
Open with real rapport, not a scripted icebreaker
The Mariners/Blue Jays banter is specific, personal, and unscripted — it only works because it's genuine back-and-forth, not a template line. It buys the trust needed for a stranger to hand over exact account balances twenty minutes later.
Tactic 11
State the anti-sales frame explicitly, before numbers
Said before anything financial is discussed: "I don't know if we're the right people to help at all until we go through the discovery process." This is also Tactic 1 — it does double duty because it's both a numbers-technique unlock and the emotional permission that makes the whole rest of the call possible.
Tactic 12
Build life-context and vision before circling back to the pitch
Kids' ages, whether Brooke should keep working, what retirement actually looks like day to day — all surfaced before any product is named. By the time the numbers get run, they're being run against a life she's already described wanting, not a generic retirement target.
Tactic 13
Reflect their stated identity back as the reason the plan fits them
Brooke volunteers that she's "a bit of a control freak" who wants to understand things herself, not hand off blind trust to an advisor. Samuel's entire pitch angle — self-sufficiency, "that advisor becomes obsolete, you don't need them" — is the exact mirror of what she said she wants. He never has to argue for it; it's already her own stated preference.

The Framework — 10 Stages

Generalized from what's above. Use this as a numbers-discovery layer inside your existing call — it slots naturally into Current Reality and Problem Awareness on the standard Sales Call Script.
1
Disarm the Frame Before Any Numbers
Say plainly you're not there to upsell — before a single figure is asked for

State directly that if what they have is already working, you'll tell them so, and that's a fine outcome. People give real numbers when they believe they're being helped, not evaluated for a sale.

💡
Say something close to: "My objective is to figure out where you're actually at — regardless of whether we end up working together. If what you have is perfect, great, I'll tell you that."
2
Get the Open-Ended Story First
Ask what's hardest before opening a single spreadsheet

"What's the biggest challenge you're facing right now?" lets them self-report the real pain point unprompted, in their own words — before any number anchors or narrows the conversation.

3
Set the Target Structure Before the Actuals
Define what "on track" means before doing any math

Ask what the ideal setup would look like — e.g. living off base income with variable comp as pure upside — before pulling real monthly figures. Every actual number that follows now has something to be measured against.

4
Build Cash Flow Line by Line, Out Loud
Narrate a running total; separate critical-core from discretionary

Announce the segment ("let's talk cash flow first, then investments, then debt"), then go housing, insurance, childcare, groceries, transportation — one at a time, restating the running total after each answer. Keep discretionary spend (dining, subscriptions, shopping) in its own separate bucket, without judgment attached to either number.

💡
This is what reveals real surplus — the actual capacity available before anything gets pitched.
5
Get Every Debt's Balance AND Rate
Never accept a balance alone

Mortgage, loans, credit cards — each gets both numbers. The rate is what the entire later pay-down-vs-invest recommendation hinges on; skipping it means you can't actually answer their real question.

6
Normalize With a Real Comparison
Contextualize before they can spiral into shame or panic

Drop in a real, anonymized reference point right after hearing their numbers — a client in a genuinely worse spot. This keeps them open and talking instead of defensive, right at the point where the numbers could otherwise trigger embarrassment.

7
Calculate the Actual Finish Line, Live
Turn "are we okay?" into one concrete number, in front of them

Use a real formula (the 4% rule, or equivalent) with their own target numbers to produce one hard figure — the number they're actually working toward. Vague anxiety becomes a specific, ownable target.

8
Show Current Rate vs. Target Rate — in Both % and Years
Percentages don't land emotionally; time does

Show the gap between what they're doing now and the recommended benchmark as a percentage, then immediately translate it into years to the goal at each rate. "5 fewer years of working" moves people in a way "13% more" never will.

9
Quantify the Cost of the Default Option
Make inaction, or the passive alternative, sound expensive

Calculate what doing nothing — or handing the problem to an advisor — actually costs over the same timeframe, in real dollars. This reframes "pay us" against "pay someone/something else, or pay in lost time," not against "pay nothing."

10
Stack Their Own Gaps Into the Case for Help
List back — in their language — every unaddressed problem surfaced during discovery

Right before transitioning to the pitch, name each gap that came up naturally during the call: no tax strategy, no will, no plan for the kids, idle cash sitting uninvested. Individually minor; said together as a list, right before the ask, they compound into a case the prospect essentially built themselves.
