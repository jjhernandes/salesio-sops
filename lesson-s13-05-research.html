Research a Company in Under 10 Minutes

You do not need hours of research to know whether a company is worth applying to. You need 10 focused minutes and the right sources.

The 10-Minute Research Protocol

This protocol runs before you record your Loom, not before the discovery call. By the time you are on the call, you have already done this. The research has two purposes: filter out bad fits before you invest time, and give you specifics to reference in your Loom and in your questions on the call.

2m
Source 1
LinkedIn — Founder and Company Page
Search the founder or operator. Check: How long have they been building this company? What is their background? What have they built before? Then check the company page: size, growth trajectory, how long it has existed, how active they are. A company that has been growing steadily for 2+ years is very different from one that launched six weeks ago.
Look for: employee growth (good sign), rep profiles who have been there 6+ months (longevity signal), recent posts from leadership showing business activity.
2m
Source 2
Glassdoor — Culture and Rep Reviews
Search the company on Glassdoor. Not to disqualify them on one bad review, but to identify patterns. Do multiple reviews mention the same issues? Do reviewers mention how long they lasted? What do former sales reps specifically say about commission, pipeline quality, and leadership communication?
One 2-star review from a disgruntled ex-employee is noise. Five reviews over 12 months citing "commissions not paid on time" is signal. Read for patterns, not individual scores.
3m
Source 3
Google — Product Reviews and Press
Search the company name plus "reviews," "complaints," or "scam." Check Trustpilot, Reddit, and any industry forums. Also look for press coverage: were they featured anywhere? Have they been involved in disputes? A product with strong customer reviews is a fundamentally easier sell. A product with a reputation problem is friction on every call you make.
Also search the founder's name directly. Public credibility matters. A founder who is active, respected, and visible in their space gives you talking points in your Loom and makes the offer easier to close.
3m
Source 4
The Offer Page and Sales Materials
Go to the company's website and look at how they present the offer. What claims are they making? What does the customer journey look like? Is the offer positioned clearly, or is it vague? Can you find testimonials? A polished, specific offer page tells you the company takes their product seriously. A generic, low-effort page tells you something different.
Also check their content on Instagram, YouTube, or TikTok. Active content creation signals an engaged operator with a marketing strategy. That means more leads coming in for you to close.

The One Question Your Research Should Answer

After 10 minutes, you should be able to answer one question with confidence: Does this company treat its reps fairly and does its product deliver what it promises?

That is it. Everything else is detail. If you cannot answer that question with at least a provisional yes, you have enough to either probe harder on the discovery call or walk away before you invest more time.

What "Fairly" Actually Means

It means commission is paid on time, as agreed. It means leadership communicates when there are problems. It means reps are not blamed for systemic pipeline or product issues. It means the company is structured to support rep performance, not just extract it.

Using Research in the Discovery Call

What You Found How to Use It
Founder has been building this company for 3 years "I saw you've been building this for three years — what's the trajectory looked like on the revenue side?"
Glassdoor reviews mention communication issues "What does communication between reps and leadership look like week to week on your team?"
Strong product reviews from customers Reference it in your Loom — "Your Trustpilot reviews show a pattern of customers who got real results. That tells me the offer can back up the close."
Active social content with a real following Ask about lead source: "I see you're active on YouTube — what percentage of your leads come from content versus paid?"

Do not interrogate during the discovery call. Your research informs your questions — it does not become a list of concerns you read aloud. Ask one or two specific, curious questions that show you did your homework. That positions you as the prepared, serious rep who evaluates partners — not the desperate applicant who just wants any role.
